# ARSON – LEGISLATION
### Arson - Section 267, Crimes Act 1961 
****LEGISLATION****
> Every one commits arson and is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 14 years who—
> intentionally or recklessly damages by fire or by means of any explosive any property if he or she knows or ought to know that danger to life is likely to ensue; or
> intentionally or recklessly, and without claim of right, damages by fire or by means of any explosive any immovable property, or any vehicle, ship, or aircraft, in which that person has no interest; or
> intentionally damages by fire or by means of any explosive any immovable property, or any vehicle, ship, or aircraft, with intent to obtain any benefit, or to cause loss to any other person.
> Every one commits arson and is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 7 years who—
> intentionally or recklessly, and without claim of right, damages by fire or by means of any explosive any property in which that person has no interest (other than property referred to in subsection (1)); or
> intentionally or recklessly damages by fire or by means of any explosive any property (other than property referred to in subsection (1)) with intent to obtain any benefit, or with intent to cause loss to any other person.

# INTRODUCTION	
A charge of arson is the most serious charge under the Crimes Act 1961 in **relation to damage to property**. It extends not only to damage that has been caused by fire but also to damage caused by explosives.

As with other damage to property offences, the mental state of an offender and the status of the property that has been damaged will determine the direction of your investigation, the appropriate charge, and the length of the maximum sentence.

The main offences of arson are found in s267 (1) and 267 (2). The layout of the two sections as they appear in legislation is helpful. Essentially, the sections ‘*rank*’ the seriousness of the offence based on danger to human life and the value of the property damaged – from *‘most’* serious to ‘*least’*.

All arson charges require proof that a property has been damaged by fire or explosives. However, there are several variations within the section – **all **of which fall under the umbrella of the arson offence. Which variation a person can be liable for will depend on the existence, or absence, of the additional elements that aggravate the offending (and increase the likely sentence). They include:

- a likely risk to life,
- having no interest in the property damaged,
- an intention to obtain a benefit or cause a loss.
# UNDERSTANDING ARSON 
### The actus reus for arson 
The actus reus (that which constitutes the physical act of the offence) can be summed up as a combination of elements, depending on the section.

The actus reus for arson can be summed up as damaging property by fire or damaging property by means of an explosive. This assumes that a physical act or omission has been committed that initiates or causes a fire that in turn, damages property.

Remember, the actus reus is accompanied by a general mens rea in that the physical act of lighting a fire **must** be deliberately or intentionally lit. Arson also includes ‘acts committed recklessly as criminal’.

Finally, the act of lighting a fire and damaging property must also be committed **without** a claim of right defence.

Arson:

- Intentionally OR recklessly, (general mens rea)
- Damages by fire OR explosive,
- Without claim of right, (defence)
- Any property OR immovable property OR vehicle OR ship OR aircraft.
### The General Mens Rea of Arson – Intentionally or Recklessly
**Every** arson offence requires proof that the offender has either intentionally **or** recklessly done an act that caused damage by fire. This is the general mens rea required for the criminal act of arson.

The criminal act of arson requires proof of what was on the mind of the offender when committing the physical act. Arson can either be committed by **intending** damage to the property or being **reckless of the fact**. In the latter, the defendant only needs to have a conscious appreciation of the risk of damage and elect to take it.

A thorough investigation looks to prove what the intention of the offender was at the point the fire was lit, and to prove the damage to property that resulted.

### Intentionally vs Accidentally
Intention is not specifically defined in the Crimes Act 1961 but is a mental attitude with which an individual acts. Therefore, it cannot ordinarily be directly proved but must be inferred from surrounding facts and circumstances.

In criminal law, the concept of ‘intentionally’ comes under the umbrella of general *mens rea*, which refers to a criminal or wrongful purpose accompanying the guilty act. ‘Intentionally’ refers only to the state of mind with which the act is done, as spelt out in this definition:

An intentional Arson requires the lighting of a fire deliberately, voluntarily and purposefully. If a person innocently causes harm, then she or he lacks general mens rea and, under this concept, should not be criminally prosecuted.

### Examples of intentional arson
Consider the following examples of intentional acts to damage by fire.

Remember, ‘intentionality’ is an intent to commit the act. It can be proved with the application of **R v Collister **while you are gathering evidence of the surrounding circumstances.

- Placing a petrol-soaked rag into a petrol tank of a car and lighting it,
- Making an explosive device, placing it in a building and setting it off remotely,
- Lighting the contents of a rubbish bin,
- Using a candle to light curtains.
### Accidental fires
The cause of fires can be accidental – that is, there is no intent to damage by fire and no consideration given to the risk of damage by fire (reckless intent). As an investigator you may be required to examine the circumstances of a fire to determine whether it has been caused accidentally or recklessly, and therefore whether there is criminal liability. There may be a fine line between a reckless and accidental fire, so gathering as much evidence as possible is important.

***An accidental fire example:*** A person during a power cut uses candles as a light source. While walking down the hall with the candle in hand, he trips over. The candle falls to the floor and as a result the carpet and nearby curtains of the house ignite, and the house is burnt to the ground.

Here there is no intent or deliberate act to light the fire which caused the resulting damage, and therefore no criminality is attached.

### Recklessly 
The law criminalises damaging by fire where the act that ignites the fire is caused irresponsibly by society’s standards. Acting “*recklessly*” involves the conscious and deliberate taking of an unjustified risk. Recklessness is an element that appears in the legislation for many offences and retains the same meaning/definition.

While it is necessary to prove that the defendant foresaw the risk of injury to others or damage caused, it is not necessary to prove that they recognised the extent of the injury or damage that would result.

Recklessness is a test based on the defendant’s appreciation of the risk of the offence and their decision to run that risk anyway.

****CASE LAW****
> CAMERON v R
> Recklessness is established if:
> the defendant recognised that there was a real possibility that:
> his or her actions would bring about the proscribed result; and/or
> that the proscribed circumstances existed; and
> having regard to that risk those actions were unreasonable.

In ***R v Tipple**** *the Court* *suggested that as a general rule, “*recklessness*” is to be given the subjective meaning. The concept of recklessness is subjective in that it requires the offender to know of, or have a conscious appreciation of the relevant risk, and it may be said that it requires “*a deliberate decision to run the risk*”.

### Proving Recklessness
When recklessness is an element in an offence, the following must be proved:

- the defendant recognised that there was a real possibility that:
- his or her actions would bring about the proscribed result; and/or
- that the proscribed circumstances existed; and
- having regard to that risk those actions were unreasonable.
- That the defendant consciously and deliberately ran a risk (a subjective test)
- That the risk was one that was unreasonable to take in the circumstances as they were known to the defendant (objective test – based on whether a reasonable person would have taken the risk).
The reckless intent can be proved with the application of ***R v Collister*** – gathering evidence of the surrounding circumstances. Examples of reckless acts that may risk life or damage by fire:

- Using fireworks in a dry meadow or field,
- Throwing a lit cigarette in a rubbish bin,
- Doing a burn-off of forestry or crops,
- Using a piece of machinery that produces sparks.
### Charging
Only** **one of the elements of ‘intentionally’ or ‘recklessly’ can be charged. For example, you cannot charge a person with both intentionally **and** recklessly damaging by fire. Which level of mens rea you charge with will be based on the circumstances of the event and the evidence your investigation uncovers.

### Proving damages by fire
Damage is not specifically defined in the Act. What will constitute damage depends on the facts of a specific case and the degree to which property has been changed because of the fire or explosives.

In essence, this element requires two separate sub-elements:

- The first is that *there is damage*, and the second,
- that the damage was either *caused by fire or explosive.*
The prosecution must prove that the specified property has been *“damaged”* by fire or explosive as a result of the defendant’s actions. The damage must be causally related to either a fire or the use of explosives.

However, this does not mean that the flames of a fire need to be the **direct **cause of the damage. In a situation where property has been scorched or charred because of proximity to a fire, this will fall within the requirement of the fire causing the damage. Melting, blistering of paint or significant smoke damage may also be sufficient.

This concept of causation is explained in the DDP Basic Concepts Lesson.

### Damage
Although fire damage will often involve burning or charring, it is not necessary that the property **is set alight**; melting, blistering of paint or significant smoke damage may be sufficient.

In ***R v Archer ***the defendant was charged with arson after setting fire to some toilet paper in the bathroom of a church, resulting in *“blackening*” to the top of the sink.

In this matter, the Court ruled that there was insufficient evidence to determine whether the blackening was charring, which would have been damage, or just a deposit of soot, which might or might not have amounted to damage depending on the extent of it.

The Court of Appeal held that, in general terms, any change in an object that impairs its value or usefulness may constitute damage; whether such a change amounts to damage is a matter of fact and degree, to be determined on a case-by-case basis.

****CASE LAW****
> ***R V ARCHER ***
> *Property may be damaged if it suffers permanent or temporary physical harm or permanent or temporary impairment of its use or value.*

As damage could not be proved adequately to support a conviction for arson, ***Archer*** was instead convicted of attempted arson under section 268.

Archer not only helps define what damage is, but is a useful reminder for investigators that they must **prove **the exact nature of the damage as well as that the **cause** of that damage was specifically the result of a fire.

### Fire
Fire is the result of the process of combustion, a chemical reaction between fuel and oxygen, triggered by heat. For fire to start or continue, each of the three elements – fuel, oxygen, and heat – must be present in the correct proportions.

### Explosive

Hazardous Substances and New Organisms Act 1996

### Molotov cocktails
A common misconception is that a Molotov cocktail is an explosive. This misunderstanding can arise from the fact it is sometimes referred to as a *‘petrol bomb’*.

A Molotov cocktail, also known as a petrol bomb, gasoline bomb, bottle bomb, poor man's grenade, Molotovin koktaili, polttopullo, firebomb, fire bottle or just Molotov, sometimes shortened as Molly, is a generic name used for a variety of bottle-based improvised incendiary weapons.

****It is a crude incendiary device typically consisting of a bottle filled with flammable liquid and with a means of ignition – essentially an accelerant contained within a breakable vessel so that when it shatters the accelerant is spread and ignited by the flames from the burning rag or similar attached to the vessel.

The appropriate charge in cases where a petrol bomb/Molotov cocktail is used is “damages by fire” **NOT** by explosive. Knowledge of this classification is important for practical investigation, such as how to approach a fire/explosive scene or when interviewing a suspect.

### Claim of right
**LEGISLATION**
> Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961
> Interpretation
> Claim of right, in relation to any act, means a belief at the time of the act in a proprietary or possessory right in property in relation to which the offence is alleged to have been committed, although that belief may be based on ignorance or mistake of fact or of any matter of law other than the enactment against which the offence is alleged to have been committed.

“Claim of right” is essentially a defence to what would otherwise be a criminal act. It is an ‘internal’ defence, in that it is written into section 267.

Claim of right attaches to **all** offences dealing with **property** and stems from the idea that an individual can deal with their own property how they choose – including destroying or damaging it.

Claim of right is a **belief** held by a person they hold some sort of legal interest (often financial or ownership) in the property damaged. As that right is believed to be held, the defendant is **not** liable for arson.

### Example:
A farmer can burn her/his own crops or scrub which she/he owns. However, the farmer **may** be committing a criminal act if the farmer, in burning their own crops, damages **neighbouring** property, over which they have no claim of right.

Similarly, a person is free to burn their own documents. However, take the example of a person who creates documents in the course of employment, in this case architectural plans, which he burns, destroying them. These documents are the property of the architectural firm.

Despite this, the person genuinely believes that because he created the drawings, he owned them. It is his **belief **that is key here. As an investigator you are required to investigate this belief (evidence it is genuinely held) to determine whether the person has this defence.

Without knowledge or understanding of this defence of claim of right in relation to property damaged by fire, you will not be able to carry out the right enquiries or ask appropriate questions in an interview.

### Property 
The charge of arson sets out two different categories of property:

- Immovable property, aircraft, ships, and vehicles on the one hand.
- A broader category of property in general on the other.
**LEGISLATION**
> Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961
> Interpretation
> Property includes real and personal property, and any estate or interest in any real or personal property, money, electricity, and any debt, and anything in action, and any other right or interest.

### Immovable property
The Crimes Act 1961 does not define immovable property. In general, it relates to buildings and land and things growing on land, such as forests.

Property will be considered immovable if it is currently fixed in place and unable to be moved, even though it may be possible to make it movable. Therefore, for example, a relocatable building that is currently fixed to foundations is ‘immovable’ even if, with the expenditure of labour, it could be released from its foundations and transported elsewhere.

Vehicles, aircraft, and ships also form part of the category of property that includes immovable property. Where this category of property is the subject of the offending, a charge under s267(1) of the Act can be laid, with a maximum sentence of 14 years imprisonment, so long as there is at least one aggravating factor.

The one exception to this is where there is a danger to life. Here in s267(1), it is irrelevant whether the property is immovable or not, due to the serious nature of the charge.

### Property includes intangible property
While the statutory definition of property in Section 2 (page 23, 2(1), Crimes Act 1961: ‘Property’) is wide and includes a range of intangible things, s267 dictates that **only tangible property** is capable of being damaged directly by fire or explosive.

For example, it is impossible to set fire to data. However, the computer on which that data is held can be set alight.

In this way, intangible property may be damaged indirectly – the data stored on the computer may become irretrievable when the computer is damaged. This is indirect damage to *“any other right or interest”* *(Section 2 definition of property).*

# THE SPECIFIC *MENS REA* FOR ARSON
The mens rea (*that which constitutes the mental requirement of the offence*) can be summed up as the intention or knowledge of wrongdoing that constitutes part of the crime, or literally “guilty mind”. Mens rea may or may not be spelt out by the elements of the offence but must be proven regardless to meet evidential sufficiency.

The general mens rea accompanying the act of damaging by fire (intentionally or recklessly) has been considered on page 14. In addition to this intentional or reckless act, section 267 stipulates a range of **specific knowledge or intention** that must also be investigated depending on the charge.

Section 267 (1) (a) requires specific **knowledge** to be proved:

- knew (or ought to have known) that there was a likelihood of danger to life;
Section 267 (1) (b) (c) and (2) require specific **intentions **to be proved in relation to the act of damaging the property by fire or explosive:

- had no interest in the property;
- had an intention to obtain any benefit or cause a loss.
### Knowing or ought to know danger to life is likely
Committing arson when there is danger to life has its own specific charge. This is the most serious charge of arson. Section 267(1) (a) specifies that alternatively, the defendant either knowingly puts another person’s life at risk or that they ought to have known that another person’s life was at risk.

What is required regarding an offender’s knowledge of the danger to life can range from direct knowledge to negligence. A charge of intending to cause danger to life will be more serious than being reckless or negligent about the knowledge of danger. However, both will fall within the same charge, with 14 years imprisonment as the maximum available sentence. The difference between reckless knowledge and direct knowledge is likely to be reflected in the end sentence.

**Proof **of a *specific knowledge that danger to life was likely* is required in this section for evidential sufficiency. Knowledge is a type of mens rea and can be direct (*knew danger to life was likely*) or have similarities with recklessness (*ought to have known danger to life was likely*).

Investigation into what knowledge the defendant **actually had** is important. “Knowing” is defined in case law and is more than just a suspicion or awareness. (***R v Crooks***)

****LEARN****
> ***Simester &amp; Brookbanks**** *
> *opines that “knowing” means “knowing, or correctly believing” ... the belief must be a correct one, where the belief is wrong, a person cannot know something:*

**CASE LAW**
> R v Crooks
> Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.

The phrase *“ought to know”* then brings an objective element to the concept of knowing, which is also important to investigate. As a rule, the courts interpret *“ought to know”* from the objective point of view of the ordinary person. Here it is sufficient that the prosecution prove that an ordinary individual would have realised the risk to life that their actions posed even although that risk may not have been directly intended.

**THE TEST FOR KNOWLEDGE**
> THE TEST FOR KNOWLEDGE
> The provision of “knows or ought to know” involves a mixed subjective / objective test as to the defendant’s degree of knowledge.
> Subjective Test
> The first question (the subjective test) is: What was the defendant thinking at the time? Did the defendant know that human life was likely to be endangered by his actions?
> Objective Test
> If there is insufficient evidence that the defendant was conscious of the risk, the next question (the objective test) is: What would a reasonable person have thought in the same circumstances? Would a reasonable person have recognised the risk to life?

### Danger to life
‘Life’ is not defined in the Crimes Act 1961. However, case law makes it clear that the risk of “danger to life” must relate to a person who is a third-party. Those intending to harm themselves are not captured by this offence.

Put simply: the danger must be to the life of someone **other** than the defendant.

**CASE LAW**
> R v SMITH BC9469002
> Common sense requires that ‘danger to life’ should be interpreted as “danger to the life of some person other than the setter of the fire.”

‘Life’ in this context means human life not an animal’s. When animal life is endangered, other charges may be appropriate.

### Likely to ensue
While the use of the term “knows” in s267 of the Crimes Act 1961 in relation to danger to life appears to set a high threshold for the mental element, the later use of the word “likely” lessens this threshold. The use of “likely” means that a defendant merely has to know that there is a chance, or a risk, of danger to life.

The addition of “ought to know” extends to the concept of objectiveness. Where an ordinary, prudent individual would have known that there was a likely risk to life as a result of his or her actions, this will suffice to meet the mental requirement of “ought to know”.

However, such a defendant’s reduced culpability can be, and should be, considered at sentencing.

From an investigative point of view, the knowledge test (page 26) gives useful guidance on how to investigate and prove this element.

**APPLICATION**
> How do you investigate and gather evidence that a person ought to have known that his or her action in lighting a fire would put other people’s lives in danger?
> What questions could you ask of witnesses and suspects of arson that covers off the legal test for knowledge?

### Section 267(1)(b), No interest in property 
Another of the aggravating factors (elements required to be proved by the prosecution depending upon the subsection charged) is that the offender has no interest in the property damaged.

The reason this is considered an aggravating factor**(and/or an example of specific intent), **is that this section is often associated with the fire-setting behaviours of malicious destruction, wanton vandalism, or careless destruction.

### Example:
- A pyromaniac who lights fire to an abandoned house,
- Teenagers letting off fireworks that cause damage to a remote section of farmland,
- Setting fire to rubbish bins or letterboxes,
- Doing a crop burn that gets out of control and destroys a piece of neighbouring forest,
- Setting fire to a parked car or other vehicle.
### What is ‘no interest’?
While at first glance it may appear that determining whether someone has an interest in the property is a simple exercise, it is noteworthy that the Crimes Act 1961 does not contain a definition of *“interest*”.

Generally, interest in property includes some level of right or ownership over the property in question.

### A tenancy is considered an ‘interest’
The courts have held that tenancy of a property constitutes an interest in it.

On the face of it, the owner of a house has an interest in a property they own, while someone who rents that property does not – they are simply living there. However, the Court found that a person renting **does** have an interest in the dwelling for the purposes of section 267, despite not owning the house. The common use and idea of ownership (interest) of a house is not necessarily the legal use or idea of the word.

****CASE LAW****
> R v Wilson [2008] NZCA 505
> In R v Wilson the defendant was attempting to manufacture methamphetamine at his rented property when the clan lab ignited and the house burned down. In addition to drugs charges, Wilson was charged with recklessly damaging the house by fire under s267(1)(b).
> The Court of Appeal ruled that he could not be convicted of arson as his tenancy of the property was an interest in that property, and therefore provided him with a defence.
> The Crown were advised that a better course of action would have been to file a charge of permitting premises to be used (section 12(1) of the Misuse of Drugs Act). This charge would have sufficiently covered the alleged criminality. Upon conviction, the Crown could legitimately have pointed to the destruction of the house as an aggravating feature at sentence and obtained a sentence length which more appropriately encapsulated the degree of offending in this matter.

Section 267 provides that:

(a) where the Crown alleges that the damage by fire is intentional, a tenant may be convicted if he or she has caused loss to any other person (subs (1)(c)).

(b) where the Crown alleges that the damage by fire is either intentional or reckless, a tenant may be convicted if he or she knew that danger to life was likely to ensue (subs (1)(a)); and

(c) a tenant otherwise cannot be convicted of recklessly damaging the tenanted property by fire (subs (1)(b)).

Simply put, if somebody has an ‘interest’ in the property “*damaged by fire”,* another charge needs to be considered.

****APPLICATION****
> INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
> How do you investigate and gather evidence that a person does or does not have an interest in the property damaged or destroyed by fire?
> What questions could you ask of a suspect who alleges that they had a right to damage or destroy the property in question?

### Section 267(1)(c), Intent to obtain a benefit (gain) or cause a loss
A further aggravating factor (or element) that the prosecution must prove, before a charge of arson is made out, is that the defendant had either an intention to gain a benefit or an intention to cause a loss to any other person.

An example of this is where a person has burnt property with intent to claim insurance or with intent to cause the owner of the property a loss.

This is a specific intent that must accompany (be contemporaneous with) the intentional act of damaging by fire.

### Obtain
*“obtain”* is defined in section 217, Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 217, Crimes Act 1961
> Interpretation
> obtain, in relation to any person, means obtain or retain for himself or herself or for any other person.

### Benefit 
The term *“benefit”* is defined in section 267(4).

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 267, Crimes Act 1961
> Arson
> In this section and in section 269, benefit means any benefit, pecuniary advantage, privilege, property, service, or valuable consideration.

### Own property – Section 267, subsection (1)(c)
Unlike section 1(b), it does not matter if an individual damages their own property by fire. **This section includes damage to the defendant’s own property if it is done with intent to obtain a benefit **– for example, where the defendant burns down his own house intending to make a fraudulent claim on an insurance policy.

### Cause loss 
Similarly, under section 267(1)(c) there must be a specific intent to cause loss. As an investigator you are required to investigate this intent. The term *‘loss’* is not defined by statute, but in most cases will involve financial detriment to the victim.

****APPLICATION****
> INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
> How do you investigate and gather evidence that a person intended to cause loss?
> Do you need to investigate and gather evidence as to what loss was ‘actually suffered’ by the victim?

In ***R v Morley***, a deception case, the Court of Appeal reviewed the nature of ‘loss’ and held that criminality could only arise from direct loss; indirect losses such as expectation loss (loss of a bargain) and loss of anticipated future profits are not included.

****CASE LAW****
> R V MORLEY [2009] NZCA 618
> “Loss … is assessed by the extent to which the complainant’s position prior to the [offence] has been diminished or impaired.”

### Property categories of arson 
Section 267, Crimes Act 1961 separates the offence of arson into three levels of seriousness. These are set out in subsections (1) and (2).

Under s267(1)(a), the damage may be to *any* property.

Matters such as the nature, value and ownership of the property are not relevant; it is the risk to another person’s life that raises this offending to the highest category of arson.

By contrast, paragraphs (1)(b) &amp; (c) deal only with property that is, in general, higher in value – immovable property, vehicles, ships and aircraft. It is the value of these items that raises the offending to the higher category.

Lower value property is dealt with in subsection (2), depending on the offender’s intent and the risk to other property.

### Example:
- Setting fire to personal property of an ex-partner,
- Setting fire to letterboxes or rubbish bins.
****APPLICATION****
> INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
> Unless there is evidence of danger to life – a useful way to approach arson is to first look at the value of the property damaged. This value will give you an indication as to whether you are investigating an offence under section 267(1) or 267 (2).
> Knowing the difference between subsection (1) &amp; (2) is important for correct charging and investigation.

### Understanding attempted arson 
Attempted arson is found in Section 268, Crimes Act 1961. The main and obvious difference between arson and attempted arson is the fact that the damage to property by fire **has not happened **– often as the lighting of the fire was unsuccessful.

While attempted arson has its own offence, the elements and concepts of section 72 CA1961 (attempts) are applicable.

Under section 268 of the Crimes Act 1961, you must prove the identity of the defendants and that:

- they attempted to commit arson.
- the attempted arson was in respect of any immovable property or any vehicle, ship, or aircraft.
### What must you prove?
Whereas arson may be committed by a reckless act done without direct intention to burn or damage (under section 267(1) and (2)), an attempt to commit arson requires clear intent – a mere recklessness cannot suffice.

Section 268 covers situations where the offender *intends* to commit arson and takes steps towards doing so, but for whatever reason the full offence is not committed.

Although attempted arson is a lesser charge, attracting a penalty of 10 years rather than 14 years, it is often harder to prove. There must be evidence that the offender had *intent* to commit the full offence. Recklessness is **not** sufficient.

To be guilty of an attempt to commit an offence, a person must:

- *intend* to commit the offence, and
- take a *real and substantial step* towards achieving that aim.
### S72: Intent to commit the offence
The Crown must prove that the defendant *intended* to commit the full act of arson, and acted as he or she did for the purpose of achieving that aim.

### S72: Does or omits an act for the purpose of accomplishing his object
To be guilty of an attempt, the defendant must have progressed past the stage of preparation and commenced a process intended to lead to the commission of the full offence.

His conduct must be* sufficiently proximate* to the offence – i.e., he must have taken a “*real and practical step*” towards committing it, although he does not need to have taken all the necessary steps to do so.

In ***R v Harpur***,** **it was discussed that an attempt includes: an act or omission constituting a substantial step in a course of conduct planned to culminate in his commission of the crime.

****CASELAW****
> R v Harpur [2010] 24 CRNZ 909
> [The Court may] have regard to the conduct viewed cumulatively up to the point when the conduct in question stops …the defendant’s conduct [may] be considered in its entirety. Considering how much remains to be done … is always relevant, though not determinative.

### Sufficiently proximate 
In assessing proximity, the Court will analyse the defendant’s conduct at the relevant time, in conjunction with evidence of his or her intent based on the circumstances and any steps taken leading up to that point.

This cumulative assessment may allow the Court to infer that the defendant had intended to commit the full offence and had started to do so, even though he or she may still have been several steps removed from the ultimate act.

In a drugs case, ***Police v Wylie****,* two men called at a drug dealing house and attempted to procure cocaine from a plain clothes police officer who was there executing a search warrant.

****CASELAW****
> Police v Wylie
> The Court of Appeal analysed the defendants’ conduct at the drug dealing house and considered earlier events from which their intent to commit the full offence could be inferred.

This analysis considered that the defendants had:

- phoned the address in advance and enquired about purchasing drugs,
- driven to the house without delay,
- taken a substantial sum in cash with them,
- confirmed verbally that they wanted ‘*coke*’,
- discussed the price,
- asked to see the capsules.
The Court had no difficulty in finding, in this case, that the two men were attempting to procure cocaine.

### Understanding intentional damage 
Intentional damage follows a similar pattern to the elements required for a charge of arson – the actus reus being actions carried out with the intention of destruction or damage to property **by any means** as opposed to exclusively by fire or explosive.

Adams notes that “*arson involves property damaged by fire or explosive and such damage could also be charged under Intentional Damage, but the common practice is to charge the more serious offence of Arson*”.

The mens rea, as with arson, is found within the actus reus in that the property damage is carried out intentionally or recklessly.

Further specific mens rea that requires proof for intentional damage describes the same particular states of mind or conditions existing at the time of the act as for arson.

Section 269 (1) requires specific knowledge to be proved (danger to life).

Section 269 (2) (a) and (b) require specific intentions to be proved in relation to the act of damaging the property the same as for arson – the person has no interest in the property damaged or intends to obtain a benefit or cause a loss by way of the damage.

### Charging
Charging with intentional damage, as opposed to wilful damage, will be based on the particulars of the circumstances. A small amount of damage may be charged as wilful damage whereas a large or expensive amount of damage may be intentional damage. However, the charge depends on the particular circumstances.

### Additional aspects to intentional damage
> Investigation of intentional damage is often considered a ‘lesser’ offence than arson. However, remember that millions of dollars of damage can be caused, and danger to life created in the same way as arson.
> Additionally, there is often strong public interest in the investigation of intentional damage. Public monuments, public institutions such as prisons, schools or churches, public areas such as parks and cemeteries, and properties of those that serve the public such as police stations or electoral offices, can all fall victim to significant damage. In these cases, investigation as to the motivation of the suspect committing the damage is important. For example, desecration of graves or public monuments based on political or religious motivations will lend weight to the evidence of an intentional act to damage.
### Examples of Intentional Damage
- Intentionally damaging a building prop or foundation (depending on the circumstances, this could be danger to life),
- Large scale graffiti,
- Large scale damage to property on being given an eviction notice.
### Intentional Damage Examples
> Intentional damage at a prison
> Intentional damage of an Auckland Electoral Office
> Intentional damage to the graves of stillborn babies
> Intentional damage to a motorway gantry
### Providing explosives to commit an offence 
**Section 272 Crimes Act 1961 – Providing explosives to commit an offence**

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 272, Crimes Act 1961
> Everyone is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 2 years who knowingly has in his or her possession or makes any explosive substance, or any dangerous engine, instrument, or thing, with intent to use or enable another person to use the substance, dangerous engine, instrument, or thing, to commit an offence.

### Knowledge
The crucial element of knowledge is highlighted in ***R v* *Hallam****,* where the defendant was charged with knowingly possessing explosives. In his defence, he said that he had believed the substance to be soap powder. The Court decided the prosecution must prove that the defendant knew what the substance was.

In Hallam, the Court held that: “*On a charge of knowingly having possession of an explosive substance, it must be proved that the offender knowingly had the substance in his possession and also that he knew it to be an explosive substance.”*

### Providing Explosives
This section’s title is misleading, as it is not limited to the provision of explosives but extends to any dangerous “*engine*”, *“instrument”*, or “*thing*”. While the words of this section could be construed as extending to almost any weapon or tool that could be used in criminal offending, it is suggested that the headnote and the history of the provision give both *“engine*” and *“instrument*” a special meaning: in the nature of a device intended to explode or cause fire or emission of noxious things. It is further suggested that the word *“thing”* must be read in a restricted but similar fashion.

### Possession of explosive or device
The section (272) requires that the defendant makes either the explosive, the engine etc., or has it in his or her possession. As to possession generally, the defendant must intend the explosive or device to be used by another person to commit an offence. However, it is suggested, there is no need for the defendant to be aware either of the identity of the person who will ultimately use the explosive or device, or of the precise nature of the offence to be committed.

# Key Safety Hazards and Mitigation at Fire Scenes
Your safety when attending and investigating arson is important. This will be explored through these frequently asked questions on fire safety.

# Fire Safety – FAQs 
### What is the primary aim of fire scene safety procedures?
The main objective is to enable investigators to gather evidence in the safest manner possible, ensuring their wellbeing while preserving the integrity of the investigation.

### What are the primary safety concerns at a fire scene?
Fire scenes present an inherently risky environment with numerous hazards, including:

- **Inhalation:** Fire scenes often contain toxic substances, and airborne particles from extinguished fires pose significant respiratory risks. Excavation and drying processes can also release harmful airborne particles.
- **Mitigation**: Wearing appropriate respiratory protection is essential. The specific type of respirator, ranging from simple nose and mouth coverings to full respirators, will depend on the specific hazards present at the scene.
- **Ingestion: **Toxic substances, aerosols or biological contaminants at the scene can be ingested through hand-to-mouth contact after handling fire debris.
- **Mitigation**: Wearing suitable gloves and practicing meticulous hand hygiene, such as washing with hot soapy water, are crucial to prevent ingestion. The use of double gloves is also recommended.
- **Injection:** Fire scenes are often littered with sharp objects like broken glass, nails, metal fragments, splintered wood, and potentially even discarded syringes, all posing injection risks.
- **Mitigation**: Wearing appropriate footwear, such as puncture-resistant boots, is essential to minimise the risk of stepping on sharp objects.
- **Tripping &amp; Falling:** The aftermath of a fire often leaves behind a chaotic landscape of twisted debris, creating significant tripping and falling hazards. Structural integrity may be compromised and can further exacerbate these risks.
- **Mitigation**: Maintaining a heightened awareness of the surroundings and understanding how fire impacts various structural components is crucial.
- **Structural Collapse: **Fire weakens building materials like steel, concrete, and timber, increasing the risk of collapse. Fire can severely compromise the structural integrity of buildings. Understanding how different building materials react to fire is crucial for assessing potential collapse risks.
### What are the most common causes of injuries at a fire scene?
Most injuries sustained at fire scenes result from:

- Inhaling toxic substances
- Ingesting hazardous particles
- Accidental injection from sharp objects
- Tripping or falling over debris
- Falls from heights or into compromised structures
### What protective clothing is essential for fire scene investigators?
Given the numerous hazards, wearing appropriate protective clothing at fire scenes is non-negotiable.

- **Helmet: **Protects the head from falling debris.
- **Boots:** Sturdy footwear with puncture resistance for protection from sharp objects.
- **Safety Glasses: **Shield eyes from airborne particles and debris.
- **Gloves: **Heavy-duty gloves, ideally double-layered, to prevent contact with toxic substances.
- **Overalls/Tunic: **Provide an additional layer of protection for the body.
### 
### How does fire affect the structural integrity of a building?
Fire impacts building materials in different ways:

- **Steel:** Steel expands under high temperatures, potentially pushing out walls and compromising structural integrity, particularly in masonry structures with weakened mortar. Steel loses strength at high temperatures; for example, at 1600 degrees celsius, its strength reduces to less than 10% of its original strength, meaning it can buckle or collapse.
- **Concrete: **Heat can cause trapped moisture within concrete to expand, leading to spalling (explosive chipping). Reinforcing steel within concrete loses strength at high temperatures, further increasing the risk of failure. Tilt-slab concrete structures are particularly unpredictable in their response to fire.
- **Timber:** While timber typically chars, sufficiently thick timber can retain some structural integrity.
- **Mitigation**: Continuous observation of cracks in walls is essential, noting that horizontal and diagonal cracks present more serious risks than vertical ones. Liaising with the incident controller about the structural safety and examining key structural elements like walls, roofs, floors, and stairs is crucial. As a general rule, if something appears dangerous, it likely is.
### What precautions should be taken when examining a fire scene?
- **Walls:** Watch for horizontal or diagonal cracks, which indicate a higher risk of collapse compared to vertical cracks. Monitor any significant cracks for widening.
- **Roofs:** Be aware that weakened supporting beams can lead to roof collapse. Falling tiles pose a significant risk.
- **Floors:** Floors may be overloaded with wet debris. Inspect the underside of debris for signs of burning, which weakens the structure.
### What is involved in basic planning and risk management at FIRE scenes?
Effective planning and risk management are essential for ensuring safety at fire scenes. The sources outline key steps:

- **Initial Scene Observation**: Begin with a comprehensive observation of the scene to identify potential hazards.
- **Planning Meeting**: Conduct a planning meeting with all involved parties to discuss safety procedures and assign roles.
- **Role Definition**: Clearly define the roles and responsibilities of each team member.
- **Investigation Team Organisation**: Assemble a skilled and well-equipped investigation team.
- **Risk Assessment**: Conduct a thorough risk assessment, identifying potential hazards and their likelihood and severity.
### What should be included in a Site Safety Plan?
A Site Safety Plan (whether a simple document or detailed notes) should be developed and should include:

- Hazard identification and mitigation strategies
- Equipment and safety gear briefings
- Medical kit and first-aid procedures.
**Additional Considerations**: Depending on the specific circumstances of the fire scene, the site safety plan may also need to address:

- Traffic management
- Historical preservation considerations
- The role and responsibilities of a designated site safety officer.
### Who is responsible for ensuring safety at a fire scene?
Employees and employers have a shared responsibility for safety. The Fire Incident Liaison Officer (FILO) is morally and legally responsible for the safety of all personnel on scene and must demonstrate best practices.
