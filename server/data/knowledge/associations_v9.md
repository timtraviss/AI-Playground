# UNDERSTANDING CONSPIRACY 
### INTRODUCTION
Conspiracy is the first in a group of offences that involve illegal conduct completed **before **the actual commission of an offence. In this way conspiracy can be said to be **associated **to a principal offence.

Another way to consider associated offences such as Conspiracy, is that certain conduct that falls short of the commission of a crime may be considered an offence.

In short, a conspiracy is committed when there is an agreement with another person to commit a crime and there is a shared intent to carry out an offence.

A chronological consideration of Conspiracy has a conspiracy occurring **before** the principal offence is committed. It comes after intent to commit the crime, and **before** any attempt to commit a crime.

Working backwards from the principal crime, in cases where the crime has not actually been committed, and where there is insufficient evidence to prove an attempt, a charge of conspiracy may be appropriate. It is not a crime in New Zealand to simply intend to commit a crime.

Of note:

Conspiracy is often described as one of the most difficult parts of criminal law. This is because although legislation establishes conspiracy as a crime, it does not define the elements or scope of the offence like other sections of the Crimes Act.

Section 310 simply states that everyone who “*conspires”* to commit an offence is liable to imprisonment.

What is required to criminally conspire is the same as any other criminal offence. Remember the blueprint of a criminal offence requires a criminal act (actus reus) and criminal intent (mens rea). But what is the actus reus and mens rea of “conspires”? Case Law has served to define the act, intent and the elements of the conspiracy which you as an investigator are required to prove.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 310
Put simply, the actus reus of the offence of conspiracy is **the agreement to execute an offence. **

It is now settled law that at the center of a conspiracy is an act of **agreement** or consensus between two or more people. The agreement must be to pursue a course of conduct which if carried out would amount to the commission of an offence as defined in New Zealand legislation.

**Example:**

In 1868 the court considered the case of John Mulcahy, who along with his co-offenders was charged under the Treason Felony Act 1848. The charge uses old-fashioned English, however the words used are useful descriptions of what an agreement could be. Mulcahy was charged with…

“Feloniously compassing, **imagining**, **inventing,** **devising** and **intending** to deprive or depose the Queen…from the Imperial Crown of the United Kingdom.”

Mulcahy was a member of the Fenian Brotherhood which had as its purpose the overthrow of the Queens power and authority in Ireland. He and his co-offenders were found to have conspired and agreed together to raise rebellion against the Queen.

*Mulcahy* gave rise to the definitive statement on the actus reus of conspiracy. A conspiracy must go beyond thinking or intending to do a criminal act. It must have crystallized into an agreement to do an unlawful act, or to do a lawful act by unlawful means.

****CASELAW****
> ***Mulcahy v R ***
> *“A conspiracy consists not merely in the intention of two or more, ****but in the agreement of two or more to do an unlawful act****, or to do a lawful act by unlawful means. So long as such a design rests in intention only it is not indictable. When two agree to carry it (the intended offence) into effect, the very plot is an act in itself ….”*

The physical acts, words or gestures used by the conspirators in making their agreement is what is to be considered the actus reus of a conspiratorial agreement.

An agreement can be expressed or implied. It does not need to be written. A simple verbal agreement will suffice, as will gestures or physical acts in certain circumstances.

There is no need for the details of the plan to be settled and no need for the parties to the agreement to have made any decisions as to how they will actually commit the offence.

### THE general MENS REA ACCOMPANYING the ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 310
When considering the physical act that make up the offence, there is **always** the requirement that the act is accompanied by a general intent to commit the offence, as spelt out in the following definition.

******

In section 310, the physical act of agreeing must be committed intentionally or deliberately. You cannot accidentally agree to commit an offence or be part of a conspiracy by association.

Mere passive presence or having knowledge of an agreement or intention **does not** amount to being part of a conspiracy.

If “A” plans to commit an offence and “B” simply knows that “A” has a plan or was present when "A" discussed the plan, this is not enough for the charge of conspiracy.

So, the general intent accompanying the act of agreement is intentional, deliberate and active participation in the agreement.

### THE specific mens rea FOR conspiracy
The specific mens rea is the intended purpose, aim or objective. In the case of conspiracy, the offenders’ mental intent when they make the agreement **must** be to commit the full offence. The Supreme Court of Canada states that “…the very definition of conspiracy supposes an aim.”

A person does something “intentionally” if they

- *mean *to do it;
- they desire a specific result and
So, a conspiracy requires that a person

- means to agree to commit an offence and
- means to commit the full offence agreed to.
Where this intent does not exist, no crime has been committed.

**Example:**

In 1985 the New Zealand Court of Appeal considered the case of Barry Gemmell who was charged with conspiracy to Aggravated Robbery with a sawn-off shotgun. The police had information that the robbery would take place and were placed strategically in the area and stopped the robbery before it took place. Gemmell’s part in the robbery had essentially been to drive his co-offenders to the vicinity of the post office that was the subject of the robbery. Gemmel then returned to his flat, where he was eventually arrested.

When he was interviewed, Gemmell admitted to discussing the possibility of the robbery several weeks earlier. Reconnaissance drive-bys had been conducted by the group, including Gemmell, of the post office that was the target of the planned robbery.

However, when Gemmell was told that there would be a firearm involved in the robbery, he declined to take part in it, stating that it was against his religious beliefs to take a life of any kind.

The court quashed Gemmell’s conviction as they found that Gemmell **did not intend to commit the offence of Aggravated Robbery with a firearm** for which he was charged and ordered a new trial.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Gemmell ***
> *“It is of the essence of a conspiratorial agreement that there must be not only an intention to agree but also a common design to commit some offence, that is to put the design [purpose] into effect.”*

### Completion of the offence
The offence is complete on the agreement being made with the required intent. No further progression towards the completion of the offence nor further involvement by the parties involved in the agreement is required.

### Continuation and end of the offence
****CASELAW****
> ***R v Sanders ***
> *“A conspiracy does not end with the making of the agreement. The conspiratorial agreement continues in operation and therefore in existence until it is ended by completion of its performance...”*

**Example:**

The facts of *Sanders* involve an agreement to import heroin into New Zealand. The agreement was made and planned in Australia however the court clarifies that although the agreement had its origin in and was complete in Australia, the offence of conspiracy remained alive and remained effective as a continuing agreement until the heroin arrived in New Zealand and the offender arrested.

Sanders actions in meeting co-offender who had Heroin secreted in his body on arrival at Auckland Airport showed the conspiracy was alive. She was not charged with Importing Heroin, but of Conspiracy to Import Heroin.

Another way to consider this is that the two were continuing to conspire to import heroin until the moment the heroin arrived in the country and the co-offender was arrested.

### Jurisdiction of the offence of Conspiracy
The facts in Sanders also help us understand some of the jurisdictional aspects of the offence of conspiracy.

Under common law, a person who has entered into a conspiracy overseas comes under the jurisdiction of the New Zealand Courts **only** if they are later physically present in New Zealand **and** they act in continuance of the conspiracy.

Sander’s co-offender in the conspiracy to import Heroin, made the agreement in Australia, however physically arrived in New Zealand (physically present) with the drug (an act in continuance) – therefore coming under the New Zealand justice system. If he had not arrived in New Zealand, he would **not be liable** for the offence even though the conspiracy was complete.

*Poynter v Commerce Commission*[1] reinforced the position that New Zealand courts had no jurisdiction over a conspirator who enters into the conspiracy abroad and who never comes to New Zealand.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Sanders ***
> *“It is sufficient if one act or omission forming part of the offence or an event necessary to the completion of any offence occurs in New Zealand...”*

### Conspiracy between parties in New Zealand and other country
The Court in R v Darwish[2], a drug related matter, held that where a conspiracy is made between parties in New Zealand and another country, the courts will likely take the view that the conspiracy was formed in both countries simultaneously, and given New Zealand is one of those countries in which the conspiracy falls, it would lie within the jurisdiction of the New Zealand courts.

***Conspiracy to commit an offence overseas***

Under s310 of the Crimes Act 1961, it is an offence to conspire to commit an offence or to do anything, in any part of the world that would be an offence in New Zealand.

However, if the activity agreed to is **not an offence** under the law of the place where it was to be committed, those involved in the conspiracy have a defence and **are not** liable.

**Example:**

Two people in New Zealand plan and agree to commit a bombing in Australia. They have committed a conspiracy and are able to be convicted in New Zealand despite the intended offence being offshore. They have no defence to the charge as Australian criminal law covers acts such as bombings.

**Example:**

Two people in New Zealand conspire to each take on a second wife in Saudi Arabia, knowing that such activity would be unlawful in New Zealand if done here (bigamy). They are not subject to conviction in New Zealand as they have a defence to the charge. This is because Saudi Arabian law (Sharia Law) permits a person to have up to four wives.

### Two or more people
There must be two or more people who conspire together, as a person cannot conspire alone; therefore, there must be at least two parties to the conspiracy for the offence to be committed. This can be proven circumstantially.

Although two (or more) people are required for the completion of the offence, this does not mean that you must charge both of those people, or that both of those people were capable of carrying out of the conspiracy, or even that you know who both parties are.

**Example:**

In R v White, White was charged with conspiracy with another person called ‘L’ to use forged coupons. There were several people involved in the conspiracy however their identity was not disclosed by the evidence. White was acquitted of the conspiracy with ‘L’; however, the court went on to state that ‘L’ could still be convicted with the other unknown co-conspirators.

The case illustrates that where you can prove that a suspect conspired with other parties (one or more people) whose identities are unknown, that suspect can still be convicted even if the identity of the other parties is never established and remains unknown.

This may happen in a number of ways. It might relate to situations where a party to the conspiracy, whose identity is known, is unable to be located or brought to trial, or where the party is simply unable to be established through investigation.

**Example:**

A conspirator does not need to be physically capable of carrying out the substantive offence they agreed to commit. For instance, a person who is wheelchair-bound or not strong enough to physically execute the crime can still be a full party to the conspiracy. The individual’s inability to perform the final act does not diminish their involvement or liability in the crime of conspiracy itself.

### an omission as the actus reus for Section 310
The agreement between the parties concerned may also have as its object an omission (failure to act) as opposed to the commission of an offence and as such this must not be overlooked.

**Example: **

A security guard deliberately fails to lock a door that he would normally secure (the omission), with the aim being that his associates gain entry to commit a burglary (the offence).

### CONSPIRACY BETWEEN SPOUSES OR CIVIL UNION PARTNERS
Under section 67 of the Crimes Act,** **a person is capable of conspiring with his or her spouse or civil union partner or with his or her spouse or civil union partner and any other person.

**Investigation Application:**

Generally, charges of conspiracy should not be filed in situations where the specific (substantive) offence can be proved.

Although the court has suggested “it may be appropriate to include a charge of conspiracy where a charge of the substantive offence does not adequately represent the total criminality.”

Laying both a substantive charge and a related conspiracy charge is often undesirable because:

- The evidence admissible only on the conspiracy charge may have a prejudicial effect in relation to other charges.
- The judge may disallow the evidence as it will be too prejudicial, ie the jury may assume the defendant’s guilty knowledge or intent regarding the other charge and not look at the evidence, basing its assumption on the conspiracy charge.
- The addition of a conspiracy charge may unnecessarily complicate and prolong a trial.
- Where the charge of conspiracy is not founded on evidence or is an abuse of process, it may be quashed.
- Severance may be ordered. This means that each charging document may be heard at separate trials.
# UNDERSTANDING ATTEMPTS 
### INTRODUCTION
Attempts is one of the offences that involve illegal conduct completed **before **the actual commission of a principal offence.  It criminalises actions undertaken for the purpose of committing an offence, in circumstances where the intended offence is not ultimately committed

Another way to consider the associated offence of Attempts, is that certain conduct that falls short of the commission of a crime may be considered an offence in its own right.

In short, an attempt is committed when a person, intending to commit an offence, does something for the purpose of successfully accomplishing the intended crime. The conduct falls short of the principal offence but is proximately connected with the intended offence.

A chronological consideration of Attempts has the crime occurring after the formation of an agreement (conspiracy) in the case of two or more people, and after the formation of an intent in the case of an individual, but **before** the principal offence is completed.

Working backwards from the principal crime, in cases where the crime has not actually been committed, and where there is insufficient evidence to prove the full offence, a charge of attempt may be appropriate. The crime of Attempts is closer to the principal offence than conspiracy.

### THE specific mens rea FOR ATTEMPTS
Section 72 places the specific *mens rea* of the offence of attempts first.  The order of the actus reus and mens rea does not matter, so long as both are present.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 72, Crimes Act 1961***
> *Every one who, ****having an intent to commit an offence****, does or omits an act for the purpose of accomplishing his or her object, is guilty of an attempt to commit the offence intended, whether in the circumstances it was possible to commit the offence or not.*

The specific purpose or intent of the crime of attempts is, that the offender’s intention is to commit a substantive offence The prosecution must show that the accused intended to commit the complete offence. This must be shown to be a specific, fixed and irrevocable intention to commit an offence.

As we know*, R v Collister* allows intent to be inferred from the circumstances (with evidence). This includes inference from any acts leading up to and including the attempt itself, or evidence of knowledge, or foresight of the consequences of the acts and substantive charge.

**Example – Attempted Burglary:**

To be convicted of attempted burglary the offender(s) must have had the intent to commit the actual burglary.

Intent can be inferred, for example, from the burglars admitting they went to the property intent on committing the burglary and/or them being found in possession of tools or disguises at the back door of a premises before actually entering.

**Example - Attempted Kidnapping:**

To be convicted of an attempted kidnapping, the prosecution must show that the accused had the intent to complete the full offence of kidnapping.

Here intent to commit the full offence of kidnapping could be inferred from the accused being found in possession of specific materials clearly designed for unlawful confinement, such as duct tape, cable ties, a prepared gag or a hidden soundproofed vehicle or room. Evidence that the accused had scouted the intended victim’s home or route, demonstrating a pre-planned operation. Communications such as text messages communicating a plan to abduct the victim, internet search history showing the accused was researching methods of restraint or. Lying in wait for the victim and attempting but failing to force them into a vehicle.

**Example - Fraud:**

To be convicted of an attempted Fraud the prosecution must show that the accused had the intent to complete the full offence, for example, of Obtaining by Deception.

Here intent could be inferred from created or altered false documents, conducting internet searches on method of scamming, setting up a fake company or social media profile, etc.

### R v Ring
The case of *R v Ring*[3], is a significant one to consider as it covers several important ‘attempts’ issues, including intent.

The accused was seen to hustle some women on a railway platform, putting his hand into the pocket of one of them. Unbeknown to the offender the pocket was empty. Despite this he was able to be convicted of attempted theft, because the intent to complete the full offence of theft whatever property might have been discovered inside the pocket was present in his mind and demonstrated by his actions.

### physical impossibility
The case of *Ring* is also an example of another concept in regard to attempts. The offence is factually impossible if due to interruption, ineptitude, or any other circumstance beyond the offender’s control, it is impossible for them to commit.

In *Ring* the offender did not know that the pocket was empty and that it was impossible for him to obtain any property. The fact that the full crime of theft was impossible for Ring to commit does not take away his liability, as he intended to commit the full offence.

*Higgins v Police* [4] relates to a person growing tomato plants believing they were cultivating cannabis. The Court said it was physically impossible to cultivate cannabis from tomato seed, however the intent to commit the offence of cultivation of cannabis was still present.

Similarly in *Police v Jay* [5] a man bought hedge clippings believing they were cannabis. His intent was to possess a class C controlled drug. The fact that he was in possession of harmless vegetation does not change his liability.

Other examples could be a person failing to force open a door due to being too weak, or because they were interrupted by attending police. In these cases too, even although the principal crime could not be committed, liability still attached to the offender.

In each of these cases the impossibility of a ‘successful’ outcome to the crime does not matter. It is immaterial that the facts are not what they seem to be from the accused’s perspective. As long as the specific mens rea to commit the full offence is present to this part of the equation is made out.

### THE actus reus of ATTEMPTS
****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 72, Crimes Act 1961***
> *Every one who, having an intent to commit an offence, ****does or omits an act for the purpose of accomplishing his or her object****, is guilty of an attempt to commit the offence intended, whether in the circumstances it was possible to commit the offence or not.*

Section 72 places the actus* reus* of the offence of attempts second. Put simply, the actus reus of Attempts is **any act** done in pursuance of the intended principal offence, discussed above.

An act is to take some sort of action, or to do something to bring about a particular result. This action is the physical conduct that constitutes the actus reus of attempts.

As always, the general mens rea must accompany any act done to commit a crime. That is, they must be carried out deliberately, intentionally, and not accidentally or with vague awareness. Acts done must be carried out purposefully toward the goal of committing the principal offence.

The kind of acts that the offence of Attempts includes is wide-ranging. These acts when they stand alone appear innocuous, non-threatening and are not necessarily crimes in and of themselves.

Take the examples of deliberate acts underlined below. As a singular act there is nothing illegal about them. But in the context of the specific mens rea in brackets, this conduct becomes the actus reus under section 72.

Examples of acts for the purpose of accomplishing their object (to commit a crime)

- Grabbing a crowbar out of the boot of your car, (to break into parked vehicles)
- Putting a balaclava on (to commit a robbery)
- Uplifting a firearm (to use in an armed robbery)
- Travelling to an agreed location with an accomplice (to commit a burglary)
- Showing a security guard a fake ID to gain entry to a building you want to burgle
- Getting out of a vehicle and examining a window or door (that you are going to break open).
- Doing an internet search for vegetation poisons (to purchase to use on a neighbour’s tree that is blocking your view)
- Printing off an online insurance claim (to copy it when creating a false insurance claim)
This is why these kinds of deliberate acts must be done in the context of pursuing a principal offence. The actus reus and mens rea must coincide for the completion of the criminal attempt.

### sufficiently proximate
Section 72 subsections (2) &amp;(3) places other requirements on whether or not a person has committed an attempted offence.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 72, Crimes Act 1961***
> *The question whether an act done or omitted with intent to commit an offence ****is or is not only preparation for the commission of that offence****, and too remote to constitute an attempt to commit it, is a question of law.**An act done or omitted with intent to commit an offence may ****constitute an attempt if it is immediately or proximately connected with the intended offence****, whether or not there was any act unequivocally showing the intent to commit that offence.*

Subsection 2 speaks to the actus reus having to **have gone beyond acts of preparation** for the offence to be complete. Subsection 3 considers that the act or acts must be **sufficiently proximate or immediately connected** with the intended offence.

Whether an action is too remote from the principal crime to be considered an attempt, has been the subject of debate and discussion in the court for many years. The difficulty of striking a balance between a simple intention to commit an offence and actions that together with that intent create liability can be summed up by the Court in *Ah-Chong v R* which said

*“…if too much weight is given to intention, it is possible that a person will be subjected to criminal liability essentially as a result of his or her thoughts, which is generally thought to be beyond the proper scope of the criminal law. Moreover, it is always possible that someone who commences on the path to committing an offence will change his or her mind and abandon the proposed criminal enterprise, thus undermining the justification for the imposition of liability”.*

Subsection 2 makes the matter of sufficient proximity a matter of law, setting the Judge up as gatekeeper. In a pragmatic sense, the task of the courts here is to determine when an act is sufficiently close to the intended offence to be considered an ‘attempt’ by a jury.

### Current NZ position
There have been several determinations by the court about what makes prior acts close enough to an offence to attract liability in NZ. The leading case law for some time was *R v Wilcox* where the acts of gathering balaclavas and firearms, convincing a getaway driver to transport them to and from a location, travelling together to within a kilometre of the intended location to rob, and ‘testing the firearms on the way by firing them out of the car windows, were each held to be independent acts of mere preparation.

*Wilcox* held that independent acts of mere preparation cannot take on the quality of an attempt merely by being added together – or seen cumulatively.

A criticism with the ruling in *Wilcox*, is that when applied, the charge of attempts was almost impossible to make out and created artificial judicial outcomes.

###  R v harpur
*R v Harpur* heard by the Court of Appeal, changed the law in relation to what actions could be considered ‘close enough’ to the full offence.

In R v Harpur[6], the defendant was involved in a series of text messages with a woman in which he described, in explicit detail, sexual acts that he wanted to perform on the woman’s 4-year-old niece. He arranged for the girl to be brought to him for that purpose, he booked and paid for a motel unit as a meeting place, he turned up at the agreed time and place and went out to meet the woman and child as they arrived at the motel. However, he found that the girl did not in fact exist, and the arrangements were part of a ‘sting’ operation by Police. Harpur was charged with attempted sexual violation of the fictitious girl.

Applying *Wilcox*, each of the independent act described above, when viewed in isolation, could only be construed as preparatory. However, when the same acts are **viewed collectively**, they can take on a different context and therefore amount to a criminal attempt.

The Court in *Harpur* said that in determining whether acts are proximate, it is appropriate to take into account all the circumstances in which the acts occurred, including the actor’s intent, since evidence as to intent “gives the acts meaning.”

The Court of Appeal further observed that section 72 of the Crimes Act applies to “hundreds of offences and an infinite variety of factual situations, the metes and bounds of which it was impossible for Parliament to predict”.

In concluding that Harpur’s conduct was sufficiently proximate to the full offence, the Court of Appeal overturned *Wilcox* and held that Harpur’s **actions need not be considered in isolation**; sufficient evidence was available from the events leading up to that point.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Harpur ***
> *“In assessing the conduct there must be a full evaluation in terms of time place and circumstance. What remains to be done is always relevant, but not determinative. The Court is permitted to focus more on quality of the defendant’s acts and the time, place and circumstance in which they occurred, and less on abstract tests of preparation and proximity.*

The New Zealand Supreme Court in *R v Johnson* later confirmed *Harpur* stating that the judgement rightly makes the point that there must be a factual evaluation in terms of “time, place, and circumstance.” The Court agreed that it was natural to view the alleged conduct cumulatively, up to the point when the attempt is alleged to have occurred. Sufficiently proximate permits a defendant’s conduct to be evaluated in its entirety.

The court has taken a broader view of sufficiently proximate actions and for this reason, there are no criteria set down in NZ legislation or case law to assist in determining whether a defendant’s actions did or did not amount to an attempt; each scenario must be analysed on a case-by-case basis.

The facts in Harpur, Johnson and examples the court has referred to such as the American Model Penal Code provide examples of acts that may constitute an attempt to commit an offence.

**Example:**

The American Model Penal Code as providing examples of acts that may constitute an attempt to commit an offence, such as:

- lying in wait, searching for or following the contemplated victim
- enticing the victim to go to the scene of the contemplated crime
- reconnoitering the scene of the contemplated crime
- unlawfully entering a structure, vehicle or enclosure in which it is contemplated that the crime will be committed
- possessing, collecting or fabricating materials to be employed in the commission of the crime
- soliciting an innocent agent to engage in conduct constituting an element of the crime.
###  Completion of the offence
An attempt is complete even when the defendant changes their mind or makes a voluntary withdrawal after completing an act that is sufficiently proximate to the intended offence.

As always, when the actus reus and mens rea coincide under the principle of contemporaneousness the offence is complete.

Once the acts are sufficiently proximate, the defendant has no defence that they:

- were prevented by some outside agent from doing something that was necessary to complete the offence, e.g. interruption from police.
- failed to complete the full offence due to ineptitude, inefficiency or insufficient means, e.g. insufficient explosive to blow apart a safe.
- were prevented from committing the offence because an intervening event made it physically impossible, e.g. removal of property before intended theft.
###  situations where you are unable to charge with an attempt
You are not able to charge someone with an attempt to commit an offence where:

- The criminality depends on recklessness or negligence, e.g. manslaughter.
- An attempt to commit an offence is included within the definition of that offence, e.g. assault.
# UNDERSTANDING PARTIES 
### INTRODUCTION
Parties to Offences is a section of the Crimes Act that extends criminal responsibility to all individuals who participate in a completed crime. This criminal liability addresses collective criminal action that may extend beyond the person who performs the final or actual elements of the principal offence.

The general principle behind this crime is that society considers those who jointly engage in criminal enterprise, or who assist others in doing so, share in the culpability for the crime committed.

Another way to think about this crime is that all individuals or “parties” who contribute to the main offence are treated equally and are therefore equally liable to the penalties of the offence committed.

A chronological consideration of ‘Party to’ has the crime occurring before or during the principal offence. The criminal acts occur around the principal offence and may result in another secondary offence occurring.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 66, Crimes Act 1961***
> *Parties to Offences*
> *Every one is a party to and guilty of an offence who—**actually commits the offence; or**does or omits an act for the purpose of aiding any person to commit the offence; or**abets any person in the commission of the offence; or**incites, counsels, or procures any person to commit the offence.**Where 2 or more persons form a common intention to prosecute any unlawful purpose, and to assist each other therein, each of them is a party to every offence committed by any one of them in the prosecution of the common purpose if the commission of that offence was known to be a probable consequence of the prosecution of the common purpose.*

### Distinction between principal and secondary party s66(1)
Generally, a distinction is made between the principal party (the person who actually commits the offence) and the secondary party.

*****Principal party*****
> ***Secondary party***

**A person will be a principal offender, where he or she personally satisfies the actus reus and mens rea requirements of the offence. There may be more than one principal offender in relation to the offence committed.**
> A secondary party includes people who provide advice, assistance, or encouragement to the principal or persuade or incite the principal in the committing of the offence. These actions make them liable for the full offence due to their participation, despite the fact that they themselves do not commit the offence.

The law around what party to involves and what actions are and aren’t included to make a person liable for being party to an offence is complicated. There are many ways that an individual or groups of people can be liable for party to an offence under section 66. This module will deal with only some of these.

In this module the offence will be considered in 3 parts

- Section 66(1)
- Section 66(2)
- Defence to Party To
One way to think about the difference between section 66(1) and s 66(2) is that 66(1) deals with **one** offence, while 66(2) deals with **two**.

# PARTIES – Section 66(1)
### section 66(1)(a) – The principal
Section 66(1)(a) refers to situations where there is more than one principal offender participating in the offence committed. It is possible for two or more people to be identified as principal offenders.

There are two ways in which multiple offenders are principals who **actually commit** the offence under s66(1)(a).

The first is where each individual separately satisfy the elements of an offence.

An example of this is if two people actively assault a third person. A is kicking the victim, and B is punching the victim. Both A and B are separately satisfying the elements of the offence of assault (even although they are assaulting the victim in a different way). Both individuals are each independently guilty of an assault.

While this type of ’joint’ criminal activity falls within the scope of section 66(1) the criminal liability does not require reference to section 66. There is no requirement to charge with 66(1)(a) as each are independently charged with assault.

A second way two individuals can be considered principals is if each offender separately satisfies **some part of the actus reus **of the offence, and those actions in combination complete the full actus reus requirements.

Another way to consider this is that in the course of an offence being carried out, different people contribute to the acts required to complete the offence.

R v Renata is an example of this concept.

**In R v Renata**[7]** **three offenders beat a person in a car park of a tavern in a premeditated attack on the victim to rob him of a leather jacket. As a result of the assault the victim died of his injuries. Renata is a classic case of three offenders each carrying out part of the actus reus of culpable homicide (causing the death of another by an unlawful act) and being guilty of manslaughter. In this circumstance the assailants were all considered principal offenders.

Renata established two principles under section 66(1) Firstly that the section turns on knowing participation in a criminal act – in this case assault and the dangerous application of force to the person of another. Renata established that each offender is guilty of manslaughter even if the death was unexpected.

The second principle resulted from the prosecution being unable to establish which blow was the fatal one or which of the three offenders administered it.

The Court held that in matters such as these, the prosecution should look to prove culpability through the mens rea and actus reus available in s66(1).

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Renata ***
> *“Where the principal offender cannot be identified, it is sufficient to prove that each individual accused must have been either the principal or a party in one of the ways contemplated by s66(1).”*

Other examples of different individuals completing different parts of the actus reus are;

**Example:**Two offenders want to murder a rival businessman. One offender prepares a poison. He hands it to a co-offender who administers it to the businessman, who dies as a result of ingesting the poison.

**Example:**

A married couple decide to commit insurance fraud. The husband prints off a deliberately altered false claim form. The wife signs this form and submits it to the insurance company for consideration. In this example each actually commit a fraud offence (forgery, obtains by deception)

As we know the *actus reus* of any crime must be accompanied by the mens rea and party to offences involving more than one principal are no different. In the examples above the actions of the co-offenders must be accompanied by the requisite mens rea of the offence they have committed – in this case both offenders intend to kill the businessman, and both intended to commit Fraud.

### section 66(1)(b)-(d)
Section 66 (1) (b) – (d) describe persons who involve themselves in crime by giving some kind of assistance or encouragement to its commission. Section 66(1)(b) -(d) outline 5 types of conduct which is considered by law as the actus reus  of party to an offence. The law prescribes that carrying these acts out, with the requisite mens rea, results in liability for the individual as a secondary party to the offence.

### AIDS 66(1)(b)
Section 66(1)(b) defines the actus reus as, doing (or omitting) an act for the purpose of ‘aiding’ an individual to commit an offence as attracting criminal liability. But what type of acts are considered ‘aiding’?

‘Aiding’ is a broad term and the scope of acts or omissions that assist an individual to commit an offence is wide.

**Example:**

- Giving advice as to how to break a particular lock
- Supplying blueprints of a building
- An employee leaving a door unlocked or alarm off
- Providing transport to and from the scene of a robbery
- Handing a person involved in a fight a knife
- Being a lookout for a commercial burglary
Telling an associate when a neighbour is away from their home so as to allow the opportunity to commit a burglary

What is crucial in proving an act or omission of aiding is that the person accused **did in fact** help the principal party in some way. An investigator must have evidence of actual assistance.

In the case of Larkins, the principal committed a robbery, while Larkins acted as lookout. However, the principal did not know that Larkin was acting as a lookout, and therefore did not even have the psychological comfort of knowing he was there. Larkins was not required to warn the principal offenders as someone else saw the police and warned the principals.  The judge in his decision said that even although the principal offenders did not know Larkins was acting as lookout and even although he had no cause to actually act as a lookout, he nevertheless providing them with an extra set of eyes – this was actual assistance regardless of whether they were aware of its availability, or its availability was utilized.

### Larkins v Police[8]
While it is unnecessary that the principal should be aware that he or she is being assisted, there must be proof of actual assistance.

The courts in NZ have dictated that in charging a party to an offence by aiding,

- there is no requirement that the principal agree to assistance
- there is no requirement that the principal know that they are being assisted
- there **is** a requirement that the prosecution **prove actual assistance** was given to the principal
### SPECIFIC MENS REA OF S 66(1)(b)
Unlike other sections of the s66(1), the specific mens rea is written into the legislation. The act be done with the specific intention, purpose or aim of aiding another commit the offence. Investigations and interviewing must canvas this intended purpose.

### ABETS, INCITES &amp; COUNSELS s66(c)-(d)
The various types of actus reus described in s66(1)(c)–(d) can have similar meaning or their meanings can overlap with each other as well as with aiding described in s66(1)(b).

‘*Abets*’ means to incite by aid, instigate or encourage. ‘*Incites*’ means to rouse, stir up, stimulate or spur on. Both convey the sense of urging on, instigating or encouraging. The primary meaning of ‘*counsels*’ is to advise or recommend how to commit part or all of the offence. However, counselling may also mean “urging someone to commit an offence” in which case it will overlap with incitement. Similarly procuring initially seems to indicate action prior to the offence, however, can also be completed during the commission of the offence.

As an investigator, your job is to collect the evidence and charge with the actus reus that best represents that evidence.

**Example Abets:**

Abets doesn’t require a particular way or method of communication of the encouragement – words, conduct or being part of a group could all satisfy an act of abetment. However, the party must have in fact communicated that encouragement. This is a case by case

A woman discovers her same sex marriage partner with another person. A fight breaks out between the woman and the other person. While the fight continues the partner encourages the other person to kill her same sex marriage partner.

Attendance at an illegal prizefight might be evidence of abetting assault by the fighters, simply by watching, the group encourage the fighter to inflict “dreadful injuries” or death.

**Example Incites:**

Incitement requires an instigation or urging the principal to commit the offence, as such it overlaps with abets.

For example, a sports fan spurs on another fan to assault a protester and yells approval while the offence takes place.

School fight

**Example Counsels:**

Counselling includes the giving of advice or information which overlaps with aiding or abetting. It also can mean urging someone to commit an offence which may overlap with incitement.

It is not necessary that the counsellor knows the clear detail in respect of the offence to be committed; it is sufficient that they know that an offence of that kind was intended. Thus, it is sufficient for the person to know that a robbery is to be committed; they need not know the actual target address nor the details of the intended victim.

The requisite mens rea for counselling is that the secondary party counselled (gave advice or urged criminal behaviour) with the intention that the crime would be committed

An example of counselling can be found in Baker[9] where a letter, authored by another party and instructing on how to blow a safe, was found in the possession of two offenders, at the scene of a commercial burglary. The letter writer was charged as a party, despite the fact he was not aware of the time, manner or place to be burgled.

**Example Procures: **

To procure means to set out to see that an endeavor happens. This can include by persuasion, inducement or threats. The secondary party must deliberately influence or induce a person to commit an offence.

Unlike the other terms in section 66(1) there must be a stronger ‘causal link’ where in some sense the crime was committed as a consequence of the procurement. Blakely put it this way,

“…procuring was said to require an act which in fact causes ‘to a significant extent at least’, the commission of the offence by the principal party.”

A woman obtains the services of a ‘hit man’ to kill her husband and offers money or sexual services to him as payment.

Willful trespass can be committed by someone who intentionally procures a person to enter land on his behalf.

### THE MENS REA FOR PARTICIPATION UNDER SECTION 66(1)(b)-(d)
The general mens rea accompanying the act that is required to be proved is a combination of intent and knowledge.

- The Supreme Court in *Ahsin v R* summarised the general mens rea requirement as follows,
- The secondary party must intend their own contribution – that is **intentionally and deliberately** do an act that aids, abets, incites, counsels or procures and
- They must **know** the nature of the principal’s actions. That is, the secondary party must know the “essential matters” relating to the principal’s actions which make those actions an offence.
What exactly are “essential matters” has been litigated in court and can be summarised as follows;

**Example:**

Ms Ahsin was the driver for a group of Black Power associates, one of whom attacked and killed a man named Mr Kumeroa, under the mistaken belief that he was a rival gang member. Ahsin made a U-turn to stop the car near Kumeroa and acted a getaway driver after her co-accused fatally struck Kumeroa with an axe. The Crown argued that Ahsin was a party to murder because she assisted and encouraged the assault. The Supreme Court overturned her appeal because the judge failed to instruct the jury carefully under section 66(1) of Ahsin’s intention to assist or encourage the assault on Kumeroa, knowing the essential matter that the assault had murderous intent. It stated,

*‘…the jury should have been told that A could be found guilty under **s 66(1)**… if she knew M would assault K with murderous intent and [intentionally] provided assistance and encouragement by stopping the car by K, shouting to M, and acting as a getaway driver…’*

Mere knowledge that “something illegal” is intended by the principal is not enough. Suspicion on the part of the secondary party is insufficient. The secondary must at least know either:

- The *type* of offence intended (and eventually committed) by the principal; or
- That the Principal would commit any one of a number of offences, the “list” of which includes the type of offence that eventually was committed.
**Example:**

The leading decision which explains the criteria of knowledge is the Court of Appeal decision in *R v Kimura*.

The facts of this case was that Kimura was charged with aiding and abetting an aggravated burglary committed by a principal offender. The crime was considered “aggravated because the principal had a weapon. The original trial judge ruled that it didn’t matter if the secondary party knew about the weapon or not to be guilty of party to aggravated burglary. However, the Court of Appeal overturned this, ruling that possession of a weapon is an essential element that makes this crime different from a simple burglary. The court determined that the Kimera must have specifically known that the principal had a weapon with him, not just that the principal was planning a break-in.

### PHYSICALLY PRESENT
Whether or not the secondary party is required to be present at or near the commission of the offence has been the subject of discussion in the courts. The terms ‘abets’, ‘incites’, ‘counsels’ and ‘procures’ have traditionally been separate and had different meanings at common law. This was partly based on whether the party was **present** at the time of the offence or whether their actions were completed **prior** to the crime committed. However, the body of case law developed in New Zealand has rendered these distinctions as relatively technical. As Adams states

*“Since presence at the scene of the offence is not a requirement of any form of secondary participation, a person absent from the scene may be an abettor just as a person present may be an inciter, counsellor, or procurer.”*

For all types of conduct defined under s66(1)(b)-(d) presence at the offence is not required. Help offered may take place before the commission of the actual crime. In many cases there will be a meeting between the principal and the party prior to the offence being committed, however this also is not a prerequisite – spontaneous assistance at the scene is also be considered aiding for the purpose of section 66(1)(b).

So not only do the terms have overlapping meanings, but the chronological order of events does not necessarily aid in decisions for which action to charge with.

### MERE PRESENCE
What **is** established at law is that **mere presence** at crime is not enough to establish liability.

Both R v Loper[10] and R v Makita[11] murder and robbery matters respectively, looked at offender’s culpability by way of presence and encouragement. In both cases it was held that mere presence by itself, which does not encourage, is insufficient: However, deliberate presence, intended to signify approval of the acts of the principal will support an inference of encouragement in fact.

**Example:**

An example of presence that might amount to encouragement is when there is a special relationship in existence between the person present (as above) and the principal offender or where the person present owes a legal duty to the victim or general public.

**Example:**

In *R v Russell*

This concept is highlighted in *Russell*[12]* *where, following an argument between the accused and his wife, the wife, in the presence of the accused, allegedly jumped into a swimming pool with both children, drowning them all. The accused was charged with the murder of his wife and two sons after he failed to assist his wife or their children.* *

The court held that the accused was morally bound to take active steps to save his children, but by his deliberate abstention from so doing, and by giving the encouragement and authority of his presence and approval to his wife’s act he became an aider and abettor and thus a secondary offender

**Example:**

An army sergeant who watches as a subordinate assault another person and does nothing to prevent it would be liable as a secondary party to the assault. This is because the sergeant has a power of control over the subordinate and a lawful duty to prevent such incidents and intervene.

### OFFENCE COMPLETE
In *Ahsin* the majority concluded that under s 66(1)(b)–(d), the actus reus of secondary participation was complete when the actual assistance, encouragement, inciting, counselling or procuring occurred (with the accompanying intent and knowledge), provided the principal party subsequently committed the relevant offence.

### SUMMARY
**PARTY TO – SECTION 66(2)**

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 66, Crimes Act 1961***
> *Parties to Offences*
> *Where 2 or more persons form a common intention to prosecute any unlawful purpose, and to assist each other therein, each of them is a party to every offence committed by any one of them in the prosecution of the common purpose if the commission of that offence was known to be a probable consequence of the prosecution of the common purpose.*

The wording of Section 66(2) can be confusing; however, the principle can be simply explained. In general section 66 (1) liability as a secondary party is established when an individual offers assistance or encouragement by procuring, aiding, abetting, inciting, or counselling the main offender. Section 66(1) involves the completion of **one** offence.

Section 66(2) contemplates two or more individuals jointly undertaking an offence, and during the course of that enterprise, a second offence is committed by one of the individuals. Section 66 (2) involves the completion of **two** offences.

The main question around section 66(2) is

Are **all** members of the joint criminal enterprise liable if **one** member commits a second crime during the course of the criminal enterprise?

**Example:**

A group of individuals agree to commit a crime that involves the use of violence – say an aggravated robbery. While carrying out the aggravated robbery one of those individuals causes the death of another person. Are all in the group involved in the aggravated robbery (the criminal enterprise) liable for the homicide committed by one of them?

***ACTUS REUS***

The actus reus for this offence is that each individual is participating in the “prosecution of a common purpose”. That “common purpose” must be unlawful.

Put simply, a group of people carry out a crime together, such as an assault, a robbery, a fraud, an arson, or a kidnapping to name a few. The guilty act of section 66(2) is the participation in that assault, robbery, fraud, arson, or kidnapping.

**Example:**

Two men decide to burn down a school they were expelled from. The two travel to the school and walk to the rear of a prefab classroom. One of the men scrunches up newspaper he has brought with him and pushes it under the classroom, through a hole in the exterior skirting of the building. The second man puts his arm through the hole and squirts some lighter fluid on the paper. The first man then lights a match and throws it through the hole where it ignites the paper. Both men then feed twigs and leaves onto the fire to make sure it doesn’t self-extinguish. Once the fire takes hold of the wooden skirting of the classroom both men leave in a car.

In this example both men are participating  in committing a common purpose – that is Arson. There is a joint enterprise between the two men to light fire to a building. Even although they are committing different acts in the course of lighting the fire, they are acting together to fulfill the actus reus of committing the offence of Arson under section 267.

***MENS REA REQUIRED***

While the mens rea of section 66(1) requires an intention to assist or encourage plus knowledge of essential matters, the mens rea for joint enterprise within s 66(2), requires a **knowledge of a probability**.

In other words, the guilty mind required under section 66(2) is that while participating in a criminal offence, the individual **was aware that another crime occurring was probable.**

What is “probable” in this context means something that “could well happen” or something that is “a real risk”.

Another way to put it is that there was an awareness that such a second offence was a probable consequence of pursuing common purpose. In this way a person foresees the possibility of a second offence occurring, even although they do not necessarily *intend* that offence to happen

In this respect *Ahsin* describes

*“The essence of the mens rea requirement under s66(2) is recklessness – that the alleged party, despite knowing that the crime ultimately committed was probable, nonetheless joined the venture…”*

***Foresight of Essential Matters of the Second Offence***

While the mens rea of 66(2) requires that the party must foresee the likelihood or possibility of a second offence occurring, there is no requirement that they must have foreseen the precise manner in which the second crime is committed.  All that is required is that the party recognise that an offence **of that type is probable**

**Example:**

In R v Mokaraka a group of five men  jointly committed a burglary by unlawfully entering a house in Auckland in an attempt to steal drugs. They were jointly charged with Aggravated Burglary on the basis that one was in possession of a knife during the burglary.

The Court of Appeal ruled that an essential matter of Aggravated Burglary was possession of a weapon. One of the parties argued that he had no knowledge that the knife was in possession of another in the group. The court found that to be charged with Aggravated Burglary the individual must have foreseen the probability or knew it was likely that one or more of the group would bring a weapon with them. As the party was unaware of the weapon and the jury convicted on the serious charge of aggravated burglary rather than see the party escape punishment, the conviction was quashed.

Where a homicide is committed, a secondary party could be acquitted if killing or serious injury was not a probable consequence of the common purpose, in particular if he or she did not know other members of the party were carrying weapons.

### Probable Consequence
Whether an outcome is ‘known to be a probable consequence’ is a subjective appreciation on the part of the offender, where they must actually foresee the likelihood that another offence will be committed when participating in the original offence agreed by both parties.

There is no requirement that an individual foresees the **precise manner** in which the offence is committed , however, they do need to recognise that an offence of that type is probable.  It will be sufficient where it can be demonstrated that they foresaw that there was a substantial or real risk that the offence could well happen.

Another way to phrase this is that the party can only be charged with the secondary offence if that offence was within their contemplation

However, where the person thinks the risk of the offence being committed by their co-offender is negligible or only remotely possible then the mens rea required on the part of the person will be lacking.

The issue of whether an outcome is known to be a probable consequence is judged on a case-by-case basis by the jury. The investigator is required to provide evidence to the court that the defendant foresaw that the offence committed by their co-offender was probable.

**Example – Party to:**

In *R v Betts and Ridley*[13], Victor Betts and Herbert Ridley agreed to rob a man, William Andrews, as he was on his way to the bank. Their plan was that Betts would push the individual to the ground and snatch his bag. Meanwhile Ridley would be waiting around the corner in a getaway car. However, Betts struck Andrews with such force that he died as a result of the blow.

Both were subsequently convicted of murder and sentenced to death. An appeal against the murder conviction by both men was dismissed. However, the Home Secretary advised the King to commute the death sentence in the case of Ridley to life imprisonment. Betts was subsequently hanged.* *

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Betts and Ridley ***
> *An offence where no violence is contemplated and the principal offender in carrying out the common aim uses violence, a secondary offender taking no physical part in it would not be held liable for the violence used.  *

**Example – Party to:**

Two individuals, Jack and Sam, agree to rob a liquor store. Their plan is to threaten the owner to open the till and take any cash inside. Jack knows that Sam has a history of violence and will take a pistol with him to the robbery. He doesn’t want anyone killed and thinks that the owner will just roll over and give them the cash quickly. During the robbery the owner resists opening the till, and Sam pulls the pistol out of his pocket and kills the owner.

Even although Jack did not want or intend the shooting, he is liable for the killing. He had a subjective appreciation that another violent crime was a probable consequence, because he knew Sam had a loaded weapon. He foresaw a real risk that the owner or someone else could be shot. He continued the venture despite knowing this probable consequence.

**Example – Not Party To:**

Sarah and Nico decide to ‘rough up’ a rival (Tom) who works behind a bar at the local pub. Sarah doesn’t know that Nico always carries a flick knife and has used it previously in fights and to threaten people. Sarah and Nico go to the local pub just after closing and begin to punch and kick Tom as he leaves the pub. During the fight Tom resists, making Nico angry. Nico pulls out his flick knife and stabs Tom killing him.

Sarah is unaware or has no knowledge that Nico was carrying a knife or that he was going to do anything more than the agreed rough up. Killing Tom was not a likely consequence or ever-present real risk of the common purpose which was to rough Tom up. Sarah is guilty of being party to assault of Tom but not the murder.

**Example – Party to:**

Two gang associates, Lennox and Dave agree to go to a debtor’s house to threaten him into paying a drug debt. Their plan is to intimidate him and smash up his television set to show they are serious.

Lennox knows that Dave has a history of taking things too far. On the drive over, Dave says “If he doesn’t have the cash, I’m locking him in the bathroom and will barricade him in until he comes up with away to get the cash.” Lennox tells Dave that this is outside the parameters of what they are supposed to do, and they shouldn’t go that far.

When Lennox and Dave arrive, the debtor tells them he has no money. Dave gets angry and forces the debtor out of the house and locks him in the boot of the car. Lennox and Dave drive to a local bank’s ATM facility.

Lennox and Dave shared a common purpose to commit intimidation, however Dave explicitly stated that he would detain the debtor is he didn’t give in to the intimidation. Lennox had actual knowledge of the probable consequence.

Lennox is liable for kidnapping,

- even although he did not want to go further than intimidation,
- even although never touched the debtor or helped force him into the car,
- even although the probable consequence (kidnapping) did not happen in the way that Lennox contemplated (forcing into the boot of the car rather than barricading into the bathroom)
**Example – Not Party To:**

In *Te Moni* a man agreed to and participated in a robbery. During the robbery his co-offender spontaneously committed a rape. The court found that he could not be found guilty of party to rape as this was unforeseen and therefore fell outside the scope of section 66(2).

Similarly, three men committed an aggravated robbery, assaulting the Victim and taking his debit card. One of the men left the scene and took the debit card to an ATM where he withdrew money. While he was gone his co-offenders continued the attack on the Victim, including dragging him into the ocean and drowning him. The party was charged with manslaughter.

The court found that while the party had a common purpose to attack and rob and even cause serious injuries, the act of drowning was entirely different act outside of the contemplation of the party. His manslaughter conviction was quashed.

# PARTIES - THE DEFENCE OF WITHDRAWAL
In New Zealand there is a common law defence to being party to an offence under section 66 (1) or (2) of the Crimes Act 1961. This is called the Defence of Withdrawal.  As an investigator you must be familiar with this defence and gather evidence to prove or mitigate it.

The Supreme Court in *Ahsin v R* is the leading case law on the defence of withdrawal and have identified two requirements of the defence.

- There must be conduct, whether words or actions, that demonstrates clearly to others withdrawal from the offending.
- The withdrawing party must take reasonable and sufficient steps to undo the effect of his or her previous participation or to prevent the crime.
*what has been done by way of withdrawal is reasonable and sufficient in the circumstances of the case, particular consideration must be given to the nature and degree of assistance or encouragement that has been given and the timing of the attempted withdrawal in relation to the perpetration of the offence. What is done to withdraw must be proportionate to the impact of the assistance earlier given.*

*Ahsin* went on to specify four questions that will be helpful for the jury to consider when deciding whether the defence is successful. These questions are useful to the investigator to gather evidence to prove or disprove the defence.

These questions are,

- Has the defendant demonstrated clearly, by words or actions, to the principal offender that he or she was withdrawing from the offending before the offence was committed?
- Did the defendant take steps to undo the effect of his or her previous involvement or to prevent the crime? Such steps may include, for example, retrieval of a weapon provided, warning the victim or contacting the police.
- Did the steps taken by the defendant for those purposes amount to everything that was reasonable and proportionate, having regard to the nature and extent of the defendant’s previous involvement?
- Were the steps taken by the defendant timely, in the sense that the defendant acted at a time when it was reasonably possible that he or she may be able either to undo the effect of his or her prior involvement or to prevent the crime?
If each of these questions is answered ‘yes’ there is a reasonable possibility that the defendant has a defence to being party to an offence.

Withdrawal, however, is not easy. Repentance is insufficient. The participation must not merely be discontinued. It must be countermanded. In practice withdrawal may be difficult to achieve, especially where there is spontaneous violence

### What makes a withdrawal effective?
For a withdrawal to count, your actions must show that you are trying to prevent the crime from happening, and they must be effective given the circumstances.

Timing is the key; It’s much easier for your actions to be effective if you withdraw at an early stage of planning rather than right before the crime or while it is committed.

Too Little, Too Late: If you try to withdraw when the crime is already too far along, or if your actions are too weak or happens too late, it will not be considered an effective or valid withdrawal. You can’t undo something once it has gone past a certain point. In some situations, withdrawal becomes impossible.

### How to achieve a valid withdrawal.
Communicate clearly: You need to clearly tell the other participants that you are withdrawing from the plan. This communication not only shows your withdrawal but  also works to discourage them from continuing with the crime.

Revoke Support: If you gave any instruction, encouragement, or advice before, you must clearly and explicitly take it back (countermand it). This step is necessary to actively undo the effect of prior support.

### How your withdrawal is judged:
When judging if the withdrawal was valid an investigator must investigate the following.

Is the withdrawal action considered reasonable and sufficient given the situation?

Consider what the court will look at,

- The amount and nature of the help or encouragement you previously gave.
- The timing of your attempted withdrawal.
- What is done to withdraw. Actions must be proportional to the impact of the assistance or encouragement you gave earlier. In other words, if you gave a lot of support, you must take significant action to cancel it out.
# SUMMARY
To summarise, in order to establish party liability under s 66(2), the Crown must prove beyond reasonable doubt that:

- the offence to which the defendant is alleged to be a party was committed by a principal offender; and
- there was a shared understanding or agreement to carry out something that was unlawful; and
- the person(s) accused of being parties to that agreement had all agreed to help each other and participate to achieve their common unlawful goal; and
- the offence was committed by the principal in the course of pursuing the common purpose; and
- the defendant intended that the offence that eventuated be committed or knew that the offence was a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose. This requires foresight of both the physical and mental elements of the essential facts of the offence.
### CHARGING POINTS
- A person is capable of conviction as a secondary party to an offence that has been committed, whether or not police have located, charged or convicted the principal. A secondary party can be tried and convicted alone. This includes situations where the principal party cannot be located or is not amenable to justice
- Where the principal cannot be convicted due to infancy, insanity or death a secondary party may still be convicted.
- Where there are two or more persons who are parties to an offence, each is an accomplice of the other and as such can be convicted on uncorroborated evidence of the other party, although it is unwise to do so.
- In cases where an offence is only capable of being committed by a person of a designated class, such as a motor vehicle driver, and where that person has been acquitted of a charge, no other person is able to be convicted as a secondary party.
- A person may be convicted as a secondary party to an offence for which he or she could not, themselves, commit. The limitation may be by reason of sex, age, or some other qualification or status and thus they are held to be incapable of actually committing the offence in terms of s66(1)(a). However such a person can be guilty of such an offence as a secondary party, such as a quadriplegic offering advice and information on how to commit a robbery being physically incapable of committing the offence.
- Any person who would have been a party to the completed offence is considered a party to any attempt carried out in circumstances where the substantive offence was unsuccessful.
- An innocent agent carries no liability and is not capable of conviction as a secondary party.
- Every party to an offence (who is not the principal), may be proceeded against and convicted of that offence, either together with the principal or before or after the principal is convicted of the offence.
- An accomplice can be convicted of a lesser offence than the one that the principal offender is convicted of. The Court of Appeal, in R v Hartley[14]. ruled that in a gang shooting, an accomplice could be convicted of manslaughter and the principal offender could be convicted of murder.
- Secondary liability has no application to a charge of aggravated robbery under s235(b). This is because the collective element of being together with any other person or persons displaces s66(1)(a)-(d).
# UNDERSTANDING ACCESSORY AFTER THE FACT
### I***NTRODUCTION***
The offence of Accessory after the Fact found in section 71 of the Crimes Act 1961, is a specific category of secondary liability that extends criminal responsibility to individuals who assist after a completed crime. This liability addresses actions that occur subsequent to the principal offence, focusing on obstruction of justice rather than participation in the crime itself.

The general principle behind the crime is that society considers those who knowingly assist a person who has committed a crime, by helping them to evade arrest or prosecution, share in the culpability for undermining the course of justice.

Another way to think about this offence is that it treats individuals who aid an offender as committing a distinct, separate crime – a secondary offence that is dependent on, but not equivalent to the primary offence committed by the principal. Unlike other ‘parties to offences’”, the Accessory after the Fact is not treated as being equally liable to the penalties of the principal offence.

A chronological consideration of Accessory after the Fact places the criminal act as occurring **after **the principal offence is completed. The essential criminal acts – receiving, comforting, or assisting the principal offender – occur **subsequent to the principal offence **and result in this specific secondary offence under s 71.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 71, Crimes Act 1961***
> *An accessory after the fact to an offence is one who, knowing any person to have been a party to the offence, receives, comforts, or assists that person or tampers with or actively suppresses any evidence against him or her, in order to enable him or her to escape after arrest or to avoid arrest or conviction.*

Individuals involved in committing the offence of Accessory after the Fact are not those that participate in a crime, but after it. How then is this crime connected to the principal offence?

What is crucial to the understanding of section 71 is that an accessory’s liability is linked to the principal offence, in that the principal offence must have been committed **prior** to the accessory’s help.

Another way to consider this is that liability for accessory after the fact is contingent on a completed offence committed by another person.

The term ‘completed offence’ is important. An individual cannot be charged as an accessory after the fact for an offence that has not yet been committed, however they can be charged as an accessory for a lesser offence that has been completed.

**Example:**

In *R v Mane*, the accused was initially charged with being accessory after the fact to murder. The victim was shot by two men known to Mane and with whom he was closely associated in the days leading up to the shooting. Mane was identified by a witness as being in a vehicle with the two men 10 minutes after the shooting and the firearm that was used in the shooting was located in the vehicle. Mane gave a false alibi for the men.  The Court found that the matrix of events satisfied a prima facie case that Mane had knowledge that an offence had been committed and had committed an act to avoid justice.

However, the Court confirmed early English authority that held that the principal offence must be complete at the time the assistance is given. At the time Mane gave the false alibi the Victim had not died – therefore the offence of murder was not complete, “for till death ensues there is no homicide committed.”

While Mane could have been charged with accessory after the fact to a serious assault, the Crown in this case, withdrew the charge and was given permission to substitute a charge of willfully attempting to pervert the course of justice.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Mane ***
> *To be considered an accessory the acts done by the person must be after the completion of the offence.  *

**Investigation Application:**

As an investigator you must collect evidence proving that the principal offence was completed before the accessory acted. For offences such as homicide or Fraud, where completion can take a period of time, you must ensure you charge the individual as an accessory to a lesser offence that aligns with the timing of their involvement, or alternatively pursue a charge related to misleading the course of justice.

# THE ACTUS REUS  
Section 71 clearly lays out five different acts that make up the actus reus of the offence of Accessory after the Fact.

The accessory must do one of five deliberate and intentional acts to assist the offender. They are,

- receives
- comforts
- assists
- tampers with evidence
- actively suppresses evidence.
There is no requirement that all five acts are completed – one is sufficient. The actions performed by the accessory could be captured by one or more of these actions. Section 71 was not intended to operate exclusively without allowance for overlap.

There must be **proof** of an act of receiving, comforting, or assisting the offender, or tampering with or actively suppressing evidence against him or her.

### Receiving or Comforting
Receiving and Comforting are old fashioned or formal words that in the context of section 71, mean welcome, harbor or to provide physical assistance or help. Examples would be hiding someone to stop them being found by police or providing things such as food and clothing to a person.

### Assisting
Assists is a broad term which encompasses a wide range of situations where the defendant does an act that physically helps the offender. Examples in New Zealand Case Law have included

- Providing an offender with food, drink, clothing or shelter
- Providing transport
- Acting as a lookout
- Identifying a buyer for stolen property
- Giving false information to police about the offender
- Giving advice, information, material or services to the offender
- Disclosing or circulating information designed to assist the offender to escape a successful prosecution by intimidating or influencing the conduct of the witnesses.
- Arranging for a ‘safehouse’ such as a motel or AirBnB, for another to evade detection.
- Accessing a victim’s phone or computer
- Driving a person to or from a crime.
Although the act of receiving, comforting, or assisting, need not have been successful in enabling the offender to escape after arrest or to avoid arrest or conviction, it must have **actually** helped the offender in some way. Mere silence or non-disclosure, for example by failing to report an offence to the police, will not attract liability.

### Tampers with Evidence or Actively Suppresses Evidence
**Example:**

In *R v Levy*[15], Levy was convicted of being an accessory after the fact to counterfeiting currency. Levy had removed equipment after it had been used by the offender in the counterfeiting process and after the offender’s arrest and the recovery of molds used in the counterfeiting.

It was held that Levy had done a deliberate act in relation to the evidence against the offender for the purpose of assisting that offender to evade justice.

In the case of destruction of evidence, the Court in Reddy[16] said it is not necessary to prove that what the accused destroyed would, in fact, have been evidence had it not been destroyed. The appropriate test was whether what was tampered with or suppressed “could be” evidence.

Other examples of tampering or suppressing evidence are

- Selling a vehicle used in a crime
- Disposing of a body or body part
- Providing an alibi
- Hiding a body
- Hiding a weapon used in an assault
- Intimidating or threatening a witness
- Washing or disposing of clothing or bedding used in or related to a crime.
# THE MENS REA   
### THE GENERAL MENS REA
The general mens rea of Accessory After the Fact requires not only that the act done by the offender is deliberately done, but also that it is **knowingly **done. The words of section 71 are clear, that any act carried out by the accessory is accompanied by a requisite level of knowledge that the person they are helping has committed an offence. Simester and Brookbanks defines knowledge as “*knowing or correctly believing*”.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Crooks***
> *Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.*

Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. This can be shown either by proof of actual knowledge or by evidence of “wilful blindness” on the part of the accessory. Mere suspicion on the part of the accessory is not enough. As an investigator it is important to investigate, interview and prove that the accessory had the requisite level of knowledge.

Knowing that an offence has been committed and that the person they are assisting had committed or was party to an offence, must be contemporaneous with the act of assistance.

If the person gains this knowledge **after** the assistance has been given – they are not liable as an accessory.

### THE SPECIFIC MENS REA
The accessory must do a deliberate intentional act (one of the five mentioned below), **with the purpose** of assisting the person to evade justice in one of the three ways in s71(1) – escape after arrest, avoid arrest, or avoid conviction.

The intentional and knowing acts carried out by the accessory to help the offender are not done for the purpose of simply helping or through a sense of loyalty alone. The acts described in section 71 **must be done for a required purpose**.

The words of the section itself “*in order to enable…*” import a requirement of intention or purpose. Mere knowledge that an act may assist the offender is not sufficient.

The section outlines three specific purposes that the accessory must aim to achieve with their actions. That is, the acts they are engaged in are purposeful, and done with the specific intent to help the principal offender.

The three specific intents are

- Escape after arrest or
- Avoid arrest or
- Avoid conviction
### Mixed Motives
It is common for defendants to act with mixed motives however the court has said that the existence of more than one motive is not a bar to conviction.  So one of the specific intents required does not have to be the exclusive purpose of the accessories action in assisting. It just needs to be one of the reasons the accessory is so acting. So long as one or part of an accessory’s purposes was to enable the offender to escape or to avoid arrest or conviction, it is immaterial that the accessory may also have acted to avoid personal arrest or for reasons of friendship, fear, or personal gain.

# SUMMARY   
The four essential elements must be proved to establish liability under section 71 Accessory after the Fact.

If the above cannot be met, other charges such as attempting to obstruct, prevent, pervert, or defeat the course of justice may be more appropriate.

### CHARGING ACCESSORY AFTER THE FACT
- An accessory after the fact is entitled to insist on proof that the alleged offence was committed and to challenge that proof. This rule also applies to situations where the offender has pleaded guilty to the principal offence. Despite such a plea and/or a conviction having been entered, the principal offence committed must still be proved where required.
- An accessory after the fact to an offence may be proceeded against and convicted of the offence whether or not the principal offender or any other party to the offence has been proceeded against or convicted.[17]
- An accessory after the fact may be proceeded against and convicted alone as for a substantive offence or jointly with the principal offender or other party.[18]
- Indirect assistance
- There is no requirement that the offender (person A) is directly assisted by the accessory (person B).
- Example:
- Person A commits an offence and is assisted directly by another accessory (person C). Person B assists person C directly, but does not directly assist person A. Despite this distance, person B is still an accessory after the fact to person A
- It is possible to be convicted of attempting to be an accessory after the fact.
- In *R v DH* the appellant was charged and convicted with attempting to be an accessory after the fact to murder by assisting others in the unsuccessful attempt to dispose of a weapon used in a fatal robbery
- As per s137, a charging document may be laid whether or not any party to the principal crimes is charged, convicted or otherwise amenable to justice. An accessory may be charged alone or jointly along with any party to the principal crime

### INTRODUCTION
Receiving under section 246 of the Crimes Act is a stand-alone offence that targets individuals who handle or deal with property that they know or believe to have been stolen or dishonestly obtained. Although stand-alone, Receiving has historically been seen as an accessory of theft, however the modern offence in 246 can be committed in respect of anything obtained by crime. In this sense the legislation is drafted in wide terms to capture those who benefit from the disposal of ill-gotten property.

The general principle behind this crime is to deter the market or economics of property crime, and to hold accountable those who participate in the continuation of the original unlawful act. In discouraging the receipt of such property, society aims to discourage the profit motivation for theft and protect the integrity of property ownership.

A chronological consideration of “Receiving” places the offence as occurring **after **the criminal act involves dealing with the fruits of the primary offence, thereby enabling a secondary yet distinct, crime to occur.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 246
The actus reus of the offence of receiving is defined in section 246 (3)

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 246, Crimes Act 1961 ***
> ***Receiving ***
> *The act of receiving any property stolen or obtained by any other imprisonable offence is complete as soon as the offender has, either exclusively or jointly with the thief or any other person, possession of or control over, the property or helps in concealing or disposing of the property. *

Put simply the act of receiving involves coming into **possession** or **control **over property. It may involve aiding another in concealing or disposing of property.

What investigators must prove in regard to possession is governed by case law in New Zealand in *R v Cox* and for receiving in particular *Cullen v R*

The decision in *R v Cox* found that possession requires both a **physical **and **mental **element (general mens rea accompanying the act) to be proved.

The physical element requires the physical custody or control over the item in question and can be either ‘actual’ or ‘potential’.

Actual possession arises where the thing in question is in a person’s physical custody or control. For example, carrying a stolen wallet in your pocket.

Potential possession arises when the person has the potential to have the thing in question in their control. For example, storing a stolen TV in a storage locker.

The mental element is a combination of both knowledge that the person possesses the item in question, and intention to possess the item.

For example, a man is searched, and a stolen wallet is found in a backpack that he had borrowed from a friend. He had no actual knowledge of the stolen wallet’s existence and therefore no intention to possess it.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Cox***
> *Possession involved two elements. The first, the physical element, is actual or potential physical custody or control. The second, the mental element, is a combination of knowledge and intention: knowledge in the sense of an awareness by the accused that the substance is in his possession and an intention to exercise possession.”*

### POSSESSION &amp; CONTROL IN CULLEN v R
Mr Cullen was the director of a scrap metal company, who was charged with receiving 19 stolen vehicles which were brought onto his yard by three employees. The employees did not bring the stolen vehicles onto the yard at the specific direction of Mr Cullen, and he denied knowledge of the existence of the stolen property on his yard. He was charged with receiving being reckless as to whether or not the vehicles had been stolen.

In relation to the actus reus of receiving and in particular possession, the Court discussed whether Mr Cullen could be said to have been in possession **or have control** of the vehicles for the purposes of receiving. It elaborated that,

****CASELAW****
> ***Cullen v R***
> *the two distinct legal elements of possession and control “…can usefully be sub-divided into four for the purpose of a receiving charge: “*
> *Awareness that the item is where it is;**Awareness that the item has been stolen;**Actual or potential control of the item; and**An intention to exercise that control over the item. “*

The four elements outlined in *Cullen* provide a test in relation to possession for receivers of stolen property and a useful guide for investigators when gathering evidence for receiving.

The test spells out the actus reus and general mens rea that accompanies the act of receiving – the offender must have possession or control over the property and an intention to exercise that control which by implication requires that the offender must be aware that the property is where it is.

In terms of possession or control, it has to be decided whether the given approximation is such that possession may be held sufficiently established to satisfy the relevant rule of law.

Where a person is unaware of the existence of the property, they cannot be said to be in possession of it.

Where property is located at a place, over which the receiver has control, then the prosecution **must prove** the receiver arranged for the property to be delivered there, or alternatively, that on discovering the property, he or she intentionally exercised control over it.

Control over property may still be exercised by a receiver when the property is in the possession of the receiver’s agent or servant (includes an innocent agent or party). Again, the exercise of such control must be intentional.

In Cullen the question arose as to whether Cullen received the cars as they were brought onto the yard by his three employees. The Supreme court confirmed the earlier judgement that while Cullen did not come into possession of the vehicles when they were first brought onto the yard, **he did nevertheless come into control** of the vehicles at some point. The Supreme court commented that the Crown had produced enough evidence that Cullen had indeed come into control of the vehicles. Some of the evidence included,

- Mr Cullen being sole director of the company
- Police observations of Mr Cullen working on site everyday
- Police observations of Cullen working in and around the physical proximity of the stolen cars
- Cullen filling out paperwork in regard to the cars
It was this evidence of his actions subsequent to the arrival of the cars on the yard that proved Cullen’s **assuming **possession and control of the cars.

Recklessness in the context of s 246 requires the conscious taking of an unreasonable risk: *Cullen v R* [2012] NZCA 413, at [23]. The Court added that complete indifference about whether goods were stolen would, itself, amount to recklessness.

### THE PROPERTY RECEIVED
When investigating receiving it is important to remember certain points about the property received itself.

- that the offender must have received the property that was stolen or obtained. It cannot be another item which has been exchanged or traded for the original item stolen.
- There is no requirement that the whole of what was stolen be received. Receiving part of what was originally stolen will suffice
- There is no requirement that when received the property be in the same condition or state as when first stolen
**Example:**

Where a thief steals $100 in $20 notes and subsequently provides one of these notes to a receiver, the offence of receiving is committed (part of the original stolen).

If, in the same circumstances, the thief visits a bank and exchanges the $20 notes for a smaller or larger denomination and then completes an exchange with the would-be receiver, no offence is committed even where the person receiving the funds is aware of the initial theft. (This is because the notes exchanged are not the property originally stolen or part of the original stolen.)

****CASE LAW****
> ***R v Lucinsky [1935] NZLR 575 (CA)***
> *The property received must be property stolen or illegally obtained (or part thereof), and not some other item for which the illegally obtained property had been exchanged, or which are the proceeds.*

Remember that the offence of Receiving is not limited property stolen, but includes property obtained by any act of dishonesty including fraud offences, conversion offences, and proceeds or money obtained by drug dealing.

**Investigation Application:**

It is essential to a receiving charge that the prosecution **prove** the property in question was stolen or obtained by any other imprisonable offence, this proof may be direct or circumstantial.

Section 49 of the Evidence Act 2006 provides that the conviction of an individual is conclusive proof of that person’s guilt, unless allowed to be contested by the judge in later proceedings. You are therefore able to rely on the conviction of the thief or obtainer as proof of the offence relating to the property stolen or obtained. Despite this you must still prove any conduct or mental elements of receiving against the defendant.

### THE MENS REA FOR SECTION 246
The offence of Receiving requires the offender to either

- Know ***or***
- Be reckless
As to whether the property is stolen or obtained by an imprisonable offence.

### KNOWING PROPERTY STOLEN OR OBTAINED
It does not matter if the receiver has no knowledge of the particular facts of **how** the property was stolen or obtained.

Knowing or knowingly is a recognised state of mens rea that requires the receiver to possess a particular knowledge which can range from awareness to belief to direct knowledge. (See the Basic Legal Concepts Module) *Simester and Brookbanks* and R v Crooks define the level of knowledge required to meet the legal test of knowing whether the property in question was stolen or obtained.

****DEFINITION****
> ***Simester and Brookbanks: Principles of Criminal Law ***
> *Knowing means “knowing or correctly believing”. The defendant may believe something wrongly but cannot ‘know’ something that is false.*

****CASE LAW****
> ***R v Crooks ***
> *The Court of Appeal said that a person is said to “know” something when he or she has ascertained, by physical or mental perception, a state of facts or circumstances which creates in his or her mind a certainty that the point of his or her inquiry is free from doubt..*

Willful blindness would, it is suggested, then be a matter of evidence from which belief might be inferred.

### RECKLESS AS TO WHETHER PROPERTY STOLEN OR OBTAINED
Being reckless as to whether or not property you are taking possession or control of is stolen or obtained by another crime, works in exactly the same way as the application of recklessness in other offences.

Acting “recklessly” involves consciously and deliberately taking an unjustifiable risk.

It must be proved not only that the defendant was *aware* of the risk and proceeded regardless (a subjective test), but also that it was *unreasonable* for him to do so (an objective test). This is covered by the case law *Cameron v R* and in the Basic Legal Concepts Module.

****CASELAW****
> ***CAMERON v R ***
> *Recklessness is established if:*
> *the defendant recognised that there was a real possibility that:**his or her actions would bring about the proscribed result; and/or**that the proscribed circumstances existed; and having regard to that risk those actions were unreasonable.*

Section 246 requires that the receiver be aware of a risk that the goods were stolen or obtained by crime, and the risk is an unreasonable one to disregard. The receiver need not positively believe them to be stolen, it can be enough that he or she suspects that to be the case, and where receiving the goods under those circumstances is unreasonable.

In *Cullen v R* the Court of Appeal observed that

*“A complete indifference about whether goods were or were not stolen would, itself, be enough. It seems reasonably clear that if someone receives property, the source of which is unknown, then a conscious risk is taken in determining not to make further inquiries.”*

The prosecution will, in general terms, rely on inferences from the circumstances (R* v Collister*) to establish that the accused knew the property was stolen or obtained by an imprisonable offence, or was reckless as to that possibility at the time of its receiving.

As an investigator you are required to gather evidence for the crown to be able to do this. Cullen is a good example of an investigation that did just this.

The investigation provided evidence that Cullen was reckless as to whether the cars on his yard were stolen. This evidence included

- Cullen filled out incomplete paperwork, without registration plate or VIN numbers, with no seller details, and using a generic customer number for 80% of cars on the yard.
- His acknowledgement that the cars were brought to the yard at night and during the weekend in contravention of normal practice.
- That the vehicles were in roadworthy and good condition - worth more than the average $200 scrap price ‘paid’ for them
- A number of the vehicles had broken ignition barrels and side windows
- A number of vehicles contained personal possessions still in the vehicle.
In practice a finding of recklessness will turn on the circumstances of the case. Generic circumstances commonly relied on as evidence of knowledge or recklessness on the part of a receiver can often be demonstrated circumstantially, for example, by:

- possession of recently stolen property
- nature of the property, i.e. type, value, quantity
- purchase at a gross undervalue
- secrecy in receiving the property
- receipt of goods at an unusual place
- receipt of goods at an unusual time
- receipt of good in an unusual way
- concealment of property to avoid discovery
- removal of identifying marks or features
- steps taken to disguise property, ie removal / altering of serial numbers, painting
- lack of original packaging
- type of person goods received from
- mode of payment
- absence of receipt where receipt would usually be issued
- false statements as to the source of the goods
- false statements as to the date of acquisition
- nature of explanation given, e.g. false or inconsistent or no reasonable explanation
- false denial of knowledge, existence etc.
While not conclusive evidence of guilty knowledge, these are matters that the courts will contemplate and a combination of some of the factors above may be sufficient to establish the necessary knowledge or recklessness required by the statute.

It was concluded in *Rex v Stone*[19] that a person who receives innocently does not commit an offence under this section if he or she retains the property dishonestly after acquiring such knowledge, although such retention may amount to theft by dishonestly dealing with the property.

### DOCTRINE OF RECENT POSSESSION
Proof of mens rea in receiving can be difficult. While an investigation may be able to establish possession of stolen goods, an account of how that property was acquired or what the receiver intended to do with it, or whether they had the requisite level of knowledge of the stolen property is harder to establish.

Because of this the common law has developed the doctrine of recent possession. This states that possession can, in certain circumstances, prima facie (on the face of it) infer criminality.

The doctrine is a common-sense rule where there is proof of possession by a person of recently stolen property. In this circumstance an inference can be drawn that the person is either the thief or the receiver.

There is a caveat to this inference though. Being in possession of recently stolen property is sufficient evidence to justify a finding that the person stole or received it – **but only where that person has no credible explanation** of how they came by the property.

Therefore, it is important that any investigation seek explanation (or lack of one) from a suspected receiver, as a jury may infer guilty knowledge if the receiver offers no explanation to account for their possession or if the explanation given lacks veracity.

- Proof that property stolen (or obtained)
- In possession or control
- No credible explanation
The doctrine of recent possession has no application to the concealing or disposition of property.

Where there is no proof of any theft, a person in possession of property is not bound to account for their possession of it. The onus to do so lies with the prosecution.

Whether possession is ‘recent’ is dependent on:

- the nature of the property, and
- the surrounding circumstances.
### POINTS REGARDING CHARGING
**Receiving from persons unknown**

Charges may be formulated in respect of receiving ‘from persons unknown’ in circumstances where it is not known who committed the offence by which the property was obtained, providing all elements of the offence can be proved.

**Property stolen to order**

In situations where property is stolen to order the receiver of such stolen property is liable as a party to the principal offence, pursuant to s66(1) rather than as a receiver under s246.

**Property obtained outside New Zealand**

Receiving property that was obtained by any act committed overseas is an offence under New Zealand law only if the receiving takes place in New Zealand.

Property obtained by any act committed outside New Zealand that, if it had been committed in New Zealand, would have constituted an imprisonable offence is, subject to subs (5), to be regarded as having been obtained by an imprisonable offence (s246(2)).

If a person is charged with an offence under this section and the property was obtained by an act committed outside New Zealand, it is to be presumed, unless the person charged puts the matter at issue, that the doing of the act by which the property was obtained was an offence under the law of the place where the act was done (s246(5)).

**Party to receiving**

By virtue of s66(1) of the Crimes Act 1961, where a thief delivers stolen property to a receiver who, to his knowledge, is aware that the property has been dishonestly obtained, the thief is liable as a party to that receiving that has been committed by the receiver.

# UNDERSTANDING MISLEADING JUSTICE
### INTRODUCTION
The integrity of the legal and judicial process is paramount, and a core function of criminal law is to ensure that the administration of justice operates on a foundation of truth and fairness. In New Zealand, this protection is established under the Crimes Act specifically within the provisions concerning ‘Crimes affecting the administration of justice.’

Sections 108, 111 and 116 criminalise various forms of deception and obstruction that undermine the search for truth.

Making a false statement or declaration under Section 111 of the Crimes Act 1961 is a stand-alone offence that targets individuals who deliberately provide information that they know to be untrue, either in written or verbal statement. It addresses falsehoods outside of direct court testimony. This section seeks to capture those who attempt to benefit from or introduce false information into legal or official processes.

Section 116 addresses acts of obstruction by agreement. It criminalises conspiring to defeat justice, targeting collective schemes or agreements between two or more persons intended to interfere with, obstruct, or pervert the due course of the law. This covers planned attempts to manipulate proceedings, suppress evidence, or otherwise compromise the impartiality of the justice system.

Section 108 specifically targets wilful false assertions made by a witness under oath in a judicial proceeding (whether in affidavit or in open court). It ensures that individuals giving evidence essential to the judicial process are legally bound to be truthful.

The general principle behind these crimes is to protect the integrity of official records, statutory procedures, and the administration of justice. It is also to hold accountable those who participate in undermining the public’s trust in these processes. By discouraging the introduction of falsehoods, society aims to discourage fraudulent action and protect the reliability and truthfulness of information presented to government agencies, courts, or statutory purposes.

# MAKING A FALSE STATEMENT
### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 111
****LEGISLATION****
> ***False Statements or declarations***
> ***Section 111***
> *Every one is liable to imprisonment … who on any occasion on which he is required or permitted by law to ****make any statement or declaration**** before any officer or person authorised by law to take or receive it, or before any notary public to be certified by him as such notary, makes a statement or declaration that would amount to perjury if made on oath in a judicial proceedings.  *

The actus reus of this crime requires a **declaration or statement** to be made. This is not just a chat, conversation or written opinion. It refers to a formal process or recording of a statement prescribed or permitted under law.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Evidence Act ***
> ***Section 4***
> *Statement means – *
> *a spoken or written assertion by a person of any matter; or**non-verbal conduct of a person that is intended by that person as an assertion of any matter *

The elements that must be present for completion of the actus reus under section 111 are:

- Making a Statement or Declaration: The accused must have actively made a statement or declaration (this is the physical conduct).
- Statutory Context: This act must occur on any occasion on which the person is required or permitted by law to make that statement or declaration. This limits the offence to formal settings, such as signing a statutory declaration, a company registration, an official government form, or a verbal or written statement to police.
- Authorised recipient: The statement or declaration must be made before an officer or person authorised by law to take or receive it.
- Falsity: The statement or declaration made must be false in a material particular. The assertion, fact or declaration must be objectively untrue.
In summary, the actus reus is the making of an objectively false statement or declaration in a formal context before a legally authorised person.

**Example:**

- A statement made to police
- A statement made to a customs officer
- A verbal statement as to name by a vehicle driver to a traffic officer
Note: Section 83 of the Criminal Procedure Act 2011 gives formal statements that form the evidence for a prosecution, the same status as evidence given on oath.

### THE MENS REA FOR SECTION 111
The mens rea refers to the mental element required at the time the actus reus is committed. Since this is an offence aimed at preserving the honesty of and administration of justice, the required general mens rea is one of conscious or knowing dishonesty accompanying the act of making a statement on which that justice system relies.

The making of a false statement must be coupled with a specific intent to mislead by that statement - that is to deceive, misinform or lead astray those relying on the statement to be truthful.

The legislation requires a statement and intent that would amount to perjury if the statement would be given in court. Therefore, the mens rea aligns with that of perjury in section 108.

# CONSPIRING TO DEFEAT JUSTICE
### SECTION 116 INTRODUCTION
Section 116 is an offence that covers a broad range of acts that are intended to interfere in some way with the administration of justice. Unlike making a false statement or perjury which are individual crimes, section 116 requires two or more people to act in agreement.

A conspiracy is considered in section 310 of the Crimes Act and in Part One of this module and is applied to section 116. Under section 310, a conspiracy requires an agreement between two or more people to commit any offence. There must be an intent to agree and intent to carry out the main aspects of the offence.

Unlike section 310 where the agreement has the specific intent to commit **any** offence, section 116 is limited to an agreement with the specific intent to **do an act intended to obstruct, prevent, pervert or defeat the course of justice**.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 116 
The actus reus of section 116 is the agreement.

In this case the agreement is specific to an agreement to interrupt the course of justice. As always, the agreement must be accompanied by the general intent - that is an intentional and purposeful agreement, not an accidental, casual or flippant one.

### SPECIFIC MENS REA INTERRUPTING THE COURSE OF JUSTICE
The specific intent, purpose or outcome of the agreement falls into four categories specified by section 116. These are to

- Obstruct the course of justice
- Prevent the course of justice
- Pervert the course of justice
- Defeat the course of justice.
While Section 116 specifies four specific outcomes of the agreement (obstruct, prevent, pervert or defeat), it does not define them. Therefore, the precise meanings of these acts are not clear, and they may overlap with each other.

Generally speaking, an agreement to obstruct, prevent, defeat, or pervert the course of justice, will involve acts that disrupt or adversely influence the administration of investigations, prosecution, and the ancillary functions of the court. The terms embraces conduct purposely intended to secure a wrong or unjust result

**Examples:**

- Knowingly defeating execution of a search warrant by hiding relevant objects
- Revealing that an address is to be the subject of a search warrant
- Not attending court to give evidence when summoned
- Seeking to find the wrong person liable for an offence
- Conduct intentionally breeching a court order in relation to funds made under the proceeds of crimes act.
- False affidavit in relation to bail application
When a person agrees to do one of these acts, with an accompanying intent to interfere with the course of justice, implicit in this intention is a knowledge or belief that a legal proceeding will or may occur.

### JUDICIAL PROCEEDINGS
The law is not restricted to a particular form of judicial proceedings. It is broad in its interpretation by the court and has “…extend[ed] to the determination of matters by any decision-making body which “judged” matters coming before it under a power vested in it by a statute which also required the body to act in a judicial manner...”

**Example:**

- Health and Disability Tribunal
- Health Practitioners Disciplinary Tribunal
### THE COURSE OF JUSTICE
The course of justice will include proceedings initiated and continuing before a tribunal, as defined in s 108(4) as having a judicial character or affecting personal rights and obligations. It is therefore not restricted to any particular form of proceedings.

*‘The key phrase “course of justice” is not defined in the statute, nor has any precise definition been offered by the courts. At its core lies the administration of justice by the courts or other publicly established tribunals’(Adams)*

Where the ‘course of justice starts and finishes is also not stipulated by statute. However, the courts have established that the course of justice will often begin **well before** a formal proceedings such as prosecution begins.

- Includes an enforcement agency has begun an investigation which may lead to proceedings
- When it is inevitable that an investigation will occur
# SECTION 108 PERJURY
### SECTION 108  INTRODUCTION
Perjury is an important misleading justice offence that investigators must be aware of particularly when preparing files and at trial. The distinguishing factor that sets perjury apart from other misleading justice offences, is the requirement that the false assertion must be made on oath (or its equivalent) and as part of an individual’s evidence in a formal judicial proceeding, such as in court or by affidavit.

The element of being of being formally sworn to tell the truth underscores the gravity of the offence as it attacks the integrity and reliability of the formal evidence-giving process on which the justice system fundamentally relies.

### JUDICIAL PROCEEDINGS
The offence of Perjury is unusual in that it **must** take place in a particular setting – that is in a judicial proceeding.

What a judicial proceeding is, is defined in section 108 itself, however this is broadened by other statutes also deemed under the Act to be a judicial proceeding for the purposes of section 108. This includes such tribunals as disciplinary tribunals under the Health and Safety Act, examination by the Health and Disability Commissioner, and Parliamentary commissioner under the Environment Act. Hearings before planning committee of a territorial authority and professional bodies such as Chartered Professional Engineers of New Zealand are also considered proceedings.

It is important for investigators to understand that this crime is not only limited to police processes and practice in prosecuting crime.

**Investigation Application:**

Giving evidence by Audio-visual is considered to be ‘taking place’ at the hearing. This means that the giving of false evidence by such a method by audio link from another room, city or country equates to perjury in New Zealand and falls within the New Zealand jurisdiction.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 108
Put simply the actus reus requires a person who is a witness in formal proceedings giving evidence on oath. It is the giving of evidence that is the actus reus.

The section further defines giving evidence as ‘making any assertion as to any fact, opinion, belief or knowledge forming part of that witness’s evidence on oath.

An assertion is a statement made as if it were a fact. It is something a person declares or states positively, often without providing extra proof or evidence at that moment to back up the statement’s accuracy.

A “matter of fact” is a term that the court uses to categorize a particular type of information. It helps distinguish between what actually happened and what might be someone’s opinion or interpretation or knowledge. A fact is a thing done, an actual occurrence or event – something that has truly happened.

So, a person giving evidence makes assertions or positive statements on oath about facts, opinions, beliefs and knowledge. This is how testimony evidence is put before the judicial decider

It is important to note at this point that although perjury lists opinion as a form of testimony that can be given, this is governed by the Evidence Act. The giving of evidence or making assertions about your opinion is strictly limited by the rules of evidence. While a person is allowed to give evidence about what they personally know they are **not** allowed to give evidence as to their opinions **unless** it is the only way for them to communicate or to articulate their what they saw, heard or otherwise perceived.

For example, a person can give an opinion about how fast a car was travelling without knowing what the actual speed of the car was. Or giving evidence about whether a person was drunk without actually knowing the level of alcohol per ml of blood. These opinions are acceptable to the court as it is the only way to give evidence about what they **saw.**

Opinion evidence is discussed in depth in the Evidence module.

### THE MENS REA FOR SECTION 108
As always, the actus reus must be done intentionally and in the case of perjury, the act of giving evidence must be done knowingly. That is knowing that the evidence, or assertion they are making on oath is **false. **The wilfully false statement made by the person must be about something they believe is **materially important** to the proceeding.** (***R v Goodyear-Smith)* Lying about an adjacent or minor detail may not amount to perjury.

The Court held that to be guilty of perjury, a witness must make a willfully false statement in respect of an issue that the witness believes is of material importance to the proceeding.

‘Knowing’ means ‘correctly believing so as to be free of doubt’. As per *R v Crooks*, the person has to have more than a suspicion. (Refer the Basic Legal Concepts Module)

### INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
Investigators must gather proof of knowledge on the part of the person giving evidence that what they are asserting is false. This involves investigating and furnishing proof of this knowledge. This can be inferred from the circumstances. (Collister). The mens rea requirement that the witness knows the evidence to be false would be satisfied by proof of a direct intent to lie or proof of knowledge of the falsity of the evidence.

As well as knowledge that what they are saying was false, the investigator must also prove the specific intent, purpose or aim that the evidence-giver was attempting to achieve.

Perjury requires that the specific intent required for criminality to attach is **to mislead the tribunal holding the proceeding**.

The full mens rea of perjury then is a combination of knowing and intent.  Giving evidence knowing it to be a lie stated intentionally or in order to mislead the court on the facts of the case, is the essence of perjury. These actions offend against the integrity of the court and the justice system itself. Such conduct goes against the integrity of the court and the centre of justice itself.

The offence of perjury is complete at the time the false evidence is given accompanied by an intention to mislead the tribunal. There is no defence where the witness later recants and informs the tribunal of the falsity of the earlier evidence given.

### WHAT IS NOT PERJURY
Those giving evidence in a formal proceeding are not expected at law to be perfect. The general human flaws of inaccuracy, memory and contradiction are not perjury. This includes

- Innocently getting something minor ‘wrong’ when giving evidence
- Not giving evidence that is accurate due to nerves, memory or other human character.
- Giving evidence on oath that contradicts a previous statement unless there is a confession that the evidence given on oath was perjured. (*R v Cleland*)
- The intention must be to mislead the tribunal, where this intention is absent no offence is committed
### ADDITIONAL REASON FOR EVIDENCE TO PROVE PERJURY
Section 121 of the Evidence Act discussed corroboration in a criminal proceeding. It states that, in general, there is no requirement for evidence to be corroborated or for judges to warn about uncorroborated evidence. This is **with the exception** of perjury, false oaths, false statements and treason. In this respect it is **imperative** for the investigator to gather evidence to corroborate allegations of perjury.

Corroboration is independent evidence that supports, confirms or ‘backs up’ other evidence in a trial. Think of corroboration as a safety net. If a case relies entirely on the word of one person, corroboration provides the extra “weight” needed to prove that the person is telling the truth.

The justification for this corroboration requirement is that making it too easy for someone to be prosecuted on accusations of lying on oath will discourage individuals from giving evidence at all.

# UNDERSTANDING MONEY LAUNDERING
### INTRODUCTION
Accumulation of wealth is the driving force of organised and financial crime. One of the core functions of criminal law is to ensure that individuals and organizations cannot profit from unlawful activities. In New Zealand, there are several pieces of legislation that criminalise activities involving the wealth generated by crime. These include sections in the Crimes Act such as Money Laundering and separate Acts such as the Criminal Proceeds (Recovery) Act 2009 and the Anti-Money Laundering and Countering Financing of Terrorism Act 2009

New Zealand is also subject to the obligations of several international and transnational conventions and agreements that it is a signatory of. These obligations require rigorous investigation and prosecution of activities involving the proceeds of criminal offending when identified - including money laundering.

Simply put, money laundering is the method by which criminals attempt to disguise the illegal origins of their wealth and protect their asset bases, to avoid the suspicion of law enforcement agencies and prevent leaving a trail of incriminating evidence.

Section 243 criminalises the process of handling the proceeds of crime, targeting those who deal with the profits and proceeds of their criminal activity to attempt to transform and integrate them into the legitimate economy. This provision is a stand-alone offence that targets individuals who engage in transactions involving property they know or believe to be the proceeds of a serious offence. It is in this way that money laundering is associated with the main offence.

As a detective you must be familiar with and understand that it is a **requirement** that money laundering investigations should be undertaken in parallel to the predicate (the underlying crime that generates illicit proceeds) offence.

By discouraging the introduction and ‘legitimising’ of illicit funds and goods into the economy, money laundering targets the financial infrastructure of organised crime and ensure that “crime does not pay.”

###  HOW MONEY LAUNDERING WORKS
Money Laundering behaviour is typically thought to proceed in three phases, known as **placement**, **layering** and **integration**:

- **Placement** involves funds/assets entering the financial system or moving from its original source.
- **Layering** is the disguise of the funds through a series of transactions mixing or splitting the funds and dispersing them around. The purpose is to make the money difficult to trace. Through this, the funds are distanced from their (illegal) origins.
- **Integration** is the use of unlawful funds or mixing of the laundered funds with lawful funds. In this way the laundered funds are integrated back into the mainstream economy. For example, through the sale or purchases of assets or repatriation overseas, or payment of debts/bills owed.
The last transaction in the chain is what can be pointed to, now several steps away from the original illicit funds. The criminals or their suppliers or associates or relatives can now use and control funds apparently as legitimate funds.

Although placement is the necessary first step in a money laundering transaction, stages two and three may happen swiftly, simultaneously, or sometimes spread out over years. In less sophisticated attempts, the layering/dispersion stage may be omitted.

Tying this framework back to the statutory provisions in section 243 of the Crimes Act 1961 *Adams on Criminal Law — Offences and Defences* comment that: *“It seems probable that a person may engage in a money-laundering transaction prior to the coming into existence of the tainted property.”* Tainted Property will be considered below.

### The Elements of Money Laundering
There are three main elements that require proof for the offence of Money Laundering under Section 243(2). They are

- That the property in question is the proceeds of an offence (Called the predicate offence)
- Engaging in a money laundering transaction of dealing with property in the context of concealing it.  (The Actus Reus)
- Knowledge, belief or recklessness as to the property or part of the property being the proceeds of the offence (The Mens Rea)
### THE PROPERTY IN QUESTION IS THE PROCEEDS OF A (PREDICATE) OFFENCE 
The act of a money laundering firstly requires property that has been generated by a criminal offence. This offence is referred to as the **predicate offence**. A predicate offence is the underlying crime that generates the illegal wealth.

For example, cash is generated from the sale of cocaine, and is used to purchase a Rolex watch. Here the use of the cash to purchase the watch is conducting a money laundering transaction with the proceeds of an offence. The source of the cash is the sale of a class A drug, namely Cocaine, and it is this that is the predicate offence.

As an investigator must not only be aware of this association but you must ***prove*** the source of the property as being proceeds of an offence.

It is not necessary to prove

- That the defendant was **charged** with the offence that was the source of the proceeds
- That the defendant was **convicted** of the offence that was the source of the proceeds
- That the defendant knew or believed the proceeds were generated from a **particular or specific** offence.
****CASELAW****
> *** R v Allison***
> *“Proof that the property was the proceeds of a ****particular**** proved serious offence is not necessary. Nor is it necessary to prove the accused was involved in the serious offending. ****Proof that the property is the proceeds of a serious offence can be by inference properly drawn from the evidence.”***

Proving that the property is the proceeds of an offence is an essential element of this crime and may be difficult to prove even although it can be inferred. Investigators must gather all evidence that infers the property in question is the proceeds of offending.

This link between the predicate offence and money laundering warrants that any investigation into money laundering **runs parallel** to that of the predicate offence. Police instructions make clear that every organised crime investigation and serious fraud matter **must** consider money laundering as part of the investigation plan.

You have an obligation to prosecute Money Laundering where there is evidence of such offending, whether as an individual offence or part of a larger investigation. It is important that you seek any advice needed from the Money Laundering Team early in the investigative process.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 243
The actus reus for Money Laundering requires a **deliberate act** of “engaging in a money laundering transaction”.

What exactly a money laundering transaction is, is defined in subsection (4) of section 243, and is two-pronged.

The act of engaging in a money laundering transaction is complete if, in the context of concealing any property or enabling another person to conceal any property,

- That person **deals** with that property or assisting another (directly or indirectly) to **deal **with that property
### Concealing
Concealing is defined in section 243 (1). It includes behaviours concealing or disguising the property and is broad in its interpretation. It includes actions such as

- Converting the property into another form
- Concealing or disguising the nature of the property
- Concealing or disguising the source of the property
- Concealing or disguising the location of the property
- Concealing or disguising the ownership, disposition, or other interest in the property.
### Dealing
Deal has a basic meaning of handle, manage or deal with the property in any manner and by any means and includes

- To dispose of property, including by sale, purchase, gift or otherwise
- Transfer possession of property
- Bring into or remove from New Zealand
- Remove property out of New Zealand
Both definitions of concealing and dealing are specifically described as “without limitation” indicating the intent of parliament was that the examples given in section 243 are not exhaustive. The kinds of behaviours considered concealing, and acts considered dealing cover vastly more actions than specifically listed.

***Investigation Application: ***

As an investigator is important that you gather evidence regarding the mechanics methods, processes, operations and techniques used to conceal and deal with property.

### General mens rea
A note on the general mens rea. As always criminal acts that make up the actus reus of a money laundering transaction must be deliberate and intentional, **not accidental**, or **mistaken**.

### Property
The definition of property is found in 243(1) and is again broad.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 243 (1)***
> ***Money Laundering***
> *Property; means real or personal property of any description, whether situated in New Zealand or elsewhere and whether tangible or intangible; and includes an interest in any such real or personal property.*

Examples of property include

- Cash in any currency
- Vehicles / boats / motorbikes
- Real estate / houses / apartments

### INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
You must have proof regarding the property in question and any explanations as to how the individual legitimately ‘owns’ it. For example, evidence as to what the property that makes up the proceeds of crime actually is, and how it came to be in the possession or ownership of the individual.

This may seem obvious with cash or a vehicle, for example, but may not be so obvious for intangible proceeds of crime such as funds mixed in with legitimate profits, a right or interest in a business, any cryptocurrency or joint ownership in property.

### THE mens rea FOR SECTION 243
The mens rea of the offence of money laundering involves not only that an act of dealing is deliberately done, but that is done with knowledge, belief or recklessness. When dealing with illicit wealth to conceal it, the offender must deal with the property either

- Knowing the property is the proceeds of the predicate offence
- Believing the property is the proceeds of the predicate offence or
- Being reckless as to whether the property is the proceeds of the predicate offence
### Knowledge
*Simester and Brookbanks* defines knowledge as “knowing or correctly believing” and *R v Crooks* defines knowledge as

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Crooks***
> *Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.*

Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the property is the proceeds of an offence. Mere suspicion on the part of the offender is not enough to prove knowledge or belief, however, may be relevant to recklessness.

As an investigator it is important to investigate, interview and prove that the accessory had the requisite level of knowledge.

### Recklessness
Criminal liability also attaches when an individual deals with property being ***reckless ***to the possibility that it is proceeds of an offence.  In the context of section 243 recklessness has the same meaning and application as other offences in the Crimes Act.

Acting “recklessly” involves consciously and deliberately taking an unjustifiable risk.

It must be proved not only that the defendant was *aware* of the risk and proceeded regardless (a subjective test), but also that it was *unreasonable* for him to do so (an objective test). This is covered by the case law *Cameron v R* and in the Basic Legal Concepts Module.

****CASELAW****
> ***CAMERON v R ***
> *Recklessness is established if:*
> *the defendant recognised that there was a real possibility that:**his or her actions would bring about the proscribed result; and/or**that the proscribed circumstances existed; and having regard to that risk those actions were unreasonable.*

The Court of Appeal in *Hua v R* held that in terms of money laundering, the evidence must show that defendant appreciated that there was a **real possibility** that the transactions involved the proceeds of crime. Following the test for reasonableness in *Cameron*, the evidence should prove that the defendant’s acts were unreasonable.  In relation to Money Laundering the Court of Appeal stated that this would come down to

****CASELAW****
> ***Hua v R***
> *“…whether the defendants had acted as a reasonable prudent person – that is, as a law-abiding person doing their best to comply with the law – would have done.”.*

**Investigation Application**

As always, an investigator should seek to establish knowledge, belief or recklessness by way of evidence of what the suspect knew, or ought to have known in their position or context. Ensuring investigation and interview plans seek to establish the mens rea of the offence of Money Laundering is crucial to a successful prosecution.

### Seizure of Cash found in Suspicious Circumstances (The Police Manual)
As the use of cash is a key money laundering typology, Police have the power to seize cash, gold bars, and gold ingots valued over $10,000 in suspicious circumstances pursuant to section 123B of the Search and Surveillance Act 2012.

A summary of section 123B of the Search and Surveillance Act allows the seizure of cash over $10,000 to establish its origin/intended use or to commence ARU Action.

If you are not satisfied with the explanation given for possession of the cash / gold and there are reasonable grounds to suspect that:

- the cash is not lawfully/legitimately obtained or
- is to be used for, or to further any unlawful or dishonest purpose
You may seize that cash to conduct further investigations or to commence ARU action.

This is a power of last resort and should only be used when there are no other lawful powers to seize the cash. There are strict time limits that apply to action to be taken in relation to the seized cash which must be adhered to.

If Police make a seizure using the section 123B power, they should immediately email 123aru@police.govt.nz, including notebook entries and a brief summary of the circumstances. The local ARU will assess the circumstances and, if there are grounds, will make an application to the District Court. If there are no grounds, they will direct that the cash be returned.

### THE CRIMINAL PROCEEDS (RECOVERY) ACT 2009
When asset recovery legislation was introduced, its intent was to facilitate action in stripping assets and wealth from individuals and groups involved in organised crime.

The legislation moved away from the requirement that there be a conviction recorded to allow for the seizure of assets and monies, to what is currently in place: **a civil-based process** where the Crown must prove on the balance of probabilities that wealth and benefits have been accumulated through significant criminal activity.

The Criminal Proceeds (Recovery) Act 2009 enables the proceeds of crime to be forfeited in three different ways through the **civil** jurisdiction of the High Court. Utilising a balance of probabilities test the Court can, depending on the offending, the asset, and the available evidence, issue a

**Profit Forfeiture Order**

An order issued from the High Court where benefits (profit, cash etc) from significant criminal activity are forfeited to the Crown.

**Asset Forfeiture Order**

An order issued from the High Court where specific assets (houses, motorcycles etc) that have been tainted by significant criminal activity are forfeited to the crown.

**Instrument Forfeiture Order **

An order issued from the High Court where an instrument (property, vehicle etc) has been used to facilitate significant criminal activity is forfeited to the crown.

Criminal proceeds action should be considered by investigators in all situations where criminals are believed to have benefited or acquired assets from significant criminal activity. The Police Asset Recovery Unit in conjunction with the Office of the Crown Solicitor decides which is the appropriate course of action to take in each circumstance and what assets, benefits or instruments may have action taken against them.

What you need to know when investigating in order to take the required action in regards to proceeds of crime.

If your investigation involves

- Significant Criminal Activity (SCA)
- An offence of five years or more
- $30,000 or more of criminal proceeds (including property).
you must consider prosecution.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 6***
> ***Criminal Proceeds (Recovery) Act 2009***
> *Significant Criminal Activity defined as activity engaged in by a person that if proceeded against as a criminal offence would amount to offending –*
> *That consists of, or includes, 1 or more offences punishable by a maximum term of imprisonment of 5 years or more; or**From which property, proceeds, or benefits of a value of $30,000 or more have, directly or indirectly, been acquired or derived.*

Remember - If these circumstances exist you are **required** to consider prosecution in regard to the proceeds of crime. Always seek advice from supervisors and contact the Asset Recovery Unit as soon as possible as to the best course of action.
