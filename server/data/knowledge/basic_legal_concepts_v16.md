# INTRODUCTION
Detectives are responsible for conducting thorough, impartial, and comprehensive investigations. However, achieving a high standard of investigative practice is not an inherent skill gained simply by being a member of the New Zealand Police. Each individual approaches their work differently, shaped by their unique knowledge and experiences, and this is equally true within the Police organisation.

**Becoming a Detective**

Becoming a detective requires more than technical expertise. It involves developing self-awareness, gaining new perspectives, and building foundational knowledge and skills. Additionally, it demands a deliberate approach to shaping your own investigative practice.

This module serves as the starting point for this process and is divided into two key sections:

- **Investigative Mindset and Critical Thinking**
The first section focuses on cultivating an investigative mindset and critical thinking. Detectives must recognise their own unconscious biases and assumptions that influence their thinking and decision-making processes. Developing the ability to critically evaluate beyond these natural tendencies is fundamental to conducting impartial and effective investigations.

Equally important is the structure and methodology of an investigation. A sound investigation requires a systematic approach to planning and decision-making. This includes organising, analysing, challenging, and assessing information in a logical and purposeful way. In this section, you will be introduced to the principles of an investigative mindset and learn ways to **deliberately apply** them in your work.

**2. Legal Knowledge for Evidential Sufficiency**

The second section introduces the legal knowledge necessary for evidential sufficiency in New Zealand. Detectives must have a comprehensive understanding of New Zealand’s criminal law and procedures. This knowledge is essential for ensuring that investigations are conducted effectively and lawfully.

A strong grasp of New Zealand criminal law enables investigators to accurately identify criminal offences while understanding the legal requirements for prosecution. This knowledge is crucial for conducting thorough investigations and ensuring evidentially sufficient prosecutions.

# INVESTIGATIVE MINDSET &amp; CRITICAL THINKING
An effective investigator possesses two key attributes:

- **An investigative mindset and critical thinking: **A disciplined and deliberate attitude and approach to thinking in investigation.
- **Systematic decision-making:** Utilising the appreciation technique for informed and objective choices.
### The investigative mindset
**Introduction**

An investigative mindset in the context of being a detective refers to a disciplined, analytical and critical approach to uncovering the truth behind criminal activity. An investigative mindset is critical to success. It is not something individuals are born with.

It involves several key attributes of critical thinking:

- Curiosity and Scepticism. A detective with an investigative mindset questions everything, never accepts information at face value and consistently challenges the evidence.
- Objectivity. A detective sets aside personal biases or assumptions to evaluate facts impartially and follow the evidence where it leads.
- Logical Reasoning. Detectives must piece together information with disciplined methodology, with any inferences being grounded in evidence.
- Attention to Detail. Part of practised methodology is a keen eye for detail that others might overlook.
- Persistence and Patience. The ability to slow down, work thoroughly, and revisit and challenge their own conclusions.
- Communication and Collaboration. A strong investigative mindset involves effectively sharing findings and the ability to work as part of a team, liaising with other professionals and fellow officers.
A detective’s investigative mindset is underpinned by knowledge, understanding and skills of the following:

**Four key areas of knowledge to the investigation**

- **The characteristics of crime** (including the nature of crime and criminal behaviour)
- **Core legislation**
- **National policies and procedures**
- **Investigative techniques**
When you are equipped with a solid theoretical foundation, you are better prepared to approach investigations in a principled, structured and critical manner. This foundation knowledge of your specialty area allows you to *“demystify”* the tacit knowledge that experienced investigators rely upon.

The next chapter, *Evidential Sufficiency*, will introduce and build on this foundational knowledge. For now, keep in mind that knowledge alone is not enough—without an investigative mindset guided by critical thinking, effective investigations cannot occur. It is important, therefore, to have a solid foundation in the theory of critical thinking and decision-making.

Critical thinking is a deliberate and purposeful method of planning, analysing information and making decisions. When considering critical thinking, it is important to acknowledge that individuals catalogue, process and approach thinking methodology in different ways. Implicit in humanity is that individuals think differently.

What is important is that the detective carefully considers the theories and principles of critical thinking and applies an approach that works for them. Not analysing their own thinking processes – and not deliberately applying any principles and skills – risks muddled thinking, importation of biases and ultimately unprofessional and evidentially insufficient investigations.

Once they have specialist investigative knowledge coupled with knowledge of critical thinking theories, it is then important that trainee investigators embark on **practical, experiential learning**. It is only when applying the theories through meaningful work experiences that critical thinking skills are developed and refined. It is only through experience that investigators will develop the ability to make decisive and confident decisions.

**Summary**

The investigative mindset is an attitude or state of mind that investigators develop over time with practise.

With an investigative mindset, you will intentionally:

- Apply the four key areas of knowledge to the investigation.
- Apply a principled approach to collecting and evaluating material.
- Use logical and deductive reasoning techniques to advance the investigation.
- Make appropriate investigative decisions at every stage of the investigative process.
- Evaluate and substantiate your decision-making to others.

### What informs critical thinking, planning and decision-making
Critical thinking, planning and decision-making are central to any criminal investigation. A core skill required is the ability to make appropriate decisions that you can justify to others. It is important to acknowledge and understand that many external and internal experiences unique to each individual inform this process. This is the starting point for analysing how you personally think, plan and make decisions. Some of these biases and experiences relevant to the investigator are explored in the next section.

**Background on decision-making**

Research reveals that investigators rely on heuristics – working rules developed through experience and shared practices among colleagues. These informal guidelines shape how situations and evidence are understood.

These working rules create a "*how we do things around here*" framework, guiding an investigator's understanding of situations and the evidence they need to gather.

However, over-reliance on such rules can limit adaptability and impede effective decision-making, particularly in novel scenarios.

**Working Experience**

An investigator’s working rules are shaped by personal experiences, which can sometimes constrain their decision-making abilities. The depth of their experience and their ability to adapt it to new situations directly influence their effectiveness. While diversity in experience is both beneficial and inevitable, it can pose challenges when evaluating investigations or assessing individual performance.

**The Unconscious Nature of Working Rules**

Working rules typically serve as an efficient framework for decision-making and rarely cause significant issues.

However, these rules can become so ingrained that investigators may not always be aware they are using them. This lack of awareness can create difficulties when attempting to explain how a specific decision was made.

In such cases, investigators might attribute their decisions to a hunch, gut reaction, or common sense. While these instincts may lead to sound decisions, an inability to articulate the reasoning behind instincts, can hinder others from understanding or evaluating the decision-making process. At worst, the rules and reasons for them can become ‘lost’ over time and therefore may evolve into a practice at odds with the original rule.

Although relying on intuition is not inherently problematic, investigators must be able to justify their decisions to a range of stakeholders, including victims, witnesses, supervisors, managers, and partners within the criminal justice system.

**Required knowledge**

Effective and accountable decision-making is grounded in an investigator’s knowledge of:

- The legal framework
- Crime characteristics
- National policies and procedures
An experienced investigator, who can intuitively *“know” *the right course of action and make decisions swiftly, actually possesses extensive knowledge of legislation and crime characteristics.

**Making decisions**

Good decision-making is neither a matter of luck nor guesswork – it is the result of logical reasoning and careful deliberation in conjunction with the specialist knowledge of legal frameworks, crime characteristics and national policies and procedures.

### Some factors affecting decision-making
Several factors can adversely influence the quality of decision-making. Investigators must understand and remain mindful of these factors to mitigate their impact:

**Individual bias affects decision-making**

Personal views about people, places, and situations can influence how decisions are made. Often, individuals are unaware of how these opinions shape their thoughts and choices. For instance, hidden biases related to diverse groups, criminal history, or stereotypes can subtly affect judgment, without conscious awareness.

**Example: Individual Bias in a Burglary Investigation**

Detective Smith is investigating a series of home burglaries in a neighbourhood. During the investigation, a witness mentions seeing a young man wearing a hoodie loitering near one of the crime scenes. Detective Smith, based on past experiences and societal stereotypes, associates young men in hoodies with criminal behaviour. This bias leads Smith to focus primarily on locating and questioning individuals who match this description.

Meanwhile, evidence from surveillance footage shows a middle-aged individual entering and leaving the neighbourhood at odd hours around the time of the burglaries. However, because this evidence doesn’t align with Smith’s preconceived notion of a likely suspect, it is initially dismissed as unimportant. The detective’s fixation on the “young man in a hoodie” profile results in the questioning of several innocent individuals, creating delays and strained community relations.

Eventually, further investigation reveals that the actual perpetrator was the middle-aged individual caught on camera, who used the hoodie stereotype to divert suspicion. By the time this realisation is made, valuable time has been lost, and additional crimes have occurred.

**Verification Bias**

Verification bias is a cognitive tendency where an individual becomes focused on making decisions that reinforce pre-existing viewpoints or hypotheses. This form of bias can have profound implications in investigative work, potentially leading to the neglect of alternative perspectives or valuable information.

For example, in a criminal investigation, an investigator might overly rely on a single witness account that incorrectly identifies a suspect. This fixation could result in filtering out or dismissing critical evidence or leads that do not align with the initial hypothesis. As a result, the investigation might become skewed, risking the possibility of overlooking the actual perpetrator and compromising justice.

To counteract verification bias, investigators must remain open-minded and objective throughout the investigative process. This involves actively challenging assumptions, seeking contradictory evidence, and thoroughly evaluating all available information, whether it supports or contradicts the initial theory.

**Example: Verification Bias in a Homicide Investigation**

Detective Jones is investigating a homicide case where the victim was found in their home with no signs of forced entry. Early in the investigation, a neighbour mentions that they saw the victim arguing with their estranged partner, Alex, a few days before the murder. Based on this information, Jones develops the hypothesis that Alex is the likely perpetrator.

Convinced of this theory, Detective Jones begins focusing the investigation primarily on Alex.

They:

- **Prioritise evidence that supports the theory:** Jones spends significant time collecting and interpreting evidence that might implicate Alex, such as text message arguments between Alex and the victim.
- **Overlook contradictory evidence:** Forensic evidence from the crime scene, such as fingerprints and DNA, indicates the presence of another individual who does not match Alex. Jones rationalises this as unrelated or inconsequential.
- **Dismiss alternative leads:** A witness later reports seeing a suspicious delivery person near the victim’s home on the day of the murder. However, because this doesn’t align with Jones’ hypothesis, the lead is deprioritised.
Eventually, further investigation reveals that the murder was committed by a delivery driver with a history of violent offences. The focus on Alex not only delayed identifying the actual perpetrator but also caused unnecessary stress and reputational harm to Alex.

**Impact of Verification Bias:**

- **Delayed justice:** Valuable time and resources were wasted pursuing a narrow line of inquiry while the real perpetrator remained at large.
- **Harmed objectivity:** The detective’s preconceived theory overshadowed the evaluation of new evidence and alternative possibilities.
- **Compromised trust:** The mistaken targeting of an innocent individual damaged community trust in law enforcement.
This example underscores the importance of maintaining an open and objective mindset in investigations, actively seeking evidence that challenges initial assumptions, and ensuring all leads are given due consideration.

**Availability errors**

Availability errors occur when decisions are based on readily available or easily recalled information. In a policing context, this is when decisions are based on commonly vivid, dramatic, or emotionally charged information rather than the full scope of evidence. The drama is easy for the brain to recall. This cognitive bias can significantly impair an investigator’s judgment, leading to incomplete or inaccurate conclusions.

For instance, in a high-profile jewellery store robbery, the store owner’s emotional account might describe the perpetrator as a tall man with a distinctive facial scar. This vivid description could cause investigators to focus narrowly on finding suspects who match this profile, potentially overlooking other critical evidence.

Important details that might be neglected in such a scenario include:

- Surveillance footage showing multiple perpetrators, some of whom do not match the owner’s description
- Forensic evidence pointing to the use of a getaway vehicle
- Witness statements from nearby businesses that contradict parts of the owner’s account
By focusing disproportionately on the emotionally charged description, investigators risk missing key information that could lead to a more accurate resolution of the case. This illustrates how availability errors can result in wrongful accusations or allow true perpetrators to evade capture.

To mitigate availability errors, investigators should objectively consider all evidence, irrespective of its emotional impact or vividness. Regular team discussions, structured evidence reviews, and systematic decision-making processes can help reduce the influence of this bias.

**Example: Availability Errors in a Missing Person Investigation**

Detective Riveria is leading a missing person investigation involving a teenager, Emily, who was last seen leaving a party late at night. Emily’s parents give an emotional account of how she recently argued with a close friend, Jake, over a personal dispute. They express strong suspicion that Jake may have harmed Emily, describing him as temperamental and emotionally unstable.

Moved by the vivid and emotionally charged narrative provided by Emily’s parents, Detective Riveria prioritises Jake as the primary suspect. This focus leads to the following consequences:

- **Narrowed focus:** Riveria invests significant time interrogating Jake, analysing his movements, and searching for evidence to support the theory that he is responsible for Emily’s disappearance.
- **Overlooking broader evidence:** Other critical leads, such as witness reports of an unknown vehicle seen following Emily after the party, are not pursued with urgency.
- **Missed opportunities:** Surveillance footage from nearby stores, which could have provided a clearer picture of Emily’s movements, is reviewed too late, as some recordings are overwritten after a few days.
Ultimately, Emily is found alive several days later, having been abducted by a stranger who was later identified from the overlooked vehicle reports. The detective’s initial fixation on Jake, driven by the vivid and emotionally compelling account of Emily’s parents, delayed the investigation’s progress and allowed the abductor to evade capture for a longer period.

**Impact of Availability Errors:**

- **Skewed priorities:** Emotionally charged information overshadowed a more balanced evaluation of all available evidence.
- **Wasted resources:** Time and resources were disproportionately allocated to an unproductive line of inquiry.
- **Potential harm:** The delay in focusing on the correct leads prolonged Emily’s ordeal and risked her safety.
This example highlights the importance of resisting the influence of vivid or emotionally compelling narratives. Investigators must objectively evaluate all evidence, ensuring that critical leads are not deprioritised due to cognitive biases. Structured decision-making processes and team reviews can help mitigate the impact of availability errors.

**In Summary**

Many factors can compromise the quality of the investigative mindset, critical thinking and decision-making in investigations. Therefore, investigators must adopt a disciplined approach and actively challenge their own reasoning to minimise errors. The more significant the decision’s consequences, the more thoroughly it should be reviewed by a qualified supervisor with the authority to approve and endorse it.

### Information gathered during an investigation
When running an investigation, the recognition, analysis and management of information coming into the investigation is crucial. Critical thinking and use of a methodical approach when dealing with that information is important.

### Information
**What is information?**

Information refers to any substance (including data, objects, sounds, and images) obtained during a criminal investigation that has relevance to the offence being investigated, the individual(s) involved, or the broader circumstances surrounding the case.

Information may or may not later be used as evidence.

**Sources of Information**

Information can be gathered from various sources, including:

- Victims
- Witnesses
- Suspects
- Locations, such as crime scenes, and the premises of victims or suspects
- CCTV footage, telephone records, banking and credit card statements, and intelligence databases
In practice, information is often found in the following formats:

- Statements
- Documents
- Reports
- Physical exhibits, such as weapons, clothing, stolen items, or biological and chemical substances
- Fingerprints
- Images
- Audio or video recordings
- Digital data (SMS, location data, etc)
As an investigator, you must identify and locate potential sources of information. Additionally, you need to understand the proper methods for gathering, retrieving, and storing this information to ensure its admissibility as evidence.

**Information generated by an offence**

To ensure a thorough investigation, your goal should be to collect as much relevant information as possible and carefully examine it for potential use.

Initiating an investigation promptly after an offence increases the opportunity to gather the maximum amount of information. While the objective should always be to collect information that will later be admissible as evidence, there will be instances where this cannot be achieved.

The distinction between information, intelligence, and evidence depends on the purpose it serves. As the investigation progresses, the proportion of information that can be used as evidence in court will typically be smaller than the total amount gathered.

Remember that all information needs to follow the three-step process when being gathered for an investigation:

- **Index everything:** All your information needs to be indexed regardless of type.
- **Relevance:** Determine what information is relevant and what information is NOT relevant. Information is considered relevant if it has the potential to influence the outcome of a case. This means it must relate directly to a fact that is in dispute and must help to prove or disprove an element of the case.
- Disclosure: What information can be lawfully withheld and what must be released. This is governed by section 16 of the Criminal Disclosure Act 2008.
**Information that is admissible as evidence**

The rules governing the admissibility of information as evidence in court are complex and often subject to dispute during criminal trials. Consequently, any information collected may, under certain circumstances, be judged admissible.

In serious crime investigations, decisions about the evidentiary value of collected information are made collaboratively, often involving the investigating team and Crown prosecutors. These discussions help ensure proper evaluation of its significance for evidence and disclosure.

A solid understanding of the relevant sections in **Part 2 of the Evidence Act 2006** is crucial, particularly regarding the admissibility of certain types of information, such as:

- Hearsay
- Statements of opinion
- Expert evidence
- Improperly obtained information
Understanding of the Evidence Act will be covered during the Detective Development Programme.

**Information and Intelligence**

Not all information collected will be admissible as evidence, but that does not diminish its investigative value. Such information may still play a critical role in generating leads, identifying new lines of inquiry, or supporting other aspects of the investigation.

Even if certain information is not admissible, it should still be gathered, as it may lead to the discovery of other reliable and admissible evidence. Ultimately, whether information is classified as intelligence, information, or evidence depends on its intended use.

### Applying critical thinking to investigative information
**Principle 1: Understand the source of information**

Understanding the origin of collected information is essential for effectively analysing it and explaining its characteristics and relevance to others.

**Principle 2: Plan and prepare**

Planning and preparation involve making deliberate decisions about information collection. It is crucial to obtain information as early as possible and set clear objectives for retrieving it in a way that maximises quality and avoids contamination.

When beginning a routine inquiry, consider these sources of information:

- The Law including case law and legal processes
- Re-interviewing the witness or complainant
- Information sources – NIA, Intel, etc
- Local and government agencies
- Private businesses
- Scene examination.
While a checklist can guide initial actions at a crime scene, in complex, prolonged, or challenging cases, you must develop a **unique** written plan with clear objectives for collecting and preserving additional information as the investigation progresses.

Your planning and preparation should address how to:

- Maximise the collection of information
- Meet legal, policy, and procedural requirements
- Utilise specialist or technical equipment and expertise
- Prepare the most appropriate location for information collection, such as an interview suite, a search warrant, or the Intel Office
- Appropriately store information
Sources of Information must be scrutinised thoroughly. During the collection process, ensure the following:

- Immediate actions are taken to preserve information quality
- Reliability is tested at the earliest opportunity
- Relevant records are made
**Principle 3: Examine all material**

The examination process typically involves three steps:

- Interpret
- Clarify
- Challenge
The relevance of each step depends on the source and characteristics of the information.

**Interpret**

When examining information, investigators interpret it to draw inferences or logical conclusions about its potential role in the investigation. The more information gathered, the easier it becomes to assess the contribution of a source.

Following a detailed examination, you should be prepared to explain your interpretations and remain open to alternative perspectives.

**Clarify**

Clarification involves identifying and addressing inconsistencies or ambiguities in the information. This may require cross-referencing it with other gathered information or taking further action to resolve uncertainties.

**Challenge**

Experience shows that seemingly reliable information can prove incorrect, and initial interpretations can change with additional analysis.

To ensure accuracy, investigators must continually challenge both the meaning and reliability of information. Adopting an investigative mindset means treating all information as potentially flawed or misleading. For example, every account should be checked for inconsistencies or conflicts with other information.This principle is summarised by the mnemonic **A.B.C**

**A** – Assume nothing

**B** – Believe nothing

**C** – Corroborate everything

Avoid making prima facie assumptions (accepting information at face value) or neglecting the pursuit of corroboration.

**Principle 4: Record and collate**

After examining any information, you must determine:

- The examination records needed
- Storage requirements
- Security measures
- Access permissions
- Conditions for disclosure
Guidance on exhibit management and information disclosure can be found in the “*Investigation*” section of the online Police Manual.

**Principle 5: Evaluate**

Evaluation helps identify immediate actions related to the source or the information gathered. These actions may include:

- Testing the reliability of the source or the gathered information
- Implementing fast-track measures to secure additional information
**Summary**

Applying an investigative mindset to information collection ensures:

- The maximum amount of information is gathered.
- Reliability is tested at the earliest opportunity.
- Immediate actions are taken.
- Relevant records are created.
- Information is appropriately stored.
- Logical deductions are made.
### Expanding Principle 5: Evaluate Information 
**Regular formal evaluation**

Information gathered during an investigation should undergo regular formal evaluation. This process involves ‘*stepping back*’ from the immediate demands of the investigation to take a deliberate and critically reflective view. Formal evaluation is equally applicable to investigations of volume crimes as it is to more serious or complex cases.

An investigative mindset incorporates three key types of formal evaluation:

1.	**Investigative Evaluation**

2.	**Evidential Evaluation**

3.	**Self and Peer Evaluation**

The key difference between investigative and evidential evaluation is that during an investigative evaluation, all available information is assessed, even if it is not admissible as evidence.

In contrast, evidential evaluation focuses on evaluating the strength of the case by considering only evidentially admissible information.

Self and peer evaluation is a critical component of professional practice and helps ensure thoroughness and objectivity.

In practice, all three types of evaluation may be conducted multiple times during an investigation, following the same structured method.

**Evaluation process**

A standard evaluation process can be applied to all three types of evaluation. The process outlined in the table on the previous page provides a consistent and structured approach, offering an easy-to-use and auditable format that is meaningful to others.

During the early stages of an investigation, greater emphasis should be placed on **investigative evaluation** to generate information and drive progress. Later, particularly during the suspect management phase, the emphasis shifts toward ensuring the case is evidentially robust for prosecution purposes.

In all cases, the outcomes of any evaluation should be documented. If further investigative actions cannot be identified and all existing lines of inquiry have been pursued, this should be clearly recorded.

**Undertake evaluation**

**Set a Purposeful Objective:** Begin by reviewing the information collected to identify questions or problems that need to be addressed.

**Filter the Information:** For investigative or evidential evaluation, analyse the information using three key filters: **relevance**, **reliability**, and **admissibility**.

**Relevance**

Information is relevant when it has a bearing on the investigation, any individual being investigated, or the surrounding circumstances of the case. Information is considered irrelevant only if it is incapable of influencing the outcome of the case.

Adopt a broad perspective and evaluate as much information as possible. Exclude information as irrelevant only after careful consideration or consultation with appropriate parties, such as a disclosure officer, prosecution team, or Crown prosecutor.

**Reliability**

The reliability of information may have been assessed during its collection; however, it is prudent to reevaluate reliability during the formal evaluation process. This ensures that potential issues, such as overlooked details, are addressed.

Formal evaluation provides an opportunity to reassess decision-making regarding factors like:

- Witness reliability
- Previous offending
- Known associates
These factors may not have been fully understood at the time of collection. The reliability filter emphasises the importance of obtaining independent corroboration for information intended to serve as evidence in prosecution.

**Admissibility **

An evidential evaluation involves applying the additional filter of admissibility. This filter ensures that the maximum amount of information is presented to the courts in an evidentially acceptable format.

Applying the admissibility filter may require consulting with experts to determine what constitutes an acceptable evidential format for specific information. Thoughtful planning and forethought can maximise the amount of information that is admissible as evidence.

### Knowledge gained from information 
The information collected during an investigation provides valuable knowledge about the incident, offence, or problem under review. This information can include various types of material, such as victim and witness statements, exhibits and images, intelligence reports, lists of active offenders in the area, and forensic science reports.

To maximise its utility, organise the knowledge in a way that highlights gaps in the information. Identifying these gaps can help pinpoint potential lines of inquiry.

The **5WH formula** – **Who, What, When, Where, Why, How** – is an effective method for organising the information. When used in conjunction with evaluative questions, it helps structure the investigation and guide decision-making.

Key evaluative questions to consider include:

- What is known?
- What is not known?
- What are the consistencies?
- What are the conflicts?
By systematically organising information using the 5WH formula and these questions, investigators can better understand the scope of the investigation and identify actionable next steps.

**Consider hypotheses**

A **hypothesis** is defined as “*a suggested explanation for a group of facts, either accepted as a basis for further verification or accepted as likely to be true*” (Collins English Dictionary, 2004). Another way to describe a hypothesis is as a scenario that best explains the available information.

While the investigative mindset relies heavily on logical, ordered thinking (i.e., “*So what?*”), hypothesising introduces a form of creative thinking that encourages a different perspective on the knowledge held (i.e., “*What if?*”).

The decision to use hypotheses depends on the amount of information available and whether it is sufficient to support prosecution. Generally, investigations progress because the information gathered generates actions, which in turn produce more information. This iterative process continues until enough has been gathered to identify a suspect and support a prosecution. Often, the link between the information and the subsequent actions is straightforward and logical, making the development of hypotheses unnecessary.

However, there may be instances when the available information does not clearly indicate the next steps to further the investigation. In such cases, hypotheses can help regain investigative momentum by generating new lines of inquiry.

Hypotheses are also valuable for testing whether the interpretation of the information gathered is the most reasonable one. Developing alternative hypotheses from the same information can guide further inquiries to confirm which interpretation is most likely to be accurate. This approach not only supports decision-making during the investigation but also prepares for the types of interpretations that may be proposed in court.

In the early stages of a case, when little is certain, and the available information is limited, forming hypotheses about what may have happened should be avoided. Hypotheses in such circumstances risk being influenced by personal bias or stereotyping, particularly when based on insufficient or uncertain information.

**Developing hypotheses**

Before developing hypotheses, an investigator must possess sufficient knowledge about the case to make informed and valid judgements. Hypotheses should be developed by individuals with sufficient expertise to understand the associated risks. They should be created with a specific objective in mind, addressing a gap or conflict in the evidence. For example, a gap could be: “*How did the victim get from home to the scene?*”

A hypothesis should provide a reasonable interpretation of the available information and offer the most logical explanation of the known facts. However, there is often no single, definitive explanation. Instead, investigators may develop a series of hypotheses, each presenting an alternative explanation. All relevant information should be thoroughly evaluated, and any assumptions or inferences made during this process must be clearly documented.

The purpose of using hypotheses is to identify additional information or intelligence that can bridge gaps in the investigation – such as locating the missing link or connecting a suspect or third party to a specific element of the case. These scenarios often generate multiple potential lines of inquiry requiring further investigation. However, the material gathered from pursuing one hypothesis can sometimes provide grounds to discount the others.

Hypotheses are often strengthened when they incorporate the insights of experienced investigators. Because making judgements about hypotheses can be challenging, they should only be used when absolutely necessary.

It is important to note that hypotheses are not “*gut feelings*” or based on “*common sense*.” They are the result of deliberate, creative thinking, grounded in known facts and clearly stated assumptions. When hypotheses involve critical elements of the investigation, they must be rigorously and regularly reviewed by an independent investigator to ensure objectivity and validity.

**Conclusion**

Police investigators are responsible for identifying, gathering, assessing and evaluating large volumes of information. These tasks must be carried out methodically to ensure that investigations are well-organised and logically structured. The content in this lesson provides guidance and structure to support this process. However, keep in mind that everyone processes and organises information differently. Regardless of your personal thinking style, it is important to reflect on **how you** approach information **and practise** maintaining an organised, analytical method for gathering evidence and running investigations.

# EVIDENTIAL SUFFICIENCY
### What is Evidential Sufficiency?
**Evidential sufficiency** refers to the adequacy of evidence required to meet a specific standard or threshold in legal, scientific, or academic settings. It involves having enough credible and relevant evidence to support a conclusion, claim, or decision. The exact level of sufficiency varies by context – for example, the level of sufficiency in a criminal case is “beyond a reasonable doubt” and in a civil case, the level of sufficiency is on the “preponderance of evidence”.

In New Zealand’s legal framework, the prosecution bears the burden of **proving** an accused person’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. However, before a case reaches trial, it must first pass an **evidential test of sufficiency**. This test is a critical safeguard against unfounded prosecutions, ensuring that individuals are not subjected to the stress and expense of a trial unless a reasonable prospect of conviction exists based on the available evidence.

To establish evidential sufficiency, the prosecution must demonstrate that the evidence gathered during the investigation, when considered in its most favourable light, is sufficient to prove each essential element of the alleged offence. This assessment involves a careful examination of the quality, quantity, and reliability of the evidence, including witness statements, interviews, physical evidence, forensic analysis, and other relevant information.

Importantly, the assessment of evidential sufficiency does not determine guilt or innocence. Rather, it is an objective analysis of whether the evidence, if accepted by a reasonable jury or judge, meets the legal necessities of a crime and could support a conviction. If the evidential threshold is not met, the case may be dismissed, or alternative resolutions—such as diversion or cautioning—may be considered.

**Evidential sufficiency of police investigations comprises two key aspects:** criminal law and criminal procedure.

**Criminal Law** – An investigator in the Criminal Investigation Branch (CIB) must understand fundamental New Zealand criminal law principles and the specific elements of each offence. This knowledge is essential for conducting thorough investigations – understanding what is required to prove or disprove criminal offending and resulting in successful prosecutions.

**Criminal Procedure** – Investigators must also have a comprehensive knowledge of the laws governing procedural aspects of investigations. This includes areas such as search and seizure protocols, evidence admissibility, interviewing, and understanding the rights of suspects and witnesses, as well as any relevant court rulings or guidance from the Solicitor-General.

By adhering to these principles, investigators uphold the integrity of the investigative process and safeguard the rights of all parties involved, thus supporting the principles of fairness and due process.

**Example: **Evidential Sufficiency in Practice

Imagine a case where someone is accused of breaking into a house. For the prosecution to take this case to trial, they need enough evidence to meet the threshold of evidential sufficiency – meaning the evidence should be strong enough that, if a jury or judge accepted it as true, they could reasonably consider convicting the accused.

Here’s how it might look in practice:

- **Basic Evidence Gathered**: Investigators collect evidence consistent with their understanding of the law and criminal practice, including a neighbour’s eyewitness statement saying they saw someone enter the house at night, fingerprints on the broken window that match the accused, and footage from a nearby security camera showing someone who resembles the accused near the scene. The victim reports property missing.
- **Assessing Quality and Relevance:** The prosecution reviews the evidence to see if it proves each element of the offence prescribed in law. They will ensure the evidence is credible, relevant, and connected to the alleged crime. They will also look at the practice of the investigation and whether the correct legal procedures have been followed in the gathering of the evidence. For example, the fingerprints may be linked to the accused but if they have been lifted incorrectly or cross-contaminated, they will be inadmissible in a prosecution. Likewise, the prosecution will check if the eyewitness is reliable, whether the footage is clear enough to identify the person and that identification procedures have been followed correctly.
- **Deciding on Evidential Sufficiency**: If the prosecution finds this evidence compelling enough that a reasonable jury could, based on this alone, find the accused guilty, they may decide it meets the sufficiency threshold and proceed with the trial.
If the evidence is weak – for instance, if evidence of an element is missing, the fingerprints are inconclusive, the eyewitness isn’t reliable, or the footage is too blurry – the prosecution may decide it doesn’t meet the sufficiency standard. In this case, they might drop the charges or seek more evidence before moving forward.

In essence, evidential sufficiency is like a “*gatekeeper*” check: only cases with a reasonable chance of proving guilt are brought to trial, protecting people from going through unnecessary legal proceedings when the evidence is unlikely to lead to conviction.

Remember: evidential sufficiency in investigations cannot be achieved if an investigator lacks adequate knowledge of the legal concepts that they are required to produce evidence of. Similarly, even with sufficient legal knowledge, evidence gathered improperly or in violation of criminal procedure laws will be deemed inadmissible during prosecution.

### Standard of Proof
The standard of proof in criminal cases requires the prosecution to prove the defendant’s guilt “beyond a reasonable doubt.” This means that, based on the evidence, no other reasonable explanation can exist besides the defendant’s guilt.

The defendant must be acquitted if there is reasonable doubt about his/her guilt.

As an investigator, you must investigate and produce the best possible evidence before the court. The prosecution must prove **beyond reasonable doubt** that the defendant committed the actus reus of the offence with the requisite mens rea. In addition, the prosecution must disprove beyond reasonable doubt any common law defences, if raised.

**Example**: Theft Case

A person is accused of stealing a laptop from an office. The prosecution presents evidence that the person was seen near the laptop just before it went missing and has no alibi for that time. However, if there’s a possibility that someone else could have taken it, and this creates reasonable doubt; the standard of proof is not met. In that case, the defendant should be acquitted because the evidence doesn’t exclude other explanations beyond a reasonable doubt.

### Strict Liability Offences
In strict liability offences, the prosecution only needs to show that the defendant committed the act (actus reus) without proving intent (mens rea). However, the defendant can avoid liability by proving they were not at fault (total absence of fault).

**Example: **Selling Alcohol to a Minor

A bartender sells alcohol to a customer without checking their ID. Later, it is revealed that the customer is underage. The bartender could be held liable for serving alcohol to a minor (a strict liability offence). However, the bartender may defend themselves by showing they genuinely believed the customer was of legal age, especially if the customer presented a convincing fake ID.

### Absolute Liability Offences
In absolute liability offences, the prosecution only needs to prove the defendant committed the act (actus reus), with no consideration of fault or intent. The defendant is liable simply because the act was committed, regardless of any defences.

**Example:** Speeding

A driver unintentionally goes over the speed limit while driving. Even if the driver didn’t realise they were speeding, they would still be held liable under absolute liability. The fact they were speeding is enough to establish guilt, and no defence of faultlessness (like a broken speedometer) is accepted.

These examples illustrate how strict and absolute liability offences differ in terms of the defendant’s ability to present a defence based on fault or intent.

### Legal Thresholds &amp; Tests
Legal thresholds are essentially standards or measures developed over time that are used to guide decision-making by investigators. They serve as benchmarks for determining whether sufficient evidence exists to take further action or prosecution.

Three of these are introduced here:

### The threshold of Prima Facie
Prima facie is a Latin term that means “*at first glance*” or “*on its face*”. Another way of explaining this in a legal context is that something appears to be true or valid based on initial evidence or observation.

This is not necessarily the end of it though. In law, evidence may be sufficient for you to act initially; however other evidence may come to light, or your initial observation may be contradicted, which will change your position and course of action.

**Example:** Prima Facie Evidence in a Traffic Violation

Suppose a driver is caught on a traffic camera running a red light. The footage initially shows that, on its face (prima facie), the driver committed the offence. This evidence is enough to justify issuing a ticket. However, if the driver later presents evidence showing the traffic light was malfunctioning or an emergency forced them through the red light, this new information could challenge the prima facie assumption and affect the outcome.

In both cases, these examples illustrate how “*beyond a reasonable doubt*” ensures a higher level of proof, while “prima facie” allows initial action based on first impressions, open to being disproved later.

### The threshold of ‘Reasonable Cause to Believe’.
“Reasonable cause to believe” implies a higher level of certainty than “reasonable cause to suspect.” When you have reasonable cause to believe something, you don’t just think it might be true; rather, you have a strong, well-founded belief based on credible evidence or a combination of facts that makes the conclusion highly likely. This standard requires more substantial proof and a more confident conclusion than mere suspicion.

**Example:** Reasonable Cause to Believe

Imagine a customs officer inspects a traveller’s luggage on an x-ray machine and finds items that match the exact description of prohibited goods. Here, the officer has reasonable cause to believe the traveller is carrying prohibited goods because the evidence and admission provide a solid basis for that belief.

### The threshold of ‘Reasonable Cause to Suspect’.
“Reasonable cause to suspect,” on the other hand, has a lower threshold. It means you have a justified suspicion based on evidence or facts, but this suspicion is not yet fully confirmed. The evidence or facts are enough to prompt a closer look or preliminary action, but they don’t yet reach the level of certainty required for ‘reasonable cause to believe’.

**Example:** Reasonable Cause to Suspect.

In the same customs scenario, if the officer notices that a traveller is nervous and acting unusually, along with the fact that they’re coming from a country known for exporting prohibited goods, the officer may have reasonable cause to suspect the traveller could be carrying prohibited items. This suspicion is grounded in observable behaviour and relevant circumstances but does not yet constitute a firm belief without further investigation.

### The Key Difference
*Reasonable Cause to Suspect* is an initial, justified suspicion that requires further investigation. It’s based on evidence that suggests something might be true.

*Reasonable Cause to Believe* requires more compelling evidence, leading to a well-founded, confident belief that something is true.

In short, “*reasonable cause to believe*” implies greater certainty and stronger evidence than “*reasonable cause to suspect*”.

### The Legal ‘Subjective / Objective’ Test
In New Zealand, both legislation and case law provide legal tests to assess situations, especially in determining the *mens rea (mental state)* of an offence. Investigators need to understand these tests to gather evidence that sufficiently supports a prosecution.

A key legal framework involves combining what are known as the subjective and objective tests. These tests assess aspects of mens rea, including reckless intent, knowledge, and consent, by examining both the individual’s perspective and that of a reasonable person.

- **Subjective Test: **This test focuses on the individual’s personal perspective—what they actually *believed *or *intended* or *knew* during the event. It looks at the person’s state of mind, personal knowledge, and sincere beliefs, essentially evaluating their internal reasoning.
- **Objective Test:** This test evaluates the *reasonableness* of the individual’s beliefs or actions from an *external* perspective, specifically considering what a “reasonable person” in the same situation would think or know. It assesses the situation through the lens of ordinary standards of reasonableness.
The subjective and objective tests work in combination with each other to assess belief, intent or knowledge from the defendant’s point of view as well as from a reasonable person’s point of view. By combining these subjective and objective assessments, investigators can form a comprehensive view of both personal belief, intent or knowledge and reasonable standards when evaluating evidence.

**Example:** The Subjective / Objective Test.

Jamie is driving well over the speed limit on a wet road at night, assuming they have the skills to handle the car safely, even in poor conditions.

**Subjective Test:** The subjective test would consider Jamie’s personal belief about their driving ability. Did Jamie genuinely think they could control the car at that speed on a wet road? If Jamie believed they were an exceptionally skilled driver and had no concern about losing control, they might pass the subjective test since they didn’t intend to drive recklessly.

**Objective Test:** The objective test would evaluate if a reasonable person would think Jamie’s actions were safe. A typical, reasonable person would likely see speeding on a wet road at night as dangerous, regardless of driving skill. So, from an objective standpoint, Jamie’s actions might be seen as reckless.

**Combined Outcome:** Even if Jamie truly believed they could drive safely (passing the subjective test), their actions might still be deemed reckless under the objective test, as a reasonable person would consider the conditions unsafe for speeding. This combination would suggest that Jamie’s behaviour could be criminally negligent.

### Actus Reus &amp; Mens Rea
***Introduction***

Legislation criminalises acts and defines what constitutes a crime. As investigators, our job requires us to prove or disprove criminal acts. It is New Zealand legislation that outlines the parameters for our investigations every time.

A fundamental principle of criminal law is that, before an accused can be convicted of a crime, it must be proven that they were responsible for the state of affairs that resulted in the offence defined by legislation. Additionally, it must be proven that they had the requisite guilty mind.

These concepts are known by the Latin terms: *actus reus* (the guilty act) and *mens rea* (the guilty mind). In basic terms, most crimes involve both guilty knowledge and guilty action.

Understanding what these two terms mean and how they interact within a single offence is crucial.

> **"ACTUS NON FACIT REUM NISI MENS SIT REA”**
> “AN ACT DOES NOT MAKE A PERSON GUILTY UNLESS THE MIND IS ALSO GUILTY”

### Actus Reus Explained
Actus reus is the guilty act or criminal conduct. Many statutory offences describe the sort of behaviour that will attract liability, but there are some important rules that also need to be considered. For a person to be found guilty of a criminal offence, you must show that he/she did at least one of the following:

- Acted in a particular way.
- Failed to act in a particular way (omissions).
- Brought about a state of affairs.
This is the actus reus of an offence i.e., the behavioural element or ***physical action*** of an offence. When proving the actus reus, you must show:

- The defendant’s conduct was voluntary or deliberate and not accidental, and
- That it occurred while the defendant still had the requisite mens rea.
### Mens Rea Explained
Mens rea refers to the “guilty mind” or the mental state and intent of a person at the time they commit a criminal act. This concept addresses whether a person had the necessary mindset to commit a crime.

Mens rea encompasses both a general intent to commit the act itself and, in certain cases, a specific intent to achieve a particular outcome. This specific intent is also known as **specific mens rea**.

In criminal law, two levels of intention are relevant for an offence:

- **General Intent** (general mens rea): The intent to commit the act itself.
- **Specific Intent** (specific mens rea): The intent to achieve a specific result.
### General Intent Accompanying the Actus Reus
This definition is widely used in relation to intent but not well understood:

****LEARN****
> “In a criminal law context, there are two specific types of intention in an offence. Firstly, there must be an intention to commit the act and secondly, an intention to get a specific result.”

“*Firstly, there must be an intention to commit the act…*” where the *mens rea* or intention combine with the *actus reus*. This is the general intent.

It is often said that an act alone can be criminalised, however an act can only be considered criminal if the conduct was **voluntary** and done deliberately – not accidentally.

### Voluntary or Deliberate Act
You must show that the defendant acted or omitted to act ‘*voluntarily*’ or deliberately, that is, by the operation of free will.

General intent, or *mens rea*, is often presented alongside *actus reus* to highlight its connection to the physical act.

Many pieces of legislation specifically spell out the general intent accompanying the actus reus. For example, in Section 267 Arson, the act of lighting a fire must be done either ***intentionally*** or ***recklessly***. Both “intentionally” and “recklessly" are types of general *mens rea* or types of mindsets with which the act must be done. Without this general intent, an action cannot be a criminal one.

Lighting a fire with a candle can be accidental. So, the act of lighting the fire itself does not necessarily mean a criminal act has occurred. If the fire was lit intentionally, it may be a crime. If a fire was lit accidentally, it is not a crime.

Occasionally the elements of the offence in legislation may not even mention general intent at all! Regardless of whether it is spelt out or not, the fundamental principle of **a voluntary or deliberate **actus reus **must **be proved.

When a statute does not specify the required *mens rea*, the courts must decide what level of intent applies. For example, this issue arises in the offence of kidnapping, which you will explore in the DDC.

### Intent (Intentionally)
Intention is one of the primary mental states of general mens rea. It is usually sufficient for most crimes and simply requires that the accused intended to bring about the elements of the offence. In this context, “intention” means that the act or omission was done deliberately, rather than accidentally or involuntarily.

For example, under section 2 of the Crimes Act 1961, assault is defined as “the act of intentionally applying or attempting to apply force.” A person who aims to strike another on the face is acting with the intent to apply force to the other person; this is a deliberate act.

A second example of specific intent is found in section 228(1)(b) of the Crimes Act 1961. Here, it states that anyone is liable who uses a document “with intent to obtain any property, service, pecuniary advantage, or valuable consideration.”

So, if someone uses a document with the intent to obtain financial gain, this specific result-oriented aim or purpose aligns with the mens rea requirement.

### Proof of Intent
Understanding what intent is, is one thing. What is important for investigators is that intent must be proved beyond reasonable doubt.

That means the investigator must conduct investigations, gather evidence and ask questions around intent.

The case law *R v Collister* is important as it gives guidance as to how intent can be proven.

While the offender’s own statements about their intent are valuable evidence, circumstantial evidence can also be used **to infer intent**. This evidence may include:

- The offender’s actions and words before, during, and after the event
- The surrounding circumstances of the act
- The nature of the act itself
Circumstantial evidence is often sufficient to establish intent, and it can provide strong support for proving mens rea.

- **No presumption of intention:** This means that the court does not automatically assume the accused intended the outcomes of their actions just because those outcomes were likely or foreseeable. ### Recklessness (Recklessly)
Recklessness is another of the primary mental states (or general mens rea) that accompanies an act. In New Zealand, “*recklessness*” generally has a subjective meaning, involving a person consciously and deliberately taking an unjustified risk.

This concept is a form of mens rea (part of what is going on in the mind of the offender). It is specified in legislation as an element for many offences **and must be proved** by the investigator.

Variations such as “*reckless*,” “*recklessly*,” and “*with reckless disregard*” appear across various Acts, but these terms retain a consistent legal meaning and case law interpretation.

### R v Tipple
One case law that explains what recklessness or reckless intent is *R v Tipple*.

In Tipple, the defendant was hosting a shooting party on his parents’ farm. He set up a bullseye target at the end of a grassy paddock and near a windbreak of trees. Beyond the trees was a road where a continuous flow of traffic was visible through the windbreak. The road was approximately 600 metres from where the target shooting was taking place. The party took turns shooting at the target with rifles of which one was a high velocity rifle with a range of approximately 3000 metres.

Two men had stopped their vehicle on the road and were standing together between the vehicles having a conversation *“… when a bullet from the .223 rifle hit Mr Milne's vehicle. It went through the rear pillar, crossed the rear compartment and exited through the window on the far side, shattering it. The bullet narrowly missed Mr Milne's three dogs, who were in the rear of his vehicle.”*

Tipple discussed whether the Defendant had acted with reckless disregard for the safety of others when shooting the rifle. Here are some phrases from this case law that help us to understand what reckless intent is:

- *“You are reckless if you realise that a certain consequence could well follow from what you were doing, but you go ahead and do it anyway.”*
- *“You realise … you decide to run the risk …”*
- *“You have to be satisfied that at the time Mr Tipple actually realised that what he was doing could well endanger someone's safety …” *
- *“So, recklessness is deliberate and unreasonable risk running. If he did not know of the risk that people would be endangered by what was going on, then he is not guilty of the offence.”*
As a matter of law, you are reckless if you realise that the consequence could well happen –it is not just a remote risk, and you decide to run the risk.

In the case of Mr Tipple’s shooting party, the jury had to be satisfied that at the time Mr Tipple actually realised that what he was doing could well endanger someone's safety but decided to go ahead and run that risk. They considered whether in his mind the risks existed and whether he decided to run the risk.

In summary:

****LEARN****
> ***R v TIPPLE***
> *Recklessness requires that the offender know of, or have a conscious appreciation of the relevant risk, and it may be said that it requires “a deliberate decision to run the risk”.*

### Proof of Reckless
The leading case law in New Zealand on recklessness is the Supreme Court’s judgment in *Cameron v R*, which outlines the test for recklessness. This judgment is a helpful guide in determining if an individual acted with reckless intent. It is also a useful guide for investigators as to how to investigate any reckless intent element of a charge.

### Cameron v R
The facts of Cameron revolve around numerous drug-dealing charges.

Mr Cameron was in business with his co-defendants manufacturing and selling pills containing 4-MEC which were intended to mimic MDMA (Ecstasy). 4-MEC is a drug analogue substantially similar to MDMA, and therefore classed as a Class C controlled drug.

The legal complexities of the case revolved around the mens rea of the knowledge of and recklessness regarding the substance’s legal status as a controlled drug. Put simply: Was Mr Cameron reckless in his dealing with a substance that was a Class C controlled drug?

Through its consideration of the case, the Supreme Court formally established a two-pronged definition of recklessness in New Zealand, requiring both subjective awareness of the risk and an objective assessment of the unreasonableness of taking that risk.

### Two-Pronged Test for Recklessness
****LEARN****
> ***CAMERON v R ***
> *Recklessness is established if:*
> *the defendant recognised that there was a real possibility that:**his or her actions would bring about the proscribed result; and/or**that the proscribed circumstances existed; and having regard to that risk those actions were unreasonable.*

**Part (a) of the Cameron test is completely subjective. **

A “*real possibility*” is equivalent to something that “*could well happen*” (as noted in *R v Harney*), the prior leading case on recklessness. It requires the defendant to acknowledge the risk but not necessarily to consider it significant.

**Part (b) is subjective and objective.**

Part (b) includes both subjective and objective elements. It assesses whether the defendant’s actions were objectively reasonable, considering the risk they understood. In Cameron, the Supreme Court held that if the defendant’s actions lack social utility (i.e., societal benefit), taking any subjectively appreciated risk is unreasonable and, therefore, reckless. If the defendant’s actions do have some social utility, then it depends on whether “*a reasonable and prudent person would have taken the risk*.”

Investigators should consider whether the defendant **recognised a real possibility that:**

- The prohibited circumstances existed, and
- Given this risk, their actions were unreasonable.
Another perspective on reckless intent includes:

- The defendant consciously and deliberately took a risk (subjective).
- The risk was unreasonable under the known circumstances (or objectively, would a reasonable person have taken the risk?).
**Social Utility**

What does social utility mean? Simply put, this means that some actions will never have any sort of social benefit and therefore the taking of any risk cannot be reasonable. Examples of social utility:

- **No social utility:** Playing “*Russian roulette*” or engaging in personal violence with a risk of serious injury or death.
- **High social utility:** A surgeon performing a risky but potentially life-saving operation.

### Knowledge (Knowingly)
A general mens rea that some offences in New Zealand law require proof of is that the defendant has a certain level of knowledge when committing the criminal act. Again, it is a for the investigator to prove this mental state.

Knowledge or “knowing” is a recognised state of mind under mens rea.

For some offences, liability requires that the accused possesses particular knowledge.

Knowledge can encompass a range of mental states, including awareness, belief, and direct knowledge. It is defined as the certainty of a fact or circumstance that leaves the individual free from doubt. A person is said to “know” something when, through physical or mental perception, they achieve a certainty about the facts or circumstances at hand. The following is the definition of ‘knowledge’.

****LEARN****
> **Simester and Brookbanks: Principles of Criminal Law **
> Knowing means “knowing or correctly believing”. The defendant may believe something wrongly but cannot ‘know’ something that is false.

### R v Crooks
In R v Crooks, the defendant shared a house with a man who had committed a burglary called Mr Paul. Mr Paul had stolen money during the course of the burglary and returned to the house with it. There the defendant accepted a portion of the stolen money as repayment of a debt owed to him by Mr Paul. The defendant Crooks claimed that he didn’t know the property was stolen but admitted he assumed it might have been.

The question the court considered is did this assumption equate to knowing under the offence of Receiving? The court decided it did not. Mere suspicion that Mr Paul came into the money through a burglary was not enough to say he had the required level of knowledge that an offence had been committed.

****LEARN****
> **R v Crooks**
> Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.

### OUGHT TO KNOW
As with the concept of recklessness, in some cases, knowledge involves both subjective and objective assessments. Investigators must gather evidence to address these tests.

****LEARN****
> **THE TEST FOR KNOWLEDGE**
> The provision of ‘knows or ought to know’ involves a mixed subjective / objective test as to the defendant’s degree of knowledge.
> ** Subjective Test **
> The first question (the subjective test) is: what did the defendant know at the time? Did the defendant know?
> **Objective Test**
> If there is insufficient evidence that the defendant had the required knowledge, the next question (the objective test) is: what would a reasonable person have known in the same circumstances? Would a reasonable person have known?

Again, in a similar fashion to other forms of mens rea, knowledge can be established by corroborating evidence such as an admission or an implication from circumstances surrounding the event. (*R v Collister*)

# ADDITIONAL LEGAL CONCEPTS
### Contemporaneous 
We have learnt about the blueprint for an offence requiring a criminal act and a guilty mind (actus reus and mens rea).

The concept of contemporaneous, or contemporaneity, simply means that the actus reus and the mens rea must coincide in time – they must happen at the same time.

Think of it this way: The guilty mind must be operational at the same time as the guilty act is being committed.

This is an important concept for two reasons.

- It ensures that individuals are only held responsible for actions they intended at the moment the action occurred.
- It ensures that individuals are charged accurately. If the actus reus and mens rea do not coincide, there may not be a crime or there may be a different offence which investigators should have charged with.
### Example
A common example that is used to illustrate the concept of ‘contemporaneous’ is the offence of Robbery under section 234 of the Crimes Act 1961.

A male walks down a busy street one night. He sees a young teenage boy ahead of him who has the latest Nike Air basketball shoes. The male decides to steal the shoes by punching the young teen until he falls on the ground and forcibly taking the shoes. Here the actus reus of taking the shoes with the use of violence and the mens rea of intention to steal and intention to use violence coincide. Robbery is the appropriate charge.

In the same scenario, the male walking down the street is looking for a fight and sees the young teenage boy ahead of him. He picks a fight with the young teenage boy punching him until he falls to the ground. As he turn to walk away, he sees that the young teenage boy is wearing the latest Nike Air basketball shoes. He decides that he wants the shoes and while the young teenage boy is still on the ground, he forcibly removes the shoes and walks away. Here the mens rea of intention to steal is formed after the intention to apply force to the young teenage boy by assaulting him. Assault and theft are the appropriate charges.

### CONTEMPORANEOUS EXTENDED
While it must be shown that the defendant has the requisite mens rea at the time of carrying out the actus reus, for a criminal act to be complete, it is worth noting that there are some nuances in a rigid interpretation of this concept in New Zealand law.

Depending on the offence that is being committed, there is no need for a criminal state of mind to remain unchanged throughout the entire commission of the offence.

****EXAMPLE****
> If a person (X) poisons another (Y), intending to kill (Y) at the time, it will not alter X’s criminal liability if X changes his mind immediately after giving the poison or even if X does everything, he can halt its effects.

Conversely, if the actus reus is a continuing act, it may begin without any mens rea, but the required state of mind may come later while the actus reus is continuing. If this happens, whereby the mens rea catches up with the actus reus, the offence is complete at the first moment that the two elements (actus reus and mens rea) unite.

****EXAMPLE****
> For example, in a case where a motorist was directed to pull his car over to the kerb by a police officer and drove it onto the officer’s foot, there was no proof he did it deliberately. However, it was clear he deliberately left the car there after the officer told him what he had done. His conviction for assaulting the officer was upheld on the basis that there was an ongoing act, which became a criminal assault once the motorist became aware of it.

### Causation 
### Causal Link or Chain of Causation
After establishing the actus reus, it is necessary to demonstrate a causal link between the defendant’s act or omission and the resulting consequences. This requires proving that the consequences would not have occurred “*but for*” the defendant’s actions.

In straightforward cases, such as simple criminal damage, proving this causal link is often uncomplicated. For example, if a defendant throws a brick at a window, causing it to break, the window would not have broken *but for* the defendant’s conduct.

However, proving causation becomes more complex when the defendant’s actions set off a chain of events or exacerbate preexisting conditions.

### Intervening Act
The general rule is that an intervening act does not typically break the causal link or chain of causation. Additionally, defendants must “*take their victims as they find them*.”

****EXAMPLE****
> This means that if victims have a particular characteristic, such as a very thin skull or a very nervous disposition, which makes the consequences of an act against them much more acute, that is the defendant’s bad luck.

### Chain of Causation – ‘But for’ Test
The chain of causation centres on cause and effect, often referred to as the “*but for*” test. This test asks: “*but for the actions of the defendant, would the outcome have occurred?*” A clear, unbroken connection between the defendant’s actions and the final consequence is required to establish causation.

### INVESTIGATION APPLICATION
You may be wondering why these abstract legal concepts are important to your activities as an investigator. Understanding when an offence is complete in time ensures that you are investigating the correct offence, or whether you should give consideration to different offences.

Understanding principles of causation is important to:

- **Establish Responsibility**: Causation is central to proving that a suspect's actions led to the outcome (e.g. death, injury, damage).
- **Charging Decisions:** Causation helps determine what charges can be legally sustained.
- **Defence and Liability:** Causation is key to attributing or deflecting liability.
- **Criminal Procedure:** Understanding the concept ensures that appropriate expert analysis is undertaken to determine causal links (e.g. medical experts, accident reconstruction).
### SUMMARY
Both the contemporaneous and causation concepts are foundational for establishing thought processes in investigators that ensure a coherent narrative is established, and that there is evidential integrity supporting prosecution or mitigation of defence.

# DEFENCES
### Offence Equation Summary So Far
As we have seen so far, the offence equation requires an individual to have completed a physical act which comprises the offence, (actus reus), and have the mental element required for criminal liability (mens rea). These must coincide with each other and there must be a causal link between the act and the consequences.

The last part of the offence equation that a good investigator must consider is – **does the individual have a defence available** to them that also requires investigation?

### What is a Defence?
A criminal defence is a legal argument used by a person prosecuted for a crime to challenge the charges brought against them.

A criminal defence can either prove the innocence of the person being prosecuted, reduce their level of responsibility, or minimise potential penalties against them.

Defences can take many forms including a full or partial defence to the charges brought against them. Here we introduce three kinds of defences:

- Offence Not Proven
- Statutory or Internal Defences
- Formal Defences
### Offence is not Proven 
This defence is simple. The prosecution (and by implication, the investigator) has not produced enough evidence to prove the actus reus or mens rea of the offence beyond reasonable doubt. This can result from a variety of circumstances both within and outside of the control of the prosecution. For example, evidence could be unexpectedly deemed inadmissible thus weakening the overall case, or an important witness could be unavailable or hostile to giving evidence.

However, it can also occur when investigators offer weak evidence or the investigations themselves have not been comprehensive enough to establish issues such as identity or alibi, or to prove the individual elements of the crime. This will clear the defendant of any liability.

### Statutory or Internal Defences 
Statutory simply means something is written into statute or written into law. In the case of a defence this means that there are defences that Parliament has written into statute or law.

There are generally two types of internal or statutory defence.

- Some legislation includes an internal defence **within** the offence itself.
- An offence has a specific defence written into another section of law.
The similarity between the two is that an act or series of acts that would be deemed criminal **are provided a justification** within the law itself. This means that the defendant may have an excuse or reason for their actions that the law recognises – the actions therefore are justified and not criminal.

### Internal Defence: Included within the Offence Itself
An example of this is section 202A (4) (a) of the Crimes Act which states,

*“Everyone is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 3 years – who, ****without lawful authority or reasonable excuse****, has with him or her in any public place any knife or offensive weapon or disabling substance.”*

This section of legislation makes the carrying of a knife, weapon or disabling substance a crime. However, it also includes an internal defence or a justification recognised by law within the offence itself. The action of carrying a knife is a crime unless there is* “lawful authority or reasonable excuse.”*

A further example is the internal statutory defence of Claim of Right, which attaches to offences involving property. Claim of Right is where an individual genuinely believes they have an ownership right to deal with the property in the way that they have. Therefore, their actions with the property (such as burning it, for example in Arson), which would otherwise be criminalised, are justified.

### Statutory Defence – a Standalone Defence for a Particular Crime
An example of a standalone statutory defence is written into section 134A of the Crimes Act. This is a specific defence to the offence of *Sexual conduct with a young person under 16*.

This defence involves taking reasonable steps to ascertain the age of the person was 16, reasonable belief the person was 16 and that sexual connection was consensual. If these conditions are met by the accused, **no offence has been committed. **The act of having sexual connection with an under 16-year-old, which would otherwise be criminalised, is justified by this section.

Statutory Defences will be explored in depth on the DDC.

**Application to Investigators**

The importance of the internal or statutory defence is that the investigator ***turns their mind to gathering evidence that proves or disproves the justification***. Gathering evidence of the final part of the offence equation is the responsibility of the investigator.

Internal defences will be explored in depth on the DDC.

### Formal Defences 
Formal Defences are legal defences that originate from case law over time and are often referred to as common law defences – that is, accepted over time by the courts. Common law defences are based on precedents and principles established by the Courts and can be invoked by defendants in criminal cases to avoid or reduce liability.

In New Zealand, many of the formal defences have been written (or codified) into statute and can therefore also be considered statutory defences. If a formal defence has not been codified, it will still be available to defendants, unless it has been repealed, altered or is inconsistent with statute.

When arguing a formal defence, there is generally no challenge to the evidence offered that the offence has occurred. In fact, there is often agreement by all that the defendant has committed the crime for which they are charged.

Here the defence is a fact or circumstance that means the actions of the defendant, which would normally be a crime, are **justified or excused** because of the **context** in which they occurred. This means criminal liability does NOT attach to the actions as it normally would.

**Example:** Self Defence

An example of this with which you will be familiar is Self Defence – a common law defence, established by the court over hundreds of years, that is considered a statutory defence under section 48 of the Crimes Act 1961.

This is a defence where the facts of the case are agreed – that is, an individual has hurt or killed someone. These facts are normally accepted by the prosecution and defence.

What is argued is that the action of hurting or killing the victim **was necessary in the circumstances **to defend themselves. Further, it is argued that the amount of force they used to do so was reasonable.

**Example:** Insanity

Insanity is a formal defence where it is accepted that the offence has been committed; however, the fact the offender is labouring under severe psychological illness or circumstance means that criminal culpability **does not** attach.

It is important to note that unlike other defences, it is the responsibility of the prosecution (and by implication the investigator) to bring all evidence of insanity to the attention of the court.

An example of insanity is the murder by a person who is suffering from clinically diagnosed schizophrenia.

**Example:** Automatism

Automatism occurs when someone acts unconsciously or involuntarily due to an external factor, or a mental illness. ‘Automaton’ is a Greek word and has connotations of someone acting in an automatic, robotic or mechanical way with no awareness of the mind. This negates the mens rea of an offence as an automaton cannot act deliberately, intentionally or voluntarily.

An example is: If someone committed a crime while sleepwalking, they might use this defence, claiming they didn’t act with awareness or intention.

**Example:** Intoxication

In New Zealand, being drunk or on drugs is not a defence to a crime in and of itself. However, if the accused was intoxicated, it **may** be considered when deciding if they had the mens rea (intent) to commit the crime.

**Example:** If someone was heavily intoxicated and didn’t realise they were taking someone else’s belongings, this lack of intent **could** be considered, although it might not lead to a full defence.

# CASE LAW &amp; THE COURTS
### The Courts 
New Zealand has multiple levels and types of courts, each with distinct roles and jurisdictions. This hierarchy allows cases to be heard in appropriate venues based on their seriousness and enables appeals through higher courts.

As a serious crime investigator, your cases will likely be heard in the High Court of New Zealand. The High Court handles serious civil and criminal cases and hears appeals from lower courts, such as the District Court.

A key principle of this hierarchy is that lower courts are bound by higher courts’ decisions. For investigators, this means that case law established by higher courts, like the Court of Appeal, may impact policing practices if it overturns lower court decisions.

New Zealand Police has legal sections who are up to date with case law as it is released and regularly update the organisation through internal communications. It is important that you read these communications and follow any advice provided by these subject matter specialists.

### The doctrine of precedent
The doctrine of precedent establishes that lower courts must follow the binding decisions of higher courts in the same jurisdiction, unless the facts differ significantly. Courts at the same level aren’t required to follow each other’s rulings but may do so if the reasoning is compelling.

### Case law and how it works
Case law refers to the body of law developed through judicial decisions in court cases. These decisions, known as judgments or cases, establish legal precedents that guide the application and interpretation of laws. Understanding case law is crucial for legal professionals and students.

### Case Citation Structure
Court cases are cited in a specific format that allows them to be easily identified and referenced. A typical case citation includes the following components:

- **Party Names:** This includes the names of the parties involved in the case. In criminal cases, it is common to see "R v [Defendant's Name]," where "R" stands for Regina or Rex (Crown/Prosecution).
- **Year:** The year the case was decided, usually enclosed in square brackets [ ].
- **Number:** This identifies the law report series and volume number where the **Volume and Report Series** case can be found.
- **Page Number:** The page number within the report series where the case starts.
For example: R v Maihi [1993] 2 NZLR 139

*This citation refers to the Regina (Crown) v Maihi case, decided in 1993, reported in Volume 2 of the New Zealand Law Reports (NZLR), starting on page 139*.

### Using case law
When applying case law in legal analysis or problem-solving, it is essential to understand the specific legal principles or rules established by the case. The case decision (or what was “held”) outlines the court's decision and reasoning, which in some cases may then be applied to similar factual scenarios.

Some judicial decisions, or what is '*held' *in a case, lay out the interpretation of a general legal principle. Those decisions (or interpretations) can be applied when the legal principle comes up. For example, *Cameron v R* is the leading interpretation of what Recklessness is. This means that whenever reckless intent or action is required as an element in an investigation, the principles held in *Cameron *should be applied in order to obtain evidential sufficiency.

In assessments or assignments, you may be required to cite relevant case law and explain how the principles established in those cases apply to the given situation. It is crucial to demonstrate a clear understanding of the case and its implications, rather than simply reciting verbatim information.

By understanding the structure of case citations, the doctrine of precedent, and how to apply case law effectively, you will be better equipped to navigate the legal system and analyse complex legal issues.

### Claim of right 
Claim of right is essentially a *‘defence’* to what would otherwise be a criminal act. Claim of right attaches to all offences dealing with property and stems from the idea that an individual can deal with their own property how they choose – including destroying or damaging it. Claim of right is a belief held by a person they hold some sort of legal interest (often financial or ownership) in the property damaged.

****LEARN****
> **Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961**
> **Interpretation**
> Claim of right, in relation to any act, means a belief at the time of the act in a proprietary or possessory right in property in relation to which the offence is alleged to have been committed, although that belief may be based on ignorance or mistake of fact or of any matter of law other than the enactment against which the offence is alleged to have been committed.

This definition requires that the defendant have at the time of the act, a belief in proprietary or possessory right in the property.

The development of the defence can be found in the ‘*Waihopo Case’* (NZ Herald Link). Three activists slashed an inflatable dome covering a satellite dish at the Government Communications Security Bureau base at Waihopai. The claim of right defence argued was that the damage done to the dome had disrupted intelligence communications thereby saving lives in Iraq. The activists held a genuine belief that their actions were lawful and were acquitted. In the words of former Justice Minister Simon Power on changing the legislation to its current wording,

*“As the law stands, defendants charged with certain property offences can use the defense if they genuinely believe their actions are lawful...But it's clear that the defense was not intended to be used to excuse people who ... damage property who are not claiming a personal property right, as in the Waihopai case."*
