# DEFINITIONS
### Evidence 
Evidence is any information a court can use to decide a case. It can be spoken, written, or shown in pictures or videos.

***Example:*** A witness saying, *“I saw the suspect break the window,”* is evidence. So is CCTV footage of the break-in.

### Admissible Evidence 
Admissible evidence is any evidence the court is legally allowed to consider. It must follow the rules of evidence and not be unfair or obtained illegally.

***Example:***

- A CCTV video showing the accused committing the crime is admissible.
- A confession obtained through threats is not admissible because it was obtained unfairly.
### Relevance 
Evidence is relevant if it helps prove or disprove something important in the case, *“if it has a tendency to prove or disprove anything that is of consequence to the determination of a proceeding” (s7(3) of the Evidence Act 2006)*.

***Example:*** Fingerprints on a stolen car are relevant to proving who stole it.

### Burden of Proof
- The burden of proof (also called the onus of proof) is the responsibility to prove something in court.
- In criminal cases, this burden lies with the prosecution because of the presumption of innocence. The accused does not have to prove they are innocent.
- The prosecution must provide enough credible evidence to convince the court that the accused committed the offence.
- The standard they must meet is *“beyond reasonable doubt”,* which means the judge or jury must be sure the accused is guilty before convicting.
***Example:* **If someone is charged with burglary, the prosecution must prove every element of the offence (e.g., unlawful entry, intent to steal) beyond reasonable doubt. The accused does not have to prove they are innocent, but if they raise a defence like self-defense or insanity, they may have to provide evidence to support that claim.

### Beyond Reasonable Doubt 
- This is the highest standard of proof used in criminal cases.
- It means the judge or jury must be sure the accused is guilty before convicting.
- It does not mean absolute certainty, but it does mean there should be no reasonable doubt in the mind of a fair and rational person after considering all the evidence.
- If there is a reasonable doubt, the accused must be found not guilty.
***Example:* **If the evidence strongly points to the accused committing a burglary, but there is a realistic possibility someone else could have done it, the jury should acquit because that possibility creates reasonable doubt.

### Facts in Issue 
These are the key facts that must be proven in a case:

- The prosecution must prove them to show the crime happened and the accused committed it.
- The defence must prove them if they are raising a defence (and they have the legal responsibility to prove it).
***Example:*** In an assault case, the facts in issue might include:

- Did the accused hit the victim? (Prosecution must prove this.)
- Was the accused acting in self-defense? (If claimed, the defence must prove this.)
### Exclusionary Rules 
Exclusionary rules are legal rules that allow a court to **exclude (leave out) certain evidence** from a trial, even if it is relevant, because using it would be unfair or harmful to the justice process.

In New Zealand, the main rule is in **Section 30 of the Evidence Act 2006**, which deals with evidence obtained **improperly or unfairly** (for example, through an unlawful search or a breach of rights). The court uses a **balancing test** to decide whether to exclude the evidence. This test weighs:

- How serious the police misconduct was (e.g., breaking the law to get evidence).
- How important the evidence is to the case.
- The effect on the fairness of the trial.
If admitting the evidence would make the trial unfair or damage public confidence in the justice system, the court will exclude it.

***Example:*** Police search a suspect’s house without a warrant and find stolen goods. The evidence is relevant, but because it was obtained unlawfully, the judge may exclude it under Section 30.

### Weight of Evidence 
The weight of evidence means how strong or convincing the evidence is when deciding the key facts of the case. Its strength depends on things like:

- How closely it relates to the important facts or if it clearly proves them.
- Whether other evidence supports or contradicts it.
- How truthful and reliable the witness seems.
In short, the weight of evidence is about how much trust and importance the court can give to that piece of evidence.

***Example:*** Two pieces of evidence are presented in a burglary case:

- **Eyewitness testimony:** A neighbor says they saw someone *“who looked like the accused”* near the house at night.
- **DNA evidence:** The accused’s DNA is found on the broken window frame.
Both are admissible, but the DNA evidence carries more weight because it is highly reliable and directly links the accused to the crime scene, whereas the eyewitness account is less certain.

### Offer Evidence 
- Evidence is offered when it is actually presented to the court, not just suggested.
- Simply asking a question or making a statement to a witness is not offering evidence. It only becomes evidence when the witness agrees or answers.
- Under Section 96(1) of the Evidence Act 2006, offering evidence also includes getting answers during cross-examination of a witness called by the other side.
***Example:****  *

- A lawyer asks a witness: ***“You saw the accused at the scene, didn’t you?”***
- If the witness says ***“Yes”*** that answer is now offered as evidence.
- If the witness says ***“No”****,* then that denial is also evidence.
- Just asking the question alone does not count as offering evidence.
### Give Evidence 
Giving evidence means when a witness tells the court what they know. A witness gives evidence, while a lawyer or party offers evidence by presenting it to the court. If a party testifies themselves, they are both giving and offering evidence.

**Ways evidence can be given in court:**

- Ordinary way:
- Speaking in the courtroom in front of the judge (and jury if there is one).
- Reading a written statement aloud (if both sides agree and it’s allowed).
- Submitting an affidavit (a written statement sworn to be true).
- Alternative ways:
- In the courtroom but without seeing the defendant (e.g., using a screen).
- From another location via video link.
- Using a pre-recorded video statement. (These are allowed under the Evidence Act 2006 and the Courts (Remote Participation) Act 2010.)
- Any other way allowed by law:
- For example, special provisions for vulnerable witnesses.
***Example:*** A child witness might give evidence by video link from another room, so they don’t have to face the defendant.

### Incriminate 
To incriminate means to give information that could make someone more likely to be charged with a crime.

***Example:***

- A suspect says, *“I was driving the getaway car.”* This statement incriminates them because it links them to the crime.
- A witness says, *“I saw John break the window.”* This incriminates John because it suggests he committed an offence.
### Proceeding 
A proceeding is any legal process that takes place in a court. It includes:

- A trial or hearing where a judge (and sometimes a jury) decides a case.
- Any application or request made to the court that is connected to that case.
***Example:**** *

- A criminal trial for burglary is a proceeding.
- A bail hearing or an application to suppress evidence (linked to that trial) is also part of the proceeding.
### Statement 
A statement is:

- Something a person says or writes to express what they believe happened, or
- An action (non-verbal) that is clearly meant to communicate something as true.
***Examples:***

- **Spoken:** *“I saw the suspect leave the store.”*
- **Written:** A signed witness statement describing an event.
- **Non-verbal:** Nodding your head to mean *“Yes”* when asked if you saw the accused.
### Witness 
A witness is a person who:

- Gives evidence in a court case, and
- Can be questioned (cross-examined) by the lawyers from both sides.
***Example:***

- A shop assistant who saw a robbery and testifies in court is a witness.
- They can be asked questions by both the prosecution and the defence.
### Hearsay Statement 
A hearsay statement is when someone repeats what another person said to prove that what was said is true.

It’s usually not allowed in court because the original speaker isn’t there to be questioned (cross-examined).

If the person who made the statement is in court and can be cross-examined, it’s not hearsay.

If the statement is used for another reason (e.g., just to show it was said, not to prove it’s true), it’s not hearsay.

***Examples:***

- **Hearsay:** A witness says, *“My friend told me the accused admitted the crime.”* (The friend isn’t in court, so this is hearsay.)
- **Not hearsay:** The same friend comes to court and says, *“I heard the accused admit the crime.”* (They can be cross-examined.)
- **Not hearsay for another purpose:** A witness says, *“I heard someone shout ‘Fire!’”* (Used to show why people ran, not to prove there was a fire.)
### Veracity 
Veracity means how likely a person is to tell the truth and avoid lying, either in general or during a court case.

***Example:***

- A witness with a history of honesty and no reason to lie is considered to have high veracity.
- A witness who has previously lied under oath or has a motive to lie has low veracity.
### Propensity 
Propensity evidence is information that shows a person’s tendency or pattern to behave in a certain way or think in a certain way. It often includes evidence of things the person has done before, or situations they’ve been involved in, to suggest they might act the same way again.

***Example:***

- In a fraud case, the prosecution shows that the accused has previously committed similar frauds.
- This suggests the accused has a propensity (a tendency) to commit fraud.
### Direct Evidence 
Direct evidence is evidence that directly proves a fact without needing any inference or assumption. It comes from a witness who personally **saw, heard, or experienced** the event.

***Example:***

- An eyewitness says, *“I saw the accused stab the victim.”* This is direct evidence because the witness personally observed the act.
### Circumstantial Evidence 
Circumstantial evidence is evidence that doesn’t directly prove a fact but suggests it by pointing to related circumstances. The court uses this evidence to **infer** (draw a conclusion) about what happened.

***Example:***

- The accused’s fingerprints are found on the window of a house that was broken into.
- No one saw the accused commit the burglary, but the fingerprints strongly suggested they were there.
This is **circumstantial evidence** because it requires an inference: *“If their fingerprints are on the window, they were likely involved.”*

### Enforcement Agency 
An enforcement agency is any organisation that has the legal authority to enforce laws in New Zealand. This includes:

- **New Zealand Police**
- **New Zealand Customs Service**
- **Ministry for Primary Industries (Fisheries)**
- **Inland Revenue Department (IRD)**
- And any other agency given enforcement powers by law.
**Example:**

- The **New Zealand Police** investigate and enforce criminal law.
- **Customs** enforces border security and import/export laws.
### Judicial Notice 
Judicial notice means the court accepts certain facts or laws as true without requiring formal proof. These are facts that are so well-known or easily verified that they don’t need evidence. Judicial notice saves time and avoids unnecessary evidence for things that are obvious or officially recorded.

***Example:***

- Acts of Parliament and Regulations – Courts automatically recognise New Zealand laws without needing someone to produce a copy.
- Common Knowledge Facts – For example, that Wellington is the capital of New Zealand.

## Burden of Proof 
### Woolmington Principle
Woolmington v DPP - Wikipedia – (Click this link to go to the article.)

The Woolmington Principle is a fundamental rule in criminal law that says:

- The prosecution must prove the accused’s guilt **beyond reasonable doubt**.
- This includes proving both the physical act (actus reus) and the mental element (mens rea) of the offence.
- The accused is presumed innocent unless and until the prosecution proves guilt.
This principle was famously described as the *“****golden thread****”* running through criminal law.

### Key Points
- The burden of proof stays with the prosecution, except for a few statutory exceptions (e.g., insanity or specific defences where the law says the accused must prove something).
- The defence does not usually have to prove innocence, but they may have an evidential burden (to raise enough evidence for a defence to be considered).
- If the jury has a reasonable doubt, the accused must be acquitted.
***Example:***

If someone is charged with murder, the prosecution must prove:

- The victim died.
- The accused caused the death.
- The accused intended to kill or cause serious harm.
If the accused claims self-defense, they only need to provide enough evidence to raise the issue; the prosecution still has to disprove self-defense beyond reasonable doubt.

### Practical obligation of the defence
Even though the **prosecution has the legal burden of proof**, if their evidence strongly suggests the accused committed the crime with the required intent, the defence usually needs to respond.

This is **not a legal burden**, the defence does not have to prove innocence.

Instead, it’s a **practical obligation**: if the defence wants to challenge the prosecution’s case, they should provide some evidence or explanation that creates **reasonable doubt**.

This applies when the defence is **not raising a specific legal defence** (like self-defense or insanity) but simply wants to show the prosecution’s conclusion might be wrong.

***Example:***

- The prosecution shows CCTV of the accused near the crime scene and fingerprints on the weapon.
- If the accused says nothing, the court may conclude guilt.
- The defence might point to evidence that the accused was there for an innocent reason (e.g., visiting a friend earlier).
- They don’t have to **prove** this, but providing some evidence can create reasonable doubt.
### “Evidential burden” on defence overview
The **evidential burden** is the responsibility of the defence to provide **some evidence** to support a defence they want to raise (e.g., self-defense, duress).

The prosecution does **not** have to disprove every possible defence unless the defence makes it a live issue by presenting some evidence.

Once the defence meets this evidential burden, the **prosecution must then disprove the defence beyond reasonable doubt** because the ultimate burden of proof always stays with the prosecution.

**Key Points:**

- It is **not a legal burden** to prove the defence on the balance of probabilities (except for rare statutory exceptions).
- It simply means the defence must point to enough evidence so the judge or jury can consider the defence.
***Example:***

- The accused is charged with assault but claims self-defense.
- The defence must present some evidence (e.g., testimony that the victim attacked first).
- Once raised, the prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused was **not** acting in self-defense.
### Exceptions 
Normally, the prosecution must prove the accused’s guilt beyond reasonable doubt. However, there are **exceptions** where the **legal burden of proof shifts to the defendant**. These include:

- **Insanity Defence**
- Under s23(1) of the Crimes Act 1961, if the accused claims insanity, they must prove it on the balance of probabilities.
- **Statutory Exceptions**
- Parliament can create laws that require the defendant to prove certain defences.
- ***Example:***
- s202A(4)(b) Crimes Act – It’s an offence to possess an offensive weapon with intent to cause harm.
- The law gives a defence if the accused can prove they had no such intent.
- **Evidence Act Requirements**
- Sometimes the Evidence Act 2006 places the burden on the defendant for specific issues, such as proving certain conditions for admissibility.
- ***Example:***
- s45 Evidence Act – Identification evidence rules may require the defendant to prove a point about admissibility.
**Key Point:**

Even in these exceptions, the standard for the defendant is usually balance of probabilities (more likely than not), not beyond reasonable doubt.

### Public Welfare Offences (Strict Liability Offences)
These are regulatory offences designed to protect public safety and welfare (e.g., traffic laws, health and safety rules, environmental regulations).

For these offences, the law focuses on **preventing harm**, not proving intent.

Once the prosecution proves the **actus reus** (the act happened), they **do not need to prove mens rea** (intent).

The burden then shifts to the defendant to prove they were **not at fault** (e.g., they took all reasonable care).

These are called **strict liability offences** because guilt is based on the act itself, unless the defendant can show a complete absence of fault.

***Example:***

- A company is charged with polluting a river.
- The prosecution only needs to prove the pollution occurred.
- The company must then prove it took all reasonable steps to prevent the pollution (to avoid liability).
# STANDARD OF PROOF 
### Discharging burden of proof
When a party has the **legal burden of proof**, they must meet the required **standard of proof** to succeed.

**For the prosecution in a criminal case: **

They must prove the accused’s guilt **beyond reasonable doubt.**

**For the defence (when the law puts a burden on them): **

They only need to prove their point on the **balance of probabilities** (meaning it’s more likely than not).

***Example:***

- The prosecution must prove all elements of a burglary charge beyond reasonable doubt.
- If the accused raises the defence of insanity, they must prove it on the balance of probabilities.
### Beyond reasonable doubt 
This is the **highest standard of proof** in criminal law.

It means the jury (or judge) must be sure the accused is guilty before convicting.

It does **not** mean absolute certainty, but it is much more than thinking the accused is *“probably guilty.”*

If there is an **honest and reasonable uncertainty** about guilt after considering all the evidence, the accused must be found **not guilty**.

The majority in *R v Wanhalla* suggested the following direction be given, this is generally the advice that a Judge reads to the jury during the trial:

*“The starting point is the presumption of innocence. You must treat the accused as innocent until the Crown has proved his or her guilt. The presumption of innocence means that the accused does not have to give or call any evidence and does not have to establish his or her innocence.*

*The Crown must prove that the accused is guilty beyond reasonable doubt. Proof beyond reasonable doubt is a very high standard of proof which the Crown will have met only if, at the end of the case, you are sure that the accused is guilty.*

*It is not enough for the Crown to persuade you that the accused is probably guilty or even that he or she is very likely guilty. On the other hand, it is virtually impossible to prove anything to an absolute certainty when dealing with the reconstruction of past events and the Crown does not have to do so.*

*What then is reasonable doubt? A reasonable doubt is an honest and reasonable uncertainty left in your mind about the guilt of the accused after you have given careful and impartial consideration to all of the evidence.”*

*In summary, if, after careful and impartial consideration of the evidence, you are sure that the accused is guilty you must find him or her guilty. On the other hand, if you are not sure that the accused is guilty, you must find him or her not guilty. ”*

***Example:* **If the evidence strongly suggests the accused committed a burglary, but there is a realistic possibility someone else could have done it, that creates a reasonable doubt. The jury must acquit.

### Balance of probabilities 
The **balance of probabilities** is a legal standard used to determine whether a claim or assertion is more likely to be true than not. It's commonly applied in **civil cases** and in some **defences in criminal law**, such as **insanity**.

***Example:*** a defendant claims insanity at the time of the offence. The court hears expert psychiatric testimony and reviews medical records.

If the judge or jury concludes that it's more likely than not (i.e., &gt;50%) that the defendant was suffering from a mental disorder that impaired their ability to understand their actions, then the defence of insanity is proven on the balance of probabilities.

But if the evidence is equally persuasive for and against insanity, the defence has not met its burden.

# SUMMARY 
### Law of Evidence and Proof
**Woolmington Principle:** The prosecution must prove the accused’s guilt **beyond reasonable doubt**. The accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty.

**Burden of Proof:**

- Stays with the prosecution, except for statutory exceptions (e.g., insanity).
- Defence usually has no legal burden but may have an **evidential burden** to raise a defence (e.g., self-defense).
**Practical Obligation of Defence:** While not legally required to prove innocence, the defence often needs to present evidence to create reasonable doubt.

**Evidential Burden:** Defence must provide some evidence for a defence to be considered. Once raised, the prosecution must disprove it beyond reasonable doubt.

**Exceptions:**

- **Insanity:** Accused must prove on the balance of probabilities.
- **Statutory Exceptions:** Certain laws shift the burden to the accused.
**Strict Liability Offences: **For public welfare offences, prosecution only proves the act; the accused must show they were not at fault.

### Standard of Proof
**Discharging Burden:**

Prosecution: **Beyond reasonable doubt.**

Defence (when burden applies): **Balance of probabilities.**

**Beyond Reasonable Doubt:**

- Highest standard in criminal law.
- Jury must be sure of guilt; not just “probably guilty.”
- If there’s an honest and reasonable uncertainty, the accused must be acquitted.
**Balance of Probabilities:**

- Means “more likely than not” (&gt;50%).
- Used in civil cases and some criminal defences (e.g., insanity).

# THE PURPOSE OF EVIDENCE LAW 
### Understanding Section 6 of the Evidence Act 2006
Section 6 of the Evidence Act 2006 outlines the overall purpose of the Act. It plays a key role in how courts interpret the Act, use their inherent powers, and decide whether evidence should be admitted—especially in situations where the Act or other laws don’t provide a clear answer.

The main goal of the Act is to support the just determination of legal proceedings. To achieve this, Section 6 sets out six specific objectives:

- Using logical rules to establish facts.
- Respecting rights protected under the New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990.
- Ensuring fairness for all parties and witnesses.
- Protecting confidentiality and other important public interests.
- Avoiding unnecessary cost and delay.
- Improving access to the law of evidence.
When interpreting the Act or deciding whether evidence can be admitted, courts must consider the purposes and principles in Sections 6, 7, and 8. This means that Section 6 will influence decisions about evidence in the cases you present.

However, it’s important to note, as confirmed in *R v Barlien, *that Section 6 cannot override clear exclusionary rules in the Act. If the Act explicitly excludes certain evidence, that exclusion stands, even if it seems to conflict with the broader purposes in Sections 6, 7, or 8.

Finally, while common law (past court decisions) can help guide decisions about evidence, this is only allowed when those decisions are consistent with the Act and support its purposes and principles (see Section 12).

# WHAT MAKES GOOD EVIDENCE 
### Facts that prove the charge
Good evidence directly supports what you are trying to prove in a case, while also aligning with the broader purposes outlined in Section 6 of the Evidence Act 2006.

To secure a conviction, the evidence must establish the **facts** that prove the **elements of the charge**. This means that in every case, you need to:

- Clearly understand the **specific charge** being laid.
- Identify the **legal elements** that make up that charge.
- Select evidence that is **relevant** to those elements.
- Ensure the evidence helps to **prove the accused’s guilt**.
Keeping the charge and its elements in mind will guide you in choosing evidence that is both admissible and effective.

### Facts in issue 
Facts in issue are the key facts that must be proven in law for a case to succeed. In criminal cases, these are usually the facts alleged in the **charging document** and denied by the defendant’s **not guilty plea**.

To prove a case, you don’t always need direct evidence of the *facts in issue*. Instead, you often rely on **facts that are relevant to the facts in issue**—these are facts that help to prove or disprove the key issues.

For example, in many cases, there may be no eyewitnesses to the crime itself. However, investigators might find physical evidence during a search, or speak to witnesses who each provide part of the story. When these pieces are combined—like parts of a jigsaw puzzle—they can build a picture of what happened. These supporting facts are not the facts in issue themselves, but they are **relevant** because they help establish those key facts.

Understanding the difference between *facts in issue *and *relevant facts* is essential when deciding what evidence to gather and present in court.

***Example:***

*Scenario: Burglary Charge*

*Charge: A person is charged with burglary under section 231 of the Crimes Act 1961.*

**Facts in Issue**

These are the key facts that must be proven to establish the offence:

- The accused **entered a building**.
- The entry was **without authority**.
- The accused **intended to commit an imprisonable offence** (e.g., theft) at the time of entry.
These are the core elements of the charge. If any one of them is not proven, the prosecution cannot succeed.

**Relevant Facts**

These are facts that help prove or disprove the facts in issue:

- A **broken window** was found at the scene (suggests unlawful entry).
- The accused’s **fingerprints** were found inside the building (links them to the scene).
- A **witness saw the accused** near the building around the time of the offence (supports presence).
- The accused was found with **items stolen** from the building (suggests intent to steal).
- The accused claims they had **permission to enter** (relevant to whether entry was unlawful).
Each of these facts doesn’t prove the charge on its own, but together they help build the case by supporting or challenging the facts in issue.

### Circumstantial Evidence
Circumstantial evidence refers to a fact that allows the judge or jury to **infer **the existence of another fact—usually a **fact in issue**. It does not directly prove the fact in issue, but it **suggests** it through logical reasoning.

This type of evidence provides **indirect proof**. The more pieces of circumstantial evidence that point toward the same conclusion, the stronger the overall case becomes. When viewed together, these facts can form a convincing picture that supports a finding of guilt.

Think of it like assembling a puzzle: each piece on its own may not show the full image, but when combined, they reveal what likely happened.

***Example:***

*Scenario: Theft from a Store*

*Charge: A person is accused of stealing a laptop from an electronics store.*

**Circumstantial Evidence Example:**

- The accused was **seen on CCTV** entering the store around the time the laptop went missing.
- The accused was **not seen leaving with a bag**, but later that day, was found with the **same model of laptop** in their possession.
- The store’s **security tags** were found discarded in a bin near the exit.
- The accused’s **fingerprints** were found on the shelf where the laptop had been displayed.
- The accused **gave conflicting stories** about where the laptop came from.
None of these facts directly show the accused stealing the laptop (i.e., no one saw them take it), but together they **suggest **that the theft occurred, and that the accused was responsible. This is how circumstantial evidence can build a strong case without direct proof.

# ESTABLISHING FACTS, EXCEPTIONS AND PRESUMPTIONS
### General Rule 
In any case, the law requires that both:

- **Facts in issue** (the key facts that must be proven to establish the charge), and
- **Facts relevant to those issues** (supporting facts that help prove or disprove the key facts) must be **proven through evidence**.
This means you cannot rely on assumptions or unsupported claims—every important fact must be backed up by admissible evidence presented in court.

### Exceptions to the General Rule
Normally, all facts in issue and relevant facts must be proven through evidence. However, there are two main exceptions where **no evidence needs to be presented**:

- **Judicial Notice** – The court accepts certain facts as true without requiring proof, because they are commonly known or easily verified (e.g., that Christmas falls on 25 December).
- **Formal Admissions** – If a party formally admits a fact (usually in writing or during proceedings), that fact is accepted without needing further evidence.
These exceptions help streamline the trial process by avoiding the need to prove facts that are either undisputed or universally accepted.

### Judicial notice
Sometimes, a court will accept a fact as true **without requiring formal evidence**. This is **called judicial notice**.

When judicial notice is taken, the judge (or jury, if directed by the judge) will treat the fact as proven—even though no evidence has been presented to prove it. This is only done when the fact is either:

- **Widely known and accepted**, or
- **Easily verified from reliable sources.**
***Example:***

If the date of Christmas (25 December) was somehow a fact in issue, the court wouldn’t require an expert to testify about it. Instead, it would take judicial notice of the date, because it’s a well-known and undisputed fact.

Judicial notice is covered in Sections **128** and **129** of the **Evidence Act 2006**:

**Section 128 – Notice of Uncontroverted Facts**

- Courts may accept facts that are generally known or locally accepted and cannot reasonably be questioned.
- Judges may also accept facts that can be accurately and easily verified from trustworthy sources.
**Section 129 – Admission of Reliable Published Documents**

- Judges may accept published materials (e.g. history books, scientific texts, maps) as evidence in areas like public history, literature, science, or art—without needing expert testimony.
- These documents are exempt from the usual rules about hearsay and expert evidence.
***Example:***

Under Section 128(1), a judge could take judicial notice of the date and location of a well-known annual event in the local area, even if it’s not widely known nationally.

### Facts formally admitted
In a trial, either party, **the prosecution or the defence**, can agree that certain facts are accepted as true from the outset. When this happens, those facts **do not need to be proven with evidence** during the trial.

Under Sections 9(2) and 9(3) of the Evidence Act 2006, a party may formally **admit a fact**, which means the court treats it as proven and no further proof is required.

This helps streamline the trial by focusing only on the facts that are genuinely in dispute.

***Example:***

*Scenario: Assault Charge*

*Charge: A person is charged with assault after allegedly punching another individual during an argument outside a bar.*

Before the trial begins, the defence **formally admits** that the incident occurred **outside the bar on the night in question**, and that **both the accused and the complainant were present**.

This means the prosecution **does not need to present evidence** to prove the location or the presence of the parties involved. These facts are accepted as true and the court treats them as proven.

The trial can then focus on the **disputed facts**, such as:

- Whether the accused actually punched the complainant.
- Whether the accused acted in self-defense.
- The extent of any injuries caused.
### Presumptions in Evidence
Sometimes, a fact in dispute cannot be directly proven—either because no direct evidence is available or it’s not possible to obtain it. In these situations, the court may **infer **the disputed fact from other facts that **have already been proven or are known**. This type of inference is called a **presumption**.

Presumptions come in two types: **presumptions of law** and **presumptions of fact**.

### Presumption of Law
A **presumption of law** is a legal inference that the law automatically makes from certain facts. These are built into the legal system and guide how courts approach specific situations.

Presumptions of law can be either:

- **Conclusive (or irrebuttable)** – These cannot be challenged or disproven, no matter what evidence is presented.
- ***Example:*** Under section 21(1) of the Crimes Act 1961, a child under 10 years of age **cannot be convicted of a criminal offence**. This is a conclusive presumption—it applies absolutely and cannot be argued against.
- **Rebuttable** – These are assumed to be true unless proven otherwise.
- ***Example:* **The presumption that a **defendant is innocent until proven guilty**. This is a starting point in every criminal trial, but it can be overturned if the prosecution proves guilt beyond reasonable doubt.
Conclusive presumptions are considered **rules of substantive law**, because they relate to the actual rights and responsibilities under the law—not just the procedures used in court.

### Presumptions of Fact
A **presumption of fact** is a logical conclusion that a judge or jury may naturally draw from the facts that have already been proven in a case.

These are not set by law—they are based on **common sense and reasoning**. For example, if someone is found in possession of goods that were recently stolen, it may be reasonable to **presume they knew the goods were stolen**. This is a presumption of fact.

Because these presumptions are based on logic rather than legal rules, they are **always rebuttable** meaning the accused can present evidence to challenge or disprove the presumption.

***Example:***

*Scenario: Assault Outside a Pub*

*A person is found standing over another individual who is injured and lying on the ground. The injured person has bruises consistent with being punched, and several witnesses saw the accused running from the scene shortly after the incident.*

From these proven facts, the court may **presume** that the person standing over the victim was **involved in the assault**—even though no one directly saw the punch being thrown.

This is a **presumption of fact** because it’s a logical conclusion drawn from the surrounding circumstances. However, it is **rebuttable**—the accused could present evidence showing, for example, that they arrived after the assault occurred and were trying to help the victim.

# ADMISSIBILITY AND THE PRINCIPLES OF EVIDENCE LAW
### Determining admissibility
Evidence is considered **admissible **if it can be legally accepted by the court. If it cannot be accepted under the law, it is **inadmissible.**

In most cases, there is **no specific standard of proof** required to decide whether evidence is admissible—unless the Evidence Act 2006 provides one. For example, **Section 45** sets a standard for deciding whether **identification evidence** is admissible.

The **judge **is responsible for making decisions about admissibility.

In the case *of R v Burrows*, the court clarified that:

- The party wanting to present the evidence must **prove that it is admissible**.
- It is **not logical** to require the prosecution to prove admissibility beyond reasonable doubt, because admissibility is a **legal question**, not a factual one.
- Any evidence that a juror might reasonably rely on to reach a verdict can be considered admissible.
### Key Principles Used to Decide Admissibility
When deciding whether evidence should be admitted, courts consider several core principles drawn from both **common law** and the **Evidence Act 2006**:

- **Relevance **– Does the evidence relate to the facts in issue?
- **Reliability** – Is the evidence trustworthy and credible?
- **Fairness** – Would admitting the evidence be unfair to any party?
These principles help ensure that only appropriate and legally sound evidence is used in court.

### Relevance 
The **first and most important condition** for evidence to be accepted in court is that it must be **relevant**.

Under **Section 7 of the Evidence Act 2006**, the law states:

- **Relevant evidence is admissible**, unless it is:
- Specifically ruled **inadmissible** under the Evidence Act or another law, or
- **Excluded** under the Act or another law.
- **Irrelevant evidence is never admissible**.
- Evidence is considered **relevant** if it has a tendency to **prove or disprove something that matters to the outcome of the case** (s7(3)).
This includes both:

- **Direct evidence** – which directly supports a fact in issue.
- **Circumstantial evidence** – which indirectly supports a fact through inference.
Evidence that does **not relate to the key issues** in the case is considered irrelevant and cannot be used.

### Relevance Is Necessary, But Not Always Enough
Even if evidence is relevant, it **might still be excluded** if it fails to meet other legal requirements. For example:

Evidence that was **improperly obtained** may be relevant but could be **inadmissible under Section 30 **of the Evidence Act.

Evidence may be excluded due to concerns about:

- **Reliability** – Is the evidence trustworthy?
- **Fairness** – Would admitting it be unfair to one of the parties?
- **Public interest** – Would admitting it harm broader legal or societal interests?
So, while **relevance is essential**, it is **not the only test**. Evidence must be both **relevant and admissible** to be used in court.

### Who Decides?
The **judge **decides whether evidence is admissible. If the judge finds the evidence relevant and no legal rule excludes it, the party presenting it may use it in the case.

Once admitted, the **weight or importance** of the evidence is assessed by the **judge or jury**, depending on the type of trial.

### Reliability
Unreliability is not usually a reason to make evidence inadmissible, but the Evidence Act includes some exceptions. This means that relevant evidence can sometimes be excluded—or, if allowed, the judge may give a warning—because it is considered unreliable. Section 122 is the main part of the Act that deals with this.

The Act also has rules that focus on reliability, such as:

- **Hearsay evidence** – statements made outside of court that are presented as evidence of the truth.
- **Identification evidence** – when a witness identifies a person, such as in a photo lineup or in court.
Because these types of evidence can be less reliable, it is important to make sure that any evidence collected is as accurate and trustworthy as possible.

### Fairness and the general exclusion 
Even if evidence is relevant to a case, it can still be excluded if using it would be unfair. What counts as "unfair" is decided by the trial judge, who has the discretion to make that call. There are two main situations where this might happen:

- **Unfair Prejudice:** Evidence may be excluded if it could unfairly influence the outcome of the trial. For example, if the evidence makes the defendant look bad in a way that isn’t directly related to the facts of the case, it might be considered prejudicial.
- **Unfair Circumstances of Collection:** Sometimes, evidence is not unfair in itself, but the way it was obtained makes it unfair to use. A common example is a defendant’s statement or confession. Even if the statement is accurate and relevant, it might be excluded if it was collected through improper or unfair methods—such as coercion or deception. In these cases, the judge may decide that admitting the evidence would be unjust.
### General Exclusion Provision – Section 8 
Section 8 of the Evidence Act 2006 gives judges the power—and in some cases the obligation—to exclude certain evidence, even if it is relevant. This rule is designed to keep trials fair and efficient.

### What Section 8 Says
- **Mandatory Exclusion**
- A judge must exclude evidence if its probative value (how strongly it helps prove something important in the case) is outweighed by the risk that the evidence will:
- **Unfairly prejudice the proceeding** (for example, cause the jury to give it too much weight or use it for the wrong reason), or
- **Needlessly prolong the proceeding** (waste time by adding little value).
- **Criminal Cases**
- When deciding this in a criminal case, the judge must also consider the defendant’s right to present an effective defence.
### Purpose of Section 8
- It acts as a final check on evidence that is otherwise admissible under other rules.
- The goal is to **balance fairness and efficiency**:
- Keep evidence that genuinely helps the case.
- Exclude evidence that risks unfairness or wastes time.
- Once the judge decides that either risk applies, exclusion is **mandatory**.
### The Section 8 Test
- The judge weighs:
- **Probative value:** How important and reliable is the evidence in proving a key fact?
- **Against the risk of:**
- **Unfair prejudice:** The danger that the evidence will mislead the jury, cause bias, or distract from the real issues.
- **Needless delay:** Evidence that repeats what’s already been shown or adds little new information.
### Examples of Unfair Prejudice
- Evidence that might make the jury dislike a person for reasons unrelated to the case (e.g., personal lifestyle choices).
- Evidence that could confuse the jury or lead them to decide based on emotion rather than facts.
### Relationship to Other Rules
- Section 8 applies after other admissibility rules. Even if evidence is allowed under a specific rule (like relevance under s7), it can still be excluded under s8.
- Similar balancing occurs under section 43 for propensity evidence, but s43 focuses specifically on prejudice to the defendant.
### Evidence That Would Needlessly Prolong the Proceeding
Under section 8(1)(b), a judge must exclude evidence if it would unnecessarily lengthen the trial.

***Example:*** If a defendant wants to call 20 witnesses to testify about their honesty (veracity), the judge can use s8(1)(b) to limit this to one or two witnesses.

### Considering the Defendant’s Right to an Effective Defence
Section 8(2) requires judges to consider the defendant’s right to present an effective defence when applying s8(1)(a).

This reflects rights under the **New Zealand Bill of Rights Act 1990:**

- s25(a): Right to a fair trial
- s25(e): Right to present a defence
However, this does **not** mean evidence must be admitted if its unfairly prejudicial effect outweighs its probative value.

In close cases, s8(2) can tip the balance:

- Allowing defence evidence that might otherwise be excluded, or
- Excluding prosecution evidence that could unfairly harm the defence.
### Admission by Agreement (Section 9)
Under **s9(1)**, evidence can be admitted if both parties agree—even if it would normally be inadmissible.

However, the judge has the final say. In ***R v Hannigan***, the court confirmed that a judge can:

- Refuse to admit the evidence, or
- Change the form in which it is admitted, even if both parties agree.
### Provisional Admissibility (Section 14)
If there is a question about whether evidence is admissible, the judge can **provisionally admit it**, provided more evidence will later confirm its admissibility.

If that supporting evidence is not provided, the provisional evidence must be excluded.

### Hearings in Chambers (Section 15)
Section 15 covers situations where a witness gives evidence to help the judge decide if other evidence should be admitted.

These hearings often happen **without the jury present** and are called ***“hearings in chambers”*** or ***“preliminary hearings.”***

Section 15 applies to **all witnesses**, not just defendants, and to any hearing about admissibility.

Evidence given during a **hearing in chambers** (a private hearing, usually without the jury) can only be used later in the same case if the witness’s testimony at the hearing is inconsistent with what they say later in court.

In that situation, the earlier statement is admissible **only to show the inconsistency**.

### Limited Use of Evidence and Multiple Purposes
Under **section 7(3)** of the Evidence Act 2006, evidence can be used to prove **anything that matters to deciding the case.**

Generally, once evidence is admitted, it can be used for **any relevant purpose**, not just the reason it was originally admitted.

The **Supreme Court in Hart v R** confirmed this, stating that evidence is usually either **admissible for all purposes or not admissible at all.**

### Exceptions – When Use Is Limited
Some sections of the Act restrict how certain evidence can be used. Examples include:

- **s27** – Limits the use of pre-trial statements by defendants and co-defendants.
- **s31** – Stops the prosecution from using certain evidence offered by the defence in a criminal case.
- **s32** – Prevents using a defendant’s pre-trial silence as evidence of guilt.
### Judicial Directions to Prevent Misuse
To reduce the risk of evidence being misused, the Act allows (and sometimes requires) judges to give **jury warnings.**

Example: **s124** – A warning about evidence suggesting the defendant has lied.

# SUMMARY
### Purpose of the Evidence Act 2006
The main goal of the Evidence Act 2006 is to ensure that court cases are decided fairly and justly.

### Key Principles
**Facts in issue** (the main facts the case depends on) and **facts relevant to those issues** must be proven by evidence.

**Exceptions:**

- When the court takes judicial notice of a fact (accepts it as true without proof).
- When facts are formally admitted by the parties.
When deciding if evidence is admissible, courts consider three main principles:

- **Relevance** – Does it relate to the issues in the case?
- **Reliability** – Can it be trusted?
- **Fairness **– Will it make the trial unfair?
### Relevance and Admissibility
For evidence to be accepted, it must be **both relevant and admissible.**

**Relevant evidence** (s7) is any evidence that tends to **prove or disprove something important to deciding the case.**

### Excluding Evidence – Section 8
Even if evidence is relevant, it can still be excluded under **s8**.

The judge must **balance**:

**Probative value** – How strongly the evidence helps prove something important.

**Against the risk that it will:**

- Unfairly prejudice the proceeding (e.g., mislead the jury or cause bias), or
- Needlessly prolong the proceeding (e.g., waste time without adding real value).

# THE EXCLUSIVE RULES OF EVIDENCE 
### Introduction
Most evidence rules focus on what cannot be presented in court. These rules are largely based on common sense.

The main exclusionary rules cover:

- **Veracity** – whether a person is truthful.
- **Propensity** – whether someone has a tendency to act in a certain way.
- **Hearsay** – repeating what someone else said to prove it’s true.
- **Opinion **– personal views rather than facts.
- **Identification** – evidence about recognizing a person.
- **Improperly obtained evidence** – evidence gathered unlawfully or unfairly.
In addition to these, there is a **general exclusion rule** under **section 8**, which allows judges to exclude evidence if its value is outweighed by the risk of unfairness or unnecessary delay. This was covered in the previous chapter.

### Rules of Admissibility Are Rules of Law
The Supreme Court in ***R v Gwaze*** confirmed that rules about admissibility—such as sections 7 and 8 of the Evidence Act—are **legal rules**, not just matters of discretion. While judges use judgment to apply them, these rules set clear **standards that must be followed.**

# VERACITY AND PROPENSITY
### Relationship Between Veracity and Propensity Rules
Under the **Evidence Act 2006**, what used to be called “character evidence” at common law is now divided into two categories:

- **Veracity** – a person’s tendency to tell the truth (or refrain from lying).
- **Propensity** – a person’s tendency to act in a particular way.
### When These Rules Apply
The **veracity rules** do **not** apply if veracity is an element of the offence itself.

***Example:*** In a perjury case, the person’s truthfulness is part of the charge, so the rule does not apply.

The **veracity and propensity rules** generally do **not** apply at bail or sentencing hearings, except where **section 44** applies (evidence about a complainant’s sexual experience or reputation).

### Veracity Rules – Section 37
Section 37 sets out when evidence about a person’s veracity can be used:

- **General Rule:**
A party cannot offer evidence about someone’s veracity unless it is **substantially helpful **in deciding whether that person is truthful.

- **Extra Rule for Defendants:**
In criminal cases, evidence about a defendant’s veracity must also meet the requirements of **section 38 or 39**.

- **What Makes Evidence *“Substantially Helpful”?***
The judge may consider whether the evidence shows:

- The person lied under a legal obligation to tell the truth (e.g., in court or a signed declaration).
- Previous convictions for offences involving dishonesty.
- Previous inconsistent statements.
- Bias or motive to be untruthful.
- **Limits on Challenging Your Own Witness:**
- You cannot attack your own witness’s veracity unless the judge declares them hostile.
- You can, however, present evidence that contradicts what they said about the facts in issue.
- **Definition:**
Veracity means a person’s tendency to tell the truth, either generally or in the current case.

The **veracity rules** focus only on whether a person is truthful. They do not deal with whether a person’s statement is accurate if they were genuinely trying to tell the truth.

### Challenging Your Own Witness
Under **section 37(4)(a),** you cannot challenge the veracity of your own witness unless the judge declares them hostile (see Chapter 5).

However, you can still present evidence that contradicts what the witness said about the **facts in issue**.

### Substantial Helpfulness
For veracity evidence to be admissible, it must be substantially helpful **substantially helpful **in deciding whether the person is truthful.

This is a **higher standard than relevance under section 7**—it must do more than simply suggest truthfulness or dishonesty.

When deciding if evidence is substantially helpful, the judge may consider factors in **section 37(3)(a)-(e)**, such as:

- Lying under a legal obligation to tell the truth (e.g., in court or a signed declaration).
- Convictions for offences involving dishonesty.
- Previous inconsistent statements.
- Bias or motive to be untruthful.
This test applies to both:

- Evidence given in chief (your own witness), and
- Evidence brought out in cross-examination.
### Criminal Convictions
There is **no automatic right **to ask a witness if they have previous convictions. Such questions are only allowed if they are **substantially helpful** in assessing veracity.

Old rules from previous laws and common law no longer apply.

### Reputation for Veracity
In ***R v K***, the court suggested that evidence of someone’s reputation for truthfulness **might **be admissible under section 37, but the **substantial helpfulness** test will rarely be met.

**When Substantial Helpfulness Is Not Enough**

Even if evidence is substantially helpful, it is not sufficient in two situations:

- When the **prosecution** wants to offer evidence about a defendant’s veracity (see section 38).
- When a **defendant** wants to offer veracity evidence about a co-defendant (see section 39).
### Evidence of a Defendant’s Veracity (Section 38)
**When a Defendant Can Offer Veracity Evidence**

A defendant in a criminal case can present evidence about their own truthfulness, but it must meet the **substantial helpfulness** test under **section 37**.

**When the Prosecution Can Offer Veracity Evidence**

The prosecution can only offer evidence about a defendant’s veracity if:

- **The defendant has put veracity in issue** by:
- Offering evidence about their own veracity, or
- Challenging the veracity of a prosecution witness on something other than the facts in issue (and the defendant must have initiated this challenge).
- The **judge gives permission**.
**Judge’s Considerations (s38(3))**

When deciding whether to allow the prosecution to offer veracity evidence, the judge may consider:

- How much the defendant’s veracity (or a prosecution witness’s veracity) has been questioned.
- How long ago any conviction occurred that the prosecution wants to use.
- Whether the defendant’s veracity evidence was brought out by the prosecution during questioning.
**Key Requirements**

For the prosecution to offer veracity evidence:

- Veracity must be **relevant** (the defendant’s truthfulness must be in issue).
- The evidence must meet the **substantial helpfulness** test.
- The prosecution must get the **judge’s permission**.
**Important Limitation**

If the defendant only attacked a prosecution witness’s veracity **about the facts in issue**, the prosecution **cannot** respond by attacking the defendant’s veracity.

### Propensity Evidence (Sections 40–43)
**What Is Propensity Evidence?**

Under **section 40**, propensity evidence means evidence that shows a person’s **tendency to act in a certain way** or **have a particular state of mind**.

This includes evidence of acts, omissions, events, or circumstances the person is alleged to have been involved in.

**Examples of Propensity Evidence:**

- A pattern of violent behavior (action).
- A tendency to act dishonestly or aggressively (state of mind).
**What It Does NOT Include:**

- Evidence of an act that is an **element of the current offence**.
- Evidence that is mainly about **veracity **(truthfulness), which is covered by section 37.
**General Rule**

Any party may offer propensity evidence in civil or criminal cases.

**Restrictions apply** when:

The evidence is about a **defendant in a criminal case **(sections 41–43).

The evidence is about a **complainant’s sexual experience** (section 44).

Outside these restrictions, the usual rules apply:

- **Relevance (s7)** – Is the evidence connected to the case?
- **General exclusion (s8)** – Does its value outweigh the risk of unfairness or delay?
### Propensity Evidence About Defendants (Section 41)
**What Defendants Can Do**

A defendant can offer evidence about their own propensity.

This can include:

- **Good propensity** (e.g., evidence of honesty or peaceful behavior).
- **Disreputable conduct** (sometimes for tactical reasons).
- **Neutral propensity** (e.g., regular attendance at a class to support an alibi).
Defendants can present this evidence themselves or through other witnesses.

### What Happens If a Defendant Offers Propensity Evidence
If a defendant offers evidence of their own good character, the prosecution (or another party) may, with the **judge’s permission**, offer rebuttal evidence about the defendant’s propensity.

This prevents the jury from getting a misleading impression of the defendant’s character.

**Important:**

- The judge decides whether to allow rebuttal evidence.
- In Wi v R, the Court of Appeal said permission is unlikely if the defendant only shows they have **no previous convictions.**
### Key Points
The prosecution cannot introduce propensity evidence about the defendant unless:

- The defendant introduces their own propensity evidence, or
- The conditions in section 43 are met.
- Attacking a prosecution witness’s propensity does not open the door for the prosecution to attack the defendant’s propensity.
### Propensity Evidence Offered by the Prosecution (Section 43)
When Can the Prosecution Offer Propensity Evidence?

Under **section 43**, the prosecution can only offer propensity evidence about a defendant if:

- The evidence has **probative value** (it helps prove an important issue in dispute), and
- That probative value **outweighs the risk of unfair prejudice** to the defendant.
This is a balancing test: the evidence must be useful enough to justify any risk of unfairness.

### Probative Value
**Probative value** refers to how strongly a piece of evidence helps to **prove or disprove a fact in issue** in a case. It measures both the **relevance** and **reliability **of the evidence, and how much it contributes to resolving an important point in the trial.

***Example:***

- CCTV footage showing the accused at the crime scene at the exact time of the offence has high probative value because it directly supports a key fact.
- Evidence that the accused was in another city the day before the offence has low probative value because it does little to prove or disprove the actual charge.
Courts must **balance probative value against the risk of unfair prejudice**. Even highly probative evidence can be excluded if it might cause the jury to decide based on bias rather than facts (e.g., evidence of prior convictions)

### Assessing Probative Value
When deciding how valuable the evidence is, the judge must consider:

The **issue in dispute** – How important is the issue the evidence relates to?

Other factors may include:

- How often the alleged acts or events occurred.
- How close in time they are to the current offence.
- How similar they are to the current offence.
- How many people have made similar allegations.
- Whether those allegations might be due to collusion or influence.
- Whether the acts or events are unusual (making coincidence unlikely).
### Assessing Prejudicial Effect
The judge must also consider:

- Could the evidence **unfairly bias** the jury against the defendant?
- Is there a risk the jury will give **too much weight** to the other acts when deciding the verdict?
### Requirements for Admission (Rei v R)
For propensity evidence to be admitted under section 43:

- It must actually be **propensity evidence** (showing a tendency to act in a certain way or have a certain state of mind).
- It must have **probative value** on an issue in dispute, considering the factors in section 43(3).
- Its probative value must **outweigh the risk of unfair prejudice** to the defendant.
### Key Points
- Section 43 applies to evidence offered by the prosecution, including evidence brought out in **cross-examination**.
- This rule replaced what was previously called ***“similar fact evidence”*** under common law.
- The focus is on **linkage and coincidence**: the stronger the connection between past acts and the current charge, the higher the probative value.
- Unlike section 8, section 43 looks only at **prejudice to the defendant**, not to the proceeding as a whole.
### Assessing Propensity Evidence – Key Principles
Balancing Probative Value and Prejudice

The court must carefully assess:

- How specific and relevant the propensity evidence is to the issue in dispute (its probative value).
- How much risk it carries of unfair prejudice, such as the jury using it for the wrong reason or making an improper assumption.
Unfairness usually occurs when the evidence might lead the jury to:

- Decide based on emotion rather than facts.
- Assume guilt simply because the defendant acted badly in the past.
The prosecution has the burden of proving that the evidence’s probative value outweighs the risk of unfair prejudice.

### Focus on the Act, Not Old Law
Although older “similar fact” cases can provide context, the Supreme Court **in Mahomed v R** made it clear that courts should focus on the **Evidence Act 2006**, which largely replaced previous common law rules.

### Prior Wrongdoing – Conviction Not Required
The defendant does not need to have been convicted of earlier conduct for it to count as propensity evidence.

- This includes **prior acquittal evidence** (as confirmed in *Fenemor v R*).
Even conduct **after the current offence** can be used as propensity evidence.

### Judge’s Role in Assessing Probative Value
Under **section 43(2),** the judge must consider:

- The **nature of the issue in dispute**.
- Factors in **section 43(3)** (e.g., frequency, similarity, timing, number of allegations, and whether they could be due to collusion).
- Other relevant matters, such as the strength of other evidence.
The evidence must show more than just a general tendency to do bad things—it must relate meaningfully to the facts in issue.

### Assessing Prejudicial Effect
When considering prejudice, the judge looks at factors in **section 43(4),** such as:

- Whether the evidence might unfairly bias the jury against the defendant.
- Whether the jury might give it too much weight.
Finally, the judge must weigh the probative value against the risk of unfair prejudice before deciding if the evidence is admissible.

# HEARSAY
### What Is a Hearsay Statement?
The **Evidence Act 2006** changed the old common law rules on hearsay. Most previous exceptions no longer apply.

Under **section 4**, a **hearsay statement** is:

- A statement made by someone other than a witness in court, and
- Offered as evidence to prove the truth of what it says.
A **statement** can be:

- Something spoken or written, or
- Non-verbal conduct intended to communicate something (e.g., a nod meaning “yes”).
### General Rule
Hearsay statements are **not admissible** unless:

- The Evidence Act (or another law) specifically allows it, or
- The Act says the hearsay rules do not apply (section 17).
### Who Is a “Witness”?
If the person who made the statement is in court and can be **cross-examined**, their earlier out-of-court statement is **not hearsay**.

This is because the main reason for the hearsay rule—**the inability to test credibility**—does not apply.

However, the statement might still be excluded under other rules (e.g., **veracity rules in section 37**).

### Unintended Assertions Are Not Hearsay
If someone’s actions imply something but were **not intended as an assertion**, it is not hearsay.

***Example:***

An experienced sailor checks a yacht before taking his family out. This suggests the yacht is seaworthy, but unless he intended to assert that fact, it is **not hearsay**.

### Purpose Matters
The hearsay rule depends on **why the evidence is offered**:

- If it is offered to **prove the truth** of what was said → it is hearsay.
- If it is offered for another reason (e.g., to explain someone’s actions), it is **not hearsay**.
***Example:***

A doctor says, “My patient complained of chest pain.”

If this is used to show the patient **actually had chest pain**, it is hearsay.

If it is used to explain why the doctor diagnosed angina, it is **not hearsay**.

### Hearsay Rule – Section 17
Section 17 states:

A hearsay statement is **not admissible** unless:

- The Act or another law allows it, or
- The Act says the hearsay rules do not apply, and the statement is relevant and not otherwise inadmissible.
Even if evidence is allowed under another rule (e.g., identification evidence), it must still comply with the hearsay rules unless the Act specifically excludes them.

### General Admissibility of Hearsay
**What is hearsay?**

Hearsay is a statement made outside of court that is presented in court to prove the truth of what it says. Normally, hearsay is not allowed because it can be unreliable. However, the Evidence Act 2006 provides exceptions.

### Section 18 – Main Exception to the Hearsay Rule
Section 18 of the Evidence Act 2006 is the main exception to the rule against hearsay. It says a hearsay statement can be admitted in court if:

- **Reliability:** The circumstances around the statement give reasonable assurance that it is reliable; and
- **Unavailability or Practicality:**
- The person who made the statement (the “maker”) is unavailable as a witness, or
- The Judge decides that making the person testify would cause undue expense or delay.
This rule is subject to other sections, including **Section 22**, which requires notice before hearsay can be used in criminal cases.

### Why is Reliability Important?
The rule against hearsay exists because hearsay can be unreliable. Here’s why:

- If the maker is not in court, they **cannot be cross-examined** about what they said or the conditions under which they said it.
- Juries cannot judge the **demeanor** (how someone behaves when speaking) of the person who made the statement.
- There is a risk of **misunderstanding or distortion**, like in the game “Chinese Whispers,” where a message changes as it is repeated.
The danger is that the court might give too much weight to evidence that cannot be properly tested.

### What Does “Reasonable Assurance of Reliability” Mean?
The Judge must decide if the statement is reliable enough for the court to consider. This is a **threshold test**—it doesn’t mean the statement is true, only that it is safe to admit.

**Section 16(1)** explains what “circumstances” include when assessing reliability:

- The nature and contents of the statement
- How and when it was made
- The maker’s **veracity **(truthfulness)
- The maker’s ability to **observe accurately**
Examples of relevant factors:

- Was the statement written or oral?
- Was it signed or witnessed?
- Was it made soon after the event?
- What was the relationship between the maker and the person reporting it?
The Supreme Court in **R v Gwaze** confirmed that reliability involves looking at all these factors together.

### Unavailability or Undue Expense/Delay
A hearsay statement can only be admitted if the maker is **unavailable** or if calling them would cause **undue expense or delay**.

**Section 16(2)** defines “unavailable” as when the person:

- Is dead
- Is outside New Zealand and cannot reasonably attend
- Is unfit to testify due to age or health
- Cannot be found despite reasonable efforts
- Is not legally required to give evidence
### Notice Requirement (Section 22)
In criminal cases, you must give **written notice** before using hearsay. The notice must include:

- Your intention to use the statement
- The maker’s name (if known)
- The statement’s contents (if oral)
- Why it is reliable
- Why the maker is unavailable or why calling them would cause undue expense or delay
This notice must be given early enough for the other party to respond.

### Unavailability and Use of Technology
Under **Section 16(2)(b),** the focus is on whether the person can be a “witness.” With modern technology, such as **video-link** (see s168 of the Evidence Act and the Courts (Remote Participation) Act 2010), more people—especially those overseas—can now give evidence remotely. This means they are more likely to be considered “available.”

**Section 16(2)(c)** allows the court to consider the effect of **youth or age** on a person’s ability to be a witness.

**Section 16(2)(d)** states that if a person cannot be identified or found, they are considered unavailable. This is broader than the old rule, which only referred to being unable to find the person.

Under** Section 16(2)(e),** people who cannot be legally forced to testify (for example, the Sovereign under s74 or a **defendant in a criminal case**) are also considered unavailable. However, Section 21 limits the use of a defendant’s own hearsay statements—they cannot introduce their own out-of-court statements if they choose not to testify. The prosecution, however, can use a defendant’s pre-trial statements under Sections 27–30, which are not subject to the hearsay rule.

### Preventing Abuse of the Rule
**Section 16(3)** prevents a party from making someone unavailable on purpose to use their hearsay statement. For example, if a party **kidnaps or harms** the person, or deliberately stops them from testifying because they might change their story, they cannot benefit from that action.

### Hearsay in Business Records (Section 19)
**Section 19** allows hearsay statements in **business records** to be admitted if:

- The person who provided the information is unavailable; or
- The Judge decides there is **no useful purpose** in calling them because too much time has passed and they are unlikely to remember; or
- Calling them would cause **undue expense or delay**.
A **business record** is a document made:

- As part of a business or to meet a legal duty, and
- From information supplied by someone who had personal knowledge of the matters recorded.
This includes, for example, **a statement made to a police officer and written in their notebook.**

Unlike Section 18, there is **no requirement to prove reliability** for business records. However, if reliability is challenged, it can affect the **weight **of the evidence or lead to exclusion under **Section 8.**

***Section 22 – Giving Notice Before Using Hearsay in Criminal Cases***

Before you can use a **hearsay statement** in a criminal trial, you must follow certain steps—unless:

- All other parties agree to skip the notice, or
- The Judge allows you to skip it.
### What You Need to Do
If you want to use a hearsay statement, you must give **written notice** to the other parties. This notice must include:

- That you plan to use the hearsay statement.
- The name of the person who made the statement (if known).
- What the statement says (if it was spoken).
- Why the statement is reliable (if you're relying on **Section 18(1)(a)).**
- Why the document is a business record (if you're relying on **Section (19).**
- Why the person who made the statement is unavailable **Section 18(1)(b)(i) or 19(1)(a).**
- Why calling the person would cause too much expense or delay **Section 18(1)(b)(ii) or 19(1)(c).**
- Why it wouldn’t help to call the person as a witness **Section 19(1)(b).**
- Why the requirements of **Section 22A** are met (if you're relying on that section).
### If the Statement Is Written
You must also include a copy of the document that contains the statement.

### Timing Matters
You must give notice early enough so that other parties have a fair chance to respond before the hearing.

### When Can the Judge Waive the Requirements?
The Judge can allow you to skip the notice if:

- No one is seriously disadvantaged by it,
- It wasn’t reasonably possible to give notice in time, or
- It’s necessary to ensure a fair trial.
# THE OPINION RULE
### The Opinion Rule – Section 23 of the Evidence Act 2006
**General Rule:**

Under **Section 23**, a statement of opinion is **not admissible** in court unless it meets the exceptions in **Sections 24 or 25.**

This means that a witness cannot give evidence about what they think or believe happened. Instead, they must describe what they personally saw, heard, or experienced—this is called direct evidence.

### What Is an Opinion?
The Act defines an opinion as a statement that tries to **prove or disprove a fact** by expressing a belief or inference (Section 4).

Sometimes, it’s hard to tell the difference between a **fact **and an **opinion.** For example:

Saying “the car was speeding” might be an opinion unless the witness can describe how they knew (e.g., saw the speedometer or compared it to other traffic).

### Why Are Opinions Usually Excluded?
The rule exists to keep evidence **reliable and relevant**. Here’s why opinions are generally not allowed:

- **Low probative value:** A simple opinion often doesn’t help prove anything clearly.
- **Risk of confusion:** If a witness gives opinions, they might take over the role of the judge or jury, who are supposed to decide what the facts mean.
- **Hidden inadmissible evidence:** Sometimes opinions are based on other evidence that wouldn’t be allowed in court (like propensity evidence, which suggests someone acted a certain way because they’ve done it before).
### Exceptions to the Rule
**Sections 24 and 25** allow opinion evidence in certain situations, such as:

**Expert witnesses** (Section 25), who have special knowledge or training.

**Non-expert witnesses** (Section 24), when their opinion helps explain what they saw or experienced (e.g., identifying someone, estimating speed, or describing someone’s emotional state).

Even when opinion evidence is allowed under these sections, it must still meet other rules in the Act—like those about **propensity evidence** or **relevance**.

### Opinion Evidence – Sections 24 and 25 of the Evidence Act 2006
**1. Non-Expert Opinion Evidence (Section 24)**

Normally, witnesses can only talk about what they directly saw, heard, or experienced. But Section 24 allows a witness to give an opinion if it helps them explain what they perceived, or helps the judge or jury understand it.

**When is non-expert opinion allowed?**

A witness’s opinion is admissible if:

- It’s necessary to explain what they saw, heard, or felt.
- The opinion is based on something they personally experienced.
**What kinds of opinions are allowed?**

Examples include:

- Identifying a person
- Estimating speed
- Describing someone’s emotional state
- Judging age or weather conditions
These types of opinions are often closely tied to the witness’s direct observations and can’t easily be separated from the facts.

***Example:***

If a witness says they picked someone out of an identification parade, that’s a **fact**. But saying that person is the offender is partly **opinion**, based on their memory and perception.

Similarly, if a witness says a car was speeding, they may be relying on the sound or movement of the car—this is a mix of **fact and opinion**.

### Expert Opinion Evidence (Section 25)
Some matters require special knowledge or training. In these cases, only an expert witness can give an opinion.

**When is expert opinion allowed?**

Under Section 25, expert opinion is admissible if:

- It provides substantial help to the judge or jury in understanding other evidence or deciding an important fact in the case.
**What else does Section 25 say?**

- An expert can give an opinion even if it relates to the **main issue** in the case or something that seems like **common knowledge**.
- If the expert’s opinion is based on a fact outside their usual area of expertise, that fact must be **proven or officially recognised** in court.
- If the expert’s opinion on someone’s **sanity** is based on what that person told them, the person’s statement is admissible—even if it would normally be excluded as hearsay.
**Key Points:**

- Expert evidence can include **facts, opinions, or both**.
- If it’s just factual, it follows the usual rules of evidence.
- If it’s opinion-based, it must meet the criteria in **Section 25**.
### Qualification as an Expert
**Who is an Expert?**

Under **Section 4 of the Evidence Act**, an expert is someone with **specialised knowledge or skill** gained through **training, study, or experience**.

The **judge decides **if a person is qualified to give expert evidence. If they are not properly qualified, their opinion on technical matters is **inadmissible.**

An expert must show the court they have the necessary qualifications. This can come from:

- Formal education and training
- Practical experience
- Or a combination of both
Experts can only give opinions **within their area of expertise**.

### What Does “Likely to Obtain Substantial Help” Mean?
Under **Section 25(1),** expert opinion is admissible if it gives the judge or jury **substantial help **in:

- Understanding other evidence, or
- Deciding an important fact in the case
This replaced old common law rules that:

- **Banned experts from commenting on common knowledge**, and
- **Prevented experts from giving opinions on the ultimate issue** in the case
Now, the focus is on whether the opinion is **relevant, reliable, and useful** (as confirmed in B v R [2011]).

### Evidence Must Be Based on Proven Facts
An expert’s opinion must be based on **facts that are or will be proven in court**. If the facts are wrong, incomplete, or irrelevant, the opinion has little or no value (R v Turner).

Lawyers should ask experts to **state the facts** their opinion relies on during examination-in-chief.

If the factual basis is missing or unproven, the opinion may be** excluded** or given **very little weight**. In a jury trial, the judge will instruct the jury to rely on the opinion only if the facts are proven.

### What Can Experts Rely On?
Experts can base their opinions on:

- **General body of knowledge** (e.g., research, textbooks, journal articles)
- **Facts provided by others** (e.g., lab results, observations by colleagues)
- **Assumed facts**, as long as these are clearly stated and supported by admissible evidence
If research findings were presented directly to the court instead of through the expert’s interpretation, they could be excluded as** hearsay** under Section 18.

However, **Section 129** allows published documents to be admitted if the judge considers them reliable.

### Provisional Admissibility (Section 14)
Under **Section 25(3),** an expert can give an opinion based on facts that **will be proven later**. This is called **provisional admissibility**.

If those facts are not proven, the opinion will be disregarded.

### Expert Evidence About Sanity (Section 25(4))
When a person’s **sanity** is in question, an expert (like a psychologist or psychiatrist) may rely on **statements** **made by that person** about their own state of mind. These statements are allowed as evidence to support the expert’s opinion.

However:

- The rule only applies to **statements made by the person themselves**, not by others.
- It only covers statements about the person’s **mental state**—not other topics.
- It does **not apply** to mental health issues that don’t meet the legal definition of **insanity.**
### How Experts Should Conduct Themselves
Experts have a duty to help the court impartially. They are not there to support one side, they must provide honest, unbiased opinions based on their expertise.

In *R v Hutton*, the Court of Appeal outlined key principles for expert witnesses:

✅ State your qualifications clearly.

✅ Explain the facts, assumptions, and reasoning behind your opinion.

✅ Refer to any research or materials you relied on.

✅ Stay within your area of expertise—don’t give opinions on topics you’re not qualified in.

✅ Mention any limitations or uncertainties in your opinion.

✅ Assist the court fairly and objectively.

❌ Do not act as an advocate for either side.

### Notice Requirement for Expert Evidence
Under the **Criminal Disclosure Act 2008**, the defence must give **advance notice** if they plan to call an expert witness.

- This includes a **brief of evidence**, a **report**, or a** summary** (if the full report isn’t ready).
- It must be provided at least **14 days before the trial date**.
This ensures all parties have time to prepare and respond to the expert’s evidence.

# Testimony
### Introduction
The prosecution does not have to call every person who could give evidence. However, if the court believes a witness who has not been called should have been, it can require the prosecution to call that witness — under s113(3) of the Criminal Procedure Act 2011.

Before you serve a summons on a person to appear in court, you need to work out four things:

- whether they are allowed to give evidence — **their eligibility**
- whether they can be required to give evidence — **their compellability**
- whether they can refuse to answer certain questions — **privilege**
- what type of witness they will be
### Eligibility and compellability
**The general rule — everyone is eligible and compellable**

Two key concepts govern who can give evidence and who must give it.

- Eligible means you are lawfully permitted to give evidence. Eligibility applies to witnesses for both the prosecution and the defence.
- Compellable means you can be required to give evidence even if you do not want to. Once you step into the witness box and take the oath, you are compellable — you must answer the questions put to you.
The starting point under s71 of the Evidence Act 2006 is simple: everyone is eligible, and everyone who is eligible is compellable.

**LEGISLATION**
> Evidence Act 2006 — s71
> 71  Eligibility and compellability generally
> (1)  In a civil or criminal proceeding,—
> (a)  any person is eligible to give evidence; and
> (b)  a person who is eligible to give evidence is compellable to give that evidence.
> (2)  Subsection (1) is subject to sections 72 to 75.

**Key point: **The Evidence Act 2006 removed old common law barriers to eligibility. Age and mental disability are no longer grounds to exclude a witness as ineligible. It also abolished the rule that a defendant's spouse could not be compelled to testify.

**Example — eligibility and compellability in practice**

You are investigating an assault. A 10-year-old child witnessed the attack. Under s71, that child is eligible to give evidence — their age does not make them ineligible. If the child is reluctant to testify, they can generally be compelled. The child's evidence will be given on oath or affirmation in a form appropriate to their understanding.

Some witnesses may be capable of being compelled even if they lack the capacity to give fully rational evidence. If their testimony is incoherent, it may be excluded as irrelevant under s7 of the Evidence Act 2006, or under the general exclusion in s8. But that is a separate question from compellability.

### Excusing a compellable witness
Even a witness who is eligible and compellable can be excused by a judge from giving evidence in criminal proceedings if there is just cause — under s165 of the Criminal Procedure Act 2011. This provision takes precedence over s71.

A witness can also be excused from answering specific questions — not because they are not compellable, but because of a privilege that protects certain types of information. Privileges are covered later in this chapter.

***Example — excused from testifying:***

A witness in a serious assault trial is eligible and compellable. However, they are suffering from acute anxiety disorder and giving evidence would cause serious harm to their mental health. The judge has the discretion under s165 of the Criminal Procedure Act 2011 to excuse that witness from testifying for just cause.

### Exceptions to the general rule (ss72–75)
Sections 72 to 75 of the Evidence Act 2006 set out four specific exceptions to the general rule that everyone is eligible and compellable.

### Judges, jurors, and counsel — s72
If someone is acting as a judge in a proceeding, they are not eligible to give evidence in that same proceeding. A judge cannot step from the bench to the witness box.

A juror or counsel in a proceeding is also ineligible to give evidence in that proceeding — unless the judge gives permission. If a juror is given permission to testify, they would first be discharged from the jury, and the trial would continue with the remaining jurors.

One practical point: if the defendant is acting as their own counsel, they do not need the judge's permission to give evidence.

**LEGISLATION**
> Evidence Act 2006 — s72
> 72  Eligibility of Judges, jurors, and counsel
> (1)  A person who is acting as a Judge in a proceeding is not eligible to give evidence in that proceeding.
> (2)  A person who is acting as a juror or counsel in a proceeding is not eligible to give evidence in that proceeding except with the permission of the Judge.
> (3)  In this section, counsel includes an employment advocate.

**Example — counsel giving evidence**

A defence lawyer is conducting a trial when it becomes apparent they witnessed a relevant event. Under s72, that lawyer cannot give evidence in the same proceeding they are conducting as counsel. If their evidence is genuinely needed, the defence would need to instruct new counsel and apply to the judge for the original lawyer to testify.

### Defendants and associated defendants — s73
A defendant in a criminal proceeding is eligible to give evidence — but they are not compellable. You cannot force the defendant to take the witness stand. This protects the right to silence. If the defendant chooses to give evidence, they do so voluntarily.

The same rule applies to associated defendants — people charged with related offending. An associated defendant is someone prosecuted for an offence arising from the same events as the defendant's offence, or for an offence that relates to or is connected with it.

**LEGISLATION**
> Evidence Act 2006 — s73
> 73  Compellability of defendants and associated defendants in criminal proceedings
> (1)  A defendant in a criminal proceeding is not a compellable witness for the prosecution or the defence in that proceeding.
> (2)  An associated defendant is not compellable to give evidence for or against a defendant in a criminal proceeding unless—
> (a)  the associated defendant is being tried separately from the defendant; or
> (b)  the proceeding against the associated defendant has been determined.
> (3)  A proceeding has been determined for the purposes of subsection (2) if—
> (a)  the proceeding has been stayed or the charge against the associated defendant has been withdrawn or dismissed; or
> (b)  the associated defendant has been acquitted of the offence; or
> (c)  the associated defendant, having pleaded guilty to, or having been found guilty of, the offence, has been sentenced or otherwise dealt with for that offence.
> (4)  In this section, associated defendant, in relation to a defendant in a criminal proceeding, means a person against whom a prosecution has been instituted for—
> (a)  an offence that arose in relation to the same events as did the offence
> for which the defendant is being prosecuted; or
> (b)  an offence that relates to, or is connected with, the offence for which the defendant is being prosecuted.

The key distinction for associated defendants is whether their case is still running alongside the defendant's:

- If the associated defendant is a co-defendant being tried together — non-compellable. You cannot call them as a witness in the same trial.
- If the associated defendant is being tried separately — compellable for both the prosecution and defence in the defendant's trial.
- If the associated defendant's proceeding has been determined (stayed, withdrawn, acquitted, or sentenced) — also compellable.
**Key point: **An associated defendant's evidence may attract a warning under s122 of the Evidence Act 2006 if they give evidence about a co-defendant. Section 73 deals only with compellability — not whether the evidence is reliable once given.

***Example — associated defendant:***

You are investigating a burglary. Two people are charged: one with the burglary itself, and one with receiving the stolen goods. Both are associated defendants under s73. If they are tried together, neither is compellable as a witness at the other's trial. But if the receiving charge is disposed of first — say the person is convicted and sentenced — that person is now compellable to give evidence at the burglar's subsequent trial.

### High-status persons — s74
Certain persons are not compellable to give evidence, regardless of whether they are eligible. Under s74, none of the following can be compelled to testify:

- the Sovereign (the King or Queen)
- the Governor-General
- a Sovereign or Head of State of a foreign country
- a judge, in respect of their conduct as a judge
**LEGISLATION**
> Evidence Act 2006 — s74
> 74  Compellability of Sovereign and certain other persons
> None of the following persons is compellable to give evidence:
> (a)  the Sovereign:
> (b)  the Governor-General:
> (c)  a Sovereign or Head of State of a foreign country:
> (d)  a Judge, in respect of the Judge's conduct as a Judge.

Note that a judge's non-compellability under s74 applies only to their conduct as a judge. In any other capacity — for example, as a witness to an event they observed privately — a judge may be compellable.

### Bank officers — s75
Where a bank is not a party to a proceeding, no bank officer is compellable to produce banking records or appear as a witness to prove the matters recorded in those records — provided the contents of those records can be proved through the business records exception to the hearsay rule under s19.

**LEGISLATION**
> EVIDENCE ACT 2006 (Section 75)
> 75  Bank officer not compellable to produce banking records
> (1)  In any proceedings to which a bank is not a party, no officer of the bank is
> compellable—
> (a)  to produce any banking record of the bank, the contents of which can be
> proved under section 19; or
> (b)  to appear as a witness to prove the matters, transactions, and amounts
> recorded in those records.
> (2)  Subsection (1) is subject to any contrary order of a Judge made for a special
> reason.
> (3)  In this section, bank means—
> (a)  a registered bank within the meaning of section 2 of the Banking
> (Prudential Supervision) Act 1989:
> (b)  the Reserve Bank of New Zealand:
> (c)  any other person carrying on in New Zealand the business of banking.

This provision avoids unnecessary disruption to banking operations. If you need banking records in a fraud investigation, you can obtain them and prove their contents under the s19 business records rule — without compelling a bank employee to come to court and testify about them. A judge can override this protection if there is a special reason.

***Example — bank records in a fraud investigation:***

You are investigating a fraud. You obtain bank records showing large unexplained transfers. Because the bank is not a party to the proceeding, you do not need to call a bank officer to give evidence about those records. You can prove the records' contents through the business records exception under s19 instead. A bank officer only needs to attend if the judge makes a special order requiring it.

### Introduction to privilege
Even when a witness is eligible and compellable, they may still be able to refuse to answer particular questions — or be prevented from doing so — on the grounds of privilege.

A privilege is the right to refuse to disclose, or to prevent the disclosure of, what would otherwise be admissible evidence. The court withholds certain information not because it is irrelevant, but because the public interest in protecting specific relationships outweighs the interest in having all relevant evidence before the court.

Privilege can arise from three sources:

- the content of the evidence — what the information is
- the class of the evidence — what category it belongs to
- the nature of a particular relationship — for example, lawyer and client, or doctor and patient
**Key point: **The key test: the public interest that justifies the privilege must be significant enough to outweigh the court's interest in having all relevant evidence. The most common justification is protecting confidential relationships that society depends on.

### Types of privilege under the Evidence Act 2006
The Evidence Act 2006 recognises the following specific privileges:

- Communications with legal advisers — s54
- Solicitors' trust accounts — s55
- Preparatory materials for proceedings — s56
- Settlement negotiations or mediation — s57
- Communications with ministers of religion — s58
- Information obtained by medical practitioners and clinical psychologists — s59
- Privilege against self-incrimination — s60
- Informer privilege — s64
**Note:** there is no longer a marital privilege under the Evidence Act 2006. If a party wants protection from disclosure in a marital context, this is assessed under s69 — the overriding discretion as to confidential information.

## SUMMARY
Use this section as a quick reference. Each entry is a key term from this chapter and what it means in practice.

**Eligible: **Lawfully permitted to give evidence. Under s71 of the Evidence Act 2006, all people are eligible unless an exception applies under ss72–75.

**Compellable: **Required to give evidence even if unwilling. Once in the witness box and sworn in, you must answer the questions put to you.

**s71 — General rule: **Everyone is eligible and everyone who is eligible is compellable. The Evidence Act 2006 abolished common law barriers based on age or mental disability.

**s72 — Judges, jurors, counsel: **Not eligible to give evidence in their own proceeding. Jurors and counsel need the judge's permission. Judges acting as a judge are also not compellable under s74.

**s73 — Defendants and associated defendants: **Eligible but not compellable in their own criminal proceeding. Associated defendants become compellable once their case is determined or they are tried separately.

**Associated defendant: **A person prosecuted for an offence arising from the same events as the defendant's offence, or for a related/connected offence. Defined in s73(4).

**s74 — High-status persons: **The Sovereign, Governor-General, foreign Heads of State, and judges (in respect of their judicial conduct) are not compellable.

**s75 — Bank officers: **Not compellable to produce banking records or testify about them when the bank is not a party and the records can be proved under the s19 business records rule.

**s165 Criminal Procedure Act 2011 — Excused from testifying: **Even a compellable witness can be excused by a judge for just cause. This overrides s71.

**Privilege: **The right to refuse to disclose — or prevent disclosure of — information that would otherwise be admissible. Protects important confidential relationships (e.g. lawyer–client, doctor–patient). Recognised under ss54–64 of the Evidence Act 2006.

# PRIVILEGE
### What is privilege?
Even when you are eligible and compellable as a witness — meaning you are lawfully required to give evidence — you may still be able to refuse to answer certain questions. Privilege is the legal right that allows this. It gives you the right to refuse to disclose, or to prevent the disclosure of, information that would otherwise be admissible.

Privilege arises in three ways: from the content of the evidence itself, from the type of evidence it is, or because of a particular relationship between the people involved.

Because privilege means withholding information from the court, the interest being protected must be significant — it must outweigh the value of having all relevant evidence before the court. The most common justification is preserving important social relationships that only function because the people in them can speak confidentially. Legal advice, spiritual counsel, and medical treatment are all examples where confidentiality is essential to the relationship itself.

***Example: Why privilege matters***A suspect tells their lawyer exactly what happened on the night of the offence. That communication is privileged. You, as the investigating officer, cannot compel the lawyer to reveal what their client told them — even if it would help prove the case. The privilege is absolute because without it, people could not speak openly with their lawyers, and the justice system depends on people being able to do exactly that.

### Effect and protection of privilege — s53
Section 53 of the Evidence Act 2006 sets out how privilege works in practice. It tells you what a privilege holder can refuse to disclose, and gives the court power to protect privileged material even when it has ended up in the wrong hands.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 53 — Evidence Act 2006: Effect and protection of privilege
> 53  Effect and protection of privilege
> (1) A person who has a privilege conferred by any of sections 54 to 59 in respect of a communication or any information has the right to refuse to disclose in a proceeding—
> (a) the communication; and
> (b) the information, including any information contained in the  communication; and
> (c) any opinion formed by a person that is based on the communication or information.
> (2) A person who has a privilege conferred by section 60 or 64 in respect of information has the right to refuse to disclose in a proceeding the information.
> (3) A person who has a privilege conferred by any of sections 54 to 59 and 64
> in respect of a communication, information, opinion, or document may require that the communication, information, opinion, or document not be disclosed in a proceeding—
> (a) by the person to whom the communication is made or the information  is given, or by whom the opinion is given or the information or document is prepared or compiled; or
> (b) by any other person who has come into possession of it with the authority of the person who has the privilege, in confidence and for purposes related to the circumstances that have given rise to the privilege.
> (4) If a communication, information, opinion, or document, in respect of which a person has a privilege conferred by any of sections 54 to 59 and 64, is in the possession of a person other than a person referred to in subsection (3), a Judge may, on the Judge's own initiative or on the application of the person who has the privilege, order that the communication, information, opinion, or document not be disclosed in a proceeding.
> (5) This Act does not affect the general law governing legal professional privilege, so far as it applies to the determination of claims to that privilege that are made neither in the course of, nor for the purpose of, a proceeding.

In practice, s53(4) is important for you as a detective. Before the Evidence Act 2006, there was real uncertainty about whether privilege was lost if privileged material was intercepted without authorisation — for example, if a privileged letter was stolen or a phone call was covertly recorded. Section 53(4) resolves that: the judge can order that the material not be disclosed, even if it is now in someone else's possession.

When you encounter a claim of privilege, check what type of material is involved. Different privileges protect different things — some protect 'communications', others protect 'information'. This matters because it determines the scope of what cannot be disclosed.

## Types of privilege under the Evidence Act 2006
The Evidence Act 2006 recognises the following privileges:

- Communications with legal advisers — s54
- Solicitors' trust accounts — s55
- Preparatory materials for proceedings — s56
- Settlement negotiations or mediation — s57
- Communications with ministers of religion — s58
- Information obtained by medical practitioners and clinical psychologists — s59
- Privilege against self-incrimination — s60
- Informer privilege — s64
There is no longer any marital privilege in New Zealand. If a person seeks protection for confidential communications with a spouse or partner, the court may consider this under s69, which gives a judge an overriding discretion to deal with confidential information.

## Legal professional privilege
Legal professional privilege is one of the most fundamental protections in the justice system. In R v Derby Magistrates' Court ex p B [1996] 1AC 487 it was described as 'more than an ordinary rule of evidence … it is a fundamental condition on which the administration of justice as a whole rests.' In New Zealand, as confirmed in B v Auckland District Law Society [2004] 1 NZLR 326, legal privilege takes primacy over all other competing public interests. A lawyer must be able to give their client an absolute and unconditional assurance that what the client reveals will not be disclosed without consent.

Under the Evidence Act 2006, legal professional privilege has two main forms: privilege for communications with legal advisers (s54), and privilege for preparatory materials for proceedings (s56).

### Communications with legal advisers — s54
Section 54 protects communications between you and your legal adviser when you are seeking or receiving professional legal services. If you consult a lawyer about a matter, what you say to them — and what they say to you — is privileged, provided the communication was intended to be confidential.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 54 — Evidence Act 2006: Privilege for communications with legal advisers
> 54  Privilege for communications with legal advisers
> (1) A person who requests or obtains professional legal services from a legal adviser has a privilege in respect of any communication between the person and the legal adviser if the communication was—
> (a) intended to be confidential; and
> (b) made in the course of and for the purpose of—
> (i)  the person requesting or obtaining professional legal services from the legal adviser; or
> (ii) the legal adviser giving such services to the person.
> (1A) The privilege applies to a person who requests professional legal services from a legal adviser whether or not the person actually obtains such services.

As a detective, you need to understand the limits of this privilege. There are five key points:

- The communication must be intended to be confidential.
- The communication must be for the purpose of obtaining or giving legal services. This includes communications through authorised representatives of either the client or the legal adviser.
- The privilege belongs to the person seeking or receiving the legal services. However, even if they waive it, a judge may still order under s52(1) that the evidence must not be given.
- The privilege does not protect communications made for a dishonest purpose, or to enable or assist anyone to commit or plan to commit an offence. If a suspect uses their lawyer to help plan further crimes, that is not protected.
- If the communication was intended to be confidential, the privilege is not automatically lost just because someone inadvertently overheard it — even if no steps were taken to preserve confidentiality. Section 53(4) allows a judge to order that the overheard material not be disclosed.
**Example: Legal adviser privilege in a fraud investigation**You are investigating a serious fraud offence. During the investigation, you discover that the suspect had several meetings with their lawyer before the alleged offending. You want to know what was discussed.You cannot compel the lawyer to tell you what the client said, and you cannot use a search warrant to seize notes of those meetings — the communications are protected by s54.However, if you have evidence that the meetings were for the purpose of planning the fraud itself, the privilege does not apply. You would need to put that argument to the judge.

### Preparatory materials for proceedings — s56
Section 56 extends legal professional privilege to materials prepared for the dominant purpose of getting ready for a proceeding or an apprehended proceeding. This is what used to be called 'litigation privilege' under the old law.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 56 — Evidence Act 2006: Privilege for preparatory materials for proceedings
> 56  Privilege for preparatory materials for proceedings
> (1) Subsection (2) applies to a communication or information only if the communication or information is made, received, compiled, or prepared for the dominant purpose of preparing for a proceeding or an apprehended proceeding (the proceeding).
> (2) A person (the party) who is, or on reasonable grounds contemplates becoming, a party to the proceeding has a privilege in respect of—
> (a) a communication between the party and any other person:
> (b) a communication between the party's legal adviser and any other person:
> (c) information compiled or prepared by the party or the party's legal adviser:
> (d) information compiled or prepared at the request of the party, or the party's legal adviser, by any other person.

There are five things to understand about this privilege:

- It applies to any communication or information made, received, compiled, or prepared for the dominant purpose of preparing for a proceeding or an apprehended proceeding.
- You have this privilege if you are — or on reasonable grounds contemplate becoming — a party to the proceeding.
- The privilege covers communications between you and any other person, communications between your legal adviser and any other person, and information compiled or prepared by you or your legal adviser.
- Communications will still be protected if they were carried out by an 'authorised representative' of the privilege holder or their legal adviser.
- Documents that are not privileged in themselves can become privileged once they are compiled together, because the compilation itself may reveal litigation tactics.
***Example: Preparatory materials***A defendant is charged with burglary. Their lawyer commissions a report from a private investigator to assist in preparing the defence case. That report is prepared for the dominant purpose of the proceeding and is privileged under s56. You cannot compel the lawyer or the investigator to produce it.

### Privilege for communications with ministers of religion — s58
Section 58 of the Evidence Act 2006 protects confidential communications between a person and a minister of religion. The section is broader than the previous law — it covers all religious and spiritual communications, whether or not they involve atonement for sin, and regardless of whether they take place within an organised religious community.

The focus is on communications seeking spiritual advice, benefit, or comfort. The section does not extend to communities that do not depend on belief in some god, divine force, or other spiritual basis for life.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 58 — Evidence Act 2006: Privilege for communications with ministers of religion
> 58  Privilege for communications with ministers of religion
> (1) A person has a privilege in respect of any communication between that person and a minister of religion if the communication was—
> (a) made in confidence to or by the minister in the minister's capacity as a minister of religion; and
> (b) made for the purpose of the person obtaining or receiving from the minister religious or spiritual advice, benefit, or comfort.
> (2) A person is a minister of religion for the purposes of this section if the person has a status within a church or other religious or spiritual community that requires or calls for that person—
> (a) to receive confidential communications of the kind described in
> subsection (1); and
> (b) to respond with religious or spiritual advice, benefit, or comfort.

For this privilege to apply, you need to establish the following:

- The communication must be made in confidence and within the minister's capacity as a minister of religion.
- The person must be at least partly motivated by their own religious or spiritual beliefs or practice to seek the minister out.
- The person must be seeking spiritual advice, benefit, or comfort — not just a general conversation.
- The communication must occur personally between the privilege holder and the minister.
The definition of 'minister of religion' goes beyond traditionally ordained clergy. It extends to any person who plays a pastoral role in a religious or spiritual community, including certain kaumātua in Māori communities whose role is to provide spiritual guidance and advice.

Where the communication was made for a dishonest purpose or to enable or assist an offence, the judge must disallow the privilege.

If a communication with a minister does not meet the s58 requirements, it may still attract protection under the court's overriding discretion to deal with confidential information under s69.

***Example: Minister of religion privilege***You are investigating an assault where the suspect told their local priest during a confession that they had hurt someone. You want the priest to give evidence about what was said.The confession is privileged under s58. It was made in confidence, in the minister's capacity as a minister of religion, for the purpose of receiving spiritual comfort. The priest cannot be compelled to reveal the communication.

### Privilege for medical practitioners and clinical psychologists — s59
Section 59 provides a limited privilege in criminal proceedings for information disclosed to, or obtained by, a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist. The key word is 'limited' — this privilege only applies in criminal proceedings, and only in specific circumstances.

The purpose of s59 is to encourage people with drug dependency or conditions that may lead to criminal behaviour to seek treatment, without fear that what they tell their doctor or psychologist will be used against them in a criminal proceeding.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 59 — Evidence Act 2006: Privilege in criminal proceedings for information obtained by medical practitioners and clinical psychologists
> 59  Privilege in criminal proceedings for information obtained by medical practitioners and clinical psychologists
> (1) This section—
> (a) applies to a person who consults or is examined by a medical practitioner or a clinical psychologist for drug dependency or any other condition or behaviour that may manifest itself in criminal conduct; but
> (b) does not apply in the case of a person who has been required by an order of a Judge, or by other lawful authority, to submit himself or herself to the medical practitioner or clinical psychologist for any examination, test, or for any other purpose.
> (1A) For the purpose of applying subsection (1)(b), there is no privilege under this section in relation to any communication or information (other than any previous medical record or other previous medical information about the person) that is made or obtained for the purpose of the examination or test or for the other purpose concerned.
> (2) A person has a privilege in a criminal proceeding in respect of any communication made by the person to a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist that the person believes is necessary to enable the practitioner to examine, treat, or care for the person for drug dependency or any other condition or behaviour that may manifest itself in criminal conduct.
> (3) A person has a privilege in a criminal proceeding in respect of information obtained by a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist as a result of consulting with or examining the person to enable the practitioner to examine, treat, or care for the person for drug dependency or any other condition or behaviour that may manifest itself in criminal conduct.
> (4) A person has a privilege in a criminal proceeding in respect of information consisting of a prescription, or notes of a prescription, for treatment prescribed by a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist as a result of consulting with or examining the person.
> (5) A reference in this section to a communication to, or information obtained by, a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist includes a reference to a communication to, or information obtained by, a person acting in a professional capacity on behalf of a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist in the course of the examination or treatment of, or care for, the person.

### How s59 works in practice
The privilege applies to communications the patient makes to the practitioner, and to information the practitioner obtains from treating the patient. It does not work the other way — what the practitioner tells the patient is not protected.

The privilege extends to disclosures made to a practitioner's representative (for example, a nurse or a member of the treatment team acting on behalf of the doctor or psychologist). However, the patient must make the disclosure personally — if the patient's representative discloses something to the practitioner, that is not protected by s59.

The privilege does not apply if the examination was court-ordered or required by lawful authority. If a judge orders someone to undergo a psychiatric assessment, there is no privilege over what arises from that assessment.

The privilege also does not attach to information obtained during treatment for a condition that results from unlawful conduct itself — for example, if someone seeks treatment for a wound and in doing so discloses that the wound was received while committing a crime.

Any person involved in a criminal proceeding may invoke this privilege — not only defendants. A witness called by the prosecution could claim it.

If the communication or information falls outside s59, it may still attract protection under the court's overriding discretion to deal with confidential information under s69.

***Example: Medical privilege in a criminal proceeding***A person is being prosecuted for burglary. They are also a methamphetamine user and have been seeing a clinical psychologist for drug dependency treatment. During sessions, they discussed their drug use and admitted to committing offences to fund their addiction.You want to call the psychologist as a witness to give evidence of those admissions. The psychologist can refuse — those communications are privileged under s59(2). The person voluntarily consulted the psychologist for drug dependency treatment and the admissions were made in that context.However, if the psychologist examination was court-ordered as part of sentencing, there is no privilege over what was said during that examination (s59(1)(b)).

### Privilege against self-incrimination — s60
The privilege against self-incrimination is a fundamental legal protection. The Evidence Act 2006 defines self-incrimination as 'the provision by a person of information that could reasonably lead to, or increase the likelihood of, the prosecution of that person for a criminal offence' (s4). This covers not just direct admissions to criminal acts, but also information that forms a link in the chain of proving such acts.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 60 — Evidence Act 2006: Privilege against self-incrimination60  Privilege against self-incrimination(1) This section applies if—    (a) a person is (apart from this section) required to provide specific information—        (i)   in the course of a proceeding; or        (ii)  by a person exercising a statutory power or duty; or        (iii) by a Police officer or other person holding a public in the course of an investigation into a criminal offence or possible criminal offence; and    (b) the information would, if so provided, be likely to incriminate the        person under New Zealand law for an offence punishable by a fine or        imprisonment.(2) The person—    (a) has a privilege in respect of the information and cannot be require to provide it; and    (b) cannot be prosecuted or penalised for refusing or failing to provide        the information, whether or not the person claimed the privilege when        the person refused or failed to provide the information.(3) Subsection (2) has effect—    (a) unless an enactment removes the privilege against self-incrimination        either expressly or by necessary implication; and    (b) to the extent that an enactment does not expressly or by necessary        implication remove the privilege against self-incrimination.(4) Subsection (2) does not enable a claim of privilege to be made—    (a) on behalf of a body corporate; or    (b) on behalf of any person other than the person required to provide        the information (except by a legal adviser on behalf of a client        who is so required); or    (c) by a defendant in a criminal proceeding when giving evidence about        the matter for which the defendant is being tried.

### When the privilege applies
The privilege applies when a person is compelled to provide specific information — and that information would be likely to incriminate them under New Zealand law for an offence punishable by a fine or imprisonment. This is directly relevant to you as a detective: s60(1)(a)(iii) specifically names a Police officer conducting an investigation as one of the situations in which the privilege can be invoked.

The word 'likely' in s60(1)(b) limits the privilege to situations where the risk of incrimination is real and appreciable — not merely imaginary or fanciful. This was confirmed in Singh v R [2010] NZSC 161 at [31], quoting Cooke J in Busby v Thorn EMI Video Programmes Ltd [1984] 1 NZLR 461 at 469.

The privilege only applies when the person is compelled to provide specific information — meaning particular items of information in response to specific questions or requests. If there is no compulsion to produce the information, s60 does not apply.

### Limitations on the privilege
There are important limitations you need to know:

- A company cannot claim the privilege. Only natural persons can.
- Employees or officers of a company can claim it for themselves personally, but not on behalf of the company.
- One person cannot claim the privilege on behalf of another person, except where a legal adviser asserts it on behalf of their client.
- A defendant cannot assert the privilege in relation to the offence they are currently on trial for. If a defendant chooses to testify, they can only assert the privilege if the information sought risks conviction for a different offence to the one being tried.
Section 61 of the Act gives a judge a discretion to protect a person from self-incrimination under foreign law.

### Claiming the privilege in court — s62
Section 62 sets out what happens when the privilege is claimed during a court proceeding. If it appears to the judge that a party or witness may have grounds to claim the privilege, the judge must ensure the person is aware of the privilege and its effect.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 62 — Evidence Act 2006: Claiming privilege against self-incrimination in court proceedings
> 62  Claiming privilege against self-incrimination in court proceedings
> (1) If in a court proceeding it appears to the Judge that a party or witness may have grounds to claim a privilege against self-incrimination in respect of specific information required to be provided by that person, the Judge must satisfy himself or herself that the person is aware of the privilege and its effect.
> (2) A person who claims a privilege against self-incrimination in a court proceeding must offer sufficient evidence to enable the Judge to assess whether self-incrimination is reasonably likely if the person provides the required information.

In practice, a person asserting the privilege must provide enough evidence to convince the judge that self-incrimination is reasonably likely — they cannot simply refuse without justification.

***Example: Self-incrimination during a statutory power investigation***You are investigating a possible money laundering offence. Under the Proceeds of Crime Act, a financial intelligence officer requires a person to produce financial records. The person refuses, claiming the privilege against self-incrimination under s60.For the privilege to apply, the person needs to show that providing those records would likely incriminate them for an offence punishable by a fine or imprisonment. If the risk is real and not merely speculative, the privilege applies and they cannot be penalisedfor refusing.

### Informer privilege — s64
Section 64 protects the identity of police informers and undercover officers. The purpose is to encourage the reporting and detection of serious crime, and to protect the safety of those who assist in that work.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 64 — Evidence Act 2006: Informers
> 64  Informers
> (1) An informer has a privilege in respect of information that would
> disclose, or is likely to disclose, the informer's identity.
> (2) A person is an informer for the purposes of this section if the person—
> (a) has supplied, gratuitously or for reward, information to an enforcement agency, or to a representative of an enforcement agency, concerning the possible or actual commission of an offence in circumstances in which the person has a reasonable expectation that his or her identity will not be disclosed; and
> (b) is not called as a witness by the prosecution to give evidence relating to that information.
> (3) An informer may be a member of the Police working undercover.

### Who is an informer?
An informer is someone who has supplied information — whether paid or unpaid — to an enforcement agency about a possible or actual offence, in circumstances where they had a reasonable expectation that their identity would not be disclosed. Undercover Police officers can also be informers for the purposes of this section.

The privilege is lost if the informer is called as a prosecution witness to give evidence about the information they supplied. Once they take the stand in that capacity, their identity is no longer protected.

### Effect of the privilege
The main effect of s64 is to prevent other people from disclosing the privileged information — it stops you, as a detective, from being compelled to reveal an informer's identity on the stand. If the informer is an undercover officer, their identity may also be protected by ss108 and 109 of the Evidence Act 2006.

### When the judge can disallow the privilege
The privilege is not absolute. A judge must disallow it where there is a prima facie case that the information was given for a dishonest purpose, or to help plan or commit an offence.

A judge may also disallow the privilege where they are of the opinion that disclosure is necessary to enable the defendant to present an effective defence. This is a key protection: if concealing the informer's identity would prevent the defendant from mounting a proper defence, justice requires disclosure.

***Example: Informer privilege in a drug case***During a drug trafficking prosecution, defence counsel demands to know the identity of the informer who provided the tip-off that led to the arrest. You are called as a witness and asked to name the informer.You can refuse under s64 — the informer's identity is privileged. However, if defence counsel can show that knowing the informer's identity is essential to mounting an effective defence (for example, to challenge the reliability of the tip-off), the judge may order disclosure.

### Waiving privilege
Privilege belongs to the person it was created to protect. That person can choose to give it up — this is called waiving the privilege. Waiver can be express (the person explicitly gives up the privilege) or implied (the person acts in a way that is inconsistent with keeping the communication confidential).

Waiving privilege ends the privilege holder's rights over the material — including the right to prevent it being disclosed in a proceeding. However, even after a waiver, an interested person can apply under s52 for an order that the material remains inadmissible. This reflects the fact that sometimes a party other than the privilege holder has an interest in preventing the disclosure.

***Example: Waiver of legal privilege***A defendant decides to tell the court what their lawyer advised them. By voluntarily disclosing the communication, they have waived legal privilege over it. The prosecution can now ask questions about what the lawyer said.

# SUMMARY
Use this summary as a study guide for the key concepts in this chapter.

**Privilege: **The right to refuse to disclose — or to prevent the disclosure of — information that would otherwise be admissible in evidence. It arises from the content of the evidence, the type of evidence, or the nature of a particular relationship.

**s53 — Effect and protection: **Sets out what privilege holders can refuse to disclose (the communication, any information it contains, and any opinion based on it). Also gives judges power to order that privileged material not be disclosed even when it has come into unauthorised hands.

**s54 — Legal adviser communications: **Protects confidential communications between a person and their legal adviser made for the purpose of requesting or obtaining professional legal services. Does not protect communications made for dishonest purposes or to help plan an offence.

**s56 — Preparatory materials (dominant purpose): **Protects communications and information compiled or prepared for the dominant purpose of preparing for a proceeding or apprehended proceeding. Key word is 'dominant' — not merely one purpose among several.

**s58 — Minister of religion: **Protects confidential communications with a minister of religion made for the purpose of receiving religious or spiritual advice, benefit, or comfort. Extends to kaumātua and other pastoral figures in Māori and other spiritual communities.

**s59 — Medical / clinical psychology (criminal proceedings only): **Limited privilege in criminal proceedings for communications to, or information obtained by, a medical practitioner or clinical psychologist treating a person for drug dependency or conditions that may manifest as criminal conduct. Does not apply to court-ordered examinations.

**s60 — Self-incrimination: **A person required to provide specific information cannot be compelled to provide it if doing so would likely incriminate them for an offence punishable by fine or imprisonment. Does not apply to companies, or to a defendant testifying about the offence they are being tried for.

**s62 — Claiming self-incrimination in court: **The judge must ensure a person is aware of the privilege before they are required to answer. A person claiming the privilege must show that self-incrimination is reasonably likely — not merely imaginary or fanciful.

**s64 — Informer privilege: **Protects the identity of informers — people who supply information to an enforcement agency with a reasonable expectation of anonymity. Undercover Police officers can be informers. Privilege is lost if the informer is called as a prosecution witness. Judge may disallow where disclosure is necessary for the defendant to present an effective defence.

**Waiver: **Privilege can be waived expressly or impliedly. Once waived, the privilege holder cannot prevent disclosure. However, another interested person may still apply under s52 to have the material excluded.

**No marital privilege: **There is no longer any marital privilege in New Zealand. Confidential communications between spouses or partners may be considered under the court's overriding discretion in s69.

**s69 — Overriding discretion: **A court has an overriding discretion to protect confidential information that does not fall within any formal privilege. Relevant where, for example, a communication with a minister of religion or health professional does not meet the specific requirements of ss58 or 59.

# CONFIDENTIALITY
### Learning objective
When you have completed this section, you should be able to demonstrate an understanding by explaining:

- the purpose of judicial discretion to protect confidential information
- the rule protecting jury deliberations and the exceptions to it
- how the law protects journalists’ sources under s68 of the Evidence Act 2006
- how and when a judge may use the overriding discretion under s69 to prevent disclosure of confidential information
### Overview
Privilege is not the only tool the law uses to protect confidential information. Even where no privilege applies, a judge has a separate discretion under ss68–70 of the Evidence Act 2006 to protect confidential communications or information from being disclosed in court. Section 76 provides additional protection for jury deliberations.

The key difference between privilege and confidentiality protection is this: privilege gives the holder a right to refuse disclosure. Confidentiality protection under ss68–70 does not create that right — instead it allows the judge to weigh up competing interests and decide whether disclosure serves the public good.

## Jury deliberations
### The general rule
As a general rule, no one may give evidence about what happened during jury deliberations. This covers everything that was said or done while the jury was performing its fact-finding function. The rule exists for two reasons: it promotes the finality of verdicts, and it allows jurors to discuss the case freely and frankly without fear that their views will later be exposed.

Section 76 of the Evidence Act 2006 sets out this rule and its exceptions:

**LEGISLATION**
> s76 — Evidence Act 2006: Evidence of jury deliberations
> (1) A person must not give evidence about the deliberations of a jury.
> (2) Subsection (1) does not prevent the giving of evidence about matters that do not form part of the deliberations of a jury, including (without limitation)—
> (a) the competency or capacity of a juror; or
> (b) any conduct of, or knowledge gained by, a juror that is believed to disqualify that juror from holding that position.
> (3) Subsection (1) does not prevent a person from giving evidence about the deliberations of a jury if the Judge is satisfied that the particular circumstances are so exceptional that there is a sufficiently compelling reason to allow that evidence to be given.
> (4) In determining whether to allow evidence under subsection (3), the Judge must weigh—
> (a) the public interest in protecting the confidentiality of jury deliberations generally; and
> (b) the public interest in ensuring that justice is done  in those proceedings.

### Exceptions to the general rule
There are two situations where evidence about jury matters can be given, even though it relates in some way to the jury’s work.

First, you can give evidence about matters that did not form part of the deliberations themselves. This includes evidence about a juror’s competency or capacity to serve, or evidence of conduct or knowledge that would disqualify a juror from their role. An example of the latter is a juror who conducts their own independent inquiries into the facts of the case.

Second, the judge may allow evidence of the deliberations themselves — but only in exceptional circumstances where there is a sufficiently compelling reason. The bar is high. If a party applies for this, the judge must weigh two competing interests:

- the public interest in protecting the confidentiality of jury deliberations generally
- the public interest in ensuring that justice is done in the particular proceedings
***Example:***

After a murder trial ends in conviction, a juror tells a journalist that, during deliberations, another juror made comments suggesting they had personal knowledge of the accused’s prior criminal history. The judge allows limited evidence of what was said during deliberations to investigate whether the conviction should be set aside. The deliberation rule does not prevent this — because the conduct complained of (bias or prior knowledge) goes beyond ordinary deliberation and there is a sufficiently compelling reason to allow the evidence.

## Protection of journalists’ sources
### The general rule
If a journalist has promised an informant that their identity will not be disclosed, neither the journalist nor their employer can be compelled to answer questions or produce documents that would reveal that identity. This protection exists under s68 of the Evidence Act 2006. It supports freedom of the press by allowing journalists to maintain the trust of their sources.

**LEGISLATION**
> s68 — Evidence Act 2006: Protection of journalists’ sources
> (1) If a journalist has promised an informant not to disclose the informant's identity, neither the journalist nor his or her employer is compellable in a civil or criminal proceeding to answer any question or produce any document that would disclose the identity of the informant or enable that identity to be discovered.
> (2) A Judge of the High Court may order that subsection (1) is not to apply if satisfied by a party to a civil or criminal proceeding that, having regard to the issues to be determined in that proceeding, the public interest in the disclosure of evidence of the identity of the
> informant outweighs—
> (a) any likely adverse effect of the disclosure on the informant or any other person; and
> (b) the public interest in the communication of facts and opinion to the public by the news media and, accordingly also, in the ability of the news media to access sources of facts.
> (5) In this section—
> informant means a person who gives information to a journalist in the normal course of the journalist's work in the expectation that the information may be published in a news medium. journalist means a person who in the normal course of that person's work may be given information by an informant in the expectation that the information may be published in a news medium.

### When the protection can be overridden
The s68 protection is not absolute. A High Court judge can order that the protection does not apply if a party to proceedings satisfies the court that the public interest in disclosing the identity of the source outweighs both:

- any likely adverse effect of disclosure on the informant or any other person, and
- the public interest in the news media being able to communicate facts and opinion to the public — and in particular, in the ability of the media to access sources of information.
Importantly, nothing in s68 prevents the journalist or their employer from choosing to disclose the identity voluntarily. The protection exists to prevent compulsion — not to prevent willing disclosure. In New Zealand criminal proceedings, the definition of “public interest in disclosure” explicitly includes the defendant’s right to present an effective defence.

***Example:***

A journalist publishes a story about corruption within a local council, based on information from an anonymous insider. You are investigating the matter and want to identify the source to corroborate the story. You cannot compel the journalist to reveal their identity. However, defence counsel in a connected prosecution could apply to the High Court for an order under s68(2) if they could show that knowing the source’s identity is necessary for the defendant to mount an effective defence.

## Overriding discretion as to confidential information
### What s69 does
Section 69 of the Evidence Act 2006 gives a judge a broad, flexible power to prevent confidential information from being disclosed in court. It does not require a special relationship to exist between the parties. What matters is whether the information is confidential — and whether the public interest in keeping it confidential outweighs the public interest in having it disclosed.

A direction under s69 can cover any one or more of the following:

- a confidential communication
- any confidential information
- any information that would or might reveal a confidential source of information
**LEGISLATION**
> s69 — Evidence Act 2006: Overriding discretion as to confidential information
> (1) A direction under this section is a direction that any 1 or more of the following not be disclosed in a proceeding:
> (a) a confidential communication
> (b) any confidential information
> (c) any information that would or might reveal a confidential source of information.
> (2) A Judge may give a direction under this section if the Judge considers that the public interest in the disclosure in the proceeding of the communication or
> information is outweighed by the public interest in—
> (a) preventing harm to a person by whom, about whom, or on whose behalf the confidential information was obtained, recorded, or prepared or to whom it was communicated; or
> (b) preventing harm to—
> (i) the particular relationship in the course of which the confidential communication or confidential information was made, obtained, recorded, or prepared; or
> (ii) relationships of the same kind as, or of a kind similar to, the relationship in (i); or
> (c) maintaining activities that contribute to or rely on the free flow of information.
> (3) When considering whether to give a direction, the Judge must have regard to—
> (a) the likely extent of harm from disclosure
> (b) the nature and likely importance of the information in the proceeding
> (c) the nature of the proceeding
> (d) the availability of other means of obtaining the evidence
> (e) the availability of means to restrict public disclosure of the evidence if it is given
> (f) the sensitivity of the evidence, having regard to—
> (i) the time elapsed since the communication made or the information compiled or prepared
> (ii) the extent to which the information has already been disclosed to other persons
> (g) society's interest in protecting the privacy of victims of offences and, in particular, victims of sexual offences.
> (4) The Judge may also have regard to any other matter that the Judge considers relevant.
> (5) A Judge may give a direction under this section whether or not the communication or information is privileged by another provision of this subpart or would, except for limitation or restriction, be privileged.

### How the judge decides
When deciding whether to give a direction, the judge must weigh the public interest in disclosure against the public interest in protection. The judge can act on their own initiative or on application by an “interested person”. Unlike privilege, you do not need to show a special type of relationship to seek this protection — the focus is on whether the information is genuinely confidential and what harm disclosure would cause.

The seven factors the judge must consider (s69(3)) are:

- the likely extent of harm from disclosure
- the nature of the information and how important it is to the proceedings
- the nature of the proceedings
- whether there are other ways to obtain the evidence
- whether there are ways to limit public disclosure if the evidence is given
- the sensitivity of the evidence — including how much time has passed since it was created, and how widely it has already been shared
- society’s interest in protecting the privacy of victims of offences, especially victims of sexual offences
The judge can also take into account any other relevant matter (s69(4)). A direction can be given even where the information is already privileged under another provision of the Act, or would be privileged but for some limitation or restriction (s69(5)).

***Example:***

A sexual assault complainant disclosed details of the assault to a friend in a private message. The defence seeks to have the message produced as evidence to challenge her account. The friend does not hold any formal privilege but the communication was clearly confidential. The judge may give a direction under s69 preventing disclosure if, on weighing the factors in s69(3), the public interest in protecting the complainant’s privacy and the integrity of the confidential communication outweighs the probative value of the message in the trial.

## SUMMARY
**Confidentiality protection: **Separate from privilege. Sections 68–70 of the Evidence Act 2006 give judges discretion to protect confidential communications and information from disclosure in court. No special relationship is required.

**Jury deliberations (s76): **No one may give evidence about jury deliberations. Exceptions apply for matters outside the deliberations (e.g. juror misconduct), and for truly exceptional circumstances where the judge is satisfied there is a sufficiently compelling reason. The judge must weigh the public interest in protecting deliberations against the public interest in justice being done.

**Protection of journalists’ sources (s68): **A journalist who has promised not to disclose a source’s identity cannot be compelled to answer questions or produce documents that would reveal it. A High Court judge can override this protection if the public interest in disclosure outweighs the adverse effects on the informant and the public interest in press freedom. The journalist can always choose to disclose voluntarily.

**Overriding discretion — s69: **The judge can direct that a confidential communication, confidential information, or information revealing a confidential source not be disclosed. The test is whether the public interest in disclosure is outweighed by the public interest in: (a) preventing harm to a person, (b) preventing harm to the relationship, or (c) maintaining the free flow of information. Seven specific factors must be weighed under s69(3). A s69 direction can be given even where the information is already privileged.

# CORROBORATION
## What is corroboration?
### The concept
Corroboration is independent evidence that tends to confirm or support a fact that other evidence already establishes — and that implicates the accused in the offence charged.

*"Corroboration"* is not defined in the Evidence Act 2006. The definition above comes from the common law, and it remains the working definition you apply in practice.

The key word is independent. Evidence does not corroborate if it comes from the same source as the evidence it is meant to support. To count as corroboration, the evidence must come from a separate source and must point toward the accused’s guilt.

***Example:***

A colleague tells you she saw the accused take money from the till. That is her testimony. CCTV footage showing the accused at the till at the same time, taken from a separate system, is independent evidence — it confirms her account and implicates the accused. That is corroboration.

By contrast, if she told a second colleague what she saw, and that colleague repeats it in court, that is not corroboration — it traces back to the same original source.

## The general rule: corroboration is not required
### One witness is enough
In most criminal proceedings, the prosecution does not need corroboration. A single witness’s testimony can be enough to prove a case — if the court is satisfied it is reliable and accurate and meets the required standard of proof.

This does not mean the court will always act on uncorroborated evidence. It simply means the court may do so, when it is sufficiently satisfied about the quality and credibility of what it has heard.

Section 121 of the Evidence Act 2006 sets this out:

What this means for you: in the vast majority of investigations you carry out, corroboration is not a legal requirement. A credible, reliable witness can establish a case on their own.

## When corroboration is required by law
### The four exceptions
There are four offences where the unsupported evidence of a single witness is not enough to support a conviction. Corroboration is required as a matter of law for:

- perjury (s108 of the Crimes Act 1961)
- false oaths (s110 of the Crimes Act 1961)
- false statements or declarations (s111 of the Crimes Act 1961)
- treason (s73 of the Crimes Act 1961)
All four involve offences where the legislature has decided that the serious consequences of a conviction demand additional safeguards. For perjury and the related offences, this reflects the particular difficulty of proving that someone deliberately lied under oath on the basis of a single witness’s word alone.

***Example:***

You are investigating a complaint that a witness lied under oath during a trial. Even if the complainant gives compelling evidence that the witness told them something completely different beforehand, that testimony alone is not enough to convict for perjury. You need independent corroborating evidence — for example, a written record, CCTV, or another witness who can confirm the inconsistency.

## Warning the jury about uncorroborated evidence
### No automatic warning required
Section 121(2) removes any automatic requirement to warn a jury about the dangers of acting on uncorroborated evidence. Judges are not required to give a general corroboration warning — except in the perjury and treason cases covered by s121(1).

### But a warning is still possible
The judge still has discretion to warn the jury when they believe evidence in a case may be unreliable. This power comes from s122 of the Evidence Act 2006.

Under s122, if the judge forms the view that admissible evidence may nonetheless be unreliable, they may warn the jury to take care when deciding whether to accept it and how much weight to give it.

This is not a mandatory warning — it is a discretionary tool. The judge may use it where, for example, a witness has a possible motive to give false evidence, or where the evidence involves a statement made by the accused while in custody.

So while the old-style mandatory corroboration warning is gone, it is still open to the judge to direct the jury to exercise special care in cases where evidence is uncorroborated or a witness may be of doubtful reliability.

## Child complainants
### Equal treatment — no lower standard
Section 125(1) provides important protection for child complainants. It prohibits a judge from giving a corroboration warning about a child complainant’s evidence if the judge would not have given that warning had the complainant been an adult.

The purpose is straightforward: a child’s evidence should not be treated as inherently less reliable just because they are a child. The test for whether a warning is appropriate is the same, regardless of age.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 125 — Evidence Act 2006: Judicial directions about children's evidence
> (1) In a criminal proceeding tried with a jury in which the complainant is a child at the time when the proceeding commences, the Judge must not give any warning to the jury about the absence of corroboration of the evidence of the complainant if the Judge would not have given that kind of a warning had the complainant been an adult.
> (2) In a proceeding tried with a jury in which a witness is a child, the Judge must not, unless expert evidence is given in that proceeding supporting the giving of the following direction or the making of the following comment:
> (a) instruct the jury that there is a need to scrutinise the evidence of children generally with special care; or
> (b) suggest to the jury that children generally have tendencies to invent or distort.

Section 125(2) goes further. Unless expert evidence supports it, the judge cannot direct the jury to scrutinise children’s evidence with special care, and cannot suggest to the jury that children tend to make things up or distort what they have seen.

What this means for you as a detective: when you are investigating a case with a child complainant, the law treats their evidence by the same standard as adult evidence. You should not approach a child’s account with automatic scepticism, and neither will the court.

***Example:***

You are investigating a sexual offending case. The complainant is a 10-year-old child. At trial, the defence requests that the judge warn the jury to take special care with the child’s uncorroborated testimony.

The judge must ask: would I give this warning if the complainant were an adult? If the answer is no — because there is nothing else about the evidence that makes it unreliable — the warning cannot be given. The child’s age alone is not a reason to warn the jury.

# THE COURSE OF EVIDENCE
### The adversarial system
Criminal trials in New Zealand follow the 'adversarial' system — a contest between two sides in which each party presents its own case and challenges the other's. You need to understand how this system works because it shapes every decision you make about how to prepare and present evidence.

**Key Points**

- Each party — prosecution and defence — decides which witnesses to call, the order to call them, and what questions to ask.
- Each party has the right to challenge the other's witnesses through cross-examination.
- The judge's role during the trial is to ensure evidence is given according to the rules — not to investigate or call witnesses. The judge rules on admissibility.
- Neither the judge nor the jury can generally go beyond the evidence put before them and pursue independent inquiries.
- The defendant does not have to give evidence or do anything to assist the prosecution. The Crown must prove the case beyond reasonable doubt.
- Jurors submit questions through the judge. The judge decides whether and how those questions will be put to a witness.
- Some facts are *'judicially noticed'*  that is, they are so well-known or readily verifiable that they do not need to be proved. Judicially noticed facts are treated as established.
***Example:  ***

You are the investigating officer in a burglary case. You decide which witnesses to call and in what order. The defence has the right to cross-examine each of those witnesses. You do not need to prove every fact from scratch — the address of the district court, for example, would be judicially noticed.

### The judge's role in a jury trial
When a judge presides over a trial by jury, they must decide all questions about the admissibility of evidence, explain and enforce the legal principles that apply, and instruct the jury on the rules by which the evidence is to be weighed once submitted. The jury decides the facts — the judge does not.

### Oaths and affirmations
Before a witness gives evidence, they must promise to tell the truth. The form of that promise depends on the witness's age, governed by s77 of the Evidence Act 2006.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 77 — Evidence Act 2006: Oaths and affirmations
> Witnesses who are 12 years of age or older must take an oath or affirmation before giving evidence.
> Witnesses under the age of 12 must:
> (a)  be informed by the judge of the importance of telling the truth and not telling lies; and
> (b)  after being given that information, make a promise to tell the truth before giving evidence.
> With the judge's permission, a witness of any age may give evidence without taking an oath, making an affirmation, or making a promise to tell the truth. If permission is given, the judge must inform the witness of the importance of telling the truth. The evidence of that witness is then treated as if it had been given on oath.

You may encounter this provision when dealing with child witnesses or adults with intellectual disabilities who are unable to make a formal promise. If the judge gives permission to proceed without oath, treat the evidence with the same weight as sworn testimony.

### How jury trials are conducted
The Criminal Procedure Act 2011 governs all trial processes. A standard jury trial follows a set sequence. You need to know this sequence so you can prepare your witnesses and manage the case properly.

**Key Points**

- The jury is empanelled and a foreperson selected. The judge gives brief opening instructions covering the jury's role, the burden and standard of proof, and the need to keep an open mind.
- The Crown makes an opening address — the prosecutor explains the charges, the standard of proof, and summarises the evidence the Crown will call.
- The Crown presents its case: each prosecution witness gives evidence in chief, is cross-examined by the defence, and may be re-examined by the prosecution. The judge may ask questions in the interests of justice (s100).
- If the defence intends to call evidence, it opens its case at the end of the Crown case with an opening address to the jury.
- The defence presents its case — witnesses follow the same evidence in chief, cross-examination, and re-examination sequence.
- The Crown makes a closing address. No new evidence or issues may be raised.
- The defence makes a closing address, summarising the defence case.
- The judge sums up to the jury before it retires to consider its verdict.
The defence is not obliged to call any evidence. If it does not, the trial proceeds directly to closing addresses at the conclusion of the prosecution case. Under s107 of the Criminal Procedure Act 2011, the defence may call a witness immediately after a prosecution witness — a provision designed to help the jury build a coherent picture of the evidence.

The Criminal Procedure Act 2011 created four categories of offence. Category 1 and 2 offences (not punishable by imprisonment, or punishable by less than two years) are tried by judge alone. Category 3 offences (two years or more) are tried by judge alone unless the defendant elects a jury trial. Category 4 offences — the most serious, listed in Schedule 1 of the Act — are tried by a High Court jury unless a judge-alone trial is ordered in long, complex, or juror-intimidation cases.

***Example:  ***

You are the officer in charge of a Category 3 assault case. The defendant has elected a jury trial. You call three prosecution witnesses in turn — each gives evidence in chief, is cross-examined by defence counsel, and then re-examined by the prosecutor. The defence then calls one witness who the Crown cross-examines.

### Views, demonstrations, and reconstructions
In some trials the judge may order a 'view' — an inspection of a place or thing outside the courtroom, such as the scene of the offending. All parties and their lawyers are entitled to attend. Information from a view is treated as if it had been given in evidence. Demonstrations and reconstructions may also be held if they are relevant and if the probative value outweighs any risk of unfair prejudice.

# THE DEFENDANT'S FAILURE TO GIVE OR OFFER EVIDENCE
### Right of silence at trial
The defendant does not have to give evidence. You need to understand the protections around this right, because it affects how you present your case and what can be said in court.

Section 32 of the Evidence Act 2006 protects the defendant's right of silence before trial. No person may invite the fact-finder to draw an inference of guilt from the defendant's failure to answer questions, respond to statements, or disclose a defence before trial. The judge must direct the jury that it cannot draw an inference of guilt from that silence.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 33 — Evidence Act 2006: Restrictions on comment on defendant's right of silence at trial
> In a criminal proceeding, no person other than the defendant or the defendant's
> counsel or the Judge may comment on the fact that the defendant did not give
> evidence at his or her trial.

In practice, this means you — as a detective giving evidence — must never comment on the fact that the defendant chose not to give evidence. The prosecution cannot make such a comment either. The judge has discretion to comment, but will rarely do so. When a judge does comment, they must make clear that the burden of proof remains on the Crown and must not leave the jury with the impression that an innocent person would have given evidence.

***Example:  ***

You are in the witness box in an aggravated robbery trial. Defence counsel does not put his client in the witness box. You must not — in evidence or anywhere else in the courtroom — say anything like 'the defendant had nothing to say for himself.' That comment is prohibited under s33.

# EVIDENCE IN CHIEF
### What is evidence in chief?
Evidence in chief is the evidence a witness gives to the party that called them. Its purpose is to put the witness's account of events before the court. Evidence must ordinarily be given orally after the witness has taken the oath or affirmation — though it can be given by closed-circuit television or videolink where the judge allows it (s105), or in written form where all parties consent.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 84 — Evidence Act 2006: Examination of witnesses
> (1)  Unless this Act or any other enactment provides otherwise, or the Judge directs to the
> contrary, in any proceeding—
> (a)  a witness first gives evidence in chief; and
> (b)  after giving evidence in chief, the witness may be cross-examined by all parties, other than the party calling the witness, who wish to do so; and
> (c)  after all parties who wish to do so have cross-examined the witness, the witness may be re-examined.
> (2)  If a witness gives evidence in an affidavit or by reading a written statement in a courtroom, it is to be treated for the purposes of this Act as evidence given in chief.

### Leading questions
The general rule is that you cannot ask a witness leading questions during evidence in chief or re-examination (s89). A leading question is one that directly or indirectly suggests a particular answer to the question (s4 Evidence Act 2006).

The prohibition exists because leading questions undermine the reliability of testimony. Witnesses naturally tend to agree with suggestions put to them. A lawyer who leads their own witness can effectively put words in the witness's mouth — reducing the spontaneity and genuineness of the evidence, and creating a risk of collusion or construction of the evidence.

The goal of evidence in chief is to draw out the witness's own recollections, in their own words. The trier of fact — judge or jury — needs to assess the quality of that testimony. If the testimony is shaped by the questioner rather than the witness, that assessment becomes unreliable.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 89 — Evidence Act 2006: Leading questions in examination in chief and re-examination
> (1)  In any proceeding, a leading question must not be put to a witness in examination in
> chief or re-examination unless—
> (a)  the question relates to introductory or undisputed matters; or
> (b)  the question is put with the consent of all other parties; or
> (c)  the Judge, in exercise of the Judge's discretion, allows the question.
> (2)  Subsection (1) does not prevent a Judge, if permitted by rules of court, from allowing a written statement or report of a witness to be tendered or treated as the evidence in chief of that person.

***Key Points***

- **s89(1)(a):** Leading questions are always allowed for introductory matters — name, occupation, address — or facts the other side does not dispute.
- **s89(1)(b):** If all parties agree, a leading question can be asked.
- **s89(1)(c):** The judge has discretion to allow a leading question. This is likely to be exercised where: the question directs the witness's attention to identification evidence; the question jogs the witness's memory about surrounding circumstances without suggesting the answer to the main question; the witness is very young, has difficulty with English, or has limited intelligence; or the witness has been declared hostile.
***Non-leading (correct):  ***

'What did you see when you arrived at the address?' the witness gives their account in their own words.

***Leading (not permitted in chief):  ***

'You saw the defendant holding a knife when you arrived, didn't you?' the answer is suggested in the question.

Leading questions are permitted in cross-examination. That is their natural home.

## Refreshing memory
Trials often happen months or years after the events in question. By the time a witness takes the stand, details may have faded. The law allows witnesses to refresh their memory — but the rules differ depending on whether this happens in court or before court.

### Refreshing memory in court
A witness giving evidence may, with the judge's prior leave, consult a document to refresh their memory — but only if the document was made or adopted at a time when the witness's memory was fresh (s90(5) Evidence Act 2006).

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 90(5) — Evidence Act 2006: Refreshing memory in court
> For the purposes of refreshing his or her memory while giving evidence, a witness may, with the prior leave of the Judge, consult a document made or adopted at a time when his or her memory was fresh.

***Key Points***

- The witness must get leave from the judge before consulting the document.
- The document must be shown to every other party in the proceeding.
- The document must have been 'made or adopted' by the witness when their memory was fresh.
- In Cameron v R [2010] NZCA 411, the Court identified relevant factors: the significance of the events to the witness; the time elapsed between events and making the document; evidence from the witness about the freshness of their memory; and the detail and accuracy of the recollection recorded.
- In Rongonui v R [2010] NZSC 92, the Court upheld that a statement made 6 weeks after the event could still be a document made when memory was fresh.
- The document must have been made by the witness, or by another person acting on the witness's behalf in the witness's presence and assented to by the witness.
- Important: a witness cannot use a document as a substitute for memory. If the witness has no recollection of the events at all, the document cannot create one. The purpose of refreshing memory is to assist a witness who does have some recollection, but needs help with the details.
### Refreshing memory out of court
Witnesses are free to use whatever means they choose to refresh their memory before giving evidence — reading their brief of evidence, a prior statement, or discussing the case with the interviewing officer. The Evidence Act 2006 makes no change to this common law position.

As confirmed in R v Foreman [2008] NZCA 55, witnesses may re-read their briefs before trial. However, the means used to refresh memory can affect the weight given to the evidence. Opposing counsel is entitled to explore what steps the witness took to refresh their memory, and there has long been a discretion for the court to order that relevant documents be produced to opposing counsel.

***Practical note:  ***

As a detective, you will routinely read your notebooks and statements before giving evidence. This is both permitted and advisable. Be ready to tell the court what you read and when — defence counsel may ask you about it.

### Previous consistent statements
A witness generally cannot give evidence about statements they made before the trial that are consistent with what they are saying now. Repeating an allegation does not make it more truthful — but the more a statement is repeated, the more weight a jury may give it. The law prevents this by keeping previous consistent statements out unless an exception applies.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 35 — Evidence Act 2006: Previous consistent statements rule
> (1)  A previous statement of a witness that is consistent with the witness's evidence is not admissible unless subsection (2) applies to the statement.
> (2)  A previous statement of a witness that is consistent with the witness's evidence is
> admissible if the statement—
> (a)  responds to a challenge that will be or has been made to the witness's veracity or accuracy, based on a previous inconsistent statement of the witness or on a claim of invention on the part of the witness; or
> (b)  forms an integral part of the events before the court; or
> (c)  consists of the mere fact that a complaint has been made in a criminal case.

***Key Points:***

- s35(2)(a): If the defence attacks the witness's credibility — by alleging the witness is lying or mistaken, or relying on a prior inconsistent statement — a previous consistent statement can be admitted to respond to that challenge. The judge will admit only as many previous consistent statements as necessary to respond to the challenge.
- s35(2)(b): Statements that form an integral part of the events themselves (formerly 'res gestae' at common law) are not excluded by s35(1) and are admissible in their own right. Hart v R (2010) 24 CRNZ 924 confirms this.
- s35(2)(c): In a criminal case, the fact that a complaint was made is admissible. The content of that complaint is not automatically admissible — just the fact it occurred.
- Once admitted, a previous consistent statement is admissible as evidence of its truth — not just as evidence it was made (s7). This is a significant departure from the pre-2006 law.
- In Hart v R, the New Zealand Supreme Court (Tipping J) set out the test: there must have been a challenge to the witness's veracity or accuracy. It must have been suggested the witness is being untruthful or inaccurate. And it must be necessary to admit the prior statement to do justice to the witness's testimony in light of that attack.
### Hostile witnesses
Occasionally, a witness you have called will refuse to give the evidence you expected — or will actively work against your case. You need to know how the law deals with this.

### Unfavourable vs hostile witnesses
There is an important distinction between an unfavourable witness and a hostile witness. A witness who simply fails to give the evidence you expected — or gives evidence adverse to your case — is unfavourable. That is not enough, by itself, to justify a finding that they are hostile. A witness who suffers memory loss or gives evidence inconsistent with a prior statement is not automatically hostile.

A hostile witness is defined under s4 of the Evidence Act 2006. A witness is hostile if they:

- exhibit, or appear to exhibit, a lack of veracity when giving evidence unfavourable to the party who called them, on a matter about which they may reasonably be supposed to have knowledge; or
- give evidence inconsistent with a prior statement in a way that exhibits, or appears to exhibit, an intention to be unhelpful to the party who called them; or
- refuse to answer questions or deliberately withhold evidence.
Whether a witness is hostile in one of these ways is a question of law for the judge, decided on application from the party who called the witness (s94). As R v Vagaia [2008] 2 NZLR 516 confirmed, there is no rule preventing a party from calling a witness they know is likely to be hostile.

### What happens when a witness is declared hostile
If the judge grants permission, you can cross-examine a hostile witness to the extent the judge considers necessary for the purposes of doing justice. This may include:

- asking leading questions
- asking questions designed to probe the accuracy of memory and perception
- asking questions about prior inconsistent statements
- other challenges to veracity, including evidence from other witnesses (provided that evidence is 'substantially helpful' in assessing the witness's veracity)
***Example:  ***

You call a witness in a drug supply trial who previously gave a detailed statement to police. In the witness box, she claims to remember nothing. You apply to have her declared hostile. The judge grants the application. You are now able to put her prior statement to her and ask her why she is now saying she cannot recall events she described in detail at the time.

# CROSS-EXAMINATION
## Purpose of cross-examination
Cross-examination has two purposes: to elicit information that supports your case, and to challenge the accuracy or credibility of what the witness said in evidence in chief. Leading questions are permitted in cross-examination — that is their natural home.

Every party other than the one who called the witness has the right to cross-examine. In cases involving multiple defendants, each defendant can cross-examine the prosecution's witnesses and the co-defendant's witnesses.

Cross-examination is subject to a number of controls under the Evidence Act 2006, including the duty to put the case (s92), limits on unacceptable questions (s85), and restrictions on cross-examination by parties in person (s95).

## The duty to put your case
Whenever you intend to call evidence that contradicts what a witness has said in evidence in chief, you must put that contradictory material to the witness during cross-examination. This gives the witness the opportunity to comment on or explain it. If you fail to do this, the contradictory evidence may carry little weight — or the other side may be given leave to recall their witness to address it.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 92 — Evidence Act 2006: Cross-examination duties
> (1)  In any proceeding, a party must cross-examine a witness on significant matters that  are relevant and in issue and that contradict the evidence of the witness, if the witness could reasonably be expected to be in a position to give admissible evidence on those matters.
> (2)  If a party fails to comply with this section, the Judge may—
> (a)  grant permission for the witness to be recalled and questioned about the
> contradictory evidence; or
> (b)  admit the contradictory evidence on the basis that the weight to be given to it may be affected by the fact that the witness, who may have been able to explain the contradiction, was not questioned about the evidence; or
> (c)  exclude the contradictory evidence; or
> (d)  make any other order that the Judge considers just.

The duty arises when: the cross-examination deals with a significant matter; the matter is relevant and in issue; it contradicts the witness's evidence; and the witness could reasonably be expected to give admissible evidence on it.

***Example:  ***

A prosecution witness says the offending occurred at 9pm. Your defence witness will say the defendant was with her until 10pm. You must put this alibi to the prosecution witness during cross-examination — to give them the chance to respond — before you can rely on your alibi witness's evidence.

### Unacceptable questions
The judge has a broad discretion — and in some cases a duty — to control the nature of questions put to witnesses. Section 85 of the Evidence Act 2006 was significantly amended by the Sexual Violence Legislation Act 2021.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 85 — Evidence Act 2006: Unacceptable questions (as amended December 2021)
> (1)  In any proceeding, if the Judge considers a question, or the way in which it is asked, is improper, unfair, misleading, needlessly repetitive, or expressed in language that is too complicated for the witness to understand, the Judge must disallow the question or direct that the witness is not obliged to answer it.
> (2)  Without limiting the matters that the Judge may take into account for the purposes of subsection (1), the Judge may have regard to—
> (a)  the age, maturity, or vulnerability of the witness; and
> (b)  any physical, intellectual, psychological, or psychiatric impairment of the witness; and
> (c)  the linguistic or cultural background or religious beliefs of the witness; and
> (d)  the nature of the proceeding; and
> (e)  in the case of a hypothetical question, whether the hypothesis has been or will be proved by other evidence in the proceeding; and
> (f)  the nature of previous questions and any cumulative impact the questioning may have on the witness.

Section 85 applies to all questioning of witnesses at all stages of the examination, though it most commonly operates to control cross-examination. If a question is improper, unfair, misleading, needlessly repetitive, or in language too complex for the witness, the judge must act.

***Example:  ***

Defence counsel is cross-examining a 9-year-old complainant in a sexual violence case. The questions are becoming repetitive and increasingly distressing. The judge can and must step in under s85 to disallow them or direct the witness she is not obliged to answer.

### Cross-examination on previous inconsistent statements
You may cross-examine a witness about a previous statement they made — whether written or oral — even if you do not show them the statement or disclose its contents, provided you adequately identify to the witness the time, place, and circumstances of the statement. Section 96 of the Evidence Act 2006 governs this.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 96 — Evidence Act 2006: Cross-examination on previous statements of witnesses
> (1)  A party who cross-examines a witness may question the witness about a previous statement made by that witness without showing it or disclosing its contents to the witness if the time, place, and other circumstances concerning the making of the statement are adequately identified to the witness.
> (2)  If a witness does not expressly admit making the statement and the party wishes to prove that the witness did make the statement—
> (a)  the party must show the statement to the witness if it is in writing, or disclose its contents to the witness if the statement was not in writing; and
> (b)  the witness must be given an opportunity to deny making the statement or to explain any inconsistency between the statement and the witness's testimony.

***Key Points:***

- Section 96 is not restricted to inconsistent statements — it covers any previous statement, consistent or inconsistent.
- You can cross-examine on an oral statement (including one recorded on police job sheets or notebooks) as well as written statements.
- You must adequately identify to the witness the time, place, and circumstances of the statement.
- If the witness denies making the statement, you must show or disclose it before you can prove it was made.
- The witness must be given the chance to deny or explain the inconsistency.
# RE-EXAMINATION
After cross-examination, the party who called the witness may re-examine them. Re-examination is limited to matters arising out of what was covered in cross-examination — you cannot use re-examination to introduce new topics or shore up weaknesses that existed before cross-examination began. You need the judge's permission to go beyond those limits.

If the judge gives permission to raise a new matter in re-examination, the other parties must be given the opportunity to cross-examine on that new evidence. The judge may also allow further re-examination arising from that cross-examination.

***Example:  ***

Your witness gives evidence in chief that she saw the defendant at the scene at 8pm. Defence counsel cross-examines and suggests she is mistaken about the time. In re-examination, you can take her back to the time issue to clarify her evidence. You cannot use re-examination to introduce a new identification you forgot to cover in chief.

# EVIDENCE IN REBUTTAL AND RECALLING WITNESSES
## Evidence in rebuttal
After the prosecution closes its case, it cannot normally call further evidence without the court's permission. Evidence in rebuttal — called after your case has closed — requires leave. The court may grant leave if the further evidence:

- relates to a purely formal matter
- relates to a matter arising from the conduct of the defence, the relevance of which could not reasonably have been foreseen (this is the most common ground for leave)
- was not available or admissible before the prosecution's case was closed
- is required to be admitted in the interests of justice for any other reason
The court may also grant the defendant permission to call further evidence after closing their case if the interests of justice require it. Evidence in rebuttal may be admitted up until the jury retires — or in judge-alone trials, until judgment is delivered.

### Recalling witnesses
In addition to evidence in rebuttal, the judge may recall a witness who has already given evidence if the judge considers it is in the interests of justice to do so (s99 Evidence Act 2006). This power belongs to the judge and is exercised independently of either party.

# JUDICIAL DIRECTIONS AND WARNINGS
### Overview
There are circumstances where the judge must direct or warn the jury about evidence that may carry particular risks. These are set out in ss122–127 of the Evidence Act 2006:

- s122 — judicial warning that evidence may be unreliable
- s123 — judicial directions about certain ways of giving evidence
- s124 — judicial warnings about lies
- s125 — judicial directions about children's evidence
- s126 — judicial warnings about identification evidence
- s127 — delayed complaints or failure to complain in sexual cases
### Warning that evidence may be unreliable (s122)
Section 122 gives the judge a discretion to warn the jury about evidence that may be unreliable — and in certain situations, the judge must consider doing so. Understanding this section matters because it affects how you build your case and the risks associated with certain types of evidence.

**LEGISLATION**
> Section 122 — Evidence Act 2006: Judicial directions about evidence which may be unreliable
> (1)  If, in a criminal proceeding tried with a jury, the Judge is of the opinion that any evidence given in that proceeding that is admissible may nevertheless be unreliable, the Judge may warn the jury of the need for caution in deciding—
> (a)  whether to accept the evidence; and
> (b)  the weight to be given to the evidence.
> (2)  In a criminal proceeding tried with a jury the Judge must consider whether to give a warning under subsection (1) whenever the following evidence is given:
> (a)  hearsay evidence;
> (b)  evidence of a statement by the defendant, if that evidence is the only evidence implicating the defendant;
> (c)  evidence given by a witness who may have a motive to give false evidence that is prejudicial to a defendant;
> (d)  evidence of a statement by the defendant to another person made while both the defendant and the other person were detained in prison, a police station, another place of detention;
> (e)  evidence about the conduct of the defendant if that conduct is alleged to have occurred more than 10 years previously.

***Key Points:***

- The warning under s122(1) is discretionary — the judge may give it when evidence may be unreliable.
- The obligation under s122(2) is stronger — the judge must consider giving the warning for the five listed categories of evidence. Considering is not the same as giving — the judge may still decide a warning is unnecessary.
- A 'jailhouse confession' (s122(2)(d)) — a statement the defendant made to another person while both were in custody — always triggers the obligation to consider a warning.
- Hearsay evidence (s122(2)(a)) triggers the obligation because the original statement-maker has not promised to tell the truth and cannot be cross-examined.
- No particular form of words is required for the warning.
### Directions about how evidence is given (s123)
Section 123 requires the judge to direct the jury that no adverse inference should be drawn against the defendant where a witness has given evidence in an alternative way — for example, by videolink (s105), where the defendant has not been allowed to personally cross-examine a witness (s95), or where a witness gives evidence under a witness anonymity order (s112). The jury must not read anything sinister into those arrangements.

### Warnings about lies (s124)
Section 124 deals with the situation where evidence suggests the defendant lied before or during the proceeding. It is for the jury to decide whether the defendant actually lied. A proven lie about a material matter can be circumstantial evidence of guilt — or go to the defendant's general credibility.

***Key Points:***

- No warning needs to be given unless the judge thinks the jury might place too much weight on the lie — or if the defendant requests one.
- If the defendant requests a warning, it must be given.
- When given, the warning must tell the jury: they need to be satisfied the defendant did lie before using the evidence; people lie for various reasons; the fact that the defendant lied does not mean they are guilty of the offence charged.
***Example:  ***

The defendant told police he was home all evening but CCTV shows him near the scene. The prosecution argues this lie is evidence of a guilty mind. The judge may need to warn the jury — if requested — to be cautious about drawing that inference: people lie for many reasons, including embarrassment or self-protection.

### Directions about children's evidence (s125)
Section 125 requires that children's evidence be treated in the same way as adult evidence. The judge must not give a corroboration warning about a child complainant's evidence that would not have been given if the complainant were an adult.

More broadly, s125 prohibits the judge from directing the jury or making any comment — absent expert evidence — that children have a tendency to invent or distort, or that their evidence requires special scrutiny. The thrust of the section is to prevent children from being treated as less credible than adult witnesses simply because they are children.

Special provision exists for child witnesses under 6 years of age in regulation 49 of the Evidence Regulations 2007. The regulation focuses the judge or jury on how the evidence was obtained from the child at each stage of the investigation and trial — not on any supposed tendency of young children to fabricate.

# PRACTICAL MATTERS FOR DEFENDED HEARINGS
### Preparing your witnesses
As the officer in charge of a case going to a defended hearing, there are practical steps you need to take before and during the trial.

### Witness preparation
- Advise each witness of the time, date, and place of the trial, and what exhibits will be presented.
- Check whether each witness has given evidence before. If not, explain the procedure: where to stand, how to speak clearly, how to answer only what is asked, and to say 'I don't know' rather than guess.
- Tell witnesses they may read their written statement to refresh their memory before giving evidence — but they cannot read job sheets or briefs.
- Ensure witnesses remain within call if they are excluded from the courtroom.
- Check the jury list to confirm none of the jurors are known to your witnesses.
- Warn witnesses they must not speak to jurors.
- Advise witnesses about witness expenses.
### Your conduct as officer in charge
- Present yourself properly — look, stand, and speak with confidence and professionalism.
- Identify the defendant.
- Support your witnesses and assist them as required.
- Do not mix with or gossip with jurors or members of the defence.
### Giving evidence yourself
- Answer only the question being asked.
- Say you do not know rather than guessing.
- Do not be flippant.
- Address the judge as 'Your Honour' or 'Sir/Ma'am'.
- Address the prosecutor and defence counsel as 'Sir/Ma'am'.
- If you make a mistake, advise the judge as soon as possible — or advise the prosecutor after you finish giving evidence.
- If you can give evidence that is favourable to the defence, you must do so. You are there to help the court find the truth — not just to secure a conviction. Being fair and unbiased confirms your credibility as a witness.
### Using your notebook in the witness box
If you need to consult your notebook while giving evidence, follow these steps:

- Ask the court's permission.
- Introduce the material properly: 'I interviewed the defendant and wrote the answers in my notebook at the time.'
- Remember that the defence and the jury are entitled to view your notes — seal off any unrelated entries before going into the witness box.
- You are only allowed to refresh your memory, not read the entire entry. You need the judge's permission to read the interview notes in full.
### Introducing a statement of admission
When you have obtained a statement of admission that is to be read to the court, begin by giving evidence of any prior oral discussions or admissions. This is important in case the written statement is later rejected — your oral evidence of what was said remains admissible. Keep references to irrelevant material to a minimum.

***Example:  ***

*"At [time] I spoke to the defendant. Following a general discussion on [weather/sport], I then asked him [question]. He replied, '[answer].' I then asked him if he wished to make a statement. He agreed and I now produce and read this to the Court."*

At all times: be truthful, factual, unbiased, calm, and professional.

# SUMMARY
**Adversarial system: **Criminal trials are a contest between two parties. Each party controls its own evidence. The judge rules on admissibility. The jury decides the facts.

**Oaths and affirmations: **Witnesses aged 12 and over take an oath or affirmation (s77). Witnesses under 12 must promise to tell the truth after being told of its importance. With judicial permission, any witness may give evidence without a formal oath.

**Jury trial sequence: **Empanelment → Crown opening → Crown case (evidence in chief, cross-examination, re-examination) → Defence opening (if calling evidence) → Defence case → Crown closing → Defence closing → Judge's summing up → Jury deliberates.

**Right of silence: **The defendant does not have to give evidence. No person other than the defendant, defence counsel, or the judge may comment on the defendant's failure to give evidence (s33). No inference of guilt may be drawn from silence before trial (s32).

**Evidence in chief: **The witness's direct account to the party who called them. Evidence is given orally under oath. No leading questions permitted, subject to the three exceptions in s89: introductory matters, consent of parties, or judicial discretion.

**Refreshing memory in court: **A witness may consult a document with the judge's leave, provided it was made or adopted when memory was fresh (s90(5)). The document must be shown to all parties.

**Refreshing memory out of court: **Witnesses may use any means to refresh their memory before trial. The Evidence Act makes no change to the common law on this. What a witness read before giving evidence may be explored in cross-examination.

**Previous consistent statements: **Generally inadmissible (s35(1)). Admissible under s35(2) if: the statement responds to a challenge to veracity or accuracy based on a prior inconsistent statement or claim of invention; it forms an integral part of the events before the court; or it is the mere fact of a complaint in a criminal case. Once admitted, it is evidence of truth.

**Hostile witness: **A witness who exhibits a lack of veracity, gives inconsistent evidence suggesting they intend to be unhelpful, or deliberately withholds evidence (s4 definition). The judge may declare a witness hostile on application by the calling party (s94) and permit cross-examination of that witness.

**Cross-examination duty: **You must put to a witness all significant matters that are relevant, in issue, and that contradict their evidence — provided the witness could reasonably give admissible evidence on them (s92). Failure to do this may result in your contradictory evidence being excluded or given reduced weight.

**Unacceptable questions: **The judge must disallow questions that are improper, unfair, misleading, needlessly repetitive, or too complex for the witness (s85, as amended December 2021). The judge considers factors including the witness's age, maturity, vulnerability, impairment, cultural background, and the cumulative effect of questioning.

**Cross-examination on previous statements: **You may cross-examine on any previous statement without showing it, provided you adequately identify to the witness when and where it was made. If the witness denies making it, you must show or disclose it before proving it (s96).

**Re-examination: **Limited to matters arising from cross-examination. No new topics without judicial leave. If the judge permits new matters, other parties may cross-examine on them.

**Evidence in rebuttal: **After your case closes, further evidence requires the court's leave. Most commonly granted where the defence raised an issue that could not have been foreseen, or the evidence was unavailable before the case closed.

**Judicial warning — unreliable evidence: **The judge may warn the jury about unreliable evidence (s122(1)), and must consider doing so for hearsay, sole-evidence confessions, evidence from potentially biased witnesses, jailhouse confessions, and stale conduct evidence (s122(2)).

**Judicial warning — lies: **Where the defendant is alleged to have lied, the judge warns the jury (if requested) that they must first be satisfied the defendant did lie, that people lie for various reasons, and that a lie does not prove guilt (s124).

**Children's evidence: **Children's evidence is treated the same as adult evidence. No special corroboration warning or direction about children's tendency to invent, unless supported by expert evidence (s125).
