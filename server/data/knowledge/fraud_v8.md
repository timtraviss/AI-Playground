**FRAUD - INTRODUCTION**

This syllabus focuses on Fraud and establishes the foundational knowledge required for detectives addressing fraudulent offending in New Zealand. Fraud offences are characterised by various behaviours and methods of dishonesty and are classified as serious criminal offences.

The curriculum will specifically examine the following crimes – all of which come under the umbrella of Fraud.

- Takes or Obtains a Document
- Uses or Attempts to Use a Document
- Deception
- Forgery
- Computer Crime
The Detective Development Course will cover three offences only - Section 228 (1) (a), s228 (1) (b) and section 240 (1) (a). These offences have been chosen to provide a robust understanding of concepts applicable to all practical fraud investigations.

Generally, fraud offences involve criminal acts of dishonesty aimed at acquiring property services or other forms of benefit or advantage. The scope of fraud offences is extensive - ranging from minor misappropriation to significant financial losses for individuals and organizations. These offences can be committed against individuals, businesses, charities, clubs, or other government agencies and can result in significant financial losses and other forms of harm.

The nature of charges in cases involving dishonest behaviour will be determined by the specific circumstances and the evidence gathered during each individual investigation.

A note on Victims of Fraud

It is critical for investigators to understand the significant financial, emotional and psychological impact fraud has on its victims. Victims of Fraud offences must always be at the forefront of every investigator’s mind and appropriate welfare and support offered.

**UNDERSTANDING DISHONESTLY TAKING OR USING A DOCUMENT**

### INTRODUCTION
Section 228 of the Crimes Act addresses the offence of dishonest possession of, or use of documents. The offence covers both **intended** dishonest use of documents as well as **actual** dishonest use of documents.

There are four specific offences contained within this section which may be used as alternatives or additions to the provisions of theft, obtaining by deception and forgery offences.

The mens rea of the offender in all four options is the same – that is an intent to obtain something that relates to **financial benefit**. The financial benefit is categorised under the below elements of

- Property or
- Service or
- Pecuniary advantage or
- Valuable consideration
The mens rea above must be accompanied by one of four acts categorised under the below elements.

- Takes or
- Obtains or
- Uses or
- Attempts to Use
- Any document
As always, the physical act or actus reus must be accompanied by the general mens rea of ***dishonestly***. In other words, an offender doesn’t just take a document, but ***dishonestly*** takes a document – that is the act of taking is done without authority, or consent.

As with the charge of theft, a charge of taking or using a document requires proof by the prosecution of dishonesty and that the defendant had no defence of claim of right. However, unlike theft, there is no requirement that a prosecution show an intention to permanently deprive the owner of the document that is taken or used.

**Of note:**

The legislation in section 228 arranges the offence with the mens rea first followed by the actus reus.

It does not matter which comes first as long as evidence is gathered in any investigation for both. For the purposes of this document and in the interests of consistency the actus reus will be examined first.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 228
The actus reus (that which constitutes the physical act of the offence) can be summed up as.

- Takes **or**
- Obtains **or**
- Uses **or**
- Attempts to use
### TAKES OR OBTAINS
“*Taking*” is not specifically defined by statute but can be read, for the purposes of this section, in conjunction with the definition of theft as contained within s219 of the Crimes Act 1961. The definition of taking is wider than that of theft and encompasses a greater number of situations.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 219 (4), Crimes Act 1961***
> *For tangible property, theft is committed by a taking when the offender moves the property or causes it to be moved.*

### OBTAIN 
“*Obtain*” is defined in the Crimes Act 1961. Often obtaining a document will involve coming into possession legitimately of the document but retaining or keeping it for dishonest purpose.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 217, Crimes Act 1961***
> *Obtain, in relation to any person, means obtain to retain for himself or herself or for any other person.*

### USES OR ATTEMPTS TO USE 
The prosecution must prove that the offender used or attempted to use the document. Whether the appropriate charge is uses or attempts will depend on the circumstances and evidence of the investigation. At times a use of a document is clear in that it has been handed to another party for use. At other times the difference between a use and attempted use may be unclear. As always, seek advice if you are unsure.

### USE
*Adams on Criminal Law[1] *details that “*use*” can include a single action, such as the handing over of a document to its intended recipient, or the continuing use of a document.

An example of what can be defined as a ‘use’ can be found in *R v Dakers*

**EXAMPLE: **

In the matter of *R v Dakers*[2], the Court of Appeal held that where tickets that had been presented to the TAB previously, were presented again by the accused, there was a ‘*use*’ of the tickets to obtain a pecuniary advantage. The TAB’s systems ensured that the money was not paid out, but this was not due to the characteristics of the tickets themselves. Thus, the purpose was unsuccessful but not the ‘*use*’.

### ATTEMPTED USE
If a clear use of a document is not obvious consider attempted use. The starting point for attempted use is found in section 72 Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 72, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Attempting to commit an offence***
> *Every one who, having an intent to commit an offence, does or omits an act for the purpose of accomplishing his object, is guilty of an attempt to commit the offence intended, whether in the circumstances it was possible to commit the offence or not.**The question whether an act done or omitted with intent to commit an offence is or is not only preparation for the commission of that offence, and too remote to constitute an attempt to commit it, is a question of law.**An act done or omitted with intent to commit an offence may constitute an attempt if it is immediately or proximately connected with the intended offence, whether or not there was any act unequivocally showing the intent to commit that offence.*

Here the prosecution must prove that an action with the document was sufficiently proximate (close enough) to an actual use of the document. Remember that an unsuccessful use of a document (for example a credit card transaction is declined) does not suddenly mean that a ‘*use*’ becomes an attempt only. In this case the handing over of the credit card for payment may be sufficient to fulfil the element of **uses **a document.

****CASELAW****
> ***Hayes v R ***
> *“An unsuccessful use of a document is as much use as a successful one. An unsuccessful use must not be equated conceptually with an attempted one. ****The concept of attempt relates to use**** not to the ultimate obtaining of a pecuniary advantage, which is not a necessary ingredient of the offence. Because the use does not have to be successful it may be difficult to draw a clear line between use and attempted use.”*

When considering the physical acts that make up the offence, there is **always** the requirement that those acts are accompanied by a general intent to commit the offence, as spelt out in the following definition.

******In section 228, the physical act of taking a document, obtaining a document, using a document or attempting to use a document, must be done with a dishonest intent and without a belief in claim of right.

Although not explicitly stated in the elements, these acts must be carried out with the general intent of deliberate dishonesty, knowing there was no authority or consent to deal with the document.

### DISHONESTLY
“Dishonestly” is defined in the Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 217, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Dishonestly, ****in relation to an act or omission, means done or omitted without belief that there was express or implied consent to, or authority for, the act or omission from a person entitled to give such consent or authority.*

A belief that a person has consent or authority may be formed when :

- that the act or omission was, expressly or impliedly, consented to by a person entitled to give consent, or
- that the act or omission was authorised by a person entitled to authorise it.
This definition of dishonesty was discussed in *Hayes v R*. There the Supreme Court noted that *“the objective facts of a particular case may be such that the jury can properly infer that the defendant had a dishonest mind unless he or she can raise a reasonable doubt on the basis of a relevant but mistaken belief.”*

The absence of any reference in section 217 to the relevant belief having to be reasonable or based on reasonable grounds is significant. **The Court in *Hayes*[3] decided that the question is whether the belief is actually held, not whether that belief is reasonable.**

In most circumstances the issue of whether a defendant has acted dishonestly will not arise where the other elements are made out. However, ***this element may become a live issue where a defendant has acted under a belief that his or her actions are morally just***.

What is clear is that the belief does not have to be correct, nor does it have to be a reasonable belief, it must merely be an honest belief.

### WITHOUT CLAIM OF RIGHT DEFINED
*“Claim of right*” is defined by statute.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Claim of right, ****in relation to any act, means a belief at the time of the act in a proprietary or possessory right in property in relation to which the offence is alleged to have been committed, although that belief may be based on ignorance or mistake of fact or of any matter of law other than the enactment against which the offence is alleged to have been committed.*

Claim of Right is a ‘*defence*’ to what would otherwise be a criminal act.

Claim of right attaches to offences where it is written into statute, often dealing with **property **and dishonesty. It stems from the idea that an individual can deal with their own property how they chose and in a way, they believe to be honest.

In relation to section 228, claim of right will be relevant where a person has acted under a **belief** that he or she has a proprietary or possessory right to the relevant document and has exercised this right. It will also be a relevant defence where they **believe **they had some sort of legal right to use the document in the dishonest way that they did.

What is clear is that the belief does not have to be correct, nor does it have to be a reasonable belief, it must merely be an honest belief.

See further definitions explanation of Claim of Right in the Basic Legal Concepts module.

### DOCUMENT 
Document is defined in section 217 of the Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 217, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Document ****means a document, or part of a document, in any form; and includes, without limitation,-*
> *any paper or other material used for writing or printing that is marked with matter capable of being read; or**any photograph, or any photographic negative, plate, slide, film, or microfilm, or any photostatic negative; or**any disc, tape, wire, sound track, card, or other material or device in or on which information, sounds, or other data are recorded, stored (whether temporarily or permanently), or embodied so as to be capable, with or without the aid of some other equipment, of being reproduced; or**any material by means of which information is supplied, whether directly or by means of any equipment, to any device used for recording or storing or processing information; or**any material derived, whether directly or by means of any equipment, from information recorded or stored or processed by any device used for recording or storing or processing information*

This definition was intended to be broad and encompass technological advances. Although parts are already relatively obsolete (for example disc, photostatic negative) the purpose of the definition is an extensive and non-exhaustive definition of a “*document*”, which encompasses a significant array of circumstances. Under this definition, a “*document*” includes any “*item*” that provides evidence of information or serves as a record.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Misic***
> *“Essentially a document is a thing which provides evidence or information or serves as a record.”*

**THE SPECIFIC MENS REA FOR SECTION 228**

As Adams states, *“There are three components to the mental element required to be established for [section 228].”*

The first is the general mens rea accompanying the physical actus reus – that is, dishonesty which is explained above. The second, also explained above, is that the defendant had no belief that their actions were unauthorised. These form part of the general mens rea as proof of knowledge of unauthorised dealing with the document and non-genuine belief that there was a right to deal with the document are aspects of the defendant’s state of mind.

The specific mens rea of section 228 is that the defendant intended to obtain the property, service, pecuniary advantage or valuable consideration, as the aim or purpose of dealing with the document. Another way to think about these categories is that one of them was the **intended outcome** of the dealing with the document.

Proof of intent, as always, is required for evidential sufficiency. The application of R v Collister to the investigation of the surrounding circumstances is important to ensure evidence is gathered to infer what the defendant’s intent was.

The prosecution must prove an intent to obtain something that is often – but not exclusively - of financial value or advantage. In relation to property the court has stated that

*“… as a matter of policy and practice a person may be criminally liable under s228 (1) if they dishonestly use a document to acquire property regardless of whether the property in question has economic value.”*

When there is financial benefit or advantage this offence does require proof of the economic gain or loss.

Causation is important. The defendant must intend to obtain, and he or she must intend to obtain ***by *the** dishonest dealing with the document.

This element does not require the prosecution to prove that a defendant has in fact gained or caused a loss. The element merely requires that a defendant **intended this to happen**, contemporaneously with the actus reus.

### OBTAIN 
The inclusion of the word *“obtain* “results in this section extending to situations where a defendant has obtained the property through either legitimate consent or dishonestly obtained consent and later retains that property.

Given that *‘obtains’* includes retaining an object, a wider array of circumstances will be met by this.

The thing that the defendant intends to take will often be property. However, by including phrases such as service and pecuniary advantage, the section is widened to include non-monetary items that will improve a person’s position, such as an intangible benefit (an example of this is where a person obtains the right to utilise a service that does not have any lasting effect on his or her financial position).

***WHAT OUTCOME TO CHOOSE***

One of the complicating factors of fraud offences is determining what category the intended outcome falls into. Section 228 defines four categories:

- property,
- service,
- pecuniary advantage or
- valuable consideration.
Your investigation must prove that the defendant intended to obtain some sort of gain that falls into one of these categories.

Many intended outcomes may fall into more than one category defined in section 228. The court has acknowledged that while some cases are clear, others are not. The categories have been described as *cognate concepts* - which means related or connected.

Once again, gathering evidence of **what the defendant intended to gain** by using the document will aid you in charging appropriately.

***HAYES V R ***

An important NZ Supreme Court case, *Hayes v R[4],* clarifies several important definitions not defined within the Crimes Act 1961.

In 1997, Hayes, a primary teacher, was involved in a motor accident and injured her neck. She began receiving weekly compensation payments from Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC). She continued to submit medical certificates to ACC over an eight-year period to obtain payments. She certified she was not working and was unable to work when in fact she was involved, successively, in running two companies and actively assisted her partner with physical work.

Hayes argued that she honestly believed her declarations about her capacity to work referred only to her ability to perform her pre-accident role as a teacher. She also maintained that she honestly believed she was entitled to receive weekly compensation payments (see discussion on dishonesty).

In *Hayes v R* the judge examined what was meant by pecuniary advantage. In doing so he commented on the other categories in section 228.  During his discussion the judge described the terms of property, service, valuable consideration and pecuniary advantage acknowledging the fact that what was obtained **could fall into several categories**. For example, he stated that *“…property is defined in s2 as including money. Hence money is actually included within three of the four concepts covered by s228.”*

**Example:**

If someone uses a stolen credit card to obtain shoes from a retail outlet, it is appropriate to charge with obtaining property, rather than pecuniary advantage. Although the defendant does gain a pecuniary advantage in that he / she did not have to pay for the shoes, the intent at the time of the use was to obtain the shoes themselves. The pecuniary advantage of not having to pay for the shoes, in this case is an added benefit.

**Example:**

If a person fills out a false application for a loan of $20,000, they could legitimately be charged with obtaining property, OR a pecuniary advantage, OR valuable consideration.

As you can see from these two examples the evidence may point to an obvious category of financial benefit (the shoes being property) or the evidence may fit into a number of categories (the $20,000 loan). Ultimately, you can only charge with one category, so you will need to make a choice. If you are unsure always seek advice.

The following section defines each of the intended outcomes. Your task is to understand each category and use the evidence to argue which one you believe is most appropriate. Note that more than one argument may be valid.

### PROPERTY 
“Property” is defined in the Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 2, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***property**** includes real and personal property, and any estate or interest in any real or personal property, money, electricity, and any debt, and any thing in action, and any other right or interest*

Property most obviously includes physical items such as a pair of shoes, a TV or phone, but also includes money.

**Example:**

- Monetary payment.
- Issuing a false invoice to receive payment **(money)** for goods never supplied.
- Clothing or cash obtained by a credit or EFTPOS card.
### 
### SERVICE
Service is not defined in the Crimes Act 1961. The case of *R v Cara*[5] directed that *“service is limited to financial or economic value and excludes privileges or benefits”.*

*Cara* discussed Using a document to obtain a passport. Discussion in the case law revolved around whether a passport was a service in terms of being able to get financial benefit from its use. It surmised that there were financial advantages that could be derived from a passport even though its primary function was the benefit or privilege of NZ citizenship.

**Example:**

- using a medical certificate that is not genuine to obtain home help
- using a staff card that is not yours to obtain free ferry or bus rides.
### 
### PECUNIARY ADVANTAGE
****CASELAW****
> ***Hayes v R***
> *A pecuniary advantage is “anything that enhances the accused’s financial position. It is that enhancement which constitutes the element of advantage”.*

This is a broad definition and the examples in the previous two categories (property or service) could constitute a pecuniary advantage also. Further examples are below;

**Example:**

- Changing a price tag to obtain a discount.
- Using a document to have money diverted to your account.
- Using a student ID or a gold card to obtain a discount.
- Using a document to avoid payment of a debt.
### VALUABLE CONSIDERATION
Valuable consideration again can be seen as aligning with the other categories in section 228. The scope of a valuable consideration is wider than a pecuniary advantage being,

****CASELAW****
> ***Hayes v R***
> *A valuable consideration is “anything capable of being valuable consideration, whether of a monetary kind or any other kind; in short, money or money’s worth”.*

**Example:**

- Using a document to obtain a job (and therefore wage).
- Issuing a false invoice to receive payment for goods never supplied.
**UNDERSTANDING OBTAINING BY DECEPTION**

### INTRODUCTION
An understanding of the concept of *“deception”* is integral to understanding the offences of *“Obtaining by deception or causing loss by deception”* found in section 240 (1). It is also important to understand what a criminal deception is, as many fraudulent activities carried out by individuals are captured by the definition of Deception.

Many of the dishonesty offences covered under Part 10 of the Crimes Act can also be covered under the offence of Deception in section 240.  Uses a Document (previously discussed), as well as forgery, altering or reproducing a document, as well as computer crimes, all involve an actus reus and mens rea that can also satisfy the elements for a Deception under section 240.

In other words, one set of circumstances and activities carried out by an individual can fall under several offences – your job as an investigator is to consider the evidence and decide which offence to charge with. In New Zealand the same set of circumstances and activities will attract different charges, depending on supervisor, prosecution and crown advice, as well as differences in district convention.

The offence of Deception is laid out in section 240(1)(a)

This first element of the offence is now considered in detail, and starts with the question - What is a deception anyway? Deception has three definitions found in section 240 (2) of the Crimes Act 1961. What a deception **actually is**, and what you have to prove can be found in section (2).

### SECTION 240 (2) DECEPTION DEFINED
The first definition of Deception is found in section 240 (2) (a)

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 240(2), Crimes Act 1961***
> ***In this section****, ****deception means-****A false representation, whether oral or documentary, or by conduct, where the person making the representation intends to deceive any other person and-**knows it is false in a material particular, or**Is reckless as to whether it is false in a material particular; *

This definition provides an exhaustive definition of what constitutes deception. The section is sufficiently wide to encompass situations of the extending of false information in written or oral form as well as by action and conduct.

The definition also provides that a person can act with deception where he or she has been reckless as to whether the facts they are offering are true or not.

***THE ACTUS REUS FOR DECEPTION S240***

The physical act or criminal act of Deception is an individual making a false representation. Put simply, a person must hold out a representation about themselves or a circumstance as true, when in fact it is false. The representation is a lie. This false representation can be made to another in any way – including by documentation, verbally or by actions or conduct. The following will detail this further.

###  REPRESENTATION
This is not specifically defined; however, a representation is generally held to be a statement of fact.

Examples have included representations about a past or present fact, about a future event, or about an existing intention, opinion, belief, knowledge, or other state of mind.

Simester &amp; Brookbanks[6], suggest *“it must be capable of being false so it must contain a proposition of fact”.*

**Example:**

Let us look at an example of a representation.

Your brother is the black sheep of the family and is a crook. He steals your ID and a Police shirt. He makes a representation that he is a Police Officer when he

- Tells people he is a Constable either in or out of uniform
- Shows your badge / ID to someone either in or out of uniform
- Acts like a Police Officer either in or out of uniform (say by directing traffic at a crash)
This is a representation made by your brother that he is, in fact, a Police Officer. This has been represented orally; by verbalizing he is a police officer, by a document producing an identification badge and by conduct putting on the stolen uniform and directing traffic.

Section 240(2) states that a representation can be oral, documentary or by conduct or by any combination of these. This means that it is possible to look beyond the literal meaning of express words used by the defendant and consider whether a particular meaning can also be implied from his or her conduct either specifically or as a whole.

It may be necessary to analyse what meaning was conveyed to the party that now complains of a false representation:

### FALSE REPRESENTATION
A simple way to consider a false representation is simply **a representation of fact that is untrue.**

In the example above, when you put on your uniform and direct traffic your representation is true, whereas your brothers were not – and is therefore a false representation.

Under the current law, the representation must be false, and the defendant must **know or believe** that it is false in a material particular, or be reckless as to whether it is false. Absolute certainty is not required and willful blindness as to falsity of the statement will suffice.

The falsity of the representation must be proved. In *Carlos v R[7]* there was more than one false representation alleged. It was directed that each alleged misrepresentation should be included in a separate count.

False in a material particular means that something about the representation is false. The representation does not need to be wholly false – but something that is of consequence, important or relevant to the facts of the case. This is assessed objectively.

*R v Mallett* states, *“A matter will be a ‘material particular’ if it is something important or something that matters.”*

Below are examples of false representations.

**Example:**

Orally (by spoken words)

*Example:*

- Verbally claiming to own goods that are in fact subject to a hire purchase agreement.
- In the case of *R v Caslin[8]* the defendant was held to have obtained by a false pretence because the representation made by her that she was a sex worker, prepared to engage in sexual activity and that she had a bedroom available for that purpose, were undoubtedly false on that particular occasion.
By conduct

- Representing oneself to be a collector for charity by appearing to be carrying an official collection bag.
- In *R v Barnard[9]* the defendant entered a shop in a university town wearing a cap and gown to convey the notion that he was a member of the university and as such, able to receive a discount offered to university students.
Documentary

- Presenting a false certificate of qualification
- Completing a direct debit form on an account in which there are no funds knowing the payment due will not be honoured.
***CONTINUING EFFECT***

In many cases a representation by words or conduct may have a continuing effect. For example, entering a restaurant and ordering dinner is a representation at the time of being seated, that the diner will follow the normal practice and pay for the meal.

If at the time of being seated or at any point during the course of dinner, the diner decides to avoid that payment, the continuing representation will become false.

***SILENCE AS A FALSE REPRESENTATION***

In* R v Waterfall[10]* the Court discussed that, as a general rule, silence or non-disclosure will not be regarded as a representation, but there are exceptions to this such as where an incorrect understanding is implied from a course of dealing and the defendant has failed to negate that incorrect understanding.

One exception to the rule on silence is found in “*label swapping*” cases.

**Example:**

In *Police v Dronjak[11]*, the defendant was shopping in a department store. He was approached by an assistant who, in reply to his query, advised that the cost of a particular car radio was $695.83.

He advised the assistant that he would purchase the item when he went through the checkout.

By the time the defendant arrived at the checkout the item bore two price tags, one for $695.83 and the other for $38.88.

The defendant allowed the cashier to charge an incorrect price by not pointing out the higher price tag on the goods. It was argued that this amounted to a representation that the price tag was the correct one.

It was commented that by maintaining silence in the face of a mistake known to him and by deliberately refraining from drawing the checkout assistant’s attention to the mistake, Dronjak had obtained title to the radio by a false pretence (deception).

Importantly, in *Dronjak* no evidence was offered that the defendant did not tamper with the price tags but presented the item at the counter with the correct label concealed from the shop assistant’s view. The High Court found him guilty of false pretences and dismissed his appeal.

**Example:**

In the case of *Rao* *v Police[12]* the defendant had removed price tags and replaced them with cheaper ones.

Here the Court held the representation the appellant made was the representation inherent in his conduct that the price tickets on the articles handed to the checkout operator were the same tickets as those placed on the goods by the store. The appeal against a conviction for false pretences was dismissed by the High Court.

### OTHER DEFINITIONS OF DECEPTION UNDER S240(2)
Now that we have considered the first definition of deception, we will briefly identify the other two definitions of deception found in section 240 (2).

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 240(2), Crimes Act 1961***
> ***In this section****, ****deception means-****An omission to disclose a material particular, with intent to deceive any person, in circumstances where there is a duty to disclose it**A fraudulent device, trick, or stratagem used with intent to deceive any person.** *

### AN OMISSION TO DISCLOSE A MATERIAL PARTICULAR -DUTY TO DISCLOSE
Section 240(2)(b) concerns an omission to disclose a material particular with intent to deceive, **where there is a duty to disclose. **This part of the definition requires a duty to exist before the offence is triggered.

Along with showing an intent to deceive, s240(2)(b) requires you to show that there was some material particular that was not disclosed, that the defendant was under a duty to disclose, and that the defendant failed to perform that duty.

All persons who have a fiduciary duty* will have a duty to disclose, however the section does not limit this duty to fiduciary relationships only. A sufficient duty may arise in other circumstances such as when parties are in a contractual relationship, or even more broadly a relationship where there is a person in a place of trust and confidence.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Love[13]***
> *a deliberate failure to disclose some material matter by a person who has a duty to disclose it will come within the definition of deception. *

### OMISSION
Omission is not defined within the Crimes Act 1961.  An omission is inaction, i.e. not acting. It can either be a conscious decision not to do something or not giving thought to the matter at all.

**Example:**

- Contractual Duty – If a contract or agreement includes terms that require full and honest disclosure, then failing to share relevant information is a breach.
- Trustee has a duty to act in the best interest of the beneficiaries of the trust – this includes disclosure of information in the best interest of the beneficiary.
- A financial advisor may have a duty to disclose a material particular, as they are in a place of trust and confidence to the investor.
### A FRAUDULENT DEVICE, TRICK, OR STRATAGEM, SECTION 240 (2) 
Section 240(2)(c) concerns a fraudulent device, trick, or stratagem used with intent to deceive any person. This provision introduces a general criminal offence of fraud and effectively covers any form of fraudulent conduct. Note, however, that as with the other parts of s240(2), the conduct must be accompanied by an intent to deceive.

Note too that the scheme or trick must be “*fraudulent”* and here this means dishonest in the traditional moral sense. The circumstances of fraud can range from simple trickery to more complex schemes such as pyramid moneymaking schemes.

### DEVICE, TRICK, OR STRATAGEM DEFINED
These terms are not defined in the Crimes Act 1961. The* Oxford Dictionary[14]* defines them as:

- **Device**: A ‘*plan, scheme or trick’.*
- **Trick**: An action or scheme undertaken to fool, outwit, or deceive.
- **Stratagem**: A cunning plan or scheme especially for deceiving an enemy, or trickery.
Underlying each term are the concepts of deceit and dishonesty.

**THE MENS REA FOR DECEPTION S240**

As with all offences, there is a requirement to prove the general mens rea that accompanies the act. As mentioned above, in the case of Deception, the general mens rea is the fact that the false representation was **intentional and / or deliberate and made knowingly**. Put another way - there was **a deliberate lie**.

*R v Collister* is applicable in looking at the surrounding circumstances to prove intentional and deliberate false representations as well as the knowledge that the representation made was false. Knowingly requires more than a suspicion as per *R v Crooks. *

In terms of a section 240 Deception there must also be a specific intent to deceive by way of the lie. The intent to deceive must be for the purpose to obtain one of the categories of

- Property
- Privilege
- Service
- Pecuniary advantage
- Service
- Valuable consideration
***GENERAL MENS REA – FALSE IN A MATERIAL PARTICULAR***

The general mens rea accompanying the act of a false representation, is knowing or being reckless as to whether their representation is false in a material particular.

In other words, the law is not talking about being mistaken or confused about a representation you are making.

The first thing to note is that material particular simply means some essential or relevant detail or item. In *R v Mallett[15]* it was held that *“a matter will be a ‘material particular’ if it is something important or something that matters.”* So, the representation does not need to be completely false in every detail – just some part of it. This is relevant for representations that are often grounded in an element of truth which makes the representation more believable.

The prosecution must prove that the defendant **knew** that the representation was false in a material particular or was **reckless** as to its falsity. Absolute certainty is not required.

Knowingly and recklessly are forms of general mens rea and the case law’s Crooks and Cameron can be applied here.

In regard to knowing - an honest, even though unreasonable, belief that the representation is true will negate this element of deception.

In regard to reckless - An awareness may be enough to prove that the defendant was reckless as to whether their representation was false. *Cameron v R *governs the test for recklessness so an objective test may come into consideration here. Is the awareness of the falsity enough for the person to recognise a risk of the representation being a lie and knowing this the person proceeded with the representation anyway.

This is important for deceptions where well-intentioned individuals or employees are making representations, they either genuinely believe or are instructed to give through a third party. Deception need not have been made to the person actually delivering over the property, provided the deception was the cause of obtaining of it.

**Example:**

Consider the following example.

*“A small group of individuals sell a device they call a ‘Quantum Energy Harmoniser’, claiming it can cure cancer, reverse aging, and improve financial luck by realigning the user’s quantum field with cosmic vibrations.”* They market it with definitive claims, pseudo-scientific language, and testimonials from others who claim it worked for them.

These individuals genuinely believe in the devices power. They are not trained scientists or medical professionals, but have been convinced – through online communities, personal experience and confirmation bias – that the device works. They are charging $10,000 per device.

Here we have a group of individuals who do not reach the threshold of *‘knowing’* or correctly believe that their representations are patently false in a material particular. However, there may be an argument that their representations were reckless in a material particular.

*Cameron v R* governs the test for recklessness and there may be evidence that individuals turned their mind to the possibility that the claims about the Quantum Energy Harmoniser were false but proceeded anyway.  *Cameron* also includes an objective test – whether a reasonable New Zealander would have recognised the falsity of the claims.

In this case there is no credible scientific basis for the claims to *“cure cancer, reverse aging and improve financial luck”* There is no evidence to verify their claims or show testing or peer review. None of the group are scientists or medical professionals themselves.

The question for the investigator then becomes would a reasonable New Zealander in the same position as the individuals in the group, recognise the falsity or implausibility of the claims in a material particular.

The above scenario is deliberately exaggerated to illustrate the point, but the principles apply in more complex often financial scams, where people are convinced to transfer possession of money or other assets based on false information, often described or dressed up in complex or technical language or in half-truths.

As an investigator where you may not have evidence of knowledge that a representation is false, there may be evidence of recklessness by those making the representation. This is certainly a live issue should the suspect claim they did not know the representation was false.

****INVESTIGATION APPLICATION****
> Investigation Application:
> What evidence could you gather that supports a suspect’s knowledge or awareness that their representations were false?What questions could you ask suspects around their intent, knowledge or awareness of their false representations.

### SPECIFIC MENS REA - INTENTION TO DECEIVE
All acts of deception have the common mental element of an intent to deceive. This means that the false representation is made by the defendant, with the **purpose** of, or to get a **specific result of** deceiving their victim. Implicit in this is that the Defendant had knowledge that the victim is virtually certain to be deceived.

The prosecution must prove the defendant intended the deception to be effective, but this does not extend to the defendant knowing the exact outcome. Put simply, they don’t need to know exactly how their deception is going to play out – they just have to have lied with the specific purpose of pulling the wool over someone’s eyes.

The mens rea required in Deception is captured in this caselaw.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Morley***
> *An intention to deceive requires that the deception is practised in order to deceive the affected party. Purposeful intent is necessary and must exist at the time of the deception. *

Returning to the *“Quantum Energy Harmoniser*" example. David is the self-appointed leader of the group and has so far made $220,000 personally from sales of the device. Even if you have evidence that the group or David individually knew that the claims they were making were nonsense - you must also prove that those claims were made with the **specific purpose to deceive **others into paying money for the machine.

Another way of considering this is that there are:

Two steps involved in the mens rea of Deception

Knowingly telling a lie (general intent) andHaving a deceptive reason to tell that lie (specific intent).### COLLISTER
As always, evidence of purposeful intent to deceive can be inferred by the circumstances of the deception. That is only though if you investigate and gather evidence of those circumstances!

### SECTION 240 (1) (A) OFFENCE
Now that a Deception has been defined and discussed, those concepts can be substituted into the offence itself, found in section 240 (1) (a), commonly known as Obtaining by Deception.

As we have seen the first element of the offence *“by any deception”* contains the actus reus and mens rea of section 240 (1) (a), however there are further elements that must also be proved – that is that

- the deception must be committed without claim of right and
- done in order to obtain something of value
***CLAIM OF RIGHT - DEFENCE***

As with the offence of uses a document, Claim of Right is a defence to what would otherwise be the criminal act of Deception. Claim of right attaches to many offences dealing with **property** and stems from the idea that an individual can deal with their own property how they chose.

Claim of right will be relevant where a person has **acted under a belief** that he or she has a proprietary or possessory right to the property that is obtained.

What is clear is that the belief does not have to be correct, nor does it have to be a reasonable belief, it must merely be an honest belief. Simply put, however wrongheaded if the defendant believed they had a legal right to take or retain possession of a particular item of property, they may have a claim of right.

Put conversely if the offender does not believe they have a possessory claim to the property they are obtaining by deception.

***OUTCOME - OBTAINS***

The offence of Deception requires proof that something of value has been obtained as a result of deceptive conduct.  The Court has indicated that the defendant does not have to be shown to have intended a particular outcome of the deception, but the prosecution must prove that there **was** an outcome and furnish proof of **what that outcome was**.

### WHAT OUTCOME TO CHOOSE
When a Deception is committed on a Victim there must be an outcome resulting from that deceptive practice that results in the deceiver obtaining something of value. The difference between 228 and 240 is that the value obtained by the deceiver is **not limited to financial advantage only**. This is because of the addition of the categories of privilege and benefit when compared to a section 228 offence.

In section 240, the value obtained can be in the form of

- property **or**
- **privilege or**
- service **or**
- pecuniary advantage **or**
- **benefit or**
- valuable consideration
### ANY PROPERTY, PRIVILEGE, SERVICE, PECUNIARY ADVANTAGE, BENEFIT OR VALUABLE CONSIDERATION
Property, service, pecuniary advantage and valuable consideration have been discussed previously. The concepts are the same as 228 except that service, in particular is not limited to financial advantage as it is in section 228.

Similarly, the categories of *“privilege”* or *“benefit”* are added to the categories in deception. These are also not limited to a privilege or benefit of a pecuniary nature. Both of these categories mean a *“special right or advantage”.* Where the benefit or privilege does not involve money, there does not need to be financial loss or injury to the person who has been defrauded.

**Example:**

- Using another person’s gym membership card so that you can use the gym facilities
- Access to medical services
- The withdrawal of an assault charge
- A reduction in sentence for an offence
### OWNERSHIP; OR POSSESSION OF; OR CONTROL OVER
Section 240 requires that a person has obtained ownership of or possession or control over new property or other benefits listed above. What each of these terms means is considered below.

### OWNERSHIP
Ownership is synonymous with the concept of title (see further for discussion of title). In certain instances, a person parting with goods not only relinquishes possession to the other person, but also passes on a legal right of ownership (i.e. ‘title to’) the goods.

One of those instances is when a person parts with one of the categories of value by way of a deception.

### POSSESSION
Possession is discussed in the Basic Legal Concepts module, but its simplest definition is to have on your person.

***CONTROL ***

Control is not defined in the Crimes Act 1961. The* Oxford Dictionary[16] *defines control as: *“the power of directing, command.”* Ordinary usage and the dictionary definition show that a defendant does not need to possess something in order to control it.

The term *‘control’* has been explored in the context of the offence of receiving. Although differences exist between the offences the analysis also applies for this offence.

The prosecution does not need to prove that the defendant was in actual possession of the property. It may be sufficient that the accused exercised control over the property through an agent: *Adams on Criminal Law*, *2001.*

It is insufficient that the dishonestly obtained property was found at the premises of which the defendant had control. It must be proved that the defendant did in fact exercise some control over the particular property: *Adams on Criminal Law, 2001*.

Goods can be *‘obtained’* by a defendant if the goods come under their control, even though they may not have physical possession of them.

**PROPENSITY EVIDENCE**

The Evidence Act 2006 sets clear and consistent rules about what evidence that you may have gathered during an investigation, can be used in Court. This is to ensure trials are fair, efficient, and based on reliable information.

Section 40 of the Evidence Act 2006 defines the rules of propensity evidence. Propensity evidence is particularly relevant for offences of Dishonesty and Deception.

### WHAT IS PROPENSITY EVIDENCE?
****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 40, Evidence Act 2006***
> ***Propensity rule***
> *In this section… propensity evidence-**Means evidence that tends to show a person’s propensity to act in a particular way or have a particular state of mind, being evidence of acts, omission, events, or circumstances with which a person is alleged to have been involved; *

As a general rule, evidence of other examples of offending by the defendant **IS NOT** admissible in court. You can **only** submit evidence that is relevant to the charge before the court.

For example, if you have charged an individual with a serious assault, you cannot submit evidence of a serious assault that the defendant committed a year previously. The previous assault is not relevant to the current charge and is considered too negatively prejudicial for the defendant to have a fair hearing.

This idea is best summed up in *R v Sharma[17]* which concluded that when the court considers whether evidence is admissible, *“the ultimate issue is the degree to which the probative value of the evidence outweighs its prejudicial effect.”*

However, evidence showing a person has a propensity to act in a certain way or to have a particular state of mind may be admissible in a prosecution for dishonestly offences where there is a sufficiently strong connection between the offences.

**Example:**

You have completed an investigation into a defendant for using a stolen credit card to purchase a pair of expensive shoes. During your investigation you discover that the defendant has previously been convicted of committing similar offences. During the trial, the prosecution seeks to introduce the evidence that the same defendant used stolen credit cards on three previous occasions over the past year at the same store to buy electronics and clothing.

*Adams on Criminal Law[18] *suggests that the Court will decide on whether the evidence is propensity evidence or simply regarded as relevant evidence. Persistent dishonesty across the board, embracing a number of offences, would not in itself bring the propensity rule into play. It is more likely to be deployed in **cases involving specific offences with a discernible methodology or pattern. **

Propensity evidence may also be used to disprove a defence of mistake or innocent intention. For example, if you have evidence that a defendant has committed other offences that have a similar methodology you may be able to submit this other offending to prove the intent of the offender. Similarly, this similar offending may be used to counter (or disprove) any defence that may be raise, that the defendant simply made a mistake or didn’t know that the credit card was stolen.

# TITLE
Issues of title and personal property can be complicated. As an investigator you are required to deal with property that is the subject of criminal investigation. Understanding what title means is therefore important.

Put simply, complete ownership is made up of two things:

- Possession
- Title
Title” is not defined in the Crimes Act 1961. The Oxford Dictionary defines title as *“a right or claim to the ownership of property”.* In other words, *“title”* simply means a legal right to the property.

### TITLE AND THEFT
When an item of property is stolen the person who steals the property gains **possession only** of that property and **NEVER** gains title to that property.

If you investigate a laptop computer that has been stolen, and you recover that computer, you will return it to the individual it was stolen from. This is because the thief only ever gained possession of the computer.

### TITLE AND DECEPTION
Unlike above when an item is obtained by Deception the person who gets the property by way of a deception gains not only possession of that property **but gains title** to that property as well.

If you investigate a laptop computer that has been *‘bought’ *with a stolen credit card, and you recover that computer, you need to pause. It may not be legally correct for you to return the computer to the store it was ‘bought’ from.

This is because when the computer was obtained by deception the store **gave** both possession **AND** title to the deceiver. In other words, the store has conferred ownership on the deceiver and what that person has done with that ownership will determine who the computer is returned to.

### OBTAINING BY DECEPTION OR THEFT?
Why does a deceiver get ownership when they commit a Deception? One way to consider this is the mechanism of how the deceiver gained possession.

With the mechanism of theft, the thief **takes** property without the owner’s knowledge, permission or consent.

Whereas the mechanism of deception is that property has been **freely given / handed** over to the offender with the owner’s knowledge, permission or consent (albeit by means of a deception, false representation, or lie)

Because the goods are handed over, the law recognises this as the owner wanting the offender to have the goods and as a result, ownership (title) of the goods as well.

To summarize, there is a material difference between theft and obtaining by deceit which will determine how you deal with property involved with your investigations.

A thief never gets title. But a person, who by deception or other deceit, induces another to hand over the goods with the intention that the title will pass, does get title.

While ownership (possession and title) has been conferred to the deceiver, there may be limitations on the title which we will explore now.

### CONCEPTS OF TITLE
Concepts of title are referred to in the Contract and Commercial Law Act 2017. A useful section to understand the concept of title is section 2.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Contract and Commercial Law Act 2017***
> ***Section 149***
> *The buyer acquires no better title to the goods than the seller had…*

The important concept to understand is that a person **cannot give better title to property than they own**.

### THE PASSING OF TITLE
When the deceiver is given possession and title to property, they can confer that title on anyone who acquires the goods from him or her in good faith for value.

So, to return to the computer *“bought’* with a stolen credit card. The deceiver has been given possession and title to the laptop computer by the shop even although they have been *‘duped’* by the deceiver. The deceiver decides to make some money and sell the computer for $600. He advertises it on Facebook Marketplace, and it is purchased by an individual who knows nothing about the deceptive way the computer was *‘bought’.*

The purchaser of the computer now has been given possession and title to the computer and therefore owns that computer.

In this way ownership can be passed many times. Even although the property was originally obtained by a deception, its ownership has passed to a 3rd (or 4th etc.) party. If you recover the computer at this point it is legally incorrect to return the computer to the store it originally came from.

### VOIDABLE TITLE
Although good title is obtained by the deceiver and it can be passed on, there are some limitations to this title that we will now explore.

The title gained by the deceiver is sometimes referred to as voidable title. Another way to think about the word void, is cancel. To rephrase, the title obtained by the deceiver can be made void – or cancelled.

When title is voided or cancelled it can no longer be passed on to another buyer, even if that buyer purchases the item in good faith.

Again, returning to the laptop computer *‘bought’* with a stolen credit card.

As we know from above, the deceiver has used a stolen credit card to ‘buy’ a laptop computer and now has possession and title to the laptop. However, the store where the computer was bought is informed by the bank that there is a bar on the credit card as it has been reported stolen. Payment for the computer has not been successful. The store makes a complaint to police and puts in an insurance claim for the computer.

**At this point the good title the deceiver has been given is voided or cancelled.**

From this point - the deceiver now only has possession of the computer. He decides to sell the computer for $600 and advertises it on Facebook Marketplace. It is purchased by an individual who knows nothing about the deceptive way the computer was ‘bought’.

Once bought, the genuine purchaser of the computer has been **given possession only** of the computer. The deceiver no longer has title to pass on anymore and a person **cannot give better title to property than they own**.

If you recover the computer at this point the computer is returned to the store it originally came from. Does this mean that the genuine purchaser has no laptop computer and is $600 down? Yes. They are a victim of this offending, just as the shop who ‘sold’ the laptop to the deceiver was.

Other than a court ordering the repayment of the $600 via reparation order, the course of action for the purchaser to recover their loss is via a civil action.

The passing of title is again governed by the Contract and Commercial Law Act 2017.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Contract and Commercial Law Act 2017***
> ***Section 151***
> *This section applies if –**A seller of goods has a voidable title to the goods; but**The seller’s title has not become void at the time of the sale**The buyer acquires a good title to the goods if the buyer buys the goods in good faith and without notice of the seller’s defect of title.*

### WHAT IS NECESSARY TO VOID TITLE?
A positive action must be carried out by the original owner to void title. There are different ways in which title can be avoided including,

- Communication to B. The taking of all possible steps to bring it to B’s notice, (email, text, phone call etc.)
- By advising the police that the item was obtained by fraud. (Making a complaint.)
- Seeking some judicial determination, either through a civil claim seeking an order of the Disputes Tribunal, or Small Claims Tribunal.
### SUMMARY
Until the title is voided or cancelled, the defrauder (or deceiver or liar) has title.

Thus

- if an innocent purchaser buys goods that were** **obtained by deception, then he or she is entitled to good title and obtains ownership.
- if an innocent purchaser buys goods that were** **obtained by deception, but the title has been cancelled, then he or she obtains possession only, and does not own the goods.

> *B steals or unlawfully takes a car from A.*
> *In this situation, B gains possession of the car but not the title, as A did not intend to pass the title on. *
> *A thief never gains title to the goods they steal.*

****INVESTIGATION APPLICATION****
> ***Scenario Three: Obtained by Deception. ***
> *B meets with A with the intention of viewing A’s vehicle for sale. B is a prolific fraudster and has no intention to pay for the vehicle, nor does he have any means to pay the asking price of $15,000.*
> *B creates a fake payment confirmation payment screen shot showing him depositing $15000 into A’s account and leaves with the vehicle. *
> *In this situation, B obtains possession of the car and title because A intends to pass over the title even although that intention has been gained by a lie.*

At this point because A’s consent to take the car has been obtained by deception, B’s title can be regarded as good title.

****INVESTIGATION APPLICATION****
> ***Scenario One: Title Passed to C. ***
> *If B sells the car to C ****before ****the title is voided, and C buys it innocently and legitimately without knowing of B’s deception, the Contract and Commercial Law Act 2017 applies. *
> *B has good title and accordingly when he sells the car to C, C obtains a good title. In this case if the car was recovered, it would be returned to C and A cannot get it back. *
> *The only course of action for A to recoup his 15,000 loss is to seek reparations from the court on A’s prosecution or to seek civil remedy from A*

However, this title can be rendered invalid by A - if and only if - **A actively cancels that title.**

****INVESTIGATION APPLICATION****
> ***Scenario Two: Title Voided***
> *A realises that $15,000 has not been deposited into his account. He immediately tries to text B demanding the money – but there is no reply.*
> *A also makes a complaint to the Police.*
> *At this point the good title that B has gained with the vehicle is CANCELLED (VOIDED) *
> *When B sells the car to C, ****after**** A has taken this action, C ****DOES NOT**** obtain good title. In this case if the car was recovered, it would be returned to A and C cannot get it back.*
> *The only course of action for C to recoup his loss is to seek reparations from the court on A’s prosecution or to seek civil remedy from A*

### A NOTE ON HIRE OR HIRE PURCHASE
A distinction must be made with hiring or hire purchase. When goods are given over for hire or hire purchase – title is never passed. Logically these items are lent out for periods of time, or an individual given possession of an item until it is paid for in full.

In the hire scenario – the very essence of borrowing is that the item will be returned, and ownership was never conferred to another.

In the hire purchase scenario – possession is handed over to an individual on payment of a deposit. Title or ownership is only conferred once payment in full has been made. Until this happens title remains with the with the vendor.

In these cases, there may be charges of theft, deception or receiving, but the goods – if recovered will be returned to the vendor / owner.

# UNDERSTANDING OBTAINING CREDIT BY DECEPTION – SECTION 240(1)(b)
Obtaining credit by deception is associated with section 240 (1)(a) that you studied on the DDC and found in Part 1 of this document.

Here, just as in that offence, a deception must be perpetrated on an individual in exactly the same way as described in part 1. That is

- There must be a representation
- that the offender knows to be false,
- intends to deceive with and which induces the parting of something of value. (see Deception table on page **X.)**
The difference with a charge under section 240 (1)(b) is the **outcome **of the deception. In this case the outcome obtained is a type of **credit** is.

### WHAT YOU MUST PROVE
Thus, under s240(1)(b) you must prove that:

- by deception and without claim of right (discussed in Part 1 of this lesson note.)
- in incurring any debt or liability
- obtained credit
The actus reus and mens rea of this deception is exactly the same as in section 240(1)(a) and the definition of s240(2) explained above and on the DDC. The actus reus and mens rea of the offence is found in the first element of “by any deception” broken down below.

- **Actus reus **- the act of making a representation about a fact that is false.
- **General mens rea** – accompanying the act, is that the defendant **knew** the representation was false.
- **Specific mens rea** – the false representation was conducted for the **specific purpose** of deceiving the victim.
### THE OUTCOME - OBTAINING CREDIT BY INCURRING ANY DEBT OR LIABILITY
The difference in section 240 (1)(b) then is the outcome of the deception, which we will consider now.

Obtaining credit by either incurring a debt or liability creates a **legally enforceable debt**.

### OBTAINS CREDIT
Under s240(1)(b) there must be an obtaining of credit as a result of incurring any debt or liability.

That is, there must be a causative link between the debt or liability and the obtaining of credit. Obtaining credit requires a debtor to gain a creditor’s agreement to the deferred payment of a debt or obligation. The credit obtained must be in respect of a monetary obligation.

Credit is an **intangible** thing. Unlike property for example under section 240 (1) (a), which is tangible – something you can see and touch (for example obtaining a pair of shoes or a laptop).

Simply put just as a victim of a deception hands over property such as shoes under section 240(1)(a), here the victim of a deception hands over permission to pay later in some form.

Therefore, if the defendant obtains a non-monetary benefit, the appropriate charge may be under s240(1)(a), depending on the facts of the case.

The debt (liability or credit) must relate to money and will not extend to items other than money, such as services.

The obtaining must occur contemporaneously with the mens rea of knowing the representation is false and intending to deceive. The offence will not be complete for example, where a person lawfully obtains a loan, and later dishonestly avoids payment.

### CREDIT
Credit refers to the obligation on the debtor to pay or repay, and the time given for them to do so by the creditor. The obligation to pay must be **legally enforceable** against whomever the credit is obtained (usually the defendant).

Fisher v Raven governs the definition of what credit is in New Zealand.

****CASELAW****
> ***Fisher v Raven***
> In *Fisher v Raven[19] *the Court discussed ‘credit’ and held that it refers to the obligation on the debtor to pay or repay, and the time given for them to do so by the creditor. Credit does not extend to an obligation to supply services or goods.

### DEBT OR LIABILITY INCURRED
*Simester and Brookbanks*[20] note that the debt or liability must be legally enforceable. This means that if the contract is void or illegal there will be no offence. The debt or liability (incurred) must be legally enforceable.

*‘Debt’* means money owing from one person to another.

*‘Liability’* means a legally enforceable financial obligation to pay, such as the cost of a meal or a haircut.

### TIME TO PAY
Credit may involve varying periods of time. Credit can be obtained over lengthy periods, as in loan agreements, or for a matter of minutes, such as the time taken to consume a meal or receive a haircut.  The key point is that at the end of the period of credit, payment is expected to be made.

### INTENTION TO DECEIVE
The legal concept of contemporaneous discussed in the Basic Concepts module, is important here. Gaining credit is not of itself unlawful unless it is gained as a result of a false representation accompanied by the intent to deceive the person offering credit.

The intention to deceive by a false representation is essential and must exist **at the time** when the deception is perpetrated. So, when the credit is obtained, a later decision not to repay is insufficient.

Similarly, a person who obtains a personal loan for the purpose of travel but later falls into financial difficulty and cannot repay the loan has not offended under this section.

**Example:**

In *R v McKay*[21], the defendant booked into a motel and offered to pay in advance. He was told to pay in the morning when leaving although the following morning he left without paying.

In this matter the Court of Appeal held that the credit had been obtained on booking in but at that time the accused did not possess an intent to deceive.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v McKay***
> *“…it cannot be inferred from the conduct that the credit obtained at a time when… he offered to pay was obtained by… fraud.”*

### Continuing representations
A representation can sometimes be considered continuing. In simple terms, a statement may begin as true but later become false. If it continues to be made after that point, it becomes a deception.

*Adams on Criminal Law[22]* said that “*in many cases a representation, whether by words or conduct, may be of continuing effect. Thus, entry into a restaurant and ordering a meal would usually be a representation that one will follow the normal practice and pay for the meal at the appropriate time. If, during the course of the meal, a diner decides to avoid payment, the continuing representation of an intention to pay will become false and any subsequent obtaining of food will come within S.240.”*

For example, you might have an extended line of credit with a bank or other lender granted under specific conditions. If those conditions change and you are no longer eligible, deliberately failing to inform the bank in order to keep the credit, could amount to deception.

### Delay or non-payment of debt
Intent to deceive is not disclosed merely because there is:

- delay or non-payment of the debt, or
- an inability to perform a bona fide intention
**Example:**

For example, to incur a debt and then be unable to pay through unforeseen circumstances, loss of money or oversight is **not** deceit.

### PAYMENT WITHHELD
Intent to deceive does not exist where payment is withheld because of genuine dissatisfaction with the service.

For example, where payment of the cost of a meal is refused because of the quality of the food or similar with the quality of a haircut.

### OBTAINING CREDIT BY DECEPTION EXAMPLES
- overdraft facilities on an account
- obtaining a loan from a bank or other lending institution
- obtaining credit during the course of sitting down in a restaurant, ordering and eating a meal
- obtaining credit during the course of sitting down in a barber, hairdressers or beauty salon
- If a shopkeeper mistakes the customer for someone else and gives them credit, the customer is guilty of obtaining credit by deception if they are aware of the mistake and accept the credit with no intention of paying later.
# UNDERSTANDING CAUSE LOSS BY DECEPTION – SECTION 240(1)(d)
The final outcome described as a result of a Deception offence is found in section 240 (1)(d) – causing loss by deception. The outcome is in the name - loss.

### LOSS
The term ‘loss’ is not defined by statute, but in most cases will involve financial detriment to the victim.

The leading case law in New Zealand that defines what loss is, is *R v Morley*

The charges in this case arose in relation to a property Morley attempted to purchase in the Napier area. The vendors entered into an unconditional agreement with Morley that he would purchase the property for $450,000, with a $20,000 deposit to be paid in 2 days’ time with the full amount to be paid on settlement in 2 months’ time.

The deposit was not paid but no steps were taken by the vendor to avoid the contract with Morley. There was no other indication prior to settlement that the contract with Morley would not proceed. On the settlement day, the vendors lawyers were advised that the transaction would not proceed.

Prior to signing the agreement with Morley, the vendor had signed an agreement to purchase another property, subject to the sale of her own. On the day that the vendor entered into the unconditional agreement with Morley, she agreed that the sales and purchase on her house could go unconditional.

When the vendors agreement with Morley did not proceed, she was still obliged to sell her property as she had an unconditional agreement to buy another. She was forced to find and an alternative purchaser for her home at a price $10,000 below that which the appellant had offered. Due to the time this took, she also incurred penalty interest of approximately $1900 as well as additional legal costs on the delayed settlement of the purchase of her new property. She also incurred legal costs on the alternative purchase of her home.

The court found that by entering into agreements Morley represented he had means to fulfil contracts when he knew this was not so. The vendor was deceived by the representation and acted in reliance on the truth of the representation which cause financial loss.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Morley***
> *“…the loss alleged by the victim must have been induced by, or caused in reliance, upon the deception. But the deception need not be the only operative factor, so long as it played a material part in occasioning the loss.”*

The Court considered that the loss flowing from the deception should be assessed by the extent to which the complainant’s position before the deception had been diminished or impaired.

The loss caused by deception must be in the nature of a **direct **loss. Indirect losses, such as expectation loss (loss of a bargain) and loss of anticipated future profits are not included.

Where it is alleged that a person has, by deception, caused a loss, the prosecution does not have to prove any financial gain at all by the defendant, or even any gain of something intangible. It is sufficient to prove that the victim has suffered a loss.

It is insufficient for the prosecution to prove that an alleged offender has intended to cause a loss. Actual loss must be shown and must be specified. This is due to the punishment provision, s 241, which provides for different levels of punishment depending on the amount that has been lost due to the deception — the maximum penalty of seven years is available if the amount is over $1,000, one year for amounts over $500 but no more than $1,000 and a term of only three months is available where the sum does not exceed $500.

### WHAT IS REQUIRED TO BE PROVED 
To summarise what the prosecution must prove in terms of loss, *R v Morley* holds that;

- the loss was caused by a deception
- it was reasonably foreseeable some more than trivial loss would occur, but
- the prosecution need not prove the loss was intentionally caused
Thus, there must be loss to “*any other person*”, but there is no requirement that there be any benefit to anyone

### TO ANY OTHER PERSON
Someone other than the defendant. Legislation provides a wide definition of the term *“person*” that incorporates not only real people, but also companies and other organisations.

# UNDERSTANDING THEFT BY PERSON IN A SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP
### INTRODUCTION 
While investigating deception and related offences, the investigator should consider other offences that may be disclosed. Common among these is the offence of *“Theft by person in special relationship”* (s220 CA 1961).

This section aims to hold individuals accountable for breaching the trust placed in them and misappropriating the assets or funds they are responsible for safeguarding. It applies to individuals who hold positions of trust or authority over another person’s property or financial matters.

### PROOF OF SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP AND KNOWLEDGE OF SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP
The law recognises that certain relationships require a higher standard of integrity and trust, and the law seeks to uphold the integrity of these special relationships.

This offence requires proof that a special relationship is in existence and that the parties know of the existence of the special relationship and the obligations that come within the bounds of this relationship.

In many cases the nature of the special relationship will **depend on the circumstances**, and **may** include individual relationships such as employer, employee relationship, a fiduciary relationship, a contractual relationship, or other individual or business relationships.

A fiduciary relationship is an example of a legal obligation of trust between parties. There is a duty of loyalty and care, where the parties **must** act in the best interests of the other and there is a certain standard of care, skill and diligence expected of those in this position. Not acting in accordance with these standards often attracts penalty in law and the behaviour is considered criminal.

**Example:**

Relationships that can be considered special relationships under section 220.

- treasurer of a sports club or charity
- trustee and beneficiaries
- Director of a company
- Marae trustees
- School parent teacher association president / treasurer
- Contracted services to a company
- Employer / employee relationships
Regardless of how obvious the special relationship seems – you must investigate and prove that it exists and furnish evidence of it in court who will decide if the relationship is special under section 220.

### SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP IS A QUESTION OF LAW
Subsection 4 of section 220 states

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 220, Crimes Act 1961 ***
> ***Theft by person in a special relationship ***
> *For the purposes of subsection (1), it is a question of law whether the circumstances required any person to account or act in accordance with any requirements.*

*Nisbet v R* states that the circumstances that give rise to a legal requirement obliging the defendant to deal with property in a particular way “*is properly a matter for the judge…”*

### THE OFFENCE
Section 220 (1) sets out the criteria for dealing with property in circumstances where there is a special relationship.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 220, Crimes Act 1961 ***
> ***Theft by person in a special relationship ***
> *This section applies to any person who has received or is in possession of or has control over, any property on terms or in circumstances that the person knows require the person – **To account to any other person for the property, or for any proceeds arising from that property; or**To deal with the property, or any proceeds arising from the property, in accordance with the requirements of any other person.*

Section 220 (2) sets out the actual offence, which is essentially anyone who intentionally doesn’t account for property or deal with property according to the expectations set out in in subsection 1, will be guilty of theft.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 220, Crimes Act 1961 ***
> ***Theft by person in a special relationship ***
> *Every one to whom subsection (1) applies commits theft who intentionally fails to account to the other person as so required, or intentionally deals with the property, or proceeds of the property, otherwise than in accordance with those requirements.*

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR THEFT IN A SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP
This section creates for different kinds of acts of theft.

This means that there may be an offence under s220 (2) where the defendant has intentionally:

- failed to account to any other person for property received by the defendant
- failed to account to any other person for the proceeds of property received by the defendant
- dealt with property received by the defendant otherwise than in accordance with the requirements of some other person
- dealt with the proceeds of the property received by the defendant otherwise than in accordance with the requirements of some other person.
Put simply, the actus reus of the offence in subsection (2) requires intentionally failing** to account** for property or proceeds or intentionally **dealing** with property or proceeds in a way contrary to the special relationship explained in subsection (1).

**Example:**

An action or inaction on behalf of the defendant must be proved,

- Diverting fundraiser money into a personal account rather than the account of the organisation.
- A director of a company purchasing property or investments in own name rather than the company.
- A trustee refusing to distribute trust assets fairly or in accordance to the deed of trust
### THE MENS REA FOR THEFT IN A SPECIAL RELATIONSHIP
Section 220 applies to a person who, *“… has received or is in possession of, or has control over, any property on terms or in circumstances that the person knows require the person—“*

An offence under s220 expressly requires two different mental elements.

### KNOWLEDGE 
Knowledge is discussed in the Basic Concepts module and is a mens rea in several offences in the Crimes Act 1961. As such, the case law *R v Crooks* is applicable here for what proof of knowledge requires.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Crooks***
> *Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.*

Firstly, the defendant must know the requirements of the special relationship, in that they must know that the acquisition of the property was on terms which require the defendant to account for the property or its proceeds, or to deal with the property or its proceeds **in accordance with the direction of another person. **

This requirement will be met if the defendant is aware there are terms of some kind affecting his or her freedom of action; it should not be necessary that the precise details of those obligations be known.

### INTENTIONALLY  
Secondly, the defendant must have *“intentionally”* departed from his or her obligations: Adams on Criminal Law.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Collister ***
> *Intent can be inferred from the circumstances****.***

In *R v Sizemore* the Court said there is no requirement that the accused be shown to have acted dishonestly or without claim of right. The mere fact of intentionally failing to perform one’s obligations is sufficient for liability.

### SUMMARY
**An offence under section 220 requires proof of two core elements:**

- that he or she have received property on terms or in circumstances which, to the defendant’s knowledge, affect what the defendant may do with the property or its proceeds **or**
- require the defendant to act in accordance with the requirements of another person, **and**
- that the defendant intentionally departed from the relevant obligation.
The Court of Appeal in *Tallentire v R* approved the following way to consider this offence and provides a useful checklist for investigators when considering what evidence they have to gather to prove the charge.[23] On each point ask, *“how do I prove this?”*

- Did the defendant have control over property?
- Was the property in the control of the defendant, in circumstances that required him to deal with the property, or any proceeds arising from the property, in accordance with the requirements of any other person?
- Did the defendant know of those circumstances? And,
- Did the defendant intentionally deal with the property, or any proceeds of the property, otherwise than in accordance with those requirements?
# UNDERSTANDING FORGERY
### INTRODUCTION 
Forgery under section 256 is a serious offence in New Zealand law, which essentially protects the integrity and authenticity of documents and the way in which they are intended to be used.

The essence of forgery lies in its deceptive nature, where the perpetrator fabricates a document, to appear genuine, intending it to mislead others as genuine, for personal gain or to cause harm.

The traditional idea of a talented artist copying a grand master’s piece of art is indeed a forgery. However, the offence of Forgery has a far broader definition than canvas and paint.

The two key components of Forgery to consider are

- What is a false document?
- At the time the false document was made, what was it intended to be used for?
There is no requirement the defendant *“use”* the document as long as the intention to use it is present.

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR FORGERY
The actus reus (that which constitutes the physical act of the offence) can be summed up as

- Making a false document (s256(1)) and
- Making a false document knowing it to be false (s256(2))
Remember the actus reus must be deliberately or intentionally committed and in this case must include some sort of physical action to create a false document.

### DOCUMENT
Although you do not need to learn the full definition, you should try to grasp the concept of *‘document’*. The definition starts with the word ‘means’ rather than ‘includes’ because the definition is meant to be exhaustive rather than open-ended.

If an item does not fall within the broad definition, it is not within the scope of the offence.

One way to consider a document is whether the writing conveys the same message as the spoken word in the minds of all people able to read it. Another is to consider whether the document imparts information.

### FALSE DOCUMENT
The concept of whether a document is false or not can be confusing. A starting point is to consider whether the document tells a lie or intends to convey a lie. False Document is defined in section 255 of the Crimes Act.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 255, Crimes Act 1961 ***
> ***Interpretation***
> ***False document means a document -***
> *(a)	of which the whole or any material part purports to be made by any person who did not make it, or by a fictitious person; or *
> *(b)	of which the whole or any material part purports to be made by or on behalf of any person who did not authorise its making, or on behalf of a fictitious person; or *
> *(c)	of which the whole or any material part has been altered, whether by addition, insertion, deletion, obliteration, erasure, removal, or otherwise, and that purports to have been altered by or on behalf of a person who did not alter it or authorise its alteration, or by or on behalf of a fictitious person; or *
> *(d)	that is, in whole or in part, a reproduction of any other document, and that purports to have been made by or on behalf of a person who did not make it or authorise its making, or by or on behalf of a fictitious person; or *
> *(e)	that is made in the name of a person, either by that person or by that person's authority, with the intention that it should pass as being made by some other person who did not make it, or by a fictitious person.*

The statutory definition of a false document is intended to be exhaustive.  To have been forged it must meet the definitions of both *‘false’* and *‘document’*.

### MAKING
Making involves the defendant either creating or **doing something to or on** a document. As defined earlier the definition of a document is broad and includes anything that conveys information.

### MAKING A FALSE DOCUMENT
There are many ways listed in section 255 in which a false document can be created. The following is a list of some of the common ways a false document is made. The list is not exhaustive.

- A document claiming to be made by someone who did not make it.
- A document claiming to be made on the authorization of someone who did not in fact authorize its making.
- A document has been materially altered by
- Additions
- Insertions
- Deletions
- Obliterations
- Erasures
- Removal of material or otherwise
- Reproduction of a document claiming to be made on the authorization of a person who did not in fact authorize its reproduction.
### MATERIAL ALTERATION
An alteration is where there has been some change to a document’s content by way of the addition of fresh material (addition or insertion) or by the removal of material previously evident (deletion, erasure or removal) or by words being made illegible (obliteration). The alteration has not been authorised by a real person or has been made by a fictitious person.

A material alteration often increases the value or negotiability of a document.  A false document can be made by making material alterations to a genuine document.

**Example: **

It is legal (subject to copyright laws) to buy or own or produce a print of an artwork. The print is not a forgery. However, a painting or artwork claiming to be by a particular artist, when it is not – is a false document.

Consider the paintings below. Both are documents as the signatures on them convey information. The signatures allow the document to claim that they are made or created by Claude Monet and Jackson Pollock, thus making each painting a masterpiece, worth millions of dollars.

Both of these paintings are false documents.

The Monet was discovered to be a forgery in 2008. One of the indications of this was that the signature is traced and drawn over. Similarly, the signature of the Pollock proved the painting to be a forgery, as the signature of the artist was spelt incorrectly.  The Monet has been removed from the Wallraf-Richartz Museum in Germany. The Pollock sold just prior to its discovery as a forgery for just over 17 million dollars and was the subject of an FBI investigation.

**Example:**

A couple own a large property, that although immaculately presented, does not comply to the NZ Building Code, as it has serious defects with its cladding that will require hundreds of thousands of dollars to remedy.

The couple decide to sell their home, however, do not want to disclose the cladding defects as it would severely impact the sales price of the property.

They order a LIM (Land Information Memorandum) from the council which lists the property’s non-compliance with the NZ building Code. Using their computer the couple remove the notation regarding the non-compliance and send this altered LIM to their Real Estate Agent who uploads it to the on-line listing of the property.

The altered LIM is relied on by the purchasers of the property. The new owners do not discover the fact that the LIM was falsified until four years later when they order their own copy of the LIM from the council in preparation to sell the property again.

The original LIM has been materially altered by the deletion and erasure of the information regarding non-compliance with the building code. This passes the document off as being written or authorized by the Auckland City Council.

### THE GENERAL MENS REA - KNOWLEDGE OF FALSITY
The general mens rea accompanying the act of a making a false document, **knowingly** – that is knowing or with knowledge that the document they are making is false. The court has expressly held that such knowledge is essential for section 256.[24]

You do not need to intend that a particular person will be affected by the false document, however you must prove that there is clear knowledge that the document is false **at the time of creating or making it**. The maker must know that the document is lying in some way or that it is claiming to be something it is not or authorized by someone that has not authorized it. R v Crooks as discussed in the Basic Concepts module, requires there be more than a suspicion that the document is lying in some way. As an investigator you must produce proof of this mental element.

### THE SPECIFIC MENS REA FOR FORGERY
Section 256 does not require the false documents to be used – the offence is complete as soon as the false document is made with the required mens rea (intent)

Intent to deceive is not an essential element of forgery. Nonetheless, the prosecution must prove the specific mens rea which is having an intention to use the false document for the purpose of obtaining something. At the time of making the false document, the defendant**, knowing** that the document was false, had **intended** either:

- to use the false document to obtain, or
- that the false document be used or acted on as genuine
It is not enough that the document is false and that its maker knows it is false.  It must also be proved that the offender **intended** to use that which is false to obtain something of value.

Similar to Section 228 Take / Uses a Document, Section 256 (1) requires an intention to obtain property, service, pecuniary advantage or valuable consideration.

Subsection (2) applies where there is not necessarily any specific intention to obtain any financial or other specified advantage. However, the defendant must have intended that the document be used to mislead.

Examples of forgery

- Writing an examination paper in the name of another person who is required to sit the exam.
- Pre-dating a deed or other legal document to give it priority over another.
- Forging letters of recommendation for inclusion in a CV that are necessary to obtain a position.
- Falsely completing a statement of service on a witness summons.
# UNDERSTANDING USING FORGED DOCUMENTS
While Section 256 deals with the creation of forged or false documents, section 257 specifically deals with the **physical use** of those forged or false documents. The offence consists of a mixture of knowledge of the falsity of the document in combination with its actual use, to obtain some sort of benefit or that it is held out in some way as true. (To mislead)

### THE ACTUS REUS FOR USES A FORGED DOCUMENT
The meanings of the term use and what a use entails, is the same as in Section 228, Uses a document. In other words, some sort of physical and purposeful use.

Of course, you must also prove that the document is indeed a false document under section 255.

### THE GENERAL MENS REA ACCOMPANYING THE ACT
Here the general mens rea accompanying the act is that the defendant **knew** the document was a forgery the time of the physical act of using, dealing with or acting upon it. Another way to think about this is that they **knowingly** used a forged document.

However, it is not sufficient just to prove that they knew the document was false. Implicit in this knowledgeable act is that the *‘user’* knew that the document had been ‘made’ with the knowledge and intent required under section 256 of the Crimes Act 1961.

### THE SPECIFIC MENS REA FOR USING FORGED DOCUMENTS
Section 257 describes three specific purposes that the forged document is used for. These specific mens rea can be summarised as

- Intent to obtain something of value. (Property, privilege, service, pecuniary advantage, benefit, or valuable consideration.)
- Intentional use or dealing with the forgery as if it were genuine.
- Intentionally causing another to use or deal with the forgery as if it were genuine.
In the case of intent to obtain, it will always need to be shown that the obtaining was a result of the use of the document. This raises the same concept of causation as is posed by the offence of obtaining by deception under s 240.

It is important that any investigation into using forged documents furnishes proof as to what the intended purpose of the use was. This is similar to the offences in section 228 and 240.

Remember the difference between section 256 and 257, is in the act. Forgery is **creating** the false document and section 257 involves **actual use of it**. Many of the mental elements are similar in concept. These differences are shown in the diagram below.

**Examples:**

- Writing an examination paper in the name of another person who is required to sit the exam.
- Forging letters of recommendation CV and submitting the CV for consideration.
- Falsely completing a statement of service on a witness summons and attaching to a file.
- Forging someone else’s signature on a document and submitting it online
### SIMILARITY OF DECEPTION OFFENCES
As covered at the beginning of this module, many offences in the Crimes Act 1961 share elements, and are similar in action. A set of circumstances involving a dishonesty crime may be charged under a number of different offences.

For example, a ‘*Uses a Document’* under section 228 may equally be a 240 (1) (a) ‘*Deception’*. Similarly, a *‘Forgery’* may also be a 240 ‘*Deception’* by a document or conduct.

Many offences share the same actus reus or use the same general mens rea or intent to obtain or deceive. Many share other legal concepts such as the obligation for the evidence to show a causation between the dishonest act and financial / non-financial gain.

What is important as an investigator is that you understand the legal concepts and requirements and investigate thoroughly. You can then charge according to what the evidence dictates. It may be that there are two or more charges that are appropriate. Seek supervisor or prosecutor advice if this is the case.

For the purposes of assessment, you will be required to make one choice based on the facts of the scenario, as to what offence is appropriate. You will be asked to show your understanding of what is required for evidential sufficiency of that chosen offence. There will often be more than one appropriate charge and credit will be given for all appropriated charges chosen, so long as you make an appropriate choice and argue well.

# UNDERSTANDING COMPUTER CRIME AND DISHONESTY
### INTRODUCTION
Computers, including portable devices, are in common use for a myriad of legitimate purposes in every day, and commercial life. They are also commonly used for dishonest and fraudulent purposes. Sections 248 to 252 of the Crimes Act 1961 deal with crimes involving computers and the sections relevant to the detective development program are detailed below.

This offence has some common terms, definitions and elements shared with offences discussed earlier in this module. These include terms and definitions such as dishonestly, claim of right, by deception, property, privilege, benefit, pecuniary advantage, service, and causing loss etc.

*“Computer”* is not defined under the Act.  This is not problematic where the subject of the enquiry is a desktop computer, laptop or server. The Courts will consider the ordinary usage of the device at the time of the offence.

### A COMPUTER SYSTEM
What a computer or computer system actually is, is defined in section 248, and is intended to be broad. However, as technology advances and microprocessors are integrated into a wider variety of devices, what is a computer and what is a peripheral device may become less clear to an investigator. For the purposes of this offence what is a computer will be decided or interpreted by the courts. In most cases a computer system will be obvious on the face of it, if not seek advice.

### ACTUS REUS FOR SECTION 249 (1) &amp; (2) AND SECTION 252
The actus reus of section 249 (both subsections (1) &amp; (2)) and section 252 is the same.

It involves a physical act to access a computer system directly or indirectly. Directly, for example, via a laptop or desktop computer and keyboard, or remotely (indirectly) by way of another device, secure shell, or virus / code, with or without a GUI (Graphical user interface).

Methods of accessing a computer system are evolving quickly and recently involve scams where an offender accesses a computer system by deceiving the victim to do so themselves. Adams comments that the word ‘indirectly’ intends to ensure the section applies to cases where the defendant has procured someone, by threats or deception, to do the necessary physical acts for the defendant.

However it is initiated*, “access”* is widely defined and covers almost all interactions with a computer system and requires that the person instructing or communicating with the computer system has some form of connection with the computer system through which instructions or communications may pass.

### REMOTE ACCESS
A person is not required to be physically present in order to access a computer system.

***Example:***

The case of *R v Walker* highlights offending carried out from a remote computer.  Here a teenager developed software and code which enabled him to remotely control computers through a virtual robot, commonly known as ‘bot’ or ‘bot net’. He then used his bot code to access thousands of computer systems without authorisation.

The employment of his ‘bot’ code was the intentional and deliberate act to access a computer system, albeit from another city.

### THE MENS REA ACCOMPANYING THE ACT
The mens rea that accompanies the act of access is ‘intentional’ or *‘deliberate’*.  What is important is that an individual **intentionally** makes access to the system – **how** they do that is a matter that as an investigator you must also furnish evidence of. You must prove that the access was deliberately done – and not by means of an innocent mistake for example.

Further, you must prove that the access to any computer system is done dishonestly, or by deception. These terms encompass concepts of the fact that access was made without authority or consent or with intent to deceive (deception).

***THE SPECIFIC INTENT***

The specific intent of this offence is clearly spelt out in subsection (2), as with similar fraud offences, with intent to obtain any property, privilege, service, pecuniary advantage, benefit or valuable consideration - terms we have discussed above.

This is not so clearly defined in subsection (1) as the value obtained is described as an outcome rather than any part of a mens rea. However, the Supreme Court has clarified the legal position on this when it considered the legislative intent behind the offence in *Dixon v R.*

The court emphasised that although the word "purpose" is not explicitly stated in the section, it is implicitly central to the offence. Section 249 targets those who access computer systems with intent to obtain a benefit dishonestly.

*"The mischief that s 249(1)(a) is aimed at is directed at those whose purpose is to access computer systems with the aim of obtaining a benefit. Whilst the section does not refer to “purpose” it is implicit that that is the mischief to which it is aimed. It is the purpose for which a person wrongfully and dishonestly accesses a computer system to which the legislation is directed, and the offence arises where a person accesses a computer system with that purpose and obtains the sought after benefit."*

As an investigator you must prove a deliberate act to access a computer system contemporaneous with the purpose to obtain something of value either dishonestly or by way of deception.

***Example:***

- Using someone else’s logon to access a computer via laptop
- Remote access via another device such as a PlayStation
- Remote access via virus, bot, or shell
- Access a Program or application by way of username or password
- Accessing a computer system by way of telephone scam falsely representing themselves as a bank employee or police officer etc.
### DISTINCTION BETWEEN S249 (1) AND S 249 (2).
The distinction between these two offences is reflected in the words “*thereby” “obtains”* and *“intent”.*

Section 249(1) is directed at the situation where the actus reus is complete and a person has accessed a computer system and** actually obtains** the offending material or causes loss.

Section 249(2) is directed at someone who has completed the actus reus and accessed a computer system, as in s249 (1).  Here no outcome is required, the offence is complete as soon as the access to the computer system is achieved with the required intent **regardless of the result**.

This is similar to section 228 Uses a Document, where the actual obtaining of the category of value (property service pecuniary advantage or valuable consideration) is immaterial as the offence is complete at the time of the use with only intent to obtain required.

### CATEGORIES OF VALUE
Categories of value that have been obtained or that were intended to be obtained, have been canvassed earlier in this lesson note and are similar. The exception to this is the category of value of Benefit which will be considered below

### BENEFIT
In s228 a benefit **is** limited to a benefit of financial advantage, whereas in Deception the benefit and privilege categories **are not** limited by financial advantage. Here section 249 follows the definition of s240 Deception and **is not limited** to financial or pecuniary advantage.

***Example:***

In *Police v Le Roy* a man was charged under s249(1)(a) of the Crimes Act in that he *“... dishonestly, and without claim of right, accessed a computer system [and obtained] a benefit, namely access to the electronic mail messages and password for [Jane Doe’s] email account stored on Telstra Clear’s computer system.”*

In that case, the man had become an employee of Telstra Clear and used his position to access the email account of his former partner maintained via the internet service provider.

A protection order was in place against Le Roy and the Court held that accessing the email account gave him a meaningful benefit in that he could overcome the absence of contact enforced by that order. The Court in this matter said *“benefit” *in s 249(1)(a) CA bore its natural meaning of **any advantage and was not limited to financial or pecuniary advantage.**

Later judgements endorsed this by stating that *“privilege” *or *“benefit”* in the section can extend to such things as passwords or access codes which permit a person to access computer files or data or to effect operations on a computer which could not be done without those codes or passwords.

A non-pecuniary benefit could fall within the ambit of the section. The Court further held that requiring that the benefit be obtained dishonestly was an unsustainably narrow approach to interpretation of s249(1)(a) and that the **dishonesty related to the conduct in obtaining access** to a computer system. The Court noted that common sense strongly supported the view that, given the parties’ sour relationship, the appellant could not access the email account without the consent or authority of the ex-partner, which he clearly did not have.

To highlight important points from this case…

- A non-pecuniary benefit could fall within the ambit of the section.
- The dishonest element of the offence relates to the conduct in obtaining access to the computer system itself (not the obtaining of a category of value)
- Not having consent or authority to access the computer system is evidence of (or points to) that access being dishonest (or deceptive) as per section 217 definition of *‘Dishonestly’.*
****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 217, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Dishonestly, ****in relation to an act or omission, means done or omitted without belief that there was express or implied consent to, or authority for, the act or omission from a person entitled to give such consent or authority.*

### LOSS
One of the ways in which causes loss may be charged, is when a category of value is difficult or impossible to gather evidence of. In this case causing loss to a person may be an appropriate charge.

Here the dishonest or deceptive access is made covering the same elements, completing the actus reus of the offence. Evidence of loss caused or intended is required. There must be a strong causal link to the loss, however the loss does not have to be known to the Victim.

An example of this is found in R v Billingsley. Mr. Billingsley found himself in possession of a laptop computer owned by the local primary school. Mr. Billingsley then accessed the laptop in excess of 500 times to use the internet. He admitted that he had been using it without charge, because the school had been meeting its bills from Telecom**, oblivious to the fact that a substantial amount represented his unauthorised access to the internet.**

To summarise the person or entity that suffers loss does not have to be aware of the loss at the time of the offence. In reality, internal investigations and audits will unearth dishonest access offences that were not known about prior to the investigation and audit and often be the catalyst for a complaint to Police.

**UNDERSTANDING ACCESSING A COMPUTER WITHOUT AUTHORISATION**

Unlike section 249 above, the offence of Accessing a Computer without Authorisation does not require an obtaining or intent to obtain an outcome.

### ACTUS REUS OF SECTION 252
Access to a computer system is the same as section 249, in that there must be a deliberate or intentional act that results in access being gained to a computer system. Once again, the person does not need to be physically present, and access can be gained directly or indirectly.

### MENS REA OF SECTION 252
The person who has gained access to the computer system must **know** or be **reckless** as to whether they are authorized to have gained access to the computer system.

Both these forms of mens rea have been discussed in detail earlier in this lesson note and require evidence to be put before the court in terms of what the person knew or suspected in regard to their authorisation or consent to the access they have made. As knowledge and recklessness are form of mens rea, R v Collister applies in that proof of this can be inferred from the circumstances.

### KNOWLEDGE
Knowledge requires evidence of what the person accessing the computer system actually knew at the time of access (subjective), as well as an analysis of what a reasonable person would know in the same circumstances (objective)

Evidence of knowledge that an individual was **not** authorised to access includes the nature of the relationship with the computer ‘owner’, any contractual terms, code of conduct and course of business / life practice and other circumstances pointing to access rights of the computer system.

### RECKLESSNESS
Being reckless as to whether someone is authorised to access a computer system, will involve analysis on the part of the investigator. Has the offender turned their mind to whether their access or what they are doing on the computer system is authorised and do proceed with the access any way.

### OTHER PURPOSE
Subsection (2) is created to avoid any misinterpretation of the intent of the section. The offence does not apply to a person who has authorisation, but who accesses the computer system for a purpose **other** than the one for which he or she was given access. In those circumstances an offence will be committed if the activity involves a dishonest purpose under s249.

### THE USES OF BOTH OFFENCES IN CONJUNCTION WITH EACH OTHER
There will be situations where access to a computer system has been made and once made, further activity can lead to further access for with dishonest purpose. An example of this is again in *R v Walker* cited above.

***Example:***

The teenage Walker developed and used code to create the bots that led to two charges of accessing computer systems without authorisation. (See example above). The ways in which he **then** used the bots, led to other charges of accessing a computer system for dishonest purpose. For example, the bots went on to access *“tens of thousands”* of other computers. On some of those computers his botnet covertly installed malware and Walker was paid for this viral advertising by the owners of the adware. As a result, he was charged with 249 (2)(a) accessing a computer system with intent to dishonestly obtain approximately $36,000 NZD.
