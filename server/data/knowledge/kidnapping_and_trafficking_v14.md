# UNDERSTANDING ABDUCTION &amp; KIDNAPPING
### Introduction
The offences of abduction and kidnapping essentially involve unlawfully depriving a person of their liberty against their will. The elements of the two offences are similar. The difference is found in the specific intent of the offender at the material time.

In cases of abduction, the offender is often sexually motivated. With the offence of kidnapping, there are a number of specific intents that may be applicable such as to imprison a person or to hold them for ransom.

### The actus reus for Abduction &amp; Kidnapping 
The actus reus and elements that accompany the actus reus of Abduction &amp; Kidnapping are identical in sections 208 and 209. The difference between the offences of Kidnapping and Abduction lies in the specific intent of each offence – or the reason for the Abduction or Kidnapping.

The actus reus (that which constitutes the physical act of the offence) and elements associated with the act can be summed up in both as:

- Unlawfully
- Takes away OR Detains
- A person
- Without consent OR with consent obtained by fraud.
### General mens rea accompanying the actus reus 
When considering the physical acts that make up the offence, there is always the requirement that those acts are accompanied by a general intent to commit the offence, as spelt out in the following definition.

In sections 208 and 209, the physical act of taking away or detaining a person must be done unlawfully and without the valid consent of the Victim.

Although not spelt out in the elements themselves, the act of taking away or detaining must also be carried out with the general intent of deliberately or intentionally as well as knowingly.

This is confirmed by the courts in ***R v M**** *and will be discussed in the “understanding mens rea” section of this lesson. For now, simply remember the actus reus of taking away or detaining a person must be **intentionally** and **knowingly** committed.

### Unlawfully
Unlawfully means “*without lawful justification, authority or excuse*”.

In most cases of abduction and kidnapping, where a person is taken against their will with one of the specified criminal intents, it will be apparent from the circumstances that the offender’s actions were unlawful.

### Taking away
Taking away represents the actus reus of the offences of Kidnapping and Abduction.

“*Taking away*” generally refers to situations where the victim is physically removed from one place to another, for example a baby that is taken from its mother at gunpoint and driven to another location to be held for ransom.

Taking away is not however restricted to physical removal by the offender. Taking away can encompass the victim physically moving themselves from a place that they wish to be.

***R v Wellard*** defines the element *‘takes away’. *In ***R v Wellard*** a man approached a young couple and told them, falsely, that he was a police officer looking for drugs. He asked the young woman to accompany him, which she did, believing she had to follow his instructions. The girl walked about 100 yards with him and got into the back of his car, before being rescued by her boyfriend.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Wellard ***
> *The essence of the offence of kidnapping is the “deprivation of liberty coupled with a carrying away from the place where the victim wants to be”.*

### Detains
Detaining represents an alternative actus reus of the offences of Kidnapping and Abduction.

*“Detaining”* is an active concept rather than a passive one. It involves doing something to impose a constraint or restraint on the person detained.

Whether a person has or has not been detained will therefore be a question of fact and degree to be decided on the circumstances of each case. Whether someone is free to leave is a matter of circumstance of the case.

***R v Pryce*** defines the element ‘*detains*’:

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Pryce***
> *Detaining is an active concept meaning to “keep in confinement or custody”. This is to be contrasted to the passive concept of “harbouring” or mere failure to hand over.*

### Timing of the Detention
The Crimes Act does not specify a minimum period for which a person’s freedom must be curtailed before they are deemed to have been “*detained*”.

Mere delay or keeping back for a short or trifling period of time may not constitute detaining; there will be a point where a detention is so fleeting as to not amount to an intrusion for the purposes of sections 208 – 210.

### Example:
In ***Boyd v R*** a woman, who had driven to a secluded lake to walk her dog, was approached by a stranger who robbed her of her wallet. The man then stood next to her car talking to her while brandishing a knife for 10-15 minutes before she was able to drive off.

The Court in this matter found that the accused’s actions had detained the woman as he had *“taken over control”* of the victim who was *“hindered, retarded and restrained from proceeding”.* In this case, a period of 10 to 15 minutes was sufficient to constitute an intrusion.

### Taking away vs detaining 
*“Taking away”* and *“detaining”* are two separate and distinct acts giving rise to two different offences, and the prosecution should specify which of the acts is being alleged. Where there is evidence of both taking away and detaining, two charges should be laid.

In ***R v Crossan*** the defendant took a woman from her home at gunpoint and drove her to another address, where he detained her with intent to “*carnally know*” her. He was charged that he “*did take her away and detain her*”.

Although affirming Crossan’s convictions, the Court of Appeal criticised the formulation of the charge since it combined both offences in a single count.

****CASELAW****
> R v Crossan
> Taking away and detaining are “separate and distinct offences. The first consists of taking [the victim] away; the second of detaining her. The first offence was complete when the prisoner took the woman away against her will. Then, having taken her away, he detained her against her will, and his conduct in detaining her constituted a new and different offence”.

However, the offender’s intent need not be specific at the time the victim is detained and may be formed later in the course of the offending.

### Example:
In ***R v Waaka*** the defendant, while attending a party, assisted an associate to carry a comatose woman behind a wall some 3 – 4 metres away, where the associate subsequently raped her. During the course of carrying her away, the defendant gave some thought to raping the woman himself, but then dismissed the idea.

In this matter, the Court suggested that intent may be formed at any time during the taking away. If a taking away commences without the intent to have intercourse, but then that intent is formed during the taking away, then that is sufficient for the purposes of this section.

### Person 
The age of the victim is not relevant to offences under sections 208 and 209 but is relevant under section 210. (See Part 2 The DMP)

### Consent
### Without the Person’s consent
*“**Consent”* is a person’s conscious and voluntary agreement to something desired or proposed by another.

In a criminal law context, what is relevant is *true* or *valid* consent.

Like the elements of Unlawfully and Taking away, ‘*without consent*’ is often apparent from the circumstances – consider the images below.

As an investigator, you must gather evidence of ‘*without consent*’ no matter how clear or obvious the circumstances may seem.

### With The Person’s consent obtained by fraud
In some cases, the offender may deceive the victim into agreeing to a proposition by misrepresenting the facts or their intentions.  The case of ***R v Wellard***, explained above in the context of ‘taken away’, is also an example of consent obtained by a lie.

### Example:
In ***R v Wellard*** the offender gained the victim’s consent fraudulently by representing himself as a police officer, which he was not.

### Example:
In ***R v Cort*** a man stopped his car at several bus stops where women were waiting on their own. He falsely told them that the bus had broken down and offered to give them a lift. On two occasions women voluntarily got into his car on the basis of his deceit, and he was convicted of kidnapping.

The women in this case were unharmed and Cort denied any sinister motive, claiming that the handcuffs, condoms, string, Stanley knife and tape located in his car were there for innocent purposes.

A useful way to think about the element of consent regarding these examples is:

- That the victim in both these cases consented to take themselves to another place. They voluntarily did so. However, this consent was invalid as it was gained by a lie (fraud).
Enticing is another form of non-valid consent. To *“entice*” means to tempt, persuade, or attract by arousing hope or desire.

This may therefore include situations where a stranger tempts a child away by offering sweets or to look at something exciting.

Where a parent persuades their child to come with them, contrary to the terms of a court order, by promising to take the child to the playground or similar, the enticement is the same in that it forms a false representation which invalidates consent. However, where a parent or guardian is involved, the appropriate charge is under section 210, discussed in **Part Two**.

In either case (whether a guardian or ‘stranger’ is involved), section 209A and section 210 (3)(a) provides that it is immaterial that the child consents to or even instigates the departure, although a person “*takes*” a child only if his or her conduct is an effective cause of it.

### With Person’s consent obtained by duress
In other cases, a victim may acquiesce (agree to or give in to) to an offender’s demands based on fear of the consequences if they refuse.

Duress may arise from the actual or implied threat of force to the victim or another person but can also include other forms of pressure or coercion.

### Example:
In a civil case, ***Hirani v Hirani***, a young Indian Hindi girl successfully sought a divorce after being forced by her family to enter an arranged marriage.

The girl went through with the ceremony under threat of being kicked out of the family home if she did not, which would have left her with nowhere to live and no means to support herself.

The critical question in relation to duress is whether the threats, pressure or coercion are such that they destroy the reality of consent and overbear the will of the individual.

In the following example, the *“consent”* of the victims was obtained by a combination of fraud and duress.

### Example:
A man approaches a sex worker and fraudulently identifies himself as a police officer. He threatens to arrest her unless he receives free or discounted sexual services. As a result of his fraudulent identification and subsequent threat of arrest, the sex worker complies with his wishes.

# THE MENS REA FOR KIDNAPPING &amp; ABDUCTION
### General intent accompanying the actus reus. 
As with most offences, the mens rea can be broadly categorised into two – the general intent accompanying the actus reus and the specific intent, often associated with the reason, purpose or specific result of the actus reus. The definition below explains this.

In sections 208 and 209, the general intent accompanying the taking away or detaining of a person is that the act must be carried out intentionally or deliberately and with knowledge.

You will note that the elements of Kidnapping and Abduction make no reference to intentionally or knowledge and yet these must form part of your investigation into a kidnapping. The Court in ***R v M*** considered that the “seriousness of kidnapping implies that full mens rea must be proved before conviction is appropriate.”

A brief summary of the facts of R v M are as follows:

Mrs S was estranged from her husband (Mr M) and had taken out a protection order against him. The two maintained contact in order to share custody of their three children. After the children had spent the weekend with Mr M, he arrived at Mrs S’ house telling her that the youngest child had been injured and asking Mrs S to return to his house, which she did. When she arrived there, she found the youngest child uninjured and was threatened in an aggressive manner to drop the protection order. Mrs S agreed to drive to a beach with Mr M so they could talk and so the children could play on the beach. Expecting to go to a nearby beach, Mrs S and her children were instead driven past the turn off to the beach and continued on the main road for many hours much to Mrs S’ surprise and protests.

The evidence given by Mr M conflicted with his wife, in that he stated that he did not know that she did not consent to the long drive.

Although this case involved issues of credibility of the parties, what the court decided **is that the taking away in kidnapping must be intentional and that there must be knowledge on the part of the offender that there is no valid consent to the taking away.**

In this case, the Crown was required to prove that Mr M **knew** that his wife did not consent to driving past the turnoff to the local beach and did not consent to continuing the long drive.

****CASELAW****
> R v M
> The Crown must prove that the accused intended to take away or detain the complainant and that he or she knew that the complainant was not consenting.

This case law draws attention to the need to** prove** the legal concept of intentionally when it comes to the general mens rea of both kidnapping and abduction, as well as knowledge on the part of the defendant when it comes to consent.

The concepts of **intentionally** and **knowingly** are covered in the Basic Legal Concepts module, with the associated case law of *Collister *and *Crooks. Collister* guides investigators as to how to prove intent. *Crooks* guides investigators in gathering evidence to prove an offender had the requisite knowledge or awareness.

### Specific intent
Sections 208 and 209 stipulate a range of specific intents that must accompany the intentional acts described above. The specific intents for abduction and kidnapping are found in subsections (a) (b) &amp; (c). Listed, they are:

- intent to hold for ransom or
- intent to hold for service or
- intent to cause to be confined or
- intent to be imprisoned or
- intent to cause to be sent out of New Zealand or
- intent to cause to be taken out of New Zealand or
**The definition of ‘Intent’ in the Basic Legal Concepts module applies** to these subsections. One way to consider these subsections is as the ‘*why*’ or the reason for the Abduction or Kidnapping.

### Specific intent in abduction cases
With intent to go through a form of marriage or civil union with P.

Section 208 was replaced on 3 December 2018 by the Family Violence (Amendments) Act 2018 to include civil union.  ‘*Form of marriage*’ takes a broader meaning now than did *‘marry’* under the provision of the Marriage Act 1955.

### With intent to have sexual connection with a person
*“Sexual connection*” is defined by statute.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 2, Crimes Act 1961
> Sexual connection
> sexual connection means—
> connection effected by the introduction into the genitalia or anus of one person, otherwise than for genuine medical purposes, of—
> a part of the body of another person; or
> an object held or manipulated by another person; or
> connection between the mouth or tongue of one person and a part of another person’s genitalia or anus; or
> the continuation of connection of a kind described in paragraph (a) or paragraph (b)

With intent to cause P to go through a form of marriage, or civil union, or to have sexual connection with some other person: This relates to situations where the abductor takes away or detains a victim to enable another person to go through a form of marriage or civil union with them, for example detaining a young Hindu woman for the purposes of enforcing an arranged marriage.

Under this provision, the offender’s intent is to enable another person to have sexual connection with the victim, for example a gang prospect who abducts a woman and takes her back to the gang pad to be raped by other gang members.

# SPECIFIC INTENT IN KIDNAPPING CASES
### (a) With intent to hold him or her for ransom
Kidnapping is often used as a means to extort money from the victim’s family or from those who would seek to have the victim returned.

A *“ransom”* is a sum of money demanded or paid for the release of a person being held captive.

### (a) With intent to hold him or her to service
This provision relates to situations where the offender’s intent is to keep the victim as a servant or slave.

### (B) With intent to cause him or her to be confined
*“Confining”* a person can include restricting their movements to within a geographical area, but also has a wider meaning that includes curtailing their activity and exercising control and influence over them.

Note that the inclusion of the words *“to cause”* in this section suggest that although the kidnapper must intend to be responsible for the victim being confined, it is not necessary for him to directly affect the confinement himself.

### (b) With intent to cause him or her to be imprisoned
To *“imprison”* a person means to put them in prison, or to confine them as if in prison. It has a narrower meaning than *“confine”* as discussed above, and may apply in situations where the victim is, for example, locked in a room or in the boot of a car.

### (c) With intent to cause him or her to be sent or taken out of New Zealand
**SENT**: Under this provision, the offender’s intent is for the victim to leave New Zealand.

The term *“sent”* may include situations where the victim leaves the country on their own, perhaps as the result of a threat or other form of duress.

**TAKEN**: The word *“taken”* suggests that the victim is in the company or custody of a person accompanying them out of New Zealand.

In abduction, form of marriage, civil union and sexual connection appear only as matters of intent, and it is not necessary to prove that they actually occurred or were even attempted.

### When is kidnapping complete?
In ***R v Mohi*** a man who was charged with abducting his wife argued that the Crown had to prove both a lack of consent to the taking away, and a lack of consent to the subsequent intercourse.

The High Court disagreed and held that to satisfy the requirements of section 208, *“the time of importance is that of the taking away,”* and any subsequent marriage or sexual intercourse without consent would be the subject of a separate investigation.

****CASELAW****
> R v Mohi
> The offence is committed at the time of taking away, so long as there is, at that moment, the necessary intent. It has never been regarded as necessary ... that the Crown should show the intent was carried out.

So, the offence is complete, in the sense that the offender becomes criminally liable as soon as he detains the victim with one of the specified intents.  It is not necessary to prove the intent was actually carried out.

### Example 1:
A man who forces a young girl into his car with the intention of raping her may be liable for abduction the moment he grabs her. If he carries on and actually rapes the girl, then that would be the basis for a separate charge of sexual violation by rape.

### Example 2:
If it can be proved the offender kidnapped a person with intent to hold them for ransom, the offence is complete as soon as the victim is taken away – whether or not a ransom is actually sought.

# MISCELLANEOUS PROVISIONS
### Consent no defence
Consent is not a defence to charges under sections 208 – 210 when the person taken is under the age of 16 years. The charges apply even when the young person freely and voluntarily goes with the person. It is the age of the young person that negates consent, and also their lack of maturity to understand the nature and context of the consent they are giving.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 209A, Crimes Act 1961
> Young person under 16 cannot consent to being taken away or detained
> For the purposes of sections 208 and 209, a person under the age of 16 years cannot consent to being taken away or detained.

# UNDERSTANDING ABDUCTION OF A CHILD UNDER 16 SECTION 210 (1).
### Section 210 (1) Specific Mens rea 
The specific *mens rea* or intent of the offence of abduction of a young person can be summed up in the words "with intent to deprive".  The deprivation is in relation to the possession of a young person where there is some kind of legal custody or right to possession of that child.

There are three legal custodial relationships listed in section 210. They are:

- Parent
- Guardian
- other person having lawful care or charge of a young person.
Although this offence falls under the heading of “*crimes against the person”*, it is not an offence against the person of the child, but rather against the custodial rights of the parent or guardian.

The section also protects the rights of those having temporary lawful possession of a young person, such as Oranga Tamariki caregivers.

A parent may commit an offence against this section, in respect of their own child, where their actions are unlawful; for example, a father who takes his own child from its mother contrary to a court order.

Depriving a parent of possession may not be the offender’s primary intention; however, if they know that their actions will inevitably cause that outcome, then depriving the parent can also be said to have been “*intentional”*.

### Example: 
In ***R v Chartrand*** a 43-year-old man enticed an 8-year-old boy away from where he was playing and took him to a riverbank where he took photographs of the boy. He argued that his intention had not been to deprive the parents of possession of the child, but simply to take photos as a surprise for the boy’s parents, whom he did not know.

The Court in *Chartrand* went on to say that “*whether the defendant may have had an innocent motive, or intended to interfere with possession for a very short period of time is beside the point”*.

It is not necessary to prove the accused intended a permanent deprivation.

### Actus reus and general mens rea
As well as proving the specific intent to deprive a lawful caregiver of their young person, section 210 provides three kinds of acts (or ways) in which this happens. These acts must be carried out deliberately or intentionally.

- There must be a deliberate and intentional act of taking OR
- There must be a deliberate and intentional act of enticing OR
- There must be a deliberate and intentional act of depriving.
### Taking away vs Enticing vs detaining 
The difference between “*Taking away*” and “*detaining*” is described above as two separate and distinct acts giving rise to two different offences. Under section 210, “Entices away” is added as another specified act / offence.

To *“entice*” means to tempt, persuade, or attract by arousing hope or desire.

Under Abduction and Kidnapping, “enticing” can be part of the actus reus in respect of luring someone with a false representation away from the place they want to be. Here, enticing away is separated out and is often the mechanism by which a guardian is deprived of their young person. In this way, section 210 recognises the custodial context of the offence.

Just as taking away or detaining requires evidence of the mechanism of the act, so too does enticing.

Subsection (3)(a) provides that it is immaterial that the child consents to or even instigates the departure, although a person “*takes*” a child only if his or her conduct is an effective cause of it.

### Young person
The term “*young person*” for the purposes of section 210 is defined in subsection (4).

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 210, Crimes Act 1961
> Abduction of young person under 16
> (4)	In this section, “young person” means a person under the age of 16 years.

### Proof of age
Where age is an essential ingredient of a charge, the prosecution must prove the victim’s age at the time of the alleged offence, using the *best evidence* available to do so.

In practice this generally involves producing the victim’s birth certificate in conjunction with independent evidence that identifies the victim as the person named in the certificate.

Ideally the independent evidence will be that of a parent, but that will not always be possible.

In ***R v Forrest &amp; Forrest****,* two men were charged with having sexual intercourse with a 14-year-old girl who had run away from Child Welfare custody. At trial the girl produced her birth certificate and gave evidence herself that she was the person named in the certificate. The men successfully appealed their convictions on the grounds that the Crown had not adequately proved the girl’s age.

****CASELAW****
> R v Forrest and Forrest [1970] NZLR 545 (CA)
> “The best evidence possible in the circumstances should be adduced by the prosecution in proof of [the victim's] age”.

The Court of Appeal suggested that in that case it might have been sufficient if, along with producing her birth certificate, the girl had given additional testimony, for instance as to the day on which she habitually celebrated her birthday, the age at which she first went to school, and in what year, and so on.

### Example:
In a similar case, ***R v Clancy***, the victim’s birth certificate was not produced; however, it was held that in this case evidence of the victim’s date of birth, given by her mother was sufficient to prove the girl’s age.

So, there is no hard and fast rule, and every case must be dealt with on its own facts, but generally the best evidence available as to proof of age should be adduced.

### Possession (see Basic legal concepts module)
****CASELAW****
> R v Cox
> Possession involves two… elements. The first, often called the physical element, is actual or potential physical custody or control. The second, often described as the mental element… is a combination of knowledge and intention: knowledge in the sense of an awareness by the accused that the substance is in his possession… and an intention to exercise possession.

# UNDERSTANDING RECEIVING A CHILD UNDER 16 SECTION 210 (2).
### Section 210 (2) Receives a young person
Section 210, subsection (2) creates the offence of “*receiving*” a young person, knowing the young person has been unlawfully taken with the necessary intent.

The actus reus of this is an act of intentionally “receiving” the young person into their care. The accompanying *mens rea* consists of the knowledge they had at the time of the receiving.

### Actus reus – section 210 (2) receives a young person
Subsection (2) could be read as broadening the scope of the offence to include people or third parties who receive a young person taken from the lawful custody of their caregiver.

What amounts to the act of “*receiving*” a young person will in each case be a question of fact and degree. It will include receiving physical custody of the young person. Several people could jointly “*receive*” a young person for the purposes of liability under this provision.

The act of receiving must be an intentional and deliberate act.

### mens rea section 210 (2) receives a young person
To be liable for an offence under section 210(2) the offender must *know* that the young person they are receiving has been abducted.

The third party **does** **not** need an intent to deprive the person who has lawful care or possession of the young person; however, they must have **knowledge** that the young person has been unlawfully taken, enticed or detained and that the reason for this was to deprive the lawful caregiver of that young person.

Simester and Brookbanks suggest knowing means “*knowing or correctly believing*”. They further premise that “*the defendant may believe something wrongly but cannot ‘know’ something that is false*”. The levels of knowledge are defined in *R v Crooks* and discussed in the Basic Legal Concepts module.

Knowledge is a form of *mens rea* and is required, as always, to be proved. It is crucial to investigate the level of knowledge on the part of the offender as to what they knew about the custody of the young person at the time of the receiving.

They must not only believe in that fact, but they must be correct about it; if the child has not in fact been abducted, then a subsequent receiving of the child is not an offence under this provision.

### Example:
This may relate to situations where, for example, a father takes his child from its mother contrary to a court order and hides the child at the grandparents’ house.

If the grandparents knew that the child had been taken unlawfully, they may be liable for receiving the child in these circumstances.

# MISCELLANEOUS PROVISIONS.
### Statutory Defence 
****LEGISLATION****
> Section 210A, Crimes Act 1961 (GOOD FAITH)
> People claiming in good faith right to possession of young person under 16
> A person who claims in good faith a right to the possession of a young person under the age of 16 years cannot be convicted of an offence against section 209 or section 210 because he or she gets possession of the young person.

When a defence under section 210A arises, it lies with the prosecution to negate good faith beyond reasonable doubt.

The question for the jury will not be whether the defendant was, in fact and law, entitled to the possession of the child or young person – this point being covered by the word “*unlawfully*” – but whether it is shown beyond reasonable doubt that the defendant did not believe in good faith that he or she was so entitled.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 210(3)(a), Crimes Act 1961
> For the purposes of subsections (1) and (2) it is immaterial whether the young person consents, or is taken or goes or is received at his or her own suggestion.

### Belief that person was over 16 is no defence
Mistake as to age is no defence.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 210(3)(b), Crimes Act 1961
> For the purposes of subsections (1) and (2) it is immaterial whether the offender believes the young person to be of or over the age of 16.

# PEOPLE TRAFFICKING
### Introduction
Migrant smuggling and People trafficking are offences under sections 98C and 98D of the Crimes Act 1961.

The terms ‘people trafficking’ and ‘migrant smuggling’ are often confused and used interchangeably by other agencies. People trafficking and migrant smuggling are also known as ‘*human trafficking’* and ‘*people smuggling’* respectively.

An investigator should be able to recognise and understand the key concepts of People Trafficking and Migrant Smuggling including:

- An understanding of the offences themselves
- Differences between the two offences
- The inter-agency approach to these investigations
- Three main investigative approach options
### History
In 2000, a protocol to prevent, suppress and punish the trafficking of people, especially women and children, was added to the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organised Crime. New Zealand ratified this protocol in 2002.  A New Zealand Inter-Agency Working Group on people trafficking was set up in 2006 and through this working group, agencies agreed to implement various anti-trafficking initiatives. As a result, in July 2009 the New Zealand Government released a Plan of Action to Prevent People Trafficking, a proactive whole-of-government approach to people trafficking issues for New Zealand.

Each agency has roles as defined in the Plan of Action to Prevent People Trafficking. Ministry of Business, Innovation and Leadership will continue to lead the New Zealand Government’s anti-trafficking efforts and coordinate the Plan of Action.

### Investigation procedures
For more in-depth information and investigative procedures, see the following chapter in the Police Manual: People trafficking and migrant smuggling.

### Police response
The responsibilities of the New Zealand Police are divided into three areas: Prevention, Protection and Prosecution. Where a case of suspected people trafficking is identified, as defined by section 98D of the Crimes Act 1961, Police will lead the investigation and any resultant prosecutions.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 98C, Crimes Act 1961
> Smuggling migrants
> (1)	Every one is liable to the penalty stated in subsection (3) who arranges for an unauthorised migrant to enter New Zealand or any other state, if he or she—
> (a)	does so for a material benefit; and
> (b)	either knows that the person is, or is reckless as to whether the person is, an unauthorised migrant.
> (2)	Every one is liable to the penalty stated in subsection (3) who arranges for an unauthorised migrant to be brought to New Zealand or any other state, if he or she—
> (a)	does so for a material benefit; and
> (b)	either knows that the person is, or is reckless as to whether the person is, an unauthorised migrant; and
> (c)	either—
> (i)	knows that the person intends to try to enter the state; or
> (ii)	is reckless as to whether the person intends to try to enter the state.
> (3)	The penalty is imprisonment for a term not exceeding 20 years, a fine not exceeding $500,000, or both.
> (4)	Proceedings may be brought under subsection (1) even if the unauthorised migrant did not in fact enter the state concerned.
> (5)	Proceedings may be brought under subsection (2) even if the unauthorised migrant was not in fact brought to the state concerned.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 98D, Crimes Act 1961
> Trafficking in Persons
> (1)	Every person is liable to the penalty stated in subsection (2) who arranges, organises, or procures—
> (a)	the entry of a person into, or the exit of a person out of, New Zealand or any other State—
> (i)	for the purpose of exploiting or facilitating the exploitation of the person; or
> (ii)	knowing that the entry or exit of the person involves 1 or more acts of coercion against the person, 1 or more acts of deception of the person, or both; or
> (b)	the reception, recruitment, transport, transfer, concealment, or harbouring of a person in New Zealand or any other State—
> (i)	for the purpose of exploiting or facilitating the exploitation of the person; or
> (ii)	knowing that the reception, recruitment, transport, transfer, concealment, or harbouring of the person involves 1 or more acts of coercion against the person, 1 or more acts of deception of the person, or both.
> (2)	The penalty is imprisonment for a term not exceeding 20 years, a fine not exceeding $500,000, or both.

### Differences between the two offences
Sections 98C and 98D of the New Zealand Crimes Act 1961 address distinct but related offences: **smuggling migrants (98C)** and **trafficking in persons (98D)**. While both involve the illegal movement of individuals, they differ significantly in terms of consent, purpose, and the methods employed.

- **Consent is central to distinguishing between the two offences**. Migrant smuggling, under Section 98C, involves individuals who have **willingly consented** to be brought into New Zealand illegally. This consent, however, does not absolve those who arrange the illegal entry for profit. Conversely, Section 98D (trafficking in persons) centres around situations where individuals are brought into the country **without their consent**, using **coercion and/or deception**.
- The **purpose** behind the movement of individuals also distinguishes these offences. **Exploitation** forms the basis of people trafficking, with victims often forced into labour, sexual exploitation, or even organ removal. Migrant smuggling, while illegal, doesn't inherently involve such exploitation.
- The **relationship** between those being moved and those facilitating the movement provides another point of differentiation. In migrant smuggling, a degree of cooperation exists, with the smuggled individuals aware of their illegal status. People trafficking, however, often involves a power imbalance, with traffickers using their position to exploit vulnerable individuals. The presence of **violence, intimidation, or coercion** further distinguishes trafficking from smuggling.
The differences between the two offence types are:

- consent
- the purpose of the travel or movement
- the relationship between the person moved and the people enabling the movement
- violence, intimidation, or coercion
- liberty
- profit.

### Inter-Agency Approach to People Trafficking and Migrant Smuggling Investigations
While this module focuses on the role of the New Zealand Police in addressing people trafficking and migrant smuggling, it also highlights the need for an inter-agency approach.

- **New Zealand Inter-Agency Working Group on People Trafficking:** Established in 2006, this working group signifies a collaborative effort among various agencies to combat people trafficking. While the specific agencies involved aren't listed in the source, the existence of such a group suggests that investigations and preventative measures extend beyond law enforcement.
- **Plan of Action to Prevent People Trafficking:** Released in 2009, this plan represents a "*proactive whole-of-government approach to people trafficking issues for New Zealand*." This indicates that various government departments and agencies have defined roles and responsibilities in addressing this issue.
- **Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE):** Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment is identified as the lead agency coordinating the New Zealand Government's anti-trafficking efforts and the Plan of Action. This suggests a hierarchical structure within the inter-agency approach, with the Department of Labour playing a central role.
### Investigative approach – people trafficking
Depending on the specific features of each case, the investigative approach options for this crime-type broadly fall into three categories: Reactive investigation; Proactive investigation and Disruptive investigation.

- **Reactive Investigation:** This approach is **victim-led**, often initiated by the victim or someone acting on their behalf approaching the police. Reactive investigations often stem from victims feeling safe enough to disclose their situation to authorities. It might also indicate that these cases come to light through reports made by NGOs, support organisations, or concerned citizens who become aware of a victim's plight.
- **Proactive Investigation:** In this **police-led** approach, traditional investigative methods combine with intelligence resources to identify and locate traffickers, gather evidence, and initiate legal proceedings. This is a more strategic and intelligence-driven approach, potentially focusing on identifying trafficking networks, their operations, and key individuals involved. Proactive investigations might involve surveillance, undercover operations, financial investigations, and collaboration with international law enforcement agencies.
- **Disruptive Investigation:** This approach is warranted in situations where the victim faces immediate danger, and proactive or reactive methods are not feasible. This approach prioritises the victim's safety and wellbeing. It might involve urgent interventions, such as raids, rescues, or emergency protection orders, to remove the victim from immediate harm. Disruptive investigations may also aim to disrupt trafficking operations quickly, even if it means potentially sacrificing the gathering of comprehensive evidence against traffickers initially.
