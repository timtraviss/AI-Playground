### Robbery – section 234, crimes act 1961
****LEGISLATION****
> Robbery
> ***Section 234, Crimes Act 1961***
> (1)  Robbery is theft accompanied by violence or threats of violence, to any person or property, used to extort the property stolen or to prevent or overcome resistance to its being stolen.
> (2) Every one who commits robbery is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 10 years.

### Aggravated Robbery – section 235, crimes act 1961
****LEGISLATION****
> Aggravated robbery
> ***Section 235, Crimes Act 1961***
> Every one is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 14 years who—
> (a)	robs any person and, at the time of, or immediately before or immediately after, the robbery, causes grievous bodily harm to any person; or
> (b)	being together with any other person or persons, robs any person; or
> (c)	being armed with any offensive weapon or instrument, or any thing appearing to be such a weapon or instrument, robs any other person.

# INTRODUCTION TO ROBBERY
### Introduction
Robbery, as defined in section 234 of the Crimes Act 1961, is essentially an offence of aggravated theft in which violence or threats have been used to facilitate the taking of the property.

Robberies range in seriousness from “*stand over*” type street robberies to full scale armed hold ups.

Aggravating factors, such as the infliction of grievous bodily harm, the combination of two or more robbers acting together, or the use of a weapon, increase the seriousness of the offending and bring it within the realms of Aggravated Robbery under section 235.

To prove a charge of aggravated robbery, the prosecution must first establish that there was a robbery as defined in section 234, and in addition, that the circumstances included one of the aggravating features specified in paragraphs (a), (b) or (c) of section 235.

### identifying the Actus reus and mens rea of the offence
The offences of Robbery and Aggravated Robbery are examples of legislation written in a manner that on the surface may not clearly spell out an actus reus or mens rea.

Although not specifically written in the elements, it is important to remember that as an investigator you are required to prove both to meet evidential sufficiency.

Simply speaking the below may help identify the actus reus and mens rea of the offences.

# UNDERSTANDING ROBBERY
The offence of Robbery is made up of an act of **theft** combined with an act of **violence **or **threats** towards a person.

One of the essential elements of robbery is theft, and therefore the actus reus and mens rea and all the elements of theft must be proven before turning to the use of violence or threats to facilitate the crime.

***Theft***

The offence of theft is shaded blue. We will now discuss each element below.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 219, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Theft or stealing ***
> *Theft or stealing is the act of, —**dishonestly and without claim of right, taking any property with intent to deprive any owner permanently of that property or of any interest in that property; or**dishonestly and without claim of right, using or dealing with any property with intent to deprive any owner permanently of that property or of any interest in that property after obtaining possession of, or control over, the property in whatever manner.**An intent to deprive any owner permanently of property includes an intent to deal with property in such a manner that—**the property cannot be returned to any owner in the same condition; or**any owner is likely to be permanently deprived of the property or of any interest in the property.*

### The Actus reus of theft
Theft requires proof of taking property, or using property, or dealing with property

***Takes***

The term “*taking*” is covered by section 219, Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 219, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Theft or stealing***
> *In this section, taking does not include obtaining ownership or possession of, or control over, any property with the consent of the person from whom it is obtained, whether or not consent is obtained by deception.**For tangible property, theft is committed by a taking when the offender moves the property or causes it to be moved.*

Subsection 3 specifically excludes property taken or obtained by deception (in other words property handed over by the owner based on a lie or a swindle of some sort). This is covered by other fraudulent defences in the Crimes Act (See the Fraud Lesson Note).

Subsection 4 specifically defines that a taking is complete the moment the item is moved with intent to steal it.

In ***R v Lapier*** a lady had emerged from an opera house and was preparing to step into her carriage when the defendant snatched her gold and diamond earring, tearing it from her ear. Although he had the earring only momentarily and lost it again when it became tangled in the lady’s hair, the Court held that there had been a “*taking*” sufficient to constitute robbery.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Lapier ***
> *Robbery is complete the instant the property is taken, even if possession by the thief is only momentary.*

So, property is “*taken*”, and therefore theft is complete, the moment the item is moved with intent to steal it.

***Return of the Property***

Once theft is complete, the immediate return of the property will not purge the offence.

In ***R v Peat*** the defendant, on horseback, stopped a gentleman’s carriage and demanded his money. The gentleman handed his silk coin-purse to the defendant, who handed it straight back saying, *“... give me the contents of it.”* At that point he was apprehended by the gentleman’s servant and was subsequently convicted of highway robbery.

The Court said in Peat that “*as the prisoner had in fact demanded his money, and under the impulse of that threat and demand the property had been once taken ... it was in strictness of law a sufficient taking to complete the offence, although the prisoner’s possession had continued for an instant only*”.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Peat  ***
> *As in the case of theft, the immediate return of goods by the robber does not purge the offence, subject always to the necessary intent existing at the time of taking.*

***Any property (see Basic Concepts Lesson Note)***

The term “*property*” is defined by statute.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Interpretation***
> *Property includes real and personal property, and any estate or interest in any real or personal property, money, electricity, and any debt, and anything in action, and any other right or interest*

In practice, robbery will generally involve property that is physical, and tangible items, however the wide statutory definition includes such intangible things as electronic funds in a bank account or megabytes of data in an internet account.

***Possession (see Basic concepts lesson note)***

In ***R v Cox*** it was found that both a *physical* and* mental* element must be proved to satisfy possession.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Cox ***
> *Possession involves two … elements. The first, often called the physical element, is actual or potential physical custody or control. The second, often described as the mental element … is a combination of knowledge and intention: knowledge in the sense of an awareness by the accused that the substance is in his possession … and an intention to exercise possession.*

***Physical element***

The physical element requires the physical custody or control over the item in question and can be either “*actual*” or “*potential*”.

- ***Actual possession*** arises where the thing in question is in a person’s physical custody or control.
- ***Potential possession*** arises when the person has the potential to have the thing in question in their control. For example, storing the thing in question at an associate’s house or through an agent.
***Control***

To “*control*” something means to exercise authoritative or dominating influence over it.

A person can control an item that is not in their physical custody and conversely can have something in their physical custody that they have no control over.

***Mental element ***

This element is a combination of both knowledge that the person possesses the item in question, and an intention to possess the item.

***Proof of Possession***

Proof of possession requires proof of both a physical element (the actus reus) and a mental element (the mens rea) as outlined previously in *Cox*.

### The mens REA OF theft
Theft requires proof of the mens rea of

- Dishonestly
- Without claim of right
- Intent to deprive permanently
In all cases of theft under the section the prosecution must prove “the three incriminating mental states the offence specifies” (Boese v R [2014] NZCA 144, [2014] 3 NZLR 16 at [26].

The defendant’s acts must have been dishonest – that is, without a belief were consented to or authorised by a person entitled to consent to or authorise them and done without claim of right — that is, without a belief that they were entitled to the property in some way.

The difference between those elements could be seen as being that dishonestly addresses whether the defendant believed he had authority to do as he or she did; claim of right addresses whether the defendant believed the law entitled him to the property taken. The prosecution must also prove an intention to deprive an owner of the property, or of an interest in it, permanently.

***Dishonestly (see Basic Concepts Module)***

For the purposes of theft, the term “*dishonestly*” is defined by statute.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 217, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Interpretation***
> *Dishonestly, in relation to an act or omission, means done or omitted without a belief that there was express or implied consent to, or authority for, the act or omission from a person entitled to give such consent or authority*

Dishonesty is a state of mind. It is part of the mens rea or general intent that attaches to the actus reus. This means that the taking of the property (the act) must be done **without** an honest belief that they were entitled to take the property.

The test of dishonesty is subjective. The belief does not need to be reasonably held. The belief may also be incorrectly or mistakenly held.

The fact that the defendant acted without the requisite belief, and therefore dishonestly, may be inferred from the circumstances. (***R v Collister***). However, in Hayes v R the Supreme Court found that while a jury may be less likely to credit him or her with honesty if the facts did not warrant his or her belief - if there is a reasonable possibility that D did so believe then he or she must be acquitted.

***without Claim of right (see basic concepts module)***

*“Claim of right”* is defined by statute.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Interpretation***
> *Claim of right, in relation to any act, means a belief at the time of the act in a proprietary or possessory right in property in relation to which the offence is alleged to have been committed, although that belief may be based on ignorance or mistake of fact or of any matter of law other than the enactment against which the offence is alleged to have been committed*

Claim of Right is essentially a ‘*defence*’ to what would otherwise be a criminal act. Claim of right often attaches to all offences dealing with property and stems from the idea that an individual can deal with their own property how they chose – including destroying or damaging it or retrieving it.

Claim of right is considered adjacent to the mens rea in that there is a mental element - a **belief**. The **belief** revolves around a possessory right in the property taken or that there is some sort of legal interest (often financial or ownership) in the property. This perceived legal entitlement to the property is the essence of claim of right.

If the mental element of theft cannot be established, there can be no robbery. “This may arise most commonly in cases where the accused has used violence to obtain property which the accused believes him or herself to be entitled.”

***Example:***

*R v Bernhard*, where a woman who blackmailed her former lover thought she had a right to the money claimed

The absence of a belief that a person holds a legal right to take or retrieve possession of property must be proved by the prosecution as it is an element of the offence. If claim of right is raised it is up to the defence to discharge the evidentiary burden.

Remember COR attaches to belief in the right in property. The belief does not extend to the way in which the property was taken or a belief that the manner in which it was taken was legal. A genuine but mistaken belief that the defendant had a legal right to take some property may provide a defence to theft; but a belief that stealing is not a crime will not.

***An example of a Claim of right as a defence to robbery***

As explained above, if the court is satisfied that the defendant acted with claim of right, they are entitled to an acquittal on a charge of theft. Therefore, as theft is an essential element of robbery, claim of right is also a defence to robbery.

In ***R v Skivington*** the defendant went to the office where he and his wife worked and demanded their wage packets two days earlier than they were due. He threatened one of the managers and forced the man at knife point to retrieve the cash from the safe. His conviction for “*robbery with aggravation*” was quashed on the basis that he genuinely believed he was entitled to the money.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Skivington [1967] 1 All ER 483***
> *“Larceny [or theft] is an element of robbery, and if the honest belief that a man has a claim of right is a defence to larceny, then it negatives one of the elements in the offence of robbery, without proof of which the full offence is not made out.” *

The Court of Appeal in ***Skivington* **further held that, as long as the defendant genuinely believed he had a right to the money, it was unnecessary to establish that he also believed he was entitled to take the money in the way that he did. However, he could of course be convicted of common assault.

***Offender liable for other offences***

Although claim of right negates theft, and therefore provides a defence to robbery, the offender may still be criminally liable for the violence, or threats used in obtaining the property.

Although in ***Skivington* **the robbery charge did not stand, he was still convicted on a variety of assault and weapons-related charges.

****CASELAW****
> ***Lamb v R [2018] NZCA 515***
> *The complainant was parking a vehicle he had borrowed from a friend when Mr. Lamb jumped into the car, punched him, and demanded to be driven out of town. The complainant and Mr. Lamb knew each other. The complainant drove a short distance but then stopped and, fearing further violence from Mr. Lamb, got out of the car and ran off. Mr. Lamb then drove away in the car which was located later that night.*
> *At trial Mr. Lamb did not dispute punching the complainant nor did he dispute driving the car away. However, he said he thought that in running off and leaving the keys in the car, the complainant was giving him permission to drive the car out of town.*
> *While Mr. Lamb was found guilty of Robbery at trial, the court of appeal supported the inference he only wanted to borrow it temporarily and therefore could not be found to have fulfilled the element of theft. Where the subject matter of the charge is a robbery involving the taking of a vehicle, but the mental element of theft cannot be established, they cannot be charged with Robbery but there may be liability for converting the vehicle under s 226.*
> *The Robbery conviction was quashed and substituted with a conviction for conversion under s 226(1)(a) of the Crimes Act 1961*

***Intent to deprive owner permanently***

The literal meaning of an intention to deprive another permanently of property is that one desires, or foresees as virtually certain, that the other person will never regain the property. This will not require an intention that the thief permanently retains the property — an intention to destroy the property, or to pass it to another who will retain it will suffice.

Intent to replace the property taken with other similar items will not prevent there being an intention to deprive permanently (although it might negative dishonesty).

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Theft or stealing ***
> ***Section 219, Crimes Act 1961***
> *(2)	An intent to deprive any owner permanently of property includes an intent to deal with property in such a manner that—*
> *(a)	the property cannot be returned to any owner in the same condition; or*
> *(b)	any owner is likely to be permanently deprived of the property or of any interest in the property.*

The difficulty of proving an intention to deprive permanently in cases of “joyriding” in motor vehicles led to the enactment of the separate offence of car conversion in section 226.

***intention to permanently deprive: ***

The meaning of “*intention to deprive permanently*” was considered in*** R v Morunga***, where the Court of Appeal held that the wording of the section should not be paraphrased into the rather different test of whether the defendant treated the property in question as his or her own.

In that case, the defendant had, as a part of his employment, the task of banking money from gaming machines, but on many occasions, he took the coins out of the machines and reinserted them for gambling (thus returning the identical coins to their owner, his employer).

Such dealing with the coins was not theft because of the lack of intention to deprive his employers permanently of the money so used. Theft was admitted in relation to a proportion of the money, which was abstracted and used for other purposes.

***Violence***

Once the offence of theft is established, robbery requires the theft to be accompanied with an act of **violence **or **threat of violence** towards a person.

One of the essential elements of robbery is that theft is **accompanied by** the actus reus of an act of violence and the mens rea that the act of violence has the intention or aim of acquiring the property taken in the theft.

The element of violence is shaded blue. We will discuss each element below.

***accompanied by (The Nexus between the Theft and the violence)***

The prosecution must prove a connection between the violence or threats and the stealing of the property – it must be shown that the defendant not only had an intent to steal at the time the violence or threats were used, **but that the violence or threats were used for the purpose of extorting the property or preventing or overcoming resistance to its being stolen.**

It would not be sufficient for robbery, for example, if the defendant punched a person for an unrelated reason, then took the victim’s wallet as an afterthought.

The violence or threats will usually occur at, or immediately before, the time of the theft, however there may be situations where that is not the case.

****CASELAW****
> R v Maihi
> “It is implicit in ‘accompany’ that there must be a nexus (connection or link) between the act of stealing ... and a threat of violence. Both must be present.” However, the term “does not require that the act of stealing and the threat of violence be contemporaneous ...”

In ***R v Maihi,*** the complainant wore a leather jacket to a Hamilton hotel. The defendant kept on saying he liked it. Feeling under pressure the complainant handed it to the defendant who tried it on. Sometime later the complainant and his friend asked for it back. The defendant offered to fight them for it which they declined. The defendant and his group left the hotel with the jacket.

On the evidence the crown accepted that the accused had not committed theft by taking… the trial judge agreed… that there was evidence of theft by conversion, accompanied by a threat of violence that satisfied the requirement of s234. A threat made with the necessary intent may continue to affect the victim’s mind, even when the goods are eventually handed over.

### Contemporaneous summary
As with the majority of crimes within this section, there must be a relationship between theft and the violence. Whilst it is not necessary that the theft and the violence or threat to violence occur at precisely the same time, there must be sufficient proximity (which will be a matter of degree).

### The actus reus – act of Violence
At times the use of violence will be clear in the circumstances (a punch, kick or stabbing for example) however, the Courts have accepted that there is a “*recognised gradation of violence from one end of a spectrum to the other*”.  Regardless, you as an investigator must gather ***evidence*** of the violence used obvious or not.

The likely level of action required to constitute violence in terms of section 234 is a degree of force, that is, more than a mere touching (a technical assault) used in the context of seeking to obtain property from its owner.

An implied threat **will be** sufficient to constitute the threat of violence.

The nature of the threat is to be considered objectively and the presence or absence of fear on the part of the complainant is not conclusive.

In ***Peneha v Police,*** the defendant grabbed a woman’s handbag and attempted to run off with it. When the woman tried to retain her bag, her hand was twisted and pulled backwards to the point where it hurt, and she was forced to let go. The Court held that “*though at the lower end of the scale for robbery*” this was sufficient to constitute violence for the purposes of that charge.

****CASELAW****
> Peneha v Police
> It is sufficient that “the actions of the defendant forcibly interfere with personal freedom or amount to forcible powerful or violent action or motion producing a very marked or powerful effect tending to cause bodily injury or discomfort”.

Whether or not the degree of violence used is sufficient to amount to robbery is a matter of fact for determination in each case.

### Threats of violence
A “*threat*” is generally a direct or veiled warning that violence will be used if the victim does not submit to the robber’s demands.

However, threats may also be conveyed by inference through the defendant’s conduct, demeanor or even appearance, depending on the circumstances.

In ***R v Broughton,*** two “*street kids*” approached the victim, a younger male who was sitting alone at a bus stop. They sat on either side of him and asked him if he had any money. When the victim said no, Broughton leaned in close and again asked for money. His manner was not overtly aggressive, but his demeanor and the circumstances caused the victim to fear that violence would be used if he continued to refuse, and consequently he handed over some cash. The Court of Appeal held that Broughton’s actions amounted to robbery by threats of violence.

****CASELAW****
> R v Broughton
> A threat of violence is “the manifestation of an intention to inflict violence unless the money or property be handed over. The threat may be direct or veiled. It may be conveyed by words or conduct, or a combination of both.” The actual presence or absence of fear on the part of the complainant is not the yardstick. It is the conduct of the accused which has to be assessed rather than ‘the strength of the nerves of the person threatened’

The Court in Broughton went on to say that “*whether or not the conduct complained of is capable of amounting to a threat of violence must be assessed in the context in which it occurred*”. This requires consideration of all the circumstances, which in that case included:

- the relative ages of the parties
- their respective physiques
- their appearance
- their demeanor
- what was said and done by those involved.
- the manner and setting in which the incident took place.
A sufficient implied threat may be inferred from “*inherently violent*” circumstances where the victim is in a vulnerable position. Whether or not the conduct complained of is capable of amounting to a threat of violence, must be assessed in the context in which it occurred.

That requires consideration of all the circumstances including such obvious matters as the relative ages of the parties, their respective physiques, appearance, and demeanor, and what was said or done by those involved and the manner and setting in which the incident took place.

### Example:
In ***R v Butler****,* the defendant, wearing a balaclava, confronted a man who was in the process of locking up his business premises, and stole a briefcase containing $16,000 cash.

The Court in Butler held that the sudden appearance of a tall man wearing a balaclava in circumstances where the complainant was securing his premises for the night and had a large amount of cash in his briefcase was inherently violent and thus amounted to a threat of violence.

Such a combination of circumstances could well cause a person so confronted to ‘freeze’ thus facilitating a theft.

It is the conduct of the defendant that is relevant, rather than the presence or absence of fear in the victim; however, the effect on the victim may be relevant to determining whether or not the defendant’s conduct was threatening.

### Example:
The Court in ***R v Pacholko****,* said “*The actual presence or absence of fear on the part of the complainant is not the yardstick. It is the conduct of the accused which has to be assessed rather than ‘the strength of the nerves of the person threatened’*”.

### To any person or property
The section makes it clear that the threat, or the violence, need not be directed against the victim or the victim’s property. It would suffice if the defendant threatened violence to A (or his or her property) unless B hands over goods.

### mens REA OR intention / aim of violence or threats
The prosecution must prove that the purpose of the violence or threats was to extort the property stolen, or to prevent or overcome resistance to its being stolen.

It is not sufficient that the offender simply used violence or threats if such use was for some collateral purpose.

### Example:
In ***R v Newell****,* the defendant was confronted by a security guard in a parking lot after stealing meat from a supermarket. He swung a knife at the guard and ran away leaving the meat behind.

The Court of Appeal quashed his conviction for robbery on the basis that the violence had been used not to overcome resistance to the theft, but to facilitate his escape afterwards.

In Newell it was held that the theft was complete when the appellant exited the store. The violence cannot be said to be to overcome resistance to the theft.

In those circumstances, charges of theft and aggravated assault or aggravated wounding may have been more appropriate – see the serious assaults lesson note.

### Extort
To “*extort*” means “*to obtain by coercion or intimidation*”.

Extortion implies an overbearing of the will of the victim, and the prosecution must show that the threats induced the victim to part with their property.

While it is the conduct of the defendant rather than the nerves of the victim that must be assessed, the effect on the mind of the victim is relevant to the question of whether the threats induced the victim to part with their property.

Therefore, if the threats have not in fact affected the will of the victim, there is no robbery.

### Prevent
To *“prevent”* means “to keep from happening”.

This provision applies when the offender anticipates resistance from the victim and uses violence or threats to ensure it does not commence.

### Overcome 
To *“overcome”* means “*to defeat; to prevail over; to get the better of in a conflict*”.

This provision will therefore apply when the victim is resisting, and the offender uses violence or threats to overpower and subdue the victim.

# UNDERSTANDING AGGRAVATED ROBBERY
### Introduction
Where a robbery has occurred, with a specific **outcome** or in a specific **context** the more serious offence of Aggravated Robbery has occurred under section 235.

Once again, the actus reus, mens rea and all elements of the offence of Robbery must be proved as well as one of the following aggravated elements of Aggravated Robbery.

- grievous bodily harm has been caused to any person or
- two or more people have acted together to commit the robbery or
- the person committing the robbery has been armed with an offensive weapon or instrument.
The elements making a robbery ‘aggravated’ are highlighted in blue and discussed below.

### Section 235(a) – Causes GBH AT the time of, or immediately before or immediately after, the robbery
Where serious harm is caused to a person during the course of a robbery, an aggravated robbery may have occurred. The investigator will need to enquire into the seriousness of the harm caused and its timing in relation to the robbery.

***Causes grievous bodily harm***

As with serious assaults, grievous bodily harm has been defined as

****CASELAW****
> ***DPP v Smith***
> *“Bodily harm” needs no explanation and “grievous” means no more and no less than “really serious”.  *

The term “*grievous*” refers to the degree of harm, rather than to the nature of it or how it was caused. Grievous bodily harm will most commonly result from an assault, but not always; if the harm is really serious it does not matter how it was caused.

As long as the harm is serious, it need not involve life threatening or permanent injury.

***To any person***

Aggravated robbery can occur where a person other than the victim of the robbery has suffered grievous bodily harm (e.g. Where a third party has been assaulted and suffers grievous bodily harm at the hands of a defendant).

***Example:***

The Court in ***R v Wells*** held that there is no requirement that the harm be inflicted on the victim of the robbery, thus infliction of harm to a person seeking to prevent the escape of the offender would come within the section.

### At the time of
This provision relates to situations where the violence used to extort the property stolen, or overcome resistance to it being stolen, has resulted in really serious harm to the person assaulted.

### Immediately before or after 
The term “*immediately*” refers to the **connection in time** between the robbery and the infliction of grievous bodily harm. Whether or not that connection amounts to “*immediate*” is a matter of fact and degree to be considered in each case and should form part of your investigation.

### Section 235(b) – Being together with any other person
There must be proof that, in committing the robbery, the defendant was part of a joint enterprise by two or more people who were physically present at the robbery.

Each must share an intent to steal using their collective force should that be necessary, and each must play some active role in the robbery.

### Physical proximity
The term “together with” requires that two or more people are **actually present** and acting together in the commission of the robbery.

In ***R v Joyce*** the defendant was charged with aggravated robbery by being “*together with*” two co-offenders during a robbery. One of the co-offenders had gone into a service station and robbed the attendant, while Joyce and another were “lurking in the neighbourhood” but taking no physical part in the robbery.

The Court of Appeal quashed Joyce’s conviction, stating that section 235(b) is “intended to provide for cases where the victim was confronted by two or more persons acting in concert”.

****CASELAW****
> R v Joyce
> “The Crown must establish that at least two persons were physically present at the time the robbery was committed or the assault occurred.”

### Together with - Joint enterprise
As well as being physically present, the offender must be actively participating in the robbery. Mere presence during the commission of robbery, without active participation, is not sufficient; the provision applies only to cases where the forces of two or more persons acting together are deployed against the victim in the actual commission of the offence.

In ***R v Galey*** a young woman lured a Japanese seaman out of a bar on the pretense that she would have sex with him for money. Once outside he was attacked by a male who kicked him, knocked him to the ground and stole his cash.  The male’s associate, Galey, kicked the man once in the feet, but had no further participation in the assault or the theft.

The Court of Appeal quashed Galey’s conviction for aggravated robbery on the basis that the charge was not aimed at those who were present at the scene only to act as a look out or to give encouragement.

****CASELAW****
> R v Galey
> “Being together” in the context of section 235(b) involves “two or more persons having the common intention to use their combined force, either in any event or as circumstances might require, directly in the perpetration of the crime.”

The Court did, however, consider that Galey’s actions in encouraging his associate made him liable as a party to the robbery, and substituted a conviction under section 234.

### Section 235(c) – Being armed with any offensive weapon
Being armed with any offensive weapon or instrument, or anything appearing to be such a weapon or instrumentBeing armed with

The term “*being armed with*” means that the defendant is carrying the item or has it available for immediate use as a weapon.

### Offensive weapon
The term “*offensive weapon*” is defined in section 202A, Crimes Act 1961 for the purposes of possessory offences under that section, however the definition still has some relevance to section 235(c).

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 202A, Crimes Act 1961
> Possession of offensive weapons or disabling substances
> (1)	In subsection (4)(a) offensive weapon means any article made or altered for use for causing bodily injury, or intended by the person having it with him or her for such use.
> (2)	In subsection (4)(b) offensive weapon means any article capable of being used for causing bodily injury.

For the purposes of section 235 there are three broad classes of offensive weapon:

- Items that are made solely for the purpose of attacking or inflicting injury, such as firearms, swords, or knuckledusters.
- Items that may otherwise have an innocent purpose but have been altered or adapted for use for causing injury, such as a bottle that has been deliberately broken to create a jagged edge.
- Items that are intended to cause injury, which includes anything capable of causing injury that is carried by the defendant for that purpose, such as a baseball bat or a knife.
### Instrument
The term “*instrument*” is not defined by statute but will include any item intended to be used as a weapon or to intimidate and overbear the victim’s will to resist.

### Anything appearing to be such
The legislation recognises that harmless but convincing replicas of weapons, such as imitation firearms, will cause just as much apprehension in the mind of the victim as the real thing.

They have much the same capacity to frighten and enforce compliance, and those threatened have little opportunity or inclination to examine the nature of the weapon used.

It must be proved both that the object appeared to be an offensive weapon or instrument to the victim, and that the defendant intended or was at least reckless as to the possibility that it would be perceived as a weapon.

However, a “*thing*” does not include a part of a person’s body.

In **R v *Bentham*** the defendant broke into a house where the victim was asleep in bed, and put his hand under his jacket, pushing the material out to give the impression he had a gun. He threatened to shoot the victim, who handed over money as a consequence of the threat. The House of Lords held that the term “anything” did not include the defendant’s unsevered hand.

****CASELAW****
> R v Bentham
> “What is possessed must under the definition be a thing. A person’s hand or fingers are not a thing. If they were regarded as property ... the court could, theoretically, make an order depriving the offender of his rights to them and they could be taken into the possession of the Police.”

### Sentencing for aggravated robbery
The sentencing guideline for aggravated robbery is ***R v Mako***. In ***R v Mako*** the Court of Appeal set out relevant factors in determining an appropriate sentence. These factors were:

- the degree of planning and preparation;
- the number of participants and what they did;
- whether disguises or other means of concealing identity and flight were used;
- the number of types of weapons and how they were brandished;
- the type of premises or persons who were the target of the aggravated robbery (e.g. was the premises a vulnerable target such as a late-night dairy?);
- the property stolen and the extent of recovery;
- whether there were any associated crimes, such as the conversion of a “*getaway*” vehicle; and
- the impact of the offending on the victim.
The court commented that a sentencing judge should consider the combination of the above factors to determine an appropriate sentence.

# ASSAULT WITH INTENT TO ROB
### Introduction
The offences created by this section are in subsection (1) and are essentially designed to provide a more serious punishment for unsuccessful attempts at aggravated robbery than does the general law of attempts or offences such as s 192 (assault with intent to facilitate commission of an offence).

### Understanding Assault with Intent to Rob
An assault with intent to rob is essentially an unsuccessful attempt at aggravated robbery, in that the theft element is not complete.

Like section 235, the offending under section 236(1) includes aggravating factors, such as the infliction of grievous bodily harm, the use of a weapon, or the combination of two or more robbers acting together.

The three forms of the offence provided for in subsection (1) largely mirror, in different order, the different forms of aggravated robbery provided for in s 235(1), and the key concepts in subsections (1)(a), (b) and (c) are discussed earlier in this lesson note.

The offences in paragraphs (b) being armed with any offensive weapon, and (c) being together with any other person, do not require the actual infliction of physical injury to any person.

An assault with intent to rob is essentially an unsuccessful attempt at aggravated robbery, in that the theft element is not complete.

The mens rea requires

- an intent to steal property and
- an intention to use force or the threat of force to extort the relevant property.
There is no requirement that the intention to rob be evinced by any demand from the defendant. It may be established by inference from the circumstances or otherwise.

### Subsection (2) assault on robbery victim
The offence under subs (2) provides an alternative charge to that of attempting to commit robbery under s 234, that is, of assault in the course of an attempt at robbery where none of the aggravating features in s 235 are to be found. As to the elements of “*assault*” and “*intent to rob*” see above discussion.

**BLACKMAIL INTRODUCTION**

Blackmail is a form of criminal extortion that involves making threats intended to apply pressure and force an individual to give up something of value. The nature of the threats laid out in section 237 is wide and can include threats of disclosure or accusation about an individual, as well as threats of harm to an individual.

The offence of Blackmail is located in Part 10 of the Crimes Act – Crimes against rights of property, as the offence cannot be complete without the intent to obtain something of value. However, the offence may also involve a gross breach of the Victim’s privacy or create intense fear, intimidation, and shame on behalf of the victim. As such, offences **must **be treated with appropriate sensitivity and care.

These are serious offences in New Zealand, punishable by up to 14 years in prison.

### Blackmail – Actus reus
Blackmail is the use of threats to compel a victim to behave in a particular way AND to extort property (including money) or some other benefit.

The actus reus (that which constitutes the physical act of the offence) can be summed up as a combination of elements depending on the facts.

The core element of the actus reus involves a threat or threats of some description. There are four options in relation to the threats made to an individual. These are;

- Threatens to make any accusation against any person
- Threatens to disclose something about any person
- Threatens to cause serious damage to property
- Threatens to endanger the safety of any person
*that, if certain instructions or demands are not complied with, an act or omission (whether by words or action) will occur. *

### Threatens
To threaten somebody, in general terms, requires any communication that is intended to incite fear, or be interpreted as a threat in the mind of any reasonable person.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Wyatt***
> *“Threatens “carries its ordinary meaning which requires little more than to ‘make clear an intention’*

### Example:
In the Canadian case ***Barros v R***, a private investigator hired by a defendant’s lawyer was charged with extortion – the equivalent of Blackmail in New Zealand. The Defendant was facing drug dealing charges based on evidence of a confidential informant. The private investigator hired by the defence, was tasked with finding out who the informant was. This he did by illegal means. Once he had the identity of the informant, the investigator approached the police officer in charge of the case and threatened to disclose the identity of the informant if the Sergeant did not drop the charges against the defendant.

The Supreme Court of Canada discussed what constituted the threat to the Sergeant.

***WHAT CONSTITUTES A THREAT***

In this case the private investigator did not **explicitly** threaten the police sergeant to drop the charges against the defendant. The Court stated that the law did not require the threat to be direct and specific, and that a subtle, veiled, or indirect reference may constitute a threat, **if the recipient comprehended it as a threat**.

The court concluded that it was “*not the specific words, but the situation*” that conveyed the threat. The message was received by the Police Sergeant and the meaning was unmistakable to him.  The question is, what would a reasonable person in the position of the police Sergeant understand?

***SUMMARY***

To summarise, the threat itself can be expressed or implied, direct, or indirect and subtle. What is communicated must be definite enough that a person of ordinary intelligence would understand it as a threat.  What is “*definite enough*” will depend on the context or in light of all the circumstances.

Put another way, it is the conduct as a whole that is considered and whether that conduct as a whole, amounts to a threat in the mind of a reasonable person?

This aligns with ***R v Collister*** which specifically deals with what constitutes threatening behaviour. (See previous discussions re ***Collister***)

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Collister***
> *Over to the jury whether on the whole of the evidence the conduct amounts to a threat*

***COMMUNICATION OF THE THREAT BY ANOTHER PARTY***

The threat can be made to the Victim in several ways, including:

- The suspect makes the threat themselves or
- The suspect knowingly and intentionally passes a threat made by another person on with the intent on assisting the threat-maker.
- The person making the threats does not need to be the one holding themselves out as the person who might execute the offence.
The overarching importance is that the threat is conveyed to the victim in some way whether by third party involvement or not.

### Example:
In ***R v Marshall*** a large bronze sculpture worth $250,000 was stolen from the front garden of a restaurant at Waikanae Beach. The owners contacted media outlets offering a reward for the return of the sculpture. They also distributed reward notices.

The editor of the Kapiti Observer was contacted by Marshall who stated that although he had not been involved in the theft, he knew where the sculpture was and could help return it.

* “At a meeting between Mr Marshall and the owners of the sculpture Mr Marshall reiterated that he had not been involved in the “burglary”. It was agreed that the owners would pay $6,000 on the basis that Mr Marshall would organise the return of the sculpture.”*

At the time for exchange, the editor was contacted. There had been a change to the agreement and Marshall stated that “*the people who had the statue now wanted $10,000, that it wasn't him it was them, but he wouldn't get any more out of it, and that if they didn't get the $10,000 the statue would be chopped up*.”

The threat to destroy the sculpture was made for the addition of $4000 above the agreed reward, and there was the implication that Marshall himself would take a cut of the extra money.

The New Zealand Court of Appeal discussed the nature of Marshalls role in the communication of the threat. Whether Marshall was doing more than delivering a message or brokering a return on commission was a matter for the jury to decide on the circumstances.

Marshall was found guilty of doing **more** than simply communicating a message, but that he had offered intentional help and or encouragement to those threatening the destruction of the sculpture.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Marshall [2007] NZCA 583***
> *establishes that the threat made need not be received directly by the intended victim provided it is conveyed to that victim *

In his oral directions the Judge explained that there were two ways that a threat conveyed by Mr Marshall could render him guilty of blackmail. First, if Mr Marshall himself was actually making the threat and was not neutrally passing it on. Second, if he was just passing it on, but when he did so he was knowingly and intentionally helping the blackmailers to achieve their goal of getting the money.

### Threatens to make an Accusation or threatens to make a disclosure
***Accusation***

An accusation traditionally refers to an allegation of criminal conduct. In this case, it does not matter whether or not there have been any formal charges brought against the victim or even whether or not the substance of the accusation is true or false.

***Disclosure***

A disclosure has a broader meaning and will be the most common application of this offence, particularly in the modern environment of online communications. A disclosure does not need to relate to criminal offending and extends to the revelation of information which would cause serious embarrassment or emotional distress.

Examples from Canadian and New Zealand case law include discussion that threats to disclose revealing photographs of complainants posing in the nude could suffice for the offence.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Kirby-Parker, [2016] NZDC 6150 (2016)***
> *“You then asked her to send a second nude picture to you and that if she did not do that you would post the first nude picture on Facebook. Because of your threat she did so.”*
> *“These threats were very real. “Send me another nude photograph or I'll put the first one on Facebook. Break off the relationship and I'll put all the nude photographs I have of you out in the electronic media. Unblock me or I'll do the same”*

As with accusation, there is no requirement that the disclosure be true, and the disclosure need only be to any other person. The breadth of the disclosure to multiple people or unlimited disclosure such as on the internet is a consideration for sentencing.

### Threaten to Cause serious damage to property
Blackmail includes threatening expressly or by implication to cause serious damage to property. This is to be given its ordinary meaning. The charge laid in ***R v Marshall*** is an example of this element. The property involved being the sculpture *Long Horizon* by New Zealand artist Paul Dibble, and worth $250,000 at the time.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Marshall***
> *When the matter came to trial Mr. Marshall faced two counts. Count one, the blackmail count, alleged that the appellant:*
> *“… on or about the 18th day of October 2005 … did threaten by implication to cause serious damage to property, namely a bronze … sculpture, with intent to cause [the owners] to act in accordance with his will and to obtain a benefit, namely $4,000.”*

### Threaten to Endanger the safety of any person
There has been discussion in NZ Courts about the meaning of ‘endangering the safety of any person” which was added in amendment to the offence in 2003. The then Minister of Justice stated that the purpose of the addition was to better capture those involved in industrial espionage, *“…to ensure that we have sufficient force to penalise people who may want to commit blackmail by way of tampering with supermarket products”. *

The discussion involved whether threats of violence could be direct or indirect as tampering with food production would be.

This was discussed in *R v Skeet.*

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Skeet***
> *Ms. Skeet was convicted of two representative counts of blackmail. Included among the alleged threats on the first of these charges were threats to “get to” the complainant's disabled daughter and to slit the daughter's throat. The alleged threats in the second charge included a comment that the daughter would get hurt if the complainant did not comply.*

***R v Skeet*** settled the matter finding that the phrase “*a threat to endanger the safety of any person” *should be given its ordinary and natural meaning so as to encompass both threats of direct **and** indirect harm. The Court went on to say “In everyday parlance [words or language], a threat to kill the complainant's child would be considered a threat to endanger the child's safety and to hold otherwise is, with respect, to give the phrase a strained meaning.”

### Mens rea for the criminal act of blackmail
**General Intent**

As with all criminal offences, the Actus Reus or criminal acts MUST be carried out intentionally or deliberately with purpose.

The communication of the threat must be deliberate (i.e. intentionally and purposefully made) and must happen at the same time (be contemporaneous) as the specific intent detailed in legislation.

**Specific Intent**

The specific mens rea of the offence is two-fold; The threats made by the offender must

Intend to compel the Victim to comply with their will or demand andIntend to obtain something of value if they do not comply.**For more on Intent, see Basic Concepts Module. **

The offence requires that the defendant have both

- an intention to overbear the will of the person to whom the threat is made so that that person will act as required or directed by the defendant, and
- an intention to obtain any benefit or to cause loss to any other person
### With intent to cause the person to act in accordance with the will of the person making the threat
Another Canadian case considered by its Supreme Court is ***R v Davis***.

Davis was found guilty on a number of offences of a sexual nature, one of which was extortion.  Davis obtained sexual favours from the complainant by threatened exposure of compromising photographs.

Extortion in Canada criminalizes intimidation and interference with freedom of choice. It punishes those who, through threats, accusations, menaces or violence, induce or attempt to induce their victims into doing anything or causing anything to be done.

As in Canada, New Zealand law requires proof of causation or the relationship between the threats and the victim’s freedom of choice.

When threats are coupled with purposeful demands, there is an inducement to agree to the demands. This interferes with the victim’s freedom of choice, as the victim may be coerced into doing something he or she would otherwise have chosen not to do. Such as, in Davis, the Victim acquiesced to give sexual favours, something she would otherwise have chosen not to do.

As an investigator you must prove that the threats were made with the aim to “overbear the will of the person… so that they will act as required or directed by the defendant.” (Adams)

### With intent to obtain any benefit or cause any loss to any other person
### Obtains
The term ‘*obtain*’ is defined in section 217 of the Crimes Act 1961 and means ‘*to obtain or retain for himself or herself or for any other person’*. The benefit obtained by the offender may therefore be a benefit either for the offender or for some other person.

### Benefit
The term ‘*benefit*’ is specifically defined in section 237(3) of the offence of Blackmail.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Section 237, Crimes Act 1961***
> ***Blackmail***
> *(3)  In this section and in section 239, benefit means any benefit, pecuniary advantage, privilege, property, service, or valuable consideration.*

The term property is further defined by section 2 of the Crimes Act.

**Property** includes real and personal property and any estate or interest in any real or personal property, [money, electricity] and any debt and anything in action and any other right or interest.

The remaining terms do not have a specific definition in legislation but have been discussed by the Courts in the context of Fraud and Arson offences, but also in Blackmail.

What a benefit encapsulates will be judged on the facts and circumstances of each case. It is accepted that “*The word benefit is defined in wide terms in section 237 (3).”* (Stolley quoting Adams).

” *Benefit*” is defined in wide terms in the subsection. Of the words in that definition, only “*property*” is further defined in the Act. While “*property*”, “*pecuniary advantage*”, and *“valuable consideration*” all have the connotation of things of financial value, the same is not necessarily true of “*benefit*”, nor of “*privilege*” or “*service*”. A privilege, service or benefit which has no ascertainable monetary value will come within the section. Adams on Criminal Law.

Further in ***Li v R***, 2016, the Court of Appeal found.

*“Benefit”* is not [specifically] defined in the Crime Act and therefore bears its ordinary meaning of “*advantage*”, “*good*” or “*profit*”.

In ***R v HDSN*** the high court defined what could be included in the definition of benefit in a pre-trial hearing.  It was argued, as with fraud cases, “*benefit*” needs to have some financial element to it which was not present in this case.

****CASELAW****
> ***R V HDSN ***
> *The facts of this case involved Mr. N, who created a number of profiles of himself via text message and the relatively new social media site Facebook. Via these different profiles Mr. N both elicited a number of naked and semi-naked photos of the Victim and later threatened to put the images on the internet or Facebook unless furth images were sent.*

The court acknowledged that when comparing a threat from Mr N that he would post the photographs on Facebook for money, with the same threat for the non-financial reward of receiving more photographs, it would be “*at odds with the underlying purpose of the offence*” to judge one Blackmail and one not.

Further the Court considered the Canadian Case R v *Davis*, *Adams on Criminal Law*, and the fact that the wording of the legislation does not exclude non-financial benefit and rewards to reach the conclusion that,

*“… I am of the view that although the offence contained in s 237 was not enacted with actions such as are alleged in this case in mind, its terms are broad enough to encapsulate them. If the facts here R v HDSN, alleged can be proved at trial, a properly instructed jury could convict Mr N … of blackmail.”*

Examples of what has been accepted as, or discussed in judgements as a ‘Benefit’ in NZ

- downloaded CCTV footage for possible sale
- moving to live in a particular town.
- Jobs, passports, promotions
- Naked and semi-naked photos
- Work permit, refugee status
- opportunity to apply for a job
- a lesser sentence at court
- diploma or other educational qualification
- Exposure on a television program
### Investigation Application
Whether what is demanded is a benefit in terms of the law is judged according to the circumstances of the case. As such, it is important that Detectives have evidence of **what** benefit the offender intended to obtain. A thorough investigation will include enquiries, exhibits, and interviews that cover this element.

### Statutory defence
Section 237(2) provides a defence to a charge of blackmail.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Blackmail***
> ***Section 237(2), Crimes Act 1961***
> *(2)	Every one who acts in the manner described in subsection (1) is guilty of blackmail, even though that person believes that he or she is entitled to the benefit or to cause the loss, unless the making of the threat is, in the circumstances, a reasonable and proper means for effecting his or her purpose.*

In ***R v Marshall**** *(facts outlined above) the Court of Appeal considered Marshall’s defence is that it was submitted

*“…that Mr Marshall believed that he was entitled to a benefit (payment of a commission) and communication of the threat (that the sculpture would be destroyed) was a reasonable and proper means of achieving his purpose of seeing the deal was completed so that he could collect his commission…”*

The Court stated that it is a defence to a charge of blackmail for the defendant to show they believed they were entitled to obtain the benefit or to cause the loss, and, objectively viewed, the making of the threat was a reasonable and proper means for obtaining the benefit or causing the loss. It will be for the jury to determine whether the means were reasonable and proper.

In Marshall, even if the jury concluded that he genuinely believed he was entitled as a middleman to a commission of $4000 (or part thereof), this is not in itself a defence to a charge under section 237(1), unless the threat is, in the circumstances, a reasonable and proper means for effecting his or her purpose.  The jury rejected that the passing on of the message of the destruction of the sculpture was not genuine and reasonable or proper in the circumstances.

This means that, where the defence is raised, the court will be required to determine what is reasonable and proper, given the circumstances of each case.

### Investigation Application
If during the course of an investigation into Blackmail, there is evidence that the offender believed they were entitled to the benefit demanded or received, it is important that Detectives have evidence regarding why or what formed this belief on his or her part. Enquiries or questioning on the belief of the offender as to the reasonableness of **how** the benefit was demanded or received is also important. A thorough investigation will include enquiries, exhibits, and interviews that cover this statutory defence.

**DEMANDING WITH INTENT TO STEAL  **

***INTRODUCTION***

Demanding with intent to steal, is defined by s239 of the Crimes Act 1961.

****LEGISLATION****
> ***Demanding with intent to steal***
> ***Section 239, Crimes Act 1961***
> *(1)	Every one is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 14 years who, without claim of right, by force or with any threat, compels any person to execute, make, accept, endorse, alter, or destroy any document capable of conferring a pecuniary advantage with intent to obtain any benefit.*
> *(2)	Every one is liable to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 7 years who, with menaces or by any threat, demands any property from any persons with intent to steal it.*

### Actions of the defendant
The essence of the offence contained in subsection (1), is that the defendant has used force or threats to cause another to create, alter or deal with a document which is capable of conferring a pecuniary advantage. Such conduct will not amount to robbery, because no property is taken or dealt with by the defendant.

The defendant must compel the execution of a document, which will require that the will of the other person be actually overborne. This must be done by force or by a threat.

Force in this context will require the use or threatened use of violence, but the force need not be directed at the person who will make or alter the document. Force used on another person should suffice.

In subsection (1), the defendant may compel the making of the document “*by any threat*”. In this context “*any*” will carry its normal meaning and is not restricted to a threat of the kind mentioned in s237 (Blackmail).

### Without claim of right
See previous discussion on claim of right as well as in the Basic Concepts Module

### Execute
To execute a document is normally to “*do what the law requires to give validity to the document.  It is not confined to the signing or doing something to the face of the document*.”

Entry of data in a computer program may amount to “*execution*” of a document.

### Document capable of conferring a pecuniary advantage
Document is defined in s217 of the Crimes Act, 1961.

The Court has defined a document essentially as a thing which provides evidence or information or serves as a record.

The fact that developments in technology may improve the way in which evidence or information is provided or a record is kept does not change the fundamental purpose of that technology, nor a conceptual appreciation of that function. Legislation must be interpreted with that in mind.

It can be said that a document is capable of conferring a pecuniary benefit if it could do so in certain circumstances, even if those conditions are not likely to occur, and includes such things as; cheques, airline tickets, TAB tickets, completed bank withdrawal slip, insurance claim etc.

### Demand
It is sufficient if there is a clear request, made firmly.  It is not necessary that the demand be communicated to the person to whom it is directed; it is sufficient that the defendant said something that constituted such a demand to one person, with the intention that it be passed on to another person from whom the property was demanded.

### From any persons
Although the subsection uses the plural “*persons*”, the section is to be read as including the singular unless a contrary intention appears.

### Menaces or any threat
It is not clear that there is any practical difference between “*menaces*” and “*threats*”.  It will be sufficient if the words or conduct convey a threat of something detrimental or unpleasant happening to the person to whom the threat is made.

In appropriate cases threats to cause harm to a third party, even an unidentified third party, can suffice for the offence.

### Intent to steal
Section 239 requires the prosecution to show the defendant acted dishonestly and without claim of right.
