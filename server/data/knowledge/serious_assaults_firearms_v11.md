# SERIOUS ASSAULTS - INTRODUCTION
Serious assaults vary in nature and degree, and to determine the appropriate charge, a serious assault investigation will generally focus on establishing three things:

- the intent of the offender, (mens rea) *and*
- the act of the offender and (actus reus) *and*
- the degree of harm suffered by the victim by the act of the offender. (causation)
### Identifying the correct offence
The below matrix is useful in identifying the most appropriate offence and the elements of the offence. It is important that you look for the ‘intent’ of the offender and the ‘outcome’ of their actions.

### Example:
‘X’ is angry at ‘Y’. X picks up a knife and swings the knife at Y’s arm causing a large bleeding cut.

**Intent**

****

*By swinging the knife at Y we can conclude that X intended to cause serious harm to Y. Therefore, the offence will lay within one of the top boxes. *

- With intent to cause grievous bodily harm
- To any person
**Outcome**

****

*Y sustained a large bleeding cut as a result of X’s actions. A large bleeding cut is a wound as it is a breaking of the skin evidenced by the flow of blood therefore the offence will be S188(1) CA61*

- Wounds
- Any person
**NOTE**: You must identify and define a person twice as there may be cases where the actual victim was not the intended target. If the primary intent is to escape, avoid detection or arrest then you will use the 191 offences (in part two of this document) and then will look at the outcome (resulting injury/wound) to identify the subsection.

Under section 188, subsections (1) and (2) both relate to actions that result in wounding, maiming, disfiguring or grievous bodily harm to the victim. The ***outcome*** is therefore the same; but the distinction between the two subsections lies with the offender’s ***intent*.**

In subsection (1) the offender intends to cause ***grievous bodily harm***.

In subsection (2) the offender intends only to ***injure* **the victim, although the actual outcome is a greater degree of harm than was anticipated.

Subsection (2) also allows for an alternative mens rea element involving “reckless disregard for the safety of others”.

In such cases the offender acts in a way that he foresees is likely to result in injury, but proceeds regardless of the risk, and in doing so causes serious harm to the victim.

Under section 189, both subsections (1) and (2) relate to actions that result in injury to the victim therefore the outcome is the same.  The distinction between the two subsections is drawn from the offender’s intent.

In subsection (1) the offender intends to cause grievous bodily harm, however the outcome is a lesser degree of harm than was intended.

In subsection (2) the offender intends only to injure the victim or acts with reckless disregard for the safety of others, and in doing so injures the victim.

- Firstly, consider the **outcome**, was it an injury or wound?
- Secondly, consider the **intent **was it an injury or GBH?

- Firstly, consider the **intent**, was it an injury or GBH?
- Secondly, consider the **outcome. **Was it an injury or wound?
# UNDERSTANDING SERIOUS ASSAULTS 
The serious assault offences under sections 188 and 189 are structured in the same way. They differ from each other only in the level of injury actually inflicted. The mens rea required by the two sections is identical. The following will discuss each of the concepts and definitions that appear in both sections as they have the same meaning.

### The actus reus for Serious Assaults 
What is interesting about the legislation defining serious assaults is that an actus reus is not specified. The legislation defines serious assaults into two parts;

- **A specific intent to cause a degree of injury**, and
- An outcome of some degree of injury to have been **actually caused** by the offender to another person.
The **way in which that injury has been caused is the actus reus**, which must be proved by the prosecution. In the case of section 188 and 189, this actus reus is normally an assault of some kind.

As with all offences an assault requires proof of **general** mens rea.

****LEGISLATION****
> *Section 2, ****Crimes Act 1961***
> *Assault “means the act of ****intentionally applying**** or attempting to apply force….”*

The different mens rea stipulated for Serious Assaults can be divided into Intentionally and Recklessly.

### Deliberate / intentional act: 
Intention is not specifically defined in the Crimes Act 1961. It is one category of *mens rea* required to be proved in all criminal offences in New Zealand. Mens rea could be seen as guilty mind or criminal or wrongful purpose. If a person innocently or accidentally causes harm, then she or he lacks mens rea and under this concept, should not be criminally prosecuted.

Intent is a deliberate mental attitude around the criminal act(s) as well as the results of those criminal acts. A mental attitude cannot ordinarily be directly proved but must be inferred from surrounding facts and circumstances. Intent refers only to the state of mind with which the act is done or omitted.

The general intent means that an act (in this case normally some form of assault) must be done ***deliberately***.  The act or omission must be more than involuntary or accidental.

### Example: 
An example of this is section 2, Crimes Act 1961, where the definition of assault “means the act of intentionally applying or attempting to apply force…”.  For instance, a person intends to strike the victim with their fist on the right side of the face above the eye, and they deliberately (purposely) raise their arm, close their fist, and swing it at their victim.

### The mens rea for Serious Assaults 
The specific mens rea for serious assaults stipulated in section 188 and 189 are

- With intent to cause grievous bodily harm
- Intent to injure.
- With reckless disregard for the safety of others
This mens rea “is an intent to produce a specific result”.  In this context result means “aim, object, or purpose”. (*Simester and Brookbanks*.)

Put together a serious assault can be seen as

**A specific intent to cause harm.**
> **+**

### Example:
In a serious assault context, intent may be related to a scenario as follows:

*X ****intended**** to commit a specific act (stabbing Y in the chest with a chef’s knife 6 times) with an ****intent**** to get a specific result (causing really serious harm to Y).*

### Proving intent to injure or intent to cause grievous bodily harm
The onus is generally on the prosecution to prove an offender’s intent beyond reasonable doubt.

While an offender’s admissions as to their intent are potentially good evidence, it is best practice to support these with circumstantial evidence from which their intent can also be inferred.

****CASELAW****
> ***R v Collister ***
> *In R v Collister two police officers were charged with demanding with menaces after causing a man to believe he would be arrested for soliciting homosexual acts unless he paid them money. Although no express demand for money was made, it was held that the defendants’ ****intent could be inferred from the circumstances.***

Circumstantial evidence from which an offender’s intent may be inferred can include:

- the offender’s actions and words before, during and after the event
- the surrounding circumstances
- the nature of the act itself
In serious assault cases, additional circumstantial evidence that may assist in proving the offender’s intent may include:

- prior threats
- evidence of premeditation
- the use of a weapon
- whether any weapon used was opportunistic or purposely brought
- the number of blows
- the degree of force used
- the body parts targeted by the offender (e.g. the head)
- the degree of resistance or helplessness of the victim (e.g. unconscious)
### Intent vs lack of memory
In ***R v Taisalika***, the defendant crashed a party, and in an unprovoked attack struck another partygoer on the side of the head with a glass. The glass shattered, causing a serious gash to the victim’s temple and multiple cuts to his face.

Taisalika argued unsuccessfully that he had been so intoxicated he could not remember the incident and therefore he could not have had the necessary intent. The Court held that loss of memory of past events **is not the same as lack of intent** at the time.

****CASELAW****
> R v Taisalika
> The nature of the blow and the gash which it produced point strongly to the presence of the necessary intent.

### With reckless disregard for the safety of others: 
A third specific mens rea described by sections 188 and 189 is “with reckless disregard for the safety of others.”

Here a reckless act is carried out which causes harm to another person.

As with other sections of the Crimes Act, behaviour is criminalised if it involves consciously and deliberately taking an unjustifiable risk. It must be proved not only that the defendant was aware of the risk and proceeded regardless (a subjective test), but also that it was unreasonable for him to do so (an objective test).

Recklessness is a test based on the defendant’s appreciation of the risk of the offence and their decision to run that risk anyway. Cameron v R established a two-part test which will aid the investigator in establishing reckless disregard for the safety of others. They are;

Part (a) of the Cameron test is completely subjective. A “real possibility” is substantively the same as something that “could well happen” (as stated in *R v Harney [1987] 2 NZLR 576*), formerly the leading case on the Recklessness test. Both merely require that the defendant has recognised the risk the offence anticipates as being possible. The defendant does not need to consider the risk significant.

Part (b) is subjective and objective. It looks at whether the defendant’s actions were objectively reasonable given the risk the defendant understood.

Example One

A driver speeds through a crowded pedestrian area at high speed, aware of the risk but continuing regardless. Although the driver does not intend to hit anyone, they strike a pedestrian, causing serious injury.

Is this a Reckless Act?

The driver was aware that there was a real possibility of harm to others (subjective test), yet they proceeded.

An objective assessment would find their behaviour unreasonable because most people would consider driving at high speed through a crowded area to be extremely dangerous.

Example Two:

During a crowded sporting event, one person lights fireworks and throws them into the crowd for fun. The individual knows that fireworks and dangerous and could cause injury but proceeds anyway. One firework explodes near a spectator causing burns and hearing damage.

Is this a Reckless Act?

The person recognised the risk that their actions could cause harm to others but chose to throw fireworks into the crowd anyway (subjective test).

An objective assessment would determine that throwing fireworks into a crowd is unreasonable and poses a clear danger which an average New Zealander would recognise.

### The Outcome – What harm has been caused?
The legal principle of causation must be proved when investigating serious assaults. That is –

- the degree of harm that was the outcome of the intentional or reckless act,
- and that the harm was caused by that act.
### Degree of harm
Wounding, maiming, or disfiguration need not be grievous, if in causing that harm, the defendant had the intent to cause really serious harm.

In ***R v Hunt*** the defendant, while breaking into another man’s stables, was caught by the property owner and his servant. Hunt attempted to stab the property owner with a knife, but in the ensuing struggle he unintentionally inflicted a superficial cut to the servant’s wrist.

He was found guilty of wounding the servant, as although the cut was *“slight and not in a vital part”* his *intent* had nevertheless been to cause serious harm (albeit to the property owner and not to the servant).

This decision confirms that if there was an intent to do grievous bodily harm, it was immaterial whether grievous harm was done.  The question is not what the resultant harm was, but what harm was intended.

### Bodily Harm
****CASELAW****
> R v McArthur
> “Bodily Harm’ includes any hurt or injury calculated to interfere with the health or comfort of the victim. It need not be permanent but must be more than transitory and trifling.

### Grievous Bodily Harm
Grievous bodily harm can be defined simply as *‘harm that is really serious’*.

****CASELAW****
> DPP v Smith
> ‘Bodily harm’ needs no explanation and ‘grievous’ means no more and no less than ‘really serious’.

The term *‘grievous’* refers to the degree of harm, rather than to the nature of it or how it was caused.

Grievous bodily harm will most commonly result from an assault, but not always.  If the harm is really serious, it does not matter how it was caused.

As long as the harm is serious, it need not involve life threatening or permanent injury.

Whilst most offences under section 188 will involve a physical assault, the section does not specify the manner in which the harm may be caused; there is no reference to the use of force, and it is therefore not necessary to prove an assault in all cases.

In ***R v Mwai*** the defendant faced multiple counts in relation to two women who he infected with HIV, and several others whom he put at risk, through unprotected sex.

In affirming his conviction for *“causing grievous bodily harm with reckless disregard for the safety of others*”, the Court of Appeal held that section 188 is not limited to the immediate harmful consequences of the offender’s actions, such as external assault or injury from a blow of some kind.

Expert medical evidence adduced at the time, that HIV follows “*a steady relentless progression*” leading to AIDS and then inevitably to death, was sufficient to establish that the defendant had caused grievous bodily harm.

You need to be mindful that all that is required for the actus reus is an act causing grievous bodily harm. The link between cause and effect is a physical one, not one of time. Usually of course the effect is instant: a blow causes a wound. But it is not necessarily so. The consequences may be delayed, but they are consequences, nonetheless.

### Wounds
In ***R v Waters**** *it was held that a wound involves the breaking of the skin and the flowing of blood, either externally or internally.  Despite a prolonged and violent assault, the only medical evidence of harm to the victim was a bleeding nose, which was held to be an ‘*injury*’ rather than a wound in that particular case.

However, any rupture of the tissues of the body, internal or external can amount to a wound, and whether or not it does is a matter of fact for determination in each case.

****CASELAW****
> R v Waters
> A wound is a ‘breaking of the skin evidenced by the flow of blood. May be internal or external.

### Wounds v GBH
The terms ‘*wounds*’, ‘*maims*’ and ‘*disfigures*’ refer to type of injury caused, whereas the term ‘grievous’ refers to the degree or seriousness of the injury.

For example, stabbing a person with a knife will cause, by definition, a wound, although the wound may or may not be ‘serious’.

A single stab in the arm may result in a superficial wound but may still constitute an offence against section 188 (1) if the offender’s intent was to inflict really serious harm.

By contrast, ‘*wounding*’ a person by stabbing them five times in the chest would be more appropriately classed as grievous bodily harm due to the seriousness of the wounds caused.

### Maims
To maim means “*depriving another of the use of such of his members as may render him the less able in fighting, either to defend himself or to annoy his adversary*”.

In practical terms, it will involve mutilating, crippling, or disabling a part of the body so as to deprive the victim of the use of a limb or of one of the senses.

It is suggested in legal commentaries that to constitute a maim, there needs to be some degree of permanence.

Maiming is an archaic term that has limited application today, as the loss of a limb or one of the senses would generally be considered really serious, and therefore classed as grievous bodily harm.

### Disfigures
To ‘*disfigure*’ means *“to deform or deface; to mar or alter the figure or appearance of a person”*.

A disfigurement results from the infliction of an external injury that detracts from the personal appearance of the victim; however, it does not need to be permanent.

In ***R v Rapana and Murray*** the defendants forcibly held down their victim and tattooed red marks on his face, using a needle and red ink from a broken pen. The marks faded over time and had disappeared by the time of trial.

****CASELAW****
> R v Rapana and Murray
> Disfigure covers not only permanent damage but also temporary damage.

### Injury
Injury is defined in Section 2(1), Crimes Act 1961

To **injure** means to cause actual bodily harm

### Actual bodily harm
May be internal or external and need not be permanent or dangerous.

***R v McArthur*** (discussed earlier in this lesson note in relation to ‘bodily harm’) can again be referenced here.

****CASELAW****
> R v McArthur
> “Bodily Harm’ includes any hurt or injury calculated to interfere with the health or comfort of the victim. It need not be permanent but must be more than transitory and trifling.

In ***R v McArthur*** the complainant was knocked down by a vehicle when he was walking in Vulcan Lane, Auckland City. He was assisted to his feet by a witness who gave evidence that the complainant did not appear to have any cuts or broken bones but appeared dazed. The complainant referred to himself as sitting on the footpath with his head “spinning round”. No further evidence of injury was presented.

The court found that the facts presented **did not amount to proof** that the complainant had suffered an injury in terms of the definition in either the legislation or case law.

### Psychological harm
The Court of Appeal in ***R v Chan-Fook ***held that “*the body of the victim includes all parts of his body, including his organs, his nervous system, and his brain. Bodily injury therefore may include injury to any of those parts of his body responsible for his mental and other faculties ... accordingly the phrase actual bodily harm is capable of including psychiatric injury*”.

Therefore, actual bodily harm can include psychiatric injury if medical evidence confirms an identifiable clinical condition.

The Court of Appeal cautioned however, that mere emotions, such as fear, distress, or panic, would not suffice.  Expert evidence should be called identifying a recognised psychiatric illness.

### Injury v GBH
Whether the actual bodily harm suffered by the victim is an injury or one of the more serious outcomes provided for in section 188(1) is a matter of fact for determination in each case.

Where the harm is at the lower end of the scale of seriousness, it may amount to an injury.

Where it is higher on the scale, it may amount to a wounding, maiming, or disfiguring depending on the nature of it, or to grievous bodily harm depending on its seriousness.

### Person and Doctrine of transferred malice 
It is not necessary that the person suffering the harm was the intended victim. It is important that investigators prove **who **the person injured is.

Where the defendant mistakes the identity of the person injured, or where harm intended for one person is accidentally inflicted on another, they are still criminally responsible, under the Doctrine of Transferred Malice, despite the wrong target being struck.

This principle was applied in *Hunt*, where the defendant intended to stab the property owner, but accidentally wounded the man’s servant instead.

# INTRODUCTION
Firearms not only have the capacity to kill or seriously injure a person, but also increase an offender’s ability to frighten victims and enforce compliance. Consequently, the use of a firearm during a crime raises the offending into the realms of a serious violence offence.

While most offences involving the unlawful possession and misuse of firearms are found in the Arms Act 1983, this module focuses on s198B of the Crimes Act 1961.

### Understanding what a firearm is
The terms ‘*firearm*’ and ‘*airgun*’ are defined by the Arms Act 1983.  The primary difference between a firearm and airgun is that a firearm acts by force of explosive, whereas an airgun acts by force of compressed air or gas.

Firearm is defined in Section 2(1), Arms Act 1983.

****LEGISLATION****
> Interpretation
> firearm—
> means anything from which any shot, bullet, missile, or other projectile can be discharged by force of explosive; and
> includes—
> anything that has been adapted so that it can be used to discharge a shot, bullet, missile, or other projectile by force of explosive; and
> anything which is not for the time being capable of discharging any shot, bullet, missile, or other projectile but which, by its completion or the replacement of any component part or parts or the correction or repair of any defect or defects, would be a firearm within the meaning of paragraph (a) or subparagraph (i); and
> anything (being a firearm within the meaning of paragraph (a) or subparagraph (i)) which is for the time being dismantled or partially dismantled; and
> any specially dangerous airgun

Airgun is defined in Section 2(1), Arms Act 1983.

****LEGISLATION****
> Interpretation
> Airgun includes —
> any air rifle; and
> any air pistol; and
> (c) any weapon from which, by the use of gas or compressed air (and not by force of explosive), any shot, bullet, missile, or other projectile can be discharged

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 5, Criminal Procedure Act 2011

# UNDERSTANDING FIREARM OFFENCES COMMISSION OF A CRIME WITH A FIREARM 
### Uses any firearm
Unlike other firearm offences found in the Crimes Act, (such as section 198A Using a firearm against law enforcement officer), the words “*uses any firearm*” in section **198B are not** accompanied by the phrase *“in any manner whatever*” and may therefore have a narrower meaning than other sections.

The term includes firing or presenting a firearm, or displaying it in a menacing manner, but may not extend to the use of a firearm as a club or other forms of handling of it.

### Has any firearm with him or her 
Under this provision the offender must knowingly have the firearm with them.

In ***R v Cugullere*** the defendant was stopped by police while driving a stolen van in which three pickaxe handles (offensive weapons) were located. The defendant argued successfully that it was not his van and that he had no knowledge that the illegal items were in it. It was concluded that the words “*has with him ...”* must mean “*knowingly has with him ...”.*

### Knowledge (Refer basic legal concepts module)
There must also be evidence that the defendant not only had possession, in the sense that he or she knowingly had custody or control of a firearm, but also that it was at the time available and at hand for him or her to use while committing the imprisonable offence.

In ***R v Kelt*** the offender was arrested for a robbery 3 months earlier in which he had used a firearm. The firearm in that incident had been recovered. When arrested 3 months later in the kitchen of his residence, a ‘*robbers kit’* was found in the kitchen. Along with bolt cutters ammunition, hats, and gloves, was a sawn-off shotgun. Kelt was arrested for the original robbery and also charged with having a firearm with him with intent to commit an (indictable) offence.

The court decided that mere possession would not be enough to establish the offence. The law requires the evidence to go a stage further. It went on to state “*Of course the classic case of having a gun with you is if you are carrying it. But even if you are not carrying it, you may yet have it with you if it is immediately available to you.”*

This anticipates situations such as a firearm in a car or bag or having one ‘*stashed*’ in a convenient place for use.

****CASELAW****
> R v Kelt
> Having a firearm “with him” requires “a very close physical link and a degree of immediate control over the weapon by the man alleged to have the firearm with him”.

In ***R v Manapouri*** it was concluded that more than one person might have the same weapon “*with him*” at the one time if each had the physical link and the necessary control.

### Circumstances 
In circumstances that prima facie shows an intention to use it in connection with that imprisonable offence.

Mere possession is not sufficient; there must be accompanying circumstances showing a prima facie intention to use the firearm in the commission of the imprisonable offence.

In ***Tuli v Police*** a brawl between 30 Black Power and Mongrel Mob members broke out, outside a pub in New Brighton. When Police arrived, they found Tuli standing still holding a crowbar above his head. He was charged under s202A – having in his possession an offensive weapon, in circumstances that prima facie showed an intention to use it to commit a criminal offence.

Tuli argued that he had found the crowbar on the road, and not being a member of either gang had picked it up in self-defense, should he need it, of himself and his cousin, who had also found himself in the middle of the brawl and who was also not a member of either gang.

The court found that the phrase “*prima facie*” should be given its ordinary meaning of “*at first appearance*”. The court went on to say that there was no doubt that the police in this case acted properly “because when the constable arrived and saw the appellant with the crowbar held up, he had reasonable cause to suspect that an offence may be occurring or about to occur.”

****CASELAW****
> Tuli v Police
> Prima facie circumstances are those which are [at first appearance] sufficient to show or establish an intent in the absence of evidence to the contrary.

The offence of Strangulation or Suffocation under section 189A was introduced in December 2018 with a maximum penalty of seven years imprisonment. It was aimed to address the seriousness of non-fatal strangulation, which is a risk factor to further serious violence and homicide in domestic violence situations.

Prior to this when no visible injuries resulted strangulation was commonly charged as “*male assaults female*”. This charge did not necessarily reflect the growing evidence of the severity of the coercive and controlling behaviour, and consequences reflective in the action of strangulation.

Given the relative novelty of the offence the courts are refining sentencing guidelines for this offence. What is clear from an investigative point of view, is that it is important that evidence of **all** surrounding circumstances of the act of strangulation is gathered to put before the court. The courts require the surrounding circumstances to conduct “*a full evaluation of circumstances to achieve justice in the individual case.*”

### Actus reus / Mens rea of strangulation
As for all crimes proof of the act and intent must be required for evidential sufficiency. The intentional or reckless action of blocking a person’s mouth or nose or applying pressure to their throat or neck must result in the outcome of impeding normal breathing or circulation. This does not require a total loss of consciousness on the part of the victim.

### Other evidence to support sentencing
As strangulation can occur in a wide variety of contexts, the seriousness of the offending will depend on the facts of the individual case. The following are just a few of the surrounding circumstances of which evidence must be gathered.

- The act of strangulation in the context of a domestic relationship
- Threats, other acts of violence to the victim, other person or property,
- Loss of consciousness,
- Injuries received,
- Fear, terror and helplessness experienced by the Victim
- mental harm suffered
**Note:** Positional asphyxiation is not covered by this section unless the pressure is applied to the applicable areas.

# UNDERSTANDING AGGRAVATED WOUNDING 
### Introduction
To ‘aggravate’ something is to make it worse. In the context of section 191, offending is aggravated by the fact that the offender caused harm to the victim **in the process of committing some other imprisonable offence.**

Aggravated Wounding has similarities with serious assaults in that both subsections of the offence requires **proof of a specific intent** around the circumstance of the commission of an offence where violence against a person is used.

Secondly, proof of the outcome of the act of violence is required. Like serious assaults the specific act that causes the harm is not specified – however the **outcome actually caused** to a person is.

Subsection (1) specifies the following outcomes

- wounds
- maims
- disfigures
- causes grievous bodily harm
- stupefies
- renders unconscious
- by any violent means renders any person incapable of resistance.
- Subsection (1)  – outcome is wounding, maiming or one of the other specified forms of harm.
Subsection (2) specifies the following outcome

- injury.
**Note:** Where the offender assaults a person with one of the specified intents, but the victim suffers no actual bodily harm, the appropriate charge would be one of ‘aggravated assault’ under section 192.

### The mens rea of aggravated wounding
Section 191 lists several specific intents which must be proved by the prosecution. These intents involve the committing of an imprisonable offence . Essentially to charge with this offence the offender must be intending to carry out a serious offence or intending to carry out specific actions within a close nexus of an imprisonable offence.

The specific intents listed in section 191 are the same for both subsection (1) and (2) and are as follows.

- to commit an offence (a)
- to facilitate the commission of any imprisonable offence(a)
- to avoid detection in the commission of any imprisonable offence of themselves or any other person (b)
- to avoid arrest or facilitate flight of themselves or any other person upon the commission of any imprisonable offence (c)
### Further definition of intent for aggravated wounding
**R v Tihi **provides further definition by the Court of what is required to prove the full mens rea of the offence.

One of the defendants in this matter held a knife to a taxi driver’s neck in order to steal his taxi, inflicting a small cut in the process. He admitted his intent to steal the taxi but argued that the injury to the driver was unintentional.

In ***R v Tihi*** it was held that in proving an offence against section 191, the prosecution must satisfy a ‘**two-fold’ test for intent**:

- The defendant intended to facilitate the commission of an imprisonable offence (or one of the other intents specified in paragraphs (a), (b) or (c)), ***and* **
- He intended to cause the specified harm or was reckless as to that risk.
****CASELAW****
> R v Tihi
> In addition to one of the specific intents outlined in paragraphs (a) – (c) it must be shown that the offender meant to cause the specified harm or foresaw that the actions undertaken by him were likely to expose others to the risk of suffering it.

So, whereas with the first element of the test, a specific intent is required (i.e. to commit an imprisonable offence, avoid detection or avoid arrest and so on), the second element may **involve either the mens rea of intent or recklessness.**

### Facilitate the commission of any imprisonable offence
To ‘facilitate’ means to make possible or make easy / easier.

An imprisonable offence is an offence punishable by imprisonment.

Under section 191(1)(a) therefore, the offender intentionally or recklessly causes specified harm in order to **make it easier to commit** the intended imprisonable offence.

### Offence complete
Provided the offender has the necessary intent at the time the harm is caused, it is **immaterial **whether they actually commit the intended imprisonable offence or not.

In R v Sturm the defendant was charged with sexually violated four young men over a period of time. The defendant gained the attention of the young men through his sports car, his businesses and lifestyle. His approach was to invite young men to his office in the early hours of the morning where he would then provide them with alcohol and substances such as MDMA (Ecstasy) and methamphetamine to stupefy them in order to facilitate his intended sexual violations of them

The defendant was found guilty of stupefying with intent to facilitate the commission of a crime (sexual violation) contrary to s 191 of the Crimes Act 1961.

The case on appeal, confirmed that "providing the stupefying is carried out with the intention at the time of the stupefying, that it should facilitate the commission of a crime, it is not necessary for the prosecution to prove the intended crime was actually subsequently committed."

****CASELAW****
> R v Sturm
> Under section 191(1)(a) it is not necessary for the prosecution to prove the intended crime was actually subsequently committed.

Another example considers a man who threatens a woman with a gun to make her stop struggling and submit to being raped commits an offence against section 191(1)(a) whether or not he actually rapes her. If he goes on to commit the rape, he will face a separate charge of ‘*sexual violation*’.

### With intent to avoid the detection of himself or of any other person 
Section 191 (1) (b) states that everyone is liable ... who with intent to avoid the detection of themselves or another in the commission of any imprisonable offence, causes a specified violent outcome

Offences under section 191(1)(b) arise during the commission of an imprisonable offence, where the offender causes the specified harm to prevent himself or another person from being ‘*caught in the act’*.

### Example:
A ‘*look out’* knocks a security guard over the head with a crow bar to prevent him from walking in on a burglary in progress. The security guard is left unconscious.

The specific intent in this case is the intent to stop the security guard from “detecting” the crime. As well as this you must prove an intent to cause security guard to lose consciousness, or at least be reckless as to whether hitting him over the head with a crowbar would risk this outcome.

### With intent to Avoid Arrest or facilitate flight 
“ …with intent to avoid the arrest or facilitate the flight of himself or of any other person upon the commission or attempted commission of any imprisonable offence…”

Offences under section 191(1)(c) arise **after **an imprisonable offence has been committed or attempted. The specified harm is caused to enable the offender or offenders to affect their escape more easily, or to prevent their capture.

The prosecution must therefore prove that an imprisonable offence was actually committed or attempted before a person can be convicted of aggravated wounding while escaping after it.

In ***R v Wati*** the defendant was present during a riot in which police officers were attacked and a police vehicle set on fire. Wati fled the scene, but was caught by a constable, who he kicked and punched in an attempt to avoid arrest. He was charged with “*riot*” and “*aggravated assault*” on the constable.

Wati was acquitted on the riot charge, and it was held that he therefore “*could not be guilty upon the count of assault with the aggravating feature of avoiding arrest for riot*”. He was, however, convicted of common assault.

****CASELAW****
> R v Wati
> There must be proof of the commission or attempted commission of a crime either by the person committing the assault or by the person whose arrest or flight he intends to avoid or facilitate.

### The Outcome – What Harm has been caused?
Aggravated Wounding shares the same specified outcomes as Wounding or Injuring with intent in sections 188 and 189. These are

- wounds or
- maims or
- disfigures or
- causes grievous bodily harm
In addition to the terms above which you are already familiar with, Aggravated Wounding specifies three other outcomes. They are,

- stupefies or
- rendering a person unconscious or
- by any violent means renders a person incapable of resistance.
### Stupefies 
To ‘*stupefy*’ means to induce a state of stupor, to make stupid, groggy, or insensible; to dull the senses or faculties.

Stupefying can be achieved by a variety of means such as drugging a person or stunning them with a blunt instrument.

In ***R v Sturm**** *the defendant was convicted after administering alcohol, ecstasy, and other drugs to a number of male victims in order to dull their senses sufficiently to enable him to sexually violate them.

****CASELAW****
> R v Sturm
> To stupefy means to cause an effect on the mind or nervous system of a person which really seriously interferes with that persons mental or physical ability to act in any way which might hinder an intended crime.

### Renders unconscious any person 
To ‘*render*’ means to *‘cause to be’* or ‘*cause to become’*.

So, to render a person unconscious, the offender’s actions must cause the victim to lose consciousness.

There are no limits / specifics as to how this may be achieved. Ways in which unconsciousness could be achieved include for example, knocking them out with a blow to the head, strangling them or administering a noxious substance.

### By any violent means renders any person incapable of resistance
The term ‘*violent means’* includes the application of force that physically incapacitates a person, such as tying the victim’s hands and feet or inflicting debilitating injuries.

In ***R v Claridge*** the defendant tried to hit a prison officer on the head with an iron bar while attempting to escape from prison. The prison officer blocked the blow, but in the process, fell from the prison wall and sustained injuries, including a broken ankle.

Claridge argued unsuccessfully that he was not liable for an offence against section 191(1)(c) as the injuries had been caused by the fall and not by the iron bar.

However, the term “*violent means*” is not limited to physical violence and may include threats of violence, depending on the circumstances.

In ***R v Crossan**** *the defendant, intending to rape the victim, presented a loaded revolver at her and threatened to shoot her unless she submitted to sexual intercourse.

Crossan discussed that a mere threat **may not** in itself be sufficient to constitute “violent means”. However, when the person making the threat is brandishing a loaded revolver causing the victim to submit to his will in the belief that he will carry out his threat, **it could** **be said** that she was “rendered incapable of resistance by violent means”

The threat in these circumstances was just as effective as if she were physically incapable.

****CASELAW****
> R v Crossan
> Incapable of resistance includes a powerlessness of the will as well as a physical incapacity.

### A note on injury – Psychiatric injury
As in Section 189 an outcome for the Victim in section 191 (2) is “*injures any person*”. Psychiatric harm can be encompassed (with evidence) of injury for the purposes of both offences.

In ***Owen v Residential Health Management Unit,*** the Court highlighted that “*bodily harm*” may include psychiatric injury but does not include mere emotions such as fear, distress, panic, or a hysterical or nervous condition.

It may be necessary that the *“injury*” should amount to an identifiable clinical condition. Expert evidence will be required before an issue of psychiatric injury arises.

It may be that a charge under s189 or 191 (2) can be justified where there has been relevant psychiatric injury, but the victim is unaware of the assault. An example is ***R v Donaldson**** *where the defendant performed indecent acts on the victim while he was unconscious. The victim had no recollection of the events but, once he learned of them, they had a profound psychological impact on him.

*“Bodily harm”* in s188 includes really serious psychiatric injury identified as such by appropriate specialist evidence.

# UNDERSTANDING DMP FIREARMS 
# INTRODUCTION
These firearm sections create criminal liability based on the **intentions and actions** of the offender, rather than on the outcome or consequences of those actions.

So, unlike some other serious violence offences, it is not necessary under section 198 to prove that the victim suffered actual bodily harm; what is relevant is that the offender did one of the specified acts, with one of the specified intents.

Where actual bodily harm results, the appropriate charge would be one of wounding or injuring with intent under sections 188 or 189 of the Crimes Act 1961, or murder or manslaughter, depending on the outcome.

### Understanding Section 198- discharging a firearm or doing a dangerous act
Section 198 creates a variety of offences involving a firearm or dangerous act. There are  three options of mens rea:

The mens rea required for a charge under Section 198

- Intent to do grievous bodily harm.
- Intent to injure.
- Reckless disregard for the safety of others.
### The Mens Rea of Section 198 (1) With Intent to Do Grievous Bodily Harm 
Section 198(1) specifies the most serious offence of this section of the Crimes Act, which requires the most serious form of mens rea - the intent to seriously hurt another.

This specific intent has been canvassed in the serious assaults chapter, and in the context of section 198 means "aim, object, or purpose". (Simester and Brookbanks.)

In this way you must investigate and gather evidence that proves the offender's "purpose' is to cause another serious injury. If there is no evidence of this or of a lesser intent another offence should be considered.

Remember circumstantial evidence from the surrounding circumstances of the act can infer the intent of the offender and therefore must be investigated. (*R v Collister*)

Remember that grievous bodily harm can be defined simply as “*harm that is really serious*”.

So, the term ‘*grievous*’ refers to the degree of harm, rather than to the nature of it or how it was caused.

Grievous bodily harm will most commonly result from an assault, but not always; if the harm is really serious it does not matter how it was caused.

As long as the harm is serious, it need not involve life threatening or permanent injury.

****CASELAW****
> DPP v Smith
> ‘Bodily harm’ needs no explanation and ‘grievous’ means no more and no less than “really serious”.

### The Actus reus of section 198 (1) Discharge A firearm or do a dangerous act
Section 198 specifies three 'types' of act, which if carried out with the intent described above attract criminal liability. The acts can be roughly divided into three according to subsections (a), (b) and (c).

- Discharging a firearm at a person
- Sending / Delivering or putting into place, explosives or injurious substances
- Setting fire to property
### Section 198 (1) (a) Discharges a firearm
Subsection (a) specifies the act of discharging a firearm, airgun, or other similar weapon at any person.

To ‘*discharge*’ in this context means “*to fire or shoot*”.

### Intent to shoot victim 
Under section 198(1)(a), it is not sufficient for the prosecution to show a reckless discharge of the weapon in the general direction of the person who happened to be hit. There must be a general intention of the defendant that accompanies the act of discharging a firearm, to shoot at a particular person.

In R v Pekepo several members of the Mongrel Mob went to a local tavern where they believed members of the Black Power were socializing. There had been tensions earlier that day between the two gangs. While in the car park of the tavern, a member of the Mongrel Mob pointed a shotgun through the open door of a tavern and discharged it. As a result a male with no connection to either gang was injured and left with no sight in one eye and very little in the other.

****CASELAW****
> R v Pekepo
> A reckless discharge of a firearm in the general direction of a passer-by who happens to be hit is not sufficient proof.  An intention to shoot that person must be established.

### At any person
These offences are gender neutral, and the fact that the victim is a ‘*person*’ is generally accepted by judicial notice or proved by circumstantial evidence. The age of the victim is not relevant.

### Sends or delivers an explosive or injurious substance 
A second actus reus specified under section 198 involves specific types of handling of explosives or injurious substances with one of the three intents described above.

The terms ‘*send’* and ‘*deliver*’ take their ordinary meaning and may include situations where the victim receives a dangerous thing by mail or courier; as in the case of a “*letter bomb*”.

### Explosive
The term ‘*explosive*’ is defined by statute in Section 2 of the Arms Act, and reproduced below.

****LEGISLATION****
> Section 2 (1) Arms Act 1983
> Interpretation
> Explosive—
> (a)	Means any substance or mixture or combination of substances which in its normal state is capable either of decomposition at such rapid rate as to result in an explosion or of producing a pyrotechnic effect; and
> (b)	Without limiting paragraph (a) of this definition, includes gunpowder, nitroglycerine, dynamite, guncotton, blasting powder, fulminate of mercury or of other metals, coloured flares, fog signals, fuses, rockets, percussion caps, detonators, cartridges, and ammunition of all descriptions; and
> (c)	Without limiting paragraph (a) or paragraph (b) of this definition, includes any device, contrivance, or article, which uses any substance or mixture or combination of substances to which paragraph (a) or paragraph (b) of this definition applies as an integral part of it for the purposes of producing an explosion or a ballistic or pyrotechnic effect; but does not include a firearm; and
> (d)	Does not include any firework as defined in section 2 of the Hazardous Substances and New Organisms Act 1996.

### Injurious substance or device 
The term *‘injurious substance or device’* covers a range of things capable of causing harm to a person.

### Example:
Examples of these include:

- A letter containing anthrax powder that is mailed to a political target.
- Toxic chemicals or organic material couriered or put in food
- Sending someone or putting in place an infected syringe where it will 'stick' someone
- hiding razor blades in food
- Boiling water has been held to be a ’destructive’ substance in previous Court decisions.
### Example:
In the case of ***R v Fitzgerald,*** the accused set up a perimeter fence around a gang headquarters, consisting of barbed wire connected to an electric fence unit.

His conviction was quashed as the unit was not operating at the time, however it was acknowledged that, if plugged into the mains power supply, the fence would have been a device likely to cause injury.

### When offence is complete 
Although offences under section 198(1)(a) require the actual discharge of a firearm at a person, under section 198(1)(b) it is **not** necessary for an explosion to occur or the injurious device to have harmed anyone.

The offence is complete when an explosive or an injurious substance or device is sent, delivered, or put in place. However:

**The substance must have the capacity to explode or cause injury.**

### Example:
In the case of ***R v Sheppard,*** a bottle containing gunpowder and with a wick in the neck did not come within the words “gunpowder or other explosive substance”, unless the fuse was lighted when the bottle was thrown, though it was unnecessary that the light should pass from the fuse to the powder. Merely to throw a bottle containing gunpowder which could not cause an explosion was not within the relevant statute.

In this respect a chemical substance that was a necessary component for an explosive but in and of itself was not harmful to a person, would not suffice for this offence.

### Sets fire to any property 
The final actus reus specified by section 198 is setting fire to any property, accompanied by one of the mens rea of either intent to do grievous bodily harm, intent to injure or with reckless disregard for the safety of others.

Damages property by fire is discussed in your arson lesson note, and depending on the circumstances a charge under Arson section 267 (1) (a) Damaging any property knowing that danger to life was likely, may be more appropriate

### Section 198A – Understanding using a Firearm against law enforcement officer
Section 198A creates a variety of firearm offences directed towards law enforcement officers. While this discussion will center around constables, the section includes all law enforcement officers, including those involved with traffic enforcement, prison officers and any person acting on their behalf.

### The actus reus of section 198A – using a firearm against a law enforcement officer 
The actus reus element involves three optional acts, summarised as:

- Using a firearm in any manner
- against any constable
- Acting in the course of their duty
### Uses in any manner whatever 
The primary meaning of the word ‘use’ in relation to a firearm is to fire it, but the words “*in any manner whatever”* widen the definition to include a range of acts that stop short of actually shooting at an officer. It is sufficient if the defendant has handled or manipulated the firearm so as to convey an implied threat of its further use against the police officer. It is not necessary that the firearm was presented at the officer or that it be discharged.

Although possession of a firearm alone does not in itself constitute ‘use’, handling or manipulating the firearm in a manner that conveys an implied threat may suffice.

In ***R v Swain*** the defendant, when confronted by a constable, removed a sawn-off shotgun from a bag, saying “*don’t be stupid*”, causing the officer to fear for his safety. Although he didn’t point the gun at the officer, it was held that his actions in taking the shotgun out of the bag in plain view of the constable amounted to the ‘use’ of the firearm in an intimidating and threatening manner against him.

****CASELAW****
> R v Swain (1992) 8 CRNZ 657
> To deliberately or purposely remove a sawn-off shotgun from a bag after being confronted by or called upon by a police constable amounts to a use of that firearm within the meaning of s 198A Crimes Act 1961.

In ***Police v Parker*** the defendant, when confronted by police, produced a loaded sawn-off shotgun, and aimed it at a constable. A struggle ensued and the weapon was again aimed at the officer with the offender’s finger on the trigger. The weapon was finally thrown to the ground without being fired.

****CASELAW****
> Police v Parker
> “Use in any manner whatever” is to contemplate a situation short of actually firing the weapon and to present a rifle too, I think, is equivalent to or means the same thing.

The words “*in any manner whatever*” can also include the use of firearms in ways in which they are not normally used, such as where a firearm is used as a club.

### Constable

### Acting in the course of duty 
Under section 198A the officer must be “*acting in the course of his or her duty*”.

Police duties arise under both statute and common law, and in general terms they include protecting life and property, preventing and detecting offences, apprehending offenders, and keeping the peace.

The term includes every lawful act a constable does while on duty and may include acts done where the circumstances create a professional obligation for a constable to exercise policing duties while off duty.

However, an officer who is acting unlawfully, for example using excessive force during an arrest, interfering with a person’s liberty without legal justification, or trespassing on private property without authority, cannot be said to be “*acting in the course of his or her duty*”.

### The mens rea of section 198A – using a firearm against a law enforcement officer
The mens rea required for a charge under Section 198A (1) and (2) include

- Knowledge that a person is a constable.
- Being reckless as to whether or not a person is a constable.
- Intent to resist the lawful arrest or detention of self or another.
### Knowing (refer basic concepts module)
The defendant must know the victim is a police officer and know that the officer is acting in the course of his or her duty or be reckless as to those facts.

Knowledge is defined below and involves a belief or a state of mind that is more than suspicion. It also involves a subjective and objective view of the offender’s actual knowledge and what they could have reasonably been expected to know.

For example,

- Firstly did the offender know – that is did the offender have a level of belief that the person they are using the firearm against is a police officer.
- Secondly – would an ordinary New Zealander have known in the same circumstances as the offender have had a level of belief that the person was a police officer.
****CASELAW****
> ***R v Crooks***
> *Knowledge means actual knowledge or belief in the sense of having no real doubt that the person assisted was a party to the relevant offence. Mere suspicion of their involvement in the offence is insufficient.*

****LEGISLATION****
> Simester and Brookbanks: Principles of Criminal Law
> Knowing means “knowing or correctly believing” … the defendant may believe something wrongly but cannot “know” something that is false.

### Reckless (refer basic legal concepts module)
Reckless has the same meaning as other reckless mindsets established in other sections in this module. If a person uses a firearm against a police officer, and while not having a firm belief that they are a police officer, the fact that they are reckless as to this fact, is an important consideration for the investigation.

Turning their mind to the ‘real possibility’ that the person is a police officer and deciding to act regardless is enough to establish criminality.

If the defendant’s knowledge or recklessness in this regard cannot be proved beyond reasonable doubt, a charge should be considered under one of the other related provisions depending on the circumstances.

### Intent to resist lawful arrest or detention 
It must be proved that the defendant knew an attempt was being made to arrest or detain him or the person he was assisting.

In ***Fisher v R*** the defendant had discharged a firearm several times in a building but refused to come out when called on by the AOS. He fired a further shot from the building, although there was insufficient evidence that it was directed at police. While it was accepted that Fisher must have known he had committed offences for which he was liable to arrest, there was no evidence of an attempt to arrest or detain him at the time he fired the shot.

****CASELAW****
> Fisher v R (1988) 3 CRNZ 250
> It is necessary in order to establish a charge under section 198A (2) for the Crown to prove that the accused knew someone was attempting to arrest or detain him because otherwise the element of mens rea of intending to resist lawful arrest or detention cannot be established.

Note that subsection (2) does not require the use of a firearm against a law enforcement officer specifically, so the firearm can be used in any manner against any person, as long as the offender has the necessary intent to resist arrest or detention.
