# OVERALL PURPOSE 
“Those that complete the DDP will have a thorough ***understanding*** of criminal law and procedure in New Zealand and be able to apply this understanding to produce ***evidentially sufficient* **investigations.”

### Introduction
This Document relates to the Detective Development Course Academic Program. While practical aspects of investigation come under this umbrella, practical skills in investigation are assessed through the Workplace Evidence Portfolio, completed by trainees over the duration of the program.

This document is to be read as ‘best practice’ and a guide for trainers to assess the knowledge attained by the trainee detective throughout the 30-month program.

The program is divided into three parts and is guided by the principles of graduated learning.

### Scaffold of the Detective Development Academic Program
The three parts of the DDP are as follows.

**Part One: **Pre-program Learning

An on-line module covering fundamental Basic Concepts and definitions the learner will encounter throughout the program.

**Part Two: **Detective Development Course (DDC)

A four-week intensive course that focuses on foundational knowledge and skills of evidentially sufficient investigations.

**Part Three:** Detective Modular Program (DMP)

A self-managed program of 10 modular courses focussing on additional and advanced knowledge and skills of evidentially sufficient investigation.

The purpose and intent of each part is explained below.

# PURPOSE AND INTENT – PART ONE
### Course Purpose
The purpose of the course is to introduce basic foundational knowledge prior to beginning the Detective Development Program (DDP). The foundational knowledge contained in this course is intended to be an introduction only. The Course is broken into three parts,

- **Investigative Mindset:**
- Critical thinking through a systematic thought process, decision making and objectivity.
- **Evidential Sufficiency - Legal Concepts:**
- Foundational knowledge required for evidential sufficiency in criminal law and procedure.
- **Primary Criminal Offence Elements:**
- Parent Offences of the Detective Development Course (DDC)
### Course Design 
The course is also designed to introduce the following principles,

- Graduated Learning – Basic knowledge and understanding of the Investigative Mindset and Evidential Sufficiency
- Rote learning is limited to elements of parent offences and universal case laws only.
- Emphasis on understanding and application of the concepts taught.
- Testing for understanding and application (Types of Question phraseology, testing and marking).
- To introduce assessment standards (Introduction of “own words” “own example” etc.), and the ‘from excellence perspective’.
- To introduce a base-level test to address learning or functional difficulties prior to attending the DDC (can be used in one-on-ones.).
### Course outcomes
The course will provide trainees with a base level of knowledge prior to starting the Programme,

- Ensuring everyone has a consistent and fair level of knowledge when entering the program.
- Eliminating duplication of teaching of the same legal concepts throughout the DDC and program (for example recklessness, knowing, consent etc.).
- Allowing for greater teaching time to be devoted to ***application*** of law and procedure throughout the DDC.
- The course will eliminate the culture of wholesale rote learning and surface level-learning specifically prior to the Detective Development Course. It will be replaced by a culture of learning to ***understand and apply*** the concepts learnt in a practical way.
### Course Assessment 
To test basic understanding of Investigative Mindset and Evidential Sufficiency.

To assess recognition of elements and universal definitions and case law.

To introduce questions that require application of those concepts and understanding.

To mark to the standard required on the DDP (Those sitting as the PPL level are not expected to be reaching excellent standard.)

Assessment Design

- This course will be made up of two question types, multi choice and short answer.
- It will consist of 30 marks and will take the trainee 60 minutes to complete.
- Time is allocated on the basis of 2 minutes per mark.
- There is an additional 10 minutes also added for reading time.
- The pass mark is 18/30 (60%).

# PURPOSE AND INTENT – PART TWO (DDC)
### Course Purpose
The purpose of the course is to introduce trainees to core legislation and practices of an investigator. This is a foundation course that is four weeks in length covering the concepts of evidential sufficiency and how to apply these to an investigation.

### Course Design 
The course is also designed to introduce the following principles,

- Graduated Learning, core offences are learnt on the DDP and expanded upon during the DMP.
- Rote learning is limited to elements of parent offences and universal case laws only.
- Emphasis on ***understanding and application*** of the concepts taught.
- Testing for understanding and application (Types of Question phraseology, testing and marking).
- To continue assessment standards of the PPL (Use of “own words” “own example” application to practical investigation etc.) and application of the “from an excellence perspective” point of view.
### Course outcomes
The course will provide trainees with a thorough foundational level of knowledge prior to starting in a CIB position at the conclusion of the course

- Ensuring everyone has basic understanding of core concepts required for evidential sufficiency and importantly can apply this understanding when conducting an investigation.
- Ensuring everyone has basic understanding of legislation and practical procedures to ensure that evidence is collected in an evidentially sufficient manner.
- Introduction of basic  criminal procedure through practical skillsets (such as crime scene processing, interviewing,  investigation techniques etc.)
- Eliminating duplication of teaching of the same legal concepts throughout the DDC and program (for example recklessness, knowing, consent etc.).

# PURPOSE AND INTENT – PART THREE (DMP)
### Course Purpose
The purpose of the course is to expand trainees’ knowledge of core legislation and practices expected as an investigator. Foundational knowledge is built on and new material introduced

### Course Design 
The course is also designed to introduce the following principles,

- The learning program is self-managed by the trainee under district supervision. It consists of 12 learning modules and accompanying assessment.
- Can be done in the learner’s own time.
- Each learning module continues on or introduces new offences under the categories established on the DDC.
- Each learning module is available as a hardcopy version of the learning material and as an online version of the hard copy, both versions having the same curriculum for assessment The on-line version mirrors the hardcopy version but has the addition of interactive material, such as videos, podcasts, articles, and knowledge tests.
- Limited rote learning and assessment for understanding and application is continued in the same way as parts one and two of the program. (same question phraseology, marking etc.
- Limited testing on basic concepts covered in Part one and two unless more complex assessment required. More specific assessment on understanding and application of new material (Types of Question phraseology, testing and marking).
### Course outcomes
The course will provide trainees with expanded knowledge of the concepts and offences learnt on the DDC.

- Each learning module allows the trainee time to review the parent crime and new material over about an eight-week period allowing for a more detailed examination of the topic than Part two of the DDC allowed.
- Ensuring a well-rounded knowledge of the major offences under criminal law and  understanding of the law of criminal procedure.
- Ensuring a well-rounded knowledge and understanding of new material such as the law of evidence, serious crime investigation, investigation of defences and search powers.
### Course Assessment 
- This course consists of one assessment per module.
- The Assessment follows the same format as Part One and Two of the DDP, with the exception that some modules will require two criminal liabilities be asked. A practical question worth 10 marks will take the place of the 3rd liability.
- Each exam trainee has three attempts to pass the assessment.
- If a trainee fails their second attempt they will be required to get their Crime Manager permission to sit a third attempt. They will also be offered assistance to give them the best possible chance of success.
- The exams are 120 minutes plus 10 minutes reading time, making them 130 minutes in total.
- Time is allocated on the basis of 2 minutes per mark.

# LEARNING OUTCOMES
### **Kidnapping and Trafficking Module**
By the end of this module, trainees will be able to:

**Explain the legal framework** surrounding the offences of abduction, kidnapping, and trafficking under the Crimes Act 1961 (sections 208–210, 98C–98D), including relevant case law.**Identify and differentiate the elements** of actus reus and mens rea for kidnapping, abduction, and trafficking offences.**Recognise the specific intents** associated with each offence, such as intent to hold for ransom, sexual connection, or deprive a guardian of custody.**Apply case law precedents** (e.g. *R v Wellard*, *R v Pryce*, *R v Mohi*) to factual scenarios to support investigative and charging decisions.**Assess evidential sufficiency** in investigations involving consent obtained by fraud or duress, especially in cases involving minors or vulnerable victims.**Distinguish between people trafficking and migrant smuggling**, focusing on differences in consent, purpose, coercion, and legal implications.**Describe the inter-agency approach** to trafficking investigations and the roles of various government agencies involved.**Evaluate investigative strategies** (reactive, proactive, and disruptive) and apply them appropriately to real-world trafficking and smuggling situations.**Demonstrate awareness of legal defences**, including statutory defences (e.g., good faith claims) and the irrelevance of consent or age belief in certain circumstances.**Interpret and apply key terminology**, such as “unlawfully”, “taking away”, “detains”, “possesses”, and “specific intent”, as defined within the module.### **Robbery and Blackmail Module**
By the end of this module, trainees will be able to:

**Explain the legal definitions and elements** of robbery, aggravated robbery, and blackmail under the Crimes Act 1961 (sections 234, 235, and 237).**Differentiate robbery from theft and assault**, identifying how violence, threats, or immediate force alter the offence classification.**Interpret the term “without claim of right”** and its significance in robbery and blackmail contexts.**Apply case law and precedent** (e.g. *R v Maihi*, *R v Broughton*, *R v Butler*) to evaluate evidential sufficiency and appropriate charges.**Recognise the evidential requirements** for proving intent, threats, and the use of force in robbery and blackmail scenarios.**Assess the legal and investigative considerations** when dealing with aggravated robbery, including the use of weapons and accomplice liability.**Investigate blackmail offences**, understanding the role of illegitimate threats and the absence of lawful justification.**Critically analyse real-life case studies** to identify effective investigative techniques and common evidential challenges.### **Serious Assaults and Firearms Module**
By the end of this module, trainees will be able to:

**Explain the legal elements and distinctions** between wounding with intent, injuring with intent, assaults with weapons, and other serious assault offences under the Crimes Act 1961 (e.g. sections 188–202C).**Interpret and apply key case law** (e.g. *R v Rapana and Murray*, *R v Mwai*) to evaluate intent and evidential sufficiency in serious assault prosecutions.**Assess the presence of specific intent or reckless disregard**, and its relevance in determining the appropriate charge for violent offending.**Distinguish between different levels of injury**, including wounding, grievous bodily harm, and physical versus psychological harm.**Identify the elements of firearms offences** and offences involving offensive weapons, including the relevance of possession, purpose, and use.**Investigate and evaluate offences involving police and prison officers**, recognising the enhanced protections these roles attract.**Apply tactical and legal knowledge** to complex assault and firearms scenarios, including the appropriate selection of charges based on facts and available evidence.**Demonstrate a working understanding of evidential challenges**, including proof of weapon possession, shared intent, and the credibility of witnesses.# GRADUATED LEARNING
The DDP is to follow a graduated learning structure. Here learning is structured in incremental steps and uses the educational learning theory of Bloom’s Taxonomy.

The steps are as follows,

1. Remembering

2. Understanding

3. Applying

4. Analysing

5. Evaluating

6. Creating

Trainees begin with foundational knowledge and skills and as the master each level before they progress to more complex and advance learning and workplace experience. The goal of graduated learning is to scaffold the learning and assessment process, allowing learners to build on prior knowledge. Graduated learning is a step-by -step process, where the trainee gains confidence as they master increasingly challenging content, making leaning manageable and reducing the likelihood of the trainee becoming overwhelmed.

The DDP seeks to ensure that its trainees build a solid understanding which builds to more challenging material complimented by their growing experience in the workplace as Detective Constables.

Graduated learning allows a thorough and comprehensive learning experience for the trainee, while ensuring the Police’s obligation to produce competent Detectives thoroughly versed in the understanding of evidential sufficiency.

### Application of Graduated Learning on the DDP
Graduated learning impacts assessment on any educational program. While the academic curriculum grows steadily, there is no change to the standard of assessment. Those at the start of the Detective Development Program have minimal exposure to the knowledge or skills required to practice as a Detective. As such, they should not be expected to achieve excellent marks (80-90%).

There is an entrenched culture in NZ Police assessment practice that a pass or ‘average’ mark is on par with an 80 – 90% pass. Some of this can be attributed to the 80% pass, on the DMP as well as the structure of the assessments themselves.

There are several outcomes from this practice and culture that impact the program as a whole. The reality is that candidate’s marks on the DDP are artificially raised. In turn this reduces the ability to differentiate between high achieving and borderline students. It gives false confidence to trainees, creates credibility concerns regarding the integrity and rigor of the assessment process and creates institutional pressure to mark at a higher level.

The expectation of the DDP is that it will follow the science of natural distribution and markers are expected to understand and foster this expectation and culture.

### Types of Assessment Questions
The DDP will be assessed with the same question type throughout, regardless of which part the trainee is undertaking on the program.  This will maintain consistency and allow trainees to clearly observe and manage their performance.

# CRIMINAL LIABILITY
### Purpose
To assess higher-order cognitive skills, decision-making and understanding of what is required or what will affect evidential sufficiency in the prosecution of specific offences.

To assess recognition, comprehension, and application of information in a scenario that will demonstrate understanding of the evidential sufficiency required for the prosecution of specific offences.

### Liabilities
Scenarios will be offence specific however will no longer be focused solely on elements. Any aspect of a scenario-individual’s criminal liability and the evidential sufficiency of a prosecution against them must be considered for credit by markers.

### Format
Scenarios are between 400 and 600 words.  This allows the trainee to produce more in-depth answers and an opportunity to demonstrate decision-making, comprehensive understanding of investigative mindset and evidential sufficiency rather than elements and rote learned definitions and case law.

Scenarios will include detail such as, but not limited to

- Elements
- Legal concepts
- Legal tests
- Case Law
- Corroborating evidence
- Evidence in the scenario to prove the above
- Defenses or facts that may impact evidential sufficiency and investigations.
There will no longer be teaching of a specific structure for the liability. Any form of answer will be acceptable. The previous bullet point or rote learnt definitions/case law will more likely be a hindrance on the time the candidate has to convey understanding to the marker. The previous bullet point elements or case law list will not receive credit in and of themselves.

### Marking
Liabilities will be marked out of 10 and under the guidance of a matrix.

While the candidate will be directed to analyze evidential sufficiency of a specific offence, there is room for alternative charging answers if argued well. There is no answer guide or model answer per se. A marker cannot disregard a candidate’s choice of section, subsection, or legislative choice, if it is not what they would personally charge, or is different to the majority of answers. Markers must give credit to candidate choices if argued well.

For example, Section 267 (1) (c) has the legislative choice of Arson with intent to obtain a benefit **or** cause a loss. A particular scenario may allow an argument for either case to be mounted, and as such, will be awarded credit.

### Expectations
The criminal liability is expected to be a long answer that makes an argument around the criminal liability of a person in regard to a particular category of offence. Criminal Liabilities are not an exercise in choosing the ‘correct’ offence as has been traditionally the case, where zeros are given if a candidate chooses an offence to articulate that differs from the model answer.

Decision making will be assessed in deciding what specific crime within that category of offence to charge with – and why, based on the evidence from the scenario. As in the reality of investigations, there may be more than one offence that is available for candidates to discuss.

### Rote Learning
Rote learning serves an important function in the learning process, however, is an entrenched culture in CIB Training to the detriment of the understanding and application of the rote knowledge. Credit will no longer be given for rote learnt elements or case laws.

Individually worked answers are compulsory. Answers will be run through plagiarism databases to ensure the candidate produces individual work. This will be particularly pertinent to those trainees on the DMP who sit assessment remotely.

# PRACTICAL ASSESSMENT
### Purpose
To offer the trainee an opportunity to be assessed on basic investigative practice. To enable the trainee to demonstrate their knowledge of learned (local best) practice post DDC.

### Practical Assessment
Learning practical investigations specific to the module topic will take the form of an online course / lesson which will complement the module and which the trainee will be required to complete.

Some module topics traditionally have had a practical component. These topics have previously relied on the rote learning of documentary lists or the police manual. The previous custom of learning lists for practical questions does not assess decision making or individual learned experience post completion of the DDC. They are often outdated and out of line with local best practice.

Assessment will reflect a trainee’s ability to approach a practical scenario in various and equally valid ways, reflecting the individual, and district differences to the approach of practical investigations, as well as allowing the trainee to demonstrate their own knowledge and learnings in a practical way.

###  Online Lesson
The practical online lesson will simply be required to be completed by the trainee, however there will be at least one 10-mark question that will form part of the module assessment test. The question will be designed to be open ended and allow the trainee to show their understanding and decision making in practical policing. Practical questions will include topics such as

- Initial action / crime scene examination.
- Appreciations / decision making.
- Exhibit handling.
- Note taking.
- Safety.
- Powers.
### Marking
Marking will be marked out of 10 and under the guidance of a matrix.

There will be no list or rigid model answer. This is an opportunity for trainees to draw on their knowledge and experience to demonstrate best practice in investigations.

### Liability Explanation / Instruction
**LIABILITY QUESTIONS (10 marks)**

### Instructions
You will be given a scenario to read. Read it carefully. The scenario will contain sufficient detail to enable you to demonstrate your understanding and practical application based on what you have learned.

**You will be asked:**

The question will be simple.

For example, in relation to Arson, discuss John’s liability and the evidential sufficiency of a prosecution against him.

The word discuss is purposefully broad. It includes the concepts of ‘examine’, ‘explore’, ‘analyze’, ‘consider’, ‘talk through’ and ‘review’ to name a few.

**How you will answer**

It is important to note that previously the DDP has required formulaic or rote-learnt answers. This will no longer gain a pass. Credit will be awarded to those trainees who are able to demonstrate their understanding of law, case law, definitions and crucially how the scenario presented, demonstrates those things in terms of evidential sufficiency.

How you format your answer is entirely over to you. See these questions as an opportunity for you to demonstrate your;

- Recognition of criminal liability in a scenario, based on your knowledge of the criminal law of NZ.
- Recognition of the evidence that would support or oppose that person's liability in a sound prosecution.
Another way to view the question is to view the scenario in front of you as an investigator reading a file - evaluating the evidence in front of you and analyzing if there is a sound legal basis and accompanying evidence to charge an offender. This analysis is based on what you have learned from the beginning of the Detective Development Course.

Your answer should draw on your knowledge of the content material from DDC lessons, syndicate work and this module, to provide explanations, evidence, and examples from the scenario to justify a prosecution. The best answers will also gain credit for using accurate legal terminology and using additional supporting information that may support or affect a prosecution. For example, corroborating evidence, defenses, or relevant legal tests.

It is important to note that:

- Re-stating the scenario will not gain credit.
- Listing case laws only will not gain credit.
- Listing elements only will not gain you credit.
You may have a choice of offences to choose from. For example, in Arson s267 (1) (c) you may believe there is sufficient evidence for both ‘causes loss’ and ‘obtain a benefit’. If the evidence supports it - the choice is yours. Markers are looking for you to justify your choice in the manner described above.

### Marks
You will be required to complete 3 liability questions on the DDC each with a mark allocation of 10.

You will be required to complete 2 liability questions in the DMP test each with a mark allocation of 10.

Criminal Liabilities will be marked out of 10.

To pass, a DDC trainee requires an achieved and the messaging required is that this is a strong mark for their initial level. This is not to say that exceptional answers cannot be given at this level to those that deserve them.

Accumulated knowledge, study and experience is expected to be reflected in the DMP program. Giving Excellence grade for lower standard content creates artificial and meaningless results and damages the integrity of the DDP.

### Criminal Liability Example Answer

I believe there is sufficient evidence for Alice to be criminally liable for the offence of Arson Section 267 (1) (a) Crimes Act 1961. The elements of this section are Intentionally or recklessly, damages by fire or explosive, any property if he or she knows or ought to know danger to life is likely.  The actus reus of this offence is recklessly damages by fire any property.

Recklessness is when you deliberately take a risk. It is a form of intent where while there may have been no direct intention for the consequence to happen, the person ran a risk anyway. Tipple describes recklessness as a conscious appreciation of risk and a deliberate decision to run that risk.

Alice has recklessly started the fire as she walked away not realizing the rubbish was smoldering. The fact that the Alice had no direct intention of setting fire to the cinema and did not know about the fire when she walked away does not eliminate her liability. Therefore, can only be liable under the element of recklessly.

Cameron v R provides a test that can be applied to the situation to see if there is enough evidence of reckless intention. The first part is subjective – from the persons point of view. It is subjective in that the person must recognize the risk as possible and decided to run the risk. In the scenario Alice watches rubbish ignite that is “at the base of the wall of the cinema” Although the rubbish goes out, she would have recognised the possibility that due to the position of the little fires she was creating, wall of the cinema would be at risk of igniting. An interview with Gerry the security guard would be good evidence of the conversation he had with Alice, drawing her attention to the risk. This would be hard to refute in evidence. *(Collister)*

The second part of the test in Cameron is objective and looks at whether the actions were reasonable. All fire needs to be extinguished properly and a reasonable person would know this. Would a reasonable person have taken the risk? There is evidence in the scenario that an independent person (Gerry) recognised the risk and Alice warned about it.

Cameron also says that if there is no social utility in the actions of the person then running the risk subjectively is reckless. In Alice’s case the scenario says that she is bored there is no social utility in flicking matches onto the ground like James Bond. Therefore, understanding the risk and continuing was unreasonable and meets the threshold of reckless intent.

The next element is damages by fire. This requires permanent or temporary physical harm (R v Archer) The cinema complex is described as engulfed in a large fire. This would result in damage - extensive permanent physical harm. As well as this proof of damage by fire requires clear causation that the damage was caused by the fire lit by Alice. In this case the rubbish lit by Alice’s match game has caused the wall of the cinema to catch fire and cause the significant damage to the complex.

Section 267(1)(a) requires that any property is damaged – section 2 CA1961 defines property as “any real or personal property - tangible property which is owned by Hoyts. Although not required the cinema complex would be immovable property – fixed to the ground, not easily movable and of a high financial worth. There is evidential sufficiency of the actus reus of Arson.

The final element which needs to be proved is knowing or ought to know danger to life is likely.  In this section putting others’ lives in danger is the aggravating factor and requires the specific mens rea of knowledge on the part of the offender. Knowledge is a form of intent. The courts have defined knowledge in R v Crooks as having no real doubt. Alice has watched patrons walking in and out of the cinema complex. She cannot argue that she has no real doubt that human lives weren’t in danger.

The courts have also attached a test for knowledge that can guide investigators as to whether the offender has the requisite knowledge to meet this evidential requirement.

The test for knowledge also finds Alice liable as the evidence shows that subjectively she knew that her actions could risk endangering human life - as she watches people walk in. Objectively the next part of the test asks whether the reasonable person have recognized the risk? The answer to this is yes – the average new Zealander would recognize that flicking lit matches that ignite rubbish next to a build poses a risk that the building would catch light and endanger the lives of people in or around that building.

Likely to ensue means that Alice merely had to know there was a mere chance or a risk of danger to human life other than herself (Smith) that an ordinary person would have recognised. A reasonable person would recognize the chance and see danger to life as likely should the fire take hold. The fact that the complex was evacuated is evidence that there was danger to human life.

The evidence of the actus reus and mens rea for the offence of Arson danger to life is strong.
